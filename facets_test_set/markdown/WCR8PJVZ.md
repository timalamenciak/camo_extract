# Assessing Simple Versus Complex Restoration Strategies for Industrially Disturbed Forests

Bronwyn Rayfield,1,2 Madhur Anand,1,3 and Sophie Laurence<sup>1</sup>

# Abstract

Assessing the community-level consequences of ecological restoration treatments is essential to guide future restoration efforts. We compared the vegetation composition and species richness of restored sites that received a range of restoration treatments and those of unrestored sites that experienced varying levels of disturbance. Our study was conducted in the industrially degraded landscape surrounding Sudbury, Ontario, Canada. The Great Lakes–St. Lawrence Forest once present in this area was degraded through logging, mining, and smelting activities beginning in the late 1800s until restoration of the most visibly degraded areas began in 1974. Restoration treatments ranged from simple abiotic enhancements to complex, multistage revegetation treatments using native and non-native species, which included fertilizing, spreading of ground dolomitic limestone, understory seeding, and tree planting. Canonical correspondence analysis was used to determine which restoration treatments explained differences in the community structure among sites. We found that native understory vascular species richness was similar in restored sites that received more complex restoration treatments and unrestored sites that were mildly disturbed; however, the role of planted trees and non-native species in the restored communities remains unclear. Understory vascular seeding played a key role in determining community composition of vascular understory and overstory communities, but the time since restoration commenced was a more important factor for nonvascular communities because they received no direct biotic enhancements. The use of non-native species in the vascular seed mix seems to be slowly encouraging the colonization of native species, but non-natives continue to dominate restored sites 25 years after restoration began.

Key words: complexity, disturbance, monitoring, multivariate, ordination, pollution, restoration, reference system.

# Introduction

The restoration and conservation of forest biota has become increasingly important in the face of large-scale forest degradation (Urbanska et al. 2000; Young 2000). Ecologists confronted with the task of reconstructing a native forest must take into account several considerations including the cost and timespan for change and, consequently, the intensity and nature of restoration strategy. Valuable knowledge can be gleaned from past efforts at ecological restoration. In some instances, removal of the degrading influence may be all that is needed to encourage recovery (Berger 1993; Brown 1999), particularly in the presence of a viable seed source and a long-term time frame. However, if the system has crossed a threshold at which point the disturbance affects critical ecosystem processes, then a more active restoration approach may be required to promote successional processes operating on a time scale that is compatible with human needs (Gilbert & Anderson 1998; Golley 1999).

categorized according to the level of management intervention. One-dimensional restoration efforts (e.g., involving single abiotic variables, single species, or single applications) are common but may be limited in their efficacy because recovery processes are multidimensional (e.g., Van de Koppel et al. 1997; Nystrom et al. 2000; Adams et al. 2002). Observed nonlinear or threshold responses to management and environmental factors could indicate that ecosystems exist in alternate stable states that are resilient to simplistic, one-dimensional restoration strategies (Hobbs & Norton 1996; Suding et al. 2004). Therefore, more complex, multidimensional restoration efforts that employ a combination of methods (e.g., site preparation, establishment of plantation forests, and control of non-native species) may provide a more rapid and effective intervention strategy (Fule´ et al. 2002). Similarly, one-dimensional assessment of the recovery of ecosystems (e.g., that exclusively target recovery of one species) does not serve to reflect state of recovery of the community as a whole (Anand et al. 2002; Anand & Desrochers 2004). Even the commonly studied community parameters, such as biomass, abundance, and richness (Holl & Cairns 2002), may provide an incomplete, or even misleading, picture of community structure when studied alone (Lawton et al. 1998; Anand & Desrochers 2004). A more complex forest restoration technique emerging from tropical research involves not only removing abiotic constraints but also establishing plantation forests (Jussi et al. 1995; Haggar et al. 1997; Carnevale & Montagnini 2002; Feyera et al. 2002). The establishment of tree plantations in degraded areas may facilitate regeneration of native species that could not otherwise establish by removal of abiotic constraints alone (Lugo 1992a). Both native and non-native plantations can act as catalyzers for the invasion of native species by changing understory microclimatic conditions, increasing vegetation structural complexity, and developing litter and humus layers (Parrotta et al. 1997; Cusack & Montagnini 2004). However, the use of non-native species in ecological restoration is contentious (d'Antonio & Meyerson 2002; Hoddle 2004; Ewel & Putz 2004) in large part due to the potential for these non-native species to become invasive and hence problematic in surrounding areas. Perhaps, the best known example is that of Robinia pseudoacacia, which is considered a pest in several areas where it was once used to improve microclimatic conditions on mined lands (Ashby 1987).

The efficacy of removing abiotic constraints versus establishing tree plantations to serve as ''nurse crops'' in temperate regions has not been studied as extensively. However, it has been shown that small fields in close proximity (160 m) to forest edge can quickly become diverse forests without supplemental tree planting (Kruse & Groninger 2003). Mixed plantations of native and nonnative tree species established at low densities (120 trees/ ha) further facilitated the invasion of light-seeded species within 50 years in small old-field bottomlands in Kentucky (Shear et al. 1996). Non-native plantations of Austrian pine in France were shown to act as a nurse stand, enabling the return of native broad-leaved trees and herbaceous species (Vallauri et al. 2002). This study confirmed the importance of low-density plantations to improve their role as ''seed traps'' and advocated tree thinning to promote the true restoration of native biodiversity (Vallauri et al. 2002). Bossuyt and Hermy (2000) suggest that, in addition to plantation, forest understory species should also be introduced, and Cox et al. (2004) recommend sowing native, understory seed mix followed by irrigation and rolling of seeds into soil. Hartman and McCarthy (2004) further recommend the eradication of a dominant, nonnative understory species to increase success of native tree seedlings.

The objective of our study is to assess restoration strategies of differing complexity and compare these to unrestored sites that have been subjected to varying levels of disturbance. We examine species richness and composition of nonvascular, vascular, and overstory vegetation. We hypothesize that increasing restoration complexity will produce forests more similar in species richness (but not necessarily composition) to a mildly disturbed, unrestored forest and substantially different from severely disturbed, unrestored forest. We also hypothesize that the complexity of restoration strategies will be reflected in all forest strata. This work is important for defining the most appropriate strategies for restoration and for guiding intervention practices in restored ecosystems. It also contributes to the ongoing debate of whether natural systems should be left to recover on their own or whether there should be human intervention of any kind (Brown 1999; Kirmer & Mahn 2001; Prach et al. 2001).

## Methods

Vegetation surveys were conducted in naturally recovering and restored sites within the industrially damaged landscape of Sudbury, Ontario, Canada (lat 46.5-N, long 81.0-W). Sudbury was originally surrounded by Great Lakes–St. Lawrence Forest, a transitional forest zone between the Boreal coniferous forest to the north and the Carolinian deciduous forest to the south, characterized by Red pine (Pinus resinosa Aiton) and White pine (Pinus strobus Linnaeus) (Courtin 1994; Winterhalder 1996, 2000). The landscape is characterized by a rolling topography of hills with shallow soils on Canadian Shield bedrock. Temperatures range from 30-C in summer to 240-C in winter. It receives an average rainfall of 71.3 mm in the summer, and the ground is covered in snow from December to March. The Sudbury landscape has been degraded by logging, mining, and smelting activities since the late 1800s. In 1972, increased government air pollution controls resulted in the decommissioning of one of Sudbury's smelting complexes, and shortly thereafter, restoration of the most visibly degraded areas began (Potvin & Negusanti 1995). Different restoration treatments were applied to 10,000 ha of degraded land in and around Sudbury ranging from (1) fertilizing with mineral 6-24-24 N-P-K fertilizer applied at 390 kg/ha (Lautenbach 1985); (2) manual and aerial spreading of ground dolomitic limestone; (3) seeding of non-native grass and legume species; and (4) planting of 1- to 2-year-old primarily native tree seedlings (Quercus rubra, Picea glauca, Pinus banksiana, P. resinosa, P. strobus) (Winterhalder 1995, 1996). Nonnative species in the understory seed mixture were graminoids (Poa compressa 15%, Poa pratensis 15%, Phleum pratense 20%, Agrostis gigantea 20%, and Festuca rubra 10%) and leguminous forbs (Lotus corniculatus 10% and Trifolium hybridum 10%) (Lautenbach et al. 1995).

Seven restored study sites (sites 1A–7A) were sampled within a 4-km radius just east of Sudbury (Fig. 1). These sites were all severely affected by the smelting activities. In all sites, understory vegetation was sampled during the summer of 2003 along 50- to 100-m transects perpendicular to south-facing slopes to minimize differences due to light availability. Transect length varied among sites according to site area; however, cumulative species area– species richness curves appeared to reach their asymptote in all sites. Understory vascular and nonvascular vegetation was sampled using the Braun Blanquet method of estimating cover abundance in 1 3 1–m contiguous quadrats along each transect. Understory species were identified in the field according to Chambers et al. (1996), and voucher specimens of vascular and nonvascular species were identified to species in the lab with the aid of Britton and Brown (1970), Newmaster et al. (1998), Conrad and Redfearn (1979), and Brodo et al. (2001). Overstory species were defined as woody perennials greater than 1.5 m tall, due to the stunted nature of overstory in this degraded landscape, and were sampled for tree density in 5 3 5–m contiguous quadrats. The vegetation was partitioned into three strata for analyses: vascular understory, nonvascular understory, and overstory.

Richness and percent cover of vegetation strata were compared between all restored and unrestored sites using log-likelihood tests. Restored sites were classified according to restoration complexity such that sites that received a subset of all available restoration treatments (sites 1A, 2A, & 3A) were ''simple,'' and sites that received all available restoration treatments (sites 4A, 5A, 6A, & 7A) were ''complex.'' The Wilcoxon signed rank nonparametric test was used to assess the effect of restoration complexity on species richness and Pearson's product–moment correlation coefficient was calculated to relate restoration time span to species richness at the site level.

Species composition at restored and unrestored sites was related to environmental variables and restoration treatments by performing canonical correspondence analyses (CCA). CCA is an ordination method that identifies differences in species composition among sites and relates species abundances to measured variables by constraining site and species placements in ordination space to be linear combinations of the measured variables (Legendre & Legendre 1998). We used CANOCO (ter Braak 1998) to perform CCA on the species abundances of each stratum using the following seven variables: distance to smelter, number of years of separate limings, fertilized or not fertilized, seeded or not seeded, overall timespan of restoration activities, pH, and density of planted trees. The effects of tree plantations on overstory species composition were further investigated by performing pair-wise correlations between the densities of the major planted trees and sampled trees using JMP Statistical Discovery Software (version 5, release 5.1).

### Results

#### Richness

There were significant differences between the restored and unrestored sites for native and non-native classes of vegetation in terms of species richness (G ¼ 58.57, p < 0.0001) and percent cover (G ¼ 108.37, p < 0.0001; Fig. 2). However, the highest overall richness values were observed in the mildly disturbed, unrestored site (site 6 had 43 species), and a site that received a complex restoration treatment (site 7A had 39 species; Fig. 3). Vascular plant richness was in fact higher in site 7A than in site 6, due only in part to the direct addition of non-native vascular species in the restoration treatment. To further examine differences in restored sites, we examined effects of restoration complexity and restoration timespan on species richness of native and non-native classes of vegetation. Restoration time span was not correlated with species richness in any of the vegetation classes (native nonvascular r ¼ 0.261, p ¼ 0.572; native vascular r ¼ 0.537, p ¼ 0.213; non-native vascular r ¼ 0.425, p ¼ 0.342; total richness r ¼ 0.637, p ¼ 0.124). However, restoration complexity, where restored sites were classified as having received either simple (sites 1A, 2A, & 3A) or complex (sites 4A, 5A, 6A, & 7A) restoration, resulted in significant differences in total species richness (Z ¼ 21.96, p ¼ 0.049) and near-significant differences in non-native vascular species richness (W ¼ 6.0, p ¼ 0.057). Restoration complexity did not produce differences in native nonvascular (Z ¼ 21.80, p ¼ 0.072) species richness or native vascular (Z ¼ 0.36, p ¼ 0.718) species richness. A normal approximation of the Wilcoxon test was used, and the Z statistic was reported when richness data had tied values (because exact p values cannot be computed if there are tied values).

#### **Discussion**

We found that increased complexity of the restoration strategy at a particular site was primarily reflected as an increase in non-native species richness and did not restore native vascular species, nonvascular species, or overstory richness to low disturbance reference levels. Consistent with our first hypothesis, the most complex restoration strategies have similar species richness to the mildly disturbed reference site; however, they have not served to recreate a similar species assemblage. Although there have been previous suggestions that forests exposed to small-scale disturbances that are located relatively close to remnant forest patches can reestablish native species richness within 40 years (McLachlan & Bazely 2001, 2003), studies of large-scale forest disturbances predict that the native plants will take many decades (Bratton et al. 1994) or centuries (Duffy & Meier 1992; McIntyre et al. 1994; Meier et al. 1995) to reach historic levels. It appears that although species richness has been restored to mildly disturbed reference levels relatively quickly in Sudbury's damaged landscape, rebuilding species composition will take more time or require further restoration effort. Winterhalder (1996) predicted that "areas treated in different years may end up with very different patterns and densities of woody plant cover, despite overall conditions." Although we found no significant effects of the overall time span of restoration, we suggest that more work involving both survey and historical data, but also predictive ecological modeling, may be useful to examine this question in more detail.

Ordinations showed that restored sites can be distinguished from unrestored sites by quantifying the composition of the vascular or nonvascular understory communities but not as clearly for the overstory community. Differences in restored and unrestored overstories may be minimized at the present time due to the plantation of primarily native species and reduced growth rate of overstory species. The recovery of understory vascular communities was not consistent among all sites, and ordination showed a complexity gradient among restored sites, with those sites receiving complex techniques separated from those receiving simple techniques. Sites receiving complex treatments had understory vascular species compositions that were more similar to those of mildly disturbed sites than restored sites that received more simplistic treatments. The reverse was true for overstory and nonvascular species, which falsifies our second hypothesis that the complexity of restoration strategies would be reflected in all forest strata. Effects of the different restoration treatments do not follow a clear trend in the nonvascular communities of our study, which is consistent with the lack of temporal trend in disturbed bryophyte communities' compositions found by Ross-Davis and Frego (2002). Anand et al. (2005) showed that vascular plant richness was significantly correlated with other strata for the naturally recovering sites but not for the restored sites, likely due to the fact that the restoration techniques only targeted vascular communities.

Liming on its own has been determined to have a ''trigger effect'' in the severely damaged landscape of Sudbury (see Winterhalder 1996 and references therein) leading to rapid colonization of several species, which otherwise could not establish due to the highly acidic conditions of the soil. We found that Populus tremuloides was not present in the one site that we assumed had not been limed. This supports the idea that some species could be used as an indicator species for various treatments. Winterhalder (1996) also found that P. tremuloides was absent in nonlimed areas but managed to colonize limed areas very well. He suggests that because of the ''trigger factor'' of liming, other treatments may not be absolutely essential. For example, he suggests that the act of seeding may not be necessary, at least in terms of recolonization of woody species such as P. tremuloides and Salix spp.

We found, however, that the act of seeding the understory proved to be an important restoration technique for distinguishing between sites in the CCA analyses. The impact of a viable seed source on the structuring of understory communities has proven to be very important in other studies that considered ''distance to continuous forest'' (McLachlan & Bazely 2003) and ''functional landscape connectivity'' (Lindenmayer et al. 2002). Understory seeding should increase the rate of establishment of diversity, and we indeed found that the site that was not seeded was dominated by a well-established population of Vaccinium angustifolium (site 3A). Also, the site that received the most complex restoration treatment (site 7A) had 13 native vascular species present in its understory that were not found in the understory of the mildly disturbed reference site (site 6) and seven non-native species that were not introduced in the seed mixture. This suggests that the non-native seeded species may be serving as a nurse-crop to aid the establishment of other native and non-native species.

Tree planting showed species-specific success in terms of ''return on investment.'' Planting of Pinus resinosa and Pinus banksiana appeared successful in that planting densities were correlated with actual densities. However, plantings of Pinus strobus, Quercus rubra, and Picea glauca were not found to be as successful. Although these species were planted as part of the restoration, they were planted in smaller numbers and in more selected sites than P. resinosa and P. banksiana. For example, at site 6A, the density of P. strobus was 5-fold less than that of either P. resinosa or P. banksiana alone. Despite differences in planting densities, this also suggests that a reexamination of species-specific criteria for restoration using tree planting may be required. For example, P. strobus may not have been a suitable restoration species, given that it is highly sensitive to SO2 pollution (Freedman & Hutchinson 1980; Amiro & Courtin 1981) and was only found in sites 5 and 6, which are at 16 and 36 km, respectively, from the decommissioned smelters. Nevertheless, as noted in other short-term assessments of restoration models (McLachlan & Bazely 2001, 2003; de Souza & Batista 2004), it is probable that differences in forest compositional structure attributable to restoration strategies will take a long time to appear, given the long time scale of ecological processes; therefore, long-term monitoring is also necessary.

Although diversity measures, such as overall vascular richness, are frequently used as indicators of forest recovery, they have been criticized for being too coarse and ignoring important changes in underlying species composition (Lawton et al. 1998). Our results indicate that even a more refined measure of diversity, native understory vascular species richness, is inadequate in characterizing the effect of the restoration strategy. McLachlan and Bazely (2001) also found that diversity of native vascular species did not differ between restored and undisturbed reference sites in deciduous forests in southern Canada. Similarly, Lugo (1992b) found that species richness in the understory of a mahogany plantation was similar to that of a paired secondary forest with a large number of native tree species present. However, Lugo (1992b) found important differences in the functioning of plantation and secondary growth forests in terms of root density, nutrient retranslocation, and leaf litter. Measuring ecosystem function directly using biomass or other fitness measures that may vary with level of disturbance (e.g., height of tree species; Tucker & Anand 2003) may improve our comparison of the restoration strategies because apparently successful communities in terms of composition may not be sustainable in the long term (Ewel 1987).

McLachlan and Bazely (2001) have used specific understory herbaceous species as indicators of regeneration, and others have shown the ability of understory herbs, individually and grouped as functional groups, to indicate disturbance associated with land use (Moffatt & McLachlan 2004). We found that site 6 was isolated in ordination space largely due to the species exclusively present at this site such as Corylus cornuta, which is characteristic of moist soils rich in calcium and nitrogen (Ringius & Sims 1997), suggesting the potential of indicator species to also serve as a rudimentary monitoring tool in this system. However, not all understory species can serve as indicators of recovery based on their relative abundance alone (Dufrene & Legendre 1997; Anand & Desrochers 2004). Sensitive, shade-tolerant species such as Dryopteris marginalis and Waldsteinia fragarioides, present only at the relatively undisturbed site 6 (0.18 and 0.26%), and Maianthemum racemosum, present at site 6 and the site that received the most complex restoration treatment (0.63 and 0.06%), may be too rare for reliable detection during sampling and may have difficulty becoming established even if habitat conditions are suitable (Gigon & Langenauer 1998). Other woodland species that are present at the least disturbed site, such as C. cornuta, Aralia nudicaulis, and Trientalis borealis, and constitute a considerable amount of the understory (15.74, 6.79, and 3.33%, respectively) are also absent from all other sites. Maianthemum canadense and Gaultheria procumbens, which are present at two (4.5 and 13.10%) and three (0.1, 1.56, and 1.40%) of the naturally recovering sites, respectively, may be better to use as indicators.

Given that the vascular and nonvascular communities have responded to the restoration techniques so differently in the degraded landscape of Sudbury, we suggest that nonvascular species may also be useful as indicators of recovery, and research should be focused in this area. The presence of lichens at the sites close to the pollution source is an indicator of improving air quality (Beckett 1995). Polytrichum spp. are often used for monitoring heavy metal deposition (Tyler 1990), and Polytrichum juniperinum was found at sites 1 and 2 along with Pohlia nutans, which is considered to be highly tolerant to copper and nickel (Freedman & Hutchinson 1980; Tyler 1990; Brodo et al. 2001). Cladonia chlorophaea is the only lichen present at site 1. This species has broad requirements and can grow on thin soil over rock outcrops (Thomson 1967). Various lichen species, such as Cladonia deformis and Stereocaulon spp., can grow on P. nutans once this species is established (Amiro & Courtin 1981); it is possible that C. chlorophaea appeared in site 1 after the cover of P. nutans was established. The mildly disturbed site (site 6) had the highest nonvascular plant diversity of all sites (restored and unrestored) and was strongly associated with Stereocaulon tomentosum and Brachythecium rutabulum, which suggests that these species may be indicators for this site.

Complex restoration strategies appear to increase the rate of recovery; however, this study suggests that restorations involving plantations using non-native species will encourage the establishment of native species, but the non-native vascular species will persist and dominate understory communities for at least a quarter century after restoration commences. Restoration efforts via planting should not only target vascular communities but also nonvascular communities to increase bryophyte and lichen richness and to conserve relationships between taxonomic groups (Anand et al. 2005). Steel et al. (2004) showed that the structure of bryophyte communities did not differ from that of vascular communities in undisturbed sites; however, our study showed that even the species abundances of nonvascular communities were not being restored with complex restoration strategies, whereas they were for the vascular communities. Because increasing the complexity of restoration strategies can have inevitably unpredictable effects on richness and composition of native versus non-native species in plant communities, models of recovery and restoration should take into account multiple and alternative target states, particularly in light of the fact that we are still at a stage where the role of native versus non-native species in restoration is unknown (Ewel & Putz 2004; Tucker & Anand 2004).
