#### abstract

This paper examines the effects of a single surface application of biosolids (at 20 dry Mg ha<sup>1</sup> ) on plant species composition, forage quality and quantity, and C stocks after 14 yr of rest in rangelands of the Central Interior of British Columbia. More than two times the aboveground biomass of grasses and the percent cover of plant litter were found in the biosolids treatment relative to the control, along with reductions in bare soil and microbiotic crust cover. Significantly greater plant uptake of all macronutrients (C, N, P, K, S, Ca, and Mg); most micronutrients (B, Cu, Mn, Mo, and Zn); and Al occurred in the biosolids treatment. P and Cu were the only two nutrients to be more concentrated in the biosolidstreated forage relative to the control forage, while N, Mg, and protein were more concentrated in the control forage. No significant difference in forage digestibility was found between biosolids and control treatments. Bluebunch wheatgrass, the late-seral native grass species, had significantly increased cover and aboveground biomass in the biosolids treatment relative to the control; however, between 2006 and 2016, non-native Kentucky bluegrass had reached > 25% cover in the biosolids plots, perhaps restricting the full recovery of bluebunch wheatgrass. Our findings indicate that biosolids application to ungrazed rangeland can increase long-term forage production and reduce bare soil. However, at our study site biosolids application also led to a long-term shift in the plant community composition away from the late-seral (i.e., bluebunch wheatgrass) trajectory, and the effects of this shift on rangeland health and productivity require further investigation.

Many grasslands of North America have been substantially altered following the introduction of livestock and other agricultural practices by European settlers in the 19th century and no longer support the forage levels that they once did ([Suttie et al.,](#page-9-0) [2005; Wang et al., 2014; Chen et al., 2017; Svejcar et al., 2017\)](#page-9-0). Once degraded, semiarid grasslands are slow to recover in terms of both aboveground net primary productivity (ANPP) and species composition [\(Wikeem and Wikeem, 2004](#page-9-0)), even when long periods of rest have been enforced ([Smoliak et al., 1972\)](#page-9-0). Organic amendments such as biosolids (solid by-product of municipal wastewater treatment facilities) are sometimes applied as fertilizer to rangelands because of their high levels of organic matter and nutrients (e.g., N and P) that offer valuable resources for these nutrient- and water-limited ecosystems [\(Pierce et al., 1998; Ippolito et al., 2010\)](#page-9-0). Although increased ANPP following nutrient amendments is a benefit to cattle [\(De Schrijver et al., 2011\)](#page-9-0), a shift in species composition or reductions in species richness [\(Newman et al.,](#page-9-0) [2014; Seabloom et al., 2015; Blumenthal et al., 2017; DeMalach,](#page-9-0) [2018; Melts et al., 2018\)](#page-9-0) may not be the desired outcome for all restoration efforts.

Higher forage yields due to improved supply of nutrients and water holding capacity have consistently been documented during the 35 yr after biosolids application ([Jurado and Wester, 2001;](#page-9-0) [Martínez et al., 2003; McDougall et al., 2008; Jurado-Guerra et al.,](#page-9-0) [2013\)](#page-9-0) and even up to 12 and 14 yr after biosolids application ([Sullivan et al., 2006; Ippolito et al., 2010; Wester et al., 2011\)](#page-9-0). Other studies ([Blumenthal et al., 2017\)](#page-8-0) have found that effects of organic amendments on forage productivity declined in the longer term. Hence, it remains unclear how long the effect on forage production can last. Similarly, while biosolids generally improve forage quality and protein content in the short term ([McFarland et al., 2007](#page-9-0)), the

Plant species composition is an important factor to consider in terms of forage quality, as well as ecological integrity and carbon storage. For example, [Wallace et al. \(2016\)](#page-9-0) found increased plant production 8 yr after a single biosolids application, but the increases came from annual and perennial forbs at the expense of perennial grasses, lowering the quality of the forage. A recent study on the long-term effects of organic matter applications to degraded rangeland ([Blumenthal et al., 2017\)](#page-8-0) has demonstrated that deleterious shifts in plant community composition can occur more than 22 yr after the application, reinforcing the importance of long-term studies that consider plant species composition. The expansion and dominance of Kentucky bluegrass (Poa pratensis L.) into native grasslands of the Northern Great Plains (e.g., [Printz and](#page-9-0) [Hendrickson, 2015; Sanderson et al., 2016\)](#page-9-0) is an example of how a recent shift in species dominance can have a negative effect on water and nutrient cycling, as well as wildlife habitat ([Toledo et al.,](#page-9-0) [2014\)](#page-9-0). The effects of Kentucky bluegrass and its thatch (i.e., continuous mat of roots and litter) on soil C storage have not received much attention and require future investigation.

The objective of this study was to assess the effects of a single surface application of biosolids on plant species composition, forage quality and quantity, and C stocks after 14 yr of rest from grazing in a degraded grassland of the Central Interior of British Columbia (BC). The reason for examining these effects was to determine if land application of biosolids is a beneficial practice for the longer-term restoration of these semiarid ecosystems.

Site Description

The study was conducted in 2016 on a long-term field experiment established in 2002 at a working ranch near Jesmond, in the South Cariboo region of BC (5125'N, 12209'W; elevation 1 100 m above sea level). The soil was an Orthic Dark Brown Chernozem (Typic Haploboroll) formed on glacial moraine overlaid by a thick layer of aeolian deposits ([Wikeem and Wikeem, 2004](#page-9-0)), with a loam texture (14% clay, 46% silt, and 40% sand) and negligible proportion of coarse fragments and carbonates within the main rooting depth (030 cm).

The study site was in the grassland phase of the Very Dry Mild Interior Douglas-fir (IDFxm) biogeoclimatic subzone [\(Wikeem and](#page-9-0) [Wikeem, 2004](#page-9-0)). The dominant late-seral vegetation of this zone is bluebunch wheatgrass (Pseudoroegneria spicata [Pursh] A. Love), € spreading needlegrass (Achnatherum richardsonii [Link] Barkw.), and short-awned porcupinegrass (Hesperostipa curtiseta [Hitchc.] Barkworth), along with an understory of native forbs and cryptogams. When the field experiment was established in 2002, 26% of the site area had exposed mineral soil and the vegetation was dominated by needle-and-thread grass (Hesperostipa comata [Trin. & Rupr.] Barkworth), with secondary species of Junegrass (Koeleria macrantha [Ledeb.] J.A. Schult. f.), low pussytoes (Antennaria dimorpha [Nutt.] T. & G.), white pussytoes (Antennaria microphylla Rydb.), and prairie sagewort (Artemisia frigida Willd.), collectively indicating an early to midseral stage due to historical moderate-toheavy grazing practices ([Newman et al., 2014](#page-9-0)).

This region is located in the rain shadow of the Coast Mountains, receiving a total of 415 mm precipitation annually, of which 146 mm falls as snow. The highest precipitation rates occur in late spring or early summer (May, June, and July). In the summer, dry heated air rises from the Fraser river valley and contributes to dry conditions on the adjacent plateau, which are interrupted by intense precipitation events ([Wikeem and Wikeem, 2004\)](#page-9-0). The mean annual temperature is 3.8C, while the mean January and July temperatures are 7.8C and 14.8C, respectively. The region receives 101 frost-free d per yr and 1 174 growing degree days (base 5C) ([Climate BC, 2017\)](#page-8-0). In the yr of data collection for this study (2016), the site received 276 mm of precipitation from May to September, which was 100 mm more than the long-term average (30 yr) measured at a nearby (28 km) weather station (Wildfi[re Service, 2018](#page-9-0)).

In the fall of 2002, establishment of the long-term field experiment began with the selection of four randomly located blocks with uniform plant species composition and cover [\(Newman et al.,](#page-9-0) [2014\)](#page-9-0). Each block (60 m 70 m in size) was fenced with a 1.5 mhigh barbed wire to prevent cattle grazing. The experiment was laid out in a randomized complete block design with two treatments: 1) no biosolids (control) and 2) single surface application of biosolids at 20 dry Mg ha<sup>1</sup> replicated once in each of the four blocks. The biosolids were surface applied using a pull-type manure spreader (Hydra-Spread) without subsequent incorporation. The biosolids were supplied by the Annacis Island wastewater treatment plant in Metro Vancouver, BC where the Class A biosolids had been anaerobically digested and dewatered. They contained total N and P contents of 54.9 g kg<sup>1</sup> and 30 g kg<sup>1</sup> (dry weight), respectively. More detailed information on the biosolids used in this study can be found in [Wallace et al. \(2009\).](#page-9-0)

Sampling and Analysis

Plant Sampling

Plant species composition was assessed on 2021 June, 2016, using canopy cover sampling [\(Daubenmire, 1959\)](#page-9-0) and following the same methodology and locations that were used throughout the field experiment (20022016). Percent canopy cover of individual vascular plant species and four ground cover types (mineral soil, microbiotic crust, litter, and biosolids) was estimated within 10 Daubenmire frames (0.2 m 0.5 m) located randomly along five 50-mlong transects for a total of 50 subsamples per experimental unit. Transects were spaced 5 m apart to provide balanced coverage within each experimental unit. Plant species composition was initially determined in June/July 2002 (i.e., before biosolids were applied) and annually in the same months thereafter for 4 yr (20032006; [Newman et al., 2014](#page-9-0)) until the latestmeasurement in 2016, which was the focus of this study. Plant species nomenclature followed [Douglas](#page-9-0) [et al. \(1998](#page-9-0)[2001\).](#page-9-0) Detailed measurements of bluebunch wheatgrass vigor was assessed using one randomly selected bluebunch wheatgrass individual along each of the five 50-melong transects for a total of five individuals per experimental unit. The circumference and height were measured, and the number of flower stalks on each individual was counted. The green standing biomass of each individual was then clipped at the soil surface, oven-dried (60C for 24 hr), and weighed to determine standing biomass (g per plant).

Total green standing biomass was clipped at the soil surface within a 1-m2 frame on 2021 June 2016 and separated into two functional groups of grasses and forbs. Five frames were clipped per experimental unit. The plant material was oven-dried (60C for 24 hr) and weighed by functional group to assess aboveground biomass (kg ha<sup>1</sup> ). Tissue nutrient analysis was done on the total aboveground biomass from each frame (grasses and forbs combined), ground together, and passed through a 2-mm mesh screen Wiley Mill. N, C, and S were determined by dry combustion ([Nelson](#page-9-0) [and Sommers, 1996\)](#page-9-0) using LECO analyzer (LECO Corp., St. Joseph, MI), while the remaining macronutrients and micronutrients (B, Ca, Cu, Fe, K, Mg, Mn, Mo, Na, P, and Zn) and Al were determined by microwave digestion followed by inductively coupled plasma optical emission spectrometry [\(Kalra and Maynard, 1991\)](#page-9-0). Plant tissue macronutrients and micronutrients are presented as both tissue concentrations (mg kg<sup>1</sup> ) and total nutrient uptake (kg ha<sup>1</sup> ), which was calculated as the product of nutrient concentration in plant tissue and dry matter yield ([Jarrell and Beverly, 1981](#page-9-0)). Total aboveground biomass (as above) was also analyzed for acid <span id="page-3-0"></span>detergent fiber (ADF) to determine amounts of fiber, lignin (ash free), and cellulose in the forage (Goering and Van Soest, 1970). Protein content was analyzed by acid hydrolysis followed by colorimetric analysis (Marks et al., 1985).

#### Thatch Sampling

Thatch is composed of a layer of dead and living stems (tillers, stolons, and rhizomes), roots, and enmeshed soil (Smiley, 1981). Thatch sampling was performed along the five plant transects per experimental unit, at five random locations per transect. At each location, depth (cm) of thatch was recorded, or 0 was recorded if thatch was not present. At each location that thatch was present, a  $10 \times 10$  cm area of thatch was sliced with a flat shovel and a thatch sample, which included the entire depth of the thatch, was carefully extracted. In the laboratory, thatch samples were cut to a known volume (using a sharp-edged cylinder), weighed, dried in the oven at  $60^{\circ}$ C for 24 hr, and weighed again to determine oven-dry thatch density. Because all thatch originated from Kentucky bluegrass, total thatch biomass (kg ha $^{-1}$ ) was calculated as the product of Kentucky bluegrass cover, average depth of thatch, and thatch density.

To prepare thatch samples for C analysis, plant and soil components were physically separated using a 2-mm sieve. Soil and roots were weighed separately to determine the portion (by mass) of the thatch that each represented. Roots were washed with deionized water, and C concentration of soil and roots was determined by dry combustion (Nelson and Sommers, 1996). Total soil C and N were also determined using the dry combustion method, and contents of the total soil C and N were reported in Avery et al. (2018).

#### Statistical Analysis

All data were analyzed as a randomized, complete block design with four replications. Subsamples collected within treatment plots were averaged by treatment plot before analysis. All statistical analysis was performed using R statistical software version 3.4.0 (R Core Team, 2017). Mixed-effect models were constructed using the nlme package (Pinheiro et al., 2017). For data that were sampled only once (forage quality, aboveground biomass, and aboveground and thatch C stocks), treatment (biosolids or no biosolids) was the only fixed effect in the model. For the plant species composition and soil cover data, which were assessed over multiple yr (2002 – 2006 and 2016), a repeated measures model was used, with treatment, sampling year, and a treatment × sampling year interaction as the fixed effects. Blocks were included as random effects in all models. Assumptions were assessed for each model, and log transformations were made where necessary to meet assumptions. A significance level of 0.05 was used during all analyses.

Aboveground and Thatch C Stocks

While biosolids application did not affect the soil C stock 14 yr after application (Avery et al., 2018), above ground plant biomass (i.e., vegetation) C stock was nearly double in the biosolids plots as compared with the control plots (688 kg ha $^{-1}$  and 358 kg ha $^{-1}$ , respectively; see Table 5). In addition to mineral soil and above ground biomass C, we characterized an additional C stock—the layer of thatch found be neath Kentucky bluegrass. The thatch covered around 25% of the biosolids plots to an average depth of 3.8 cm and was composed of 0.6 - 17% roots by volume (6% on average). It was not present on the control plots. The thatch on the biosolids plots represented a C stock of 6.4 Mg ha $^{-1}$  (5.6 Mg ha $^{-1}$  in the enmeshed soil held in the thatch and 0.8 Mg ha $^{-1}$  in the roots forming the thatch layer).

At these two sites, biosolids led to an increase of exotic forbs and annual grasses at the expense of perennial grasses over 4 yr, suggesting that the results of this study may be limited to rangeland sites with similar levels of precipitation and plant communities at similar seral stages.

Although biosolids have been shown to increase forage production in the short term (Jurado and Wester, 2001; Wallace et al., 2009), fewer studies have found similarly long-lasting increases in forage productivity, especially as a result of a single biosolids application, as was observed in our study. Sullivan et al. (2006) found long-term increases in aboveground biomass with increasing application rates (2.5, 5, 10, 21, and 30 Mg ha<sup>-1</sup>) on a semiarid shortgrass steppe rangeland dominated by native perennials western wheatgrass (*Pascopyrum smithii* [Rybd] A. Löve) and

needle-and-thread grass. The 21 Mg ha<sup>-1</sup> rate, which is closest to the rate in our study, achieved a 60% increase in aboveground biomass 12 yr after the application.

Another long-term (22-yr) study on the effects of organic waste amendments (composted manure, composted biosolids, and fresh manure) to a native mixed-grass prairie in Wyoming noted increases in native rangeland plant productivity 2 yr after applications of composted manure and composted biosolids (Blumenthal et al., 2017). However, 17 and 22 yr after the application, the differences in native plant productivity had given way to increases in cheatgrass (*Bromus tectorum* L.), which had significantly greater biomass in the composted manure treatment and followed the same trend, though not significantly, in the biosolids and fresh manure treatments. The authors highlighted that the full effects of organic waste application on rangeland plant species composition can manifest gradually and persist for decades, which highlights the importance of long-term studies when organic amendments are applied in semiarid environments.

In 2016, 76% of the increased perennial grass cover in the biosolids plots relative to the control was attributed to Kentucky bluegrass, which was at < 1% cover in 2002 (see [Fig. 1b](#page-4-0)). Kentucky bluegrass is a cool-season perennial that spreads though the rhizomes have good forage value, which is not the point. It has garnered much attention due to its prolific expansion in the Northern Great Plains of the United States and the southern Canadian Prairies over the past 23 decades, as well as its ability to transform the structure and function of the ecosystem via its thatch ([White et al., 2012; Toledo et al., 2014;](#page-9-0) [DeKeyser et al., 2015; Bork et al., 2017; Ot](#page-9-0)finowski et al., 2017; [Sanderson et al., 2017\)](#page-9-0). The presence of Kentucky bluegrass on the biosolids plots in our study (and the relative absence of Kentucky bluegrass on the control) suggests that the biosolids application, in the context of 14 yr of rest from grazing, has encouraged the rise in cover of this plant species. The Shannon-Wiener diversity index was 25% higher in the control (1.9 ± 0.5; P ¼ 0.02) as compared with biosolids plots (data not shown) when only native species were considered, which could have been attributed to the rise in Kentucky bluegrass cover. It is possible that Kentucky bluegrass benefited from increased available N and P in the biosolids plots, much like other cool season grasses ([Vinton and Goergen, 2006; Blumenthal et al.,](#page-9-0) [2008; Gioria and Osborne, 2014\)](#page-9-0). Another possible explanation could be that biosolids applied to the soil surface reduced evaporation, which then allowed for more plant available water early in the growing season, a mechanism suggested by [Blumenthal et al. \(2008\).](#page-8-0) Indeed, at our site, increased soil water was measured in May and June 2016 in the biosolids plots ([Avery et al., 2018](#page-8-0)) and the accumulation of litter and reduced bare soil exposure (see [Fig. 2](#page-5-0)) likely contributed to increased soil water content in the biosolids plots, which in turn may have supported the spread of Kentucky bluegrass. It should be noted that these conditions occurred in the absence of grazing, which allowed for the accumulation of litter. It is possible that Kentucky bluegrass would not have thrived in the biosolids plots had the regular removal of litter via grazing been occurring.

#### Forage Quality

Despite showing little difference in concentration values between the biosolids forage and control, most nutrients had greater uptake values (nutrient concentration yield) in the biosolids plots due to the more than double aboveground biomass on the biosolids plots. Phosphorus was the only macronutrient that had significantly greater concentration in the plants growing in biosolids plots. P was also the only macronutrient that was significantly more available in biosolids amended soil [\(Avery et al., 2018](#page-8-0)). The fact that P is more concentrated in plants that received biosolids 14 yr ago may suggest that the plants continue to benefit from elevated levels of soil P, which is typically a limiting nutrient in semiarid rangeland ecosystems. Copper, the only other nutrient more concentrated in plant tissues from biosolids plots, was more available in the biosolids amended soil at two out of four sampling dates [\(Avery et al.,](#page-8-0) [2018\)](#page-8-0).

In contrast, the forage on the control plots had higher tissue concentrations of N and Mg relative to the biosolids amended forage, while no difference was found in plant-available levels of N and Mg between biosolids amended soil and control soil [\(Avery](#page-8-0) [et al., 2018](#page-8-0)). A possible explanation for this is the "dilution effect," a well-known effect reviewed by [Jarrell and Beverly \(1981\),](#page-9-0) wherein increases in yield result in the decrease in concentration of nutrients in the plant tissue, where available soil nutrient levels are held constant. It is possible that greater soil moisture and/or soil P levels on the biosolids amended plots [\(Avery et al., 2018\)](#page-8-0) promoted the greater aboveground biomass on the biosolids plots but also caused a dilution effect on N and Mg levels.

The higher protein concentration in the forage from the control plots relative to the forage from the biosolids plots (see [Table 2](#page-4-0)) was in accordance with the finding of higher N concentration discussed earlier (see [Fig. 3](#page-5-0)a). It can be explained in the same way as the higher N concentration: likely the result of a dilution effect of the twofold increase in aboveground biomass in the biosolids plots. An average protein concentration of 12% is common for bunchgrass forage in southern and central BC ([McDougall et al., 2008](#page-9-0)), and average protein content ranges from 70 to 156 kg ha1 , which suggests that both control and biosolids plots in our study were below regional average protein concentrations but close to the average total contents of protein per hectare.

A second possible explanation for the higher N, Mg, and protein concentrations in the control plots is the increased proportion of forbs. Forbs represented 18% of the biomass from the biosolids plots and 30% from the control plots (see [Table 1](#page-3-0)). Forbs have been shown to have higher N and lower C:N ratios than grasses [\(Wilsey](#page-9-0) [and Wayne Polley, 2006\)](#page-9-0). It has also been shown that within plant functional groups, different species on the same site often have different N concentrations due to different uptake mechanisms or differences in rooting depth ([Koerselman and Meuleman, 1996\)](#page-9-0). These niche characteristics allow for coexistence of a diversity of species, especially on resource-limited sites ([Harpole and Tilman,](#page-9-0) [2007\)](#page-9-0). The increase in forbs could also account for the high variability for certain elements (Al, Fe, and Na) in the control plots.

Although other studies [\(McFarland et al., 2007](#page-9-0)) have shown that biosolids can improve forage quality by increasing protein content and digestibility in the short term, the effect was not evident in our study conducted 14 yr after biosolids application. Grass and legume forages with ADF values under 35% are considered good quality ([Van Saun, 2013\)](#page-9-0). The ADF levels found in the forage at our site (see [Table 2\)](#page-4-0) were therefore marginally higher than ideal in both the biosolids treatment and the control plots. It has been reported that Cu:Mo below 2 can result in Cu deficiency ([Miltimore and Mason,](#page-9-0) [1971; Gardner et al., 2012\)](#page-9-0). Biosolids application did not have a long-term effect on the forage Cu:Mo ratio, which was 1.2 in the biosolids plots and 1.0 in the control 14 yr after the application (see [Table 2](#page-4-0)). In a study by [Pierce et al. \(1998\)](#page-9-0) on semiarid shrubland, biosolids increased the Cu:Mo ratio from below 1.2 to above 2 for western wheatgrass and bluebunch wheatgrass 1 yr after application at low rates (10 or 15 Mg ha<sup>1</sup> ) and 2 yr after application at <span id="page-8-0"></span>intermediate rates  $(25-40\,\mathrm{Mg\,ha^{-1}})$ . It is possible that any positive effects of biosolids application on forage Cu:Mo ratio are relatively short term.

Soil Cover

The decrease in bare soil in both treatments in the first 3 yr of the experiment was likely a result of the exclusion of grazing, which allowed the plant community to recover. The greater litter cover on the biosolids plots, however, contributed to the significantly lower level of bare soil on the biosolids plots as compared with the control plots in 2016. Reducing bare soil is important to proper ecosystem function in semiarid rangelands because it leads to a lower susceptibility of erosion and evaporation. Microbiotic crust plays an important role in semiarid bunchgrass ecosystems by protecting the soil in the interspaces between bunchgrasses in ecosystems that are typically too dry, or degraded, for significant accumulation of plant litter (Wikeem and Wikeem, 2004). In the biosolids plots, however, the nearly continuous cover of plant litter (89%) and high levels of aboveground perennial grass biomass (see Fig. 2; Table 1) provided that ecosystem service.

#### Aboveground and Thatch C Stocks

The thatch of Kentucky bluegrass represented a substantial C stock: the matted thatch rhizomes contributed an additional 775 kg of C per ha, which is even greater than the C stored in the aboveground biomass on biosolids plots (688 kg ha<sup>-1</sup>). Studies rarely report thatch C stocks, and when the belowground C stock is reported, it mainly focuses on roots. For example, a study by Zhang et al. (2018) carried out on a rough fescue (*Festuca campestris* Rydb) grassland with codominant Parry's oat grass (*Danthonia parryi* Scribn) and Kentucky bluegrass reported root C stocks (from the 0- to 15-cm soil depth) that were three times greater than the standing biomass C stock.

Kentucky bluegrass commonly creates a thatch layer in grassland ecosystems, likely due to the combination of its vigorous aboveground production and the relatively slow belowground decomposition that occurs in moisture-limited grasslands (Murray and Juska, 1970; Smiley, 1981). In our study, the absence of grazing for 14 yr likely has contributed to the accumulation of thatch at the soil surface. Although the biosolids application did not result in any changes in mineral soil C stocks (Avery et al., 2018), there was certainly an accumulation of aboveground biomass (i.e., vegetation) C and thatch C on biosolids plots (see Table 5). In addition, the soil enmeshed within the thatch had a higher C concentration (9.2%) as compared with the soil below the thatch (2.6%; see Table 5), likely due to the high concentration of roots and partially decomposed organic matter in close contact with mineral soil particles in the thatch. Although the accumulation of thatch C is partly an outcome of grazing exclusion, the application of biosolids is mainly done to improve forage production. It is also done in a restoration context where grazing is excluded for long periods of time. Therefore, the long-term results of increased thatch and cover of Kentucky bluegrass with an absence of grazing are important for many scenarios where biosolids are applied in semiarid environments.

#### **IMPLICATIONS**

Assessments of plant species composition, forage quantity and quality, and C stocks 14 yr after a single surface biosolids application at 20 Mg ha<sup>-1</sup> provided a unique opportunity to quantify long-term effects of biosolids application to semiarid grassland. The existing literature on organic matter amendments to rangeland is dominated by short-term studies, despite indications that the effects of these practices may continue to unfold over decades

(Blumenthal et al., 2017). The long-term changes noted in this study in plant community composition, notably the continued persistence of bluebunch wheatgrass and recent invasion of Kentucky bluegrass, highlight the importance of remeasuring long-term experiments. In addition, the long-term effects of biosolids application to rangeland appear to be highly influenced by characteristics of the site (i.e., moisture regime, plant community at time of application) (Newman et al., 2014; Blumenthal et al., 2017). As such, more long-term studies are necessary in order to better understand the relationships between site characteristics and the effects of biosolids application, which would ultimately allow land managers to better predict the long-term effects of applications to their specific rangeland site.

The results of this study may be interpreted in several ways, depending on priorities of different stakeholder groups and management objectives for the rangeland site. For example, the documented long-term increase in forage production, reduction in bare soil, and resulting increase in soil moisture would be of value to ranchers or stakeholders valuing forage production. While the decrease of protein concentration in biosolids forage may be a concern to this group, the greater plant uptake of most nutrients in the biosolids plots (including N) due to greater aboveground biomass would be an additional benefit. The finding of greater bluebunch wheatgrass cover in the biosolids plots would likely be prioritized as a positive outcome by stakeholders focused on rangeland restoration and native species conservation, while the increase of Kentucky bluegrass cover may be of concern, given its potential ability to alter ecosystem functioning. Kentucky bluegrass, however, is generally considered a high-quality forage species, so the interpretation of this result will, again, depend on values held by the stakeholder. As such, this work suggests that when considering the application of biosolids or other organic matter amendments to rangeland, it is crucial to understand the management goals of the site, along with the likely interactions between unique site characteristics and biosolids, in order to assess whether the outcomes of the application will support the site's management goals in the long-term.
