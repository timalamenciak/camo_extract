# **How to shift unproductive** *Kalmia angustifolia – Rhododendron groenlandicum* **heath to productive conifer plantation1**

## **Nelson Thiffault and Robert Jobidon**

**Abstract:** Conifer-regeneration failure is often observed on sites invaded by ericaceous shrubs. In northeastern Quebec, Canada, these sites are frequently characterized by dense *Kalmia angustifolia* L. – *Rhododendron groenlandicum* (Oeder) K.A. Kron & Judd cover. Such failures are potential consequences of nutrient limitation, allelopathy, or low soil temperatures. Conversion of productive forests into heaths poses a threat to the maintenance of forest productivity and biodiversity. We evaluated scarification, spot fertilization, and increased seedling foliar N concentration as treatments to promote planted black spruce (*Picea mariana* (Mill.) BSP) seedling survival and growth. We measured seedling, vegetation, and soil responses to the treatments for 5 years following planting. Scarification had positive impacts on seedling growth: the differences between scarified and unscarified plots increased over time, and double-pass scarification proved slightly more effective than a single-pass treatment. Responses to scarification were enhanced when seedlings were fertilized. A slow-release fertilizer with micronutrients proved slightly more effective than the 26N– 12P–6K formulation; the latter also induced higher mortality than the former or no fertilizer. Gains due to increased N concentrations based on nursery practices were significant but short-lived. Our results demonstrate how silviculture and nursery practices can be used for resetting the secondary succession where ecosystem retrogression is observed following *K. angustifolia – R. groenlandicum* invasion.

# **Introduction**

In some ecosystems, conifer regeneration may be hindered by the presence of ericaceous species following fire or harvesting (Mallik 2003). Examples of failure of forest tree regeneration due to the presence of ericaceous shrubs are found in Sweden (Nilsson and Wardle 2005), Scotland (Mallik 1995), and the northeastern United States (Latham 2003). In Canada this phenomenon is frequently observed in western coastal forests of British Columbia (Prescott et al. 1996), as well as in eastern temperate and boreal forests of Newfoundland, Nova Scotia, and Ontario (Anonymous

In Quebec, transformation of once productive, relatively diverse forest stands into heaths dominated by sheep laurel, *Kalmia angustifolia* L., or bog Labrador tea, *Rhododendron groenlandicum* (Oeder) K.A. Kron & Judd, following harvesting or wildfire is considered a significant forestry issue (Grondin and Cimon 2003; Jobidon 1995). The rapid and lasting domination of disturbed sites by ericaceous shrubs, and the associated stunted growth of conifers, pose a threat to the maintenance of forest productivity and biodiversity.

Past research has indicated some direct and indirect impacts of ericaceous shrubs on soil nutritional processes, humus accumulation, conifer root growth, and mycorrhizal infection (Bradley et al. 2000; Damman 1971; Yamasaki et al. 1998; Zhu and Mallik 1994). Globally, "growth check" of planted conifers on sites invaded by these species is attributed to competition for nutrients (both direct and indirect), allelopathy, and low soil temperature associated with humus builtup. However, the relative importance of these mechanisms seems to vary according to site quality (Yamasaki et al. 2002).

Given the wide range of characteristics of the sites on which ericaceous species are found in eastern Canada, the related variety of ericaceous communities, and the complex nature of the mechanisms suspected to induce conifer growthcheck, local fine-tuning and optimization of management strategies for these sites are clearly needed. We have already conducted various experiments on sites in northwestern Quebec characterized by relatively thin humus (~8 cm) and a K. angustifolia – Vaccinium spp. complex (Thiffault et al. 2004b, 2005). For these sites, our experiments showed that double-pass scarification is no more beneficial than singlepass treatments in the short term. Similarly, we did not observe any positive interaction between slow-release fertilization and scarification on such sites. The hypothesis that such treatments could alleviate the adverse effects of a K. angustifolia – R. groenlandicum complex on young conifers on wetter sites with a thick humus layer remains to be verified.

Nutrient loading of seedlings at the nursery is another means to enhance their competitive ability once outplanted in the field (Timmer and Munson 1991). This approach has proved effective on a boreal mixedwood site dominated by American red raspberry, *Rubus idaeus* L. (Malik and Timmer 1996). Given the nutritional nature of the interference on ericad-dominated sites (Thiffault et al. 2004*b*; Yamasaki et al. 2002), it is hypothesized that planting conifer seedlings with enhanced nutrient reserves on *K. angustifolia – R. groenlandicum* sites significantly improves field performance.

The objective of our study was to quantify the main and interaction effects of initial foliar N concentration, scarification, and fertilization on black spruce (*Picea mariana* (Mill.) BSP) seedling survival and 5-year growth on a *K. angustifolia – R. groenlandicum*-dominated site in northeastern Quebec. We also aimed to assess treatment impacts on competing vegetation and selected environmental resources. We hypothesized that higher initial foliar N concentrations and the use of fertilizer favour seedling nutrition and early growth. ## **Materials and methods**

#### Site description

An experimental plantation was established north of the city of Baie-Comeau, Quebec, Canada, in the black spruce – feathermosses bioclimatic domain designed by Saucier et al. (1998) (49°47′18″N, 69°17′12″W). The regional climate is subpolar subhumid continental, with annual mean temperatures of -2.5-0.0 °C and a growing season of 140 days. Mean annual precipitation is 1300 mm (35%-40% falls as snow). The study site (~5 ha) is located 472 m above sea level and the soil surface deposit is a deep till (>1 m) with a sandy loam texture in the first 15 cm (62% sand; 34% loam; 5% clay). The Humo-Ferric Podzolic soil (Soil Classification Working Group 1998) is moderately well drained and covered with a Mor humus 18 cm deep. Before clear-cutting in 1993, the site supported a *P. mariana* stand more than 120 years old with a site index of 15 m (dominant height at age 50). At cutting, the stand produced 176 m<sup>3</sup>·ha<sup>-1</sup> of merchantable wood. Following cutting, a dense cover of Κ. ericaceous shrubs (mainly angustifolia R. groenlandicum) formed and advance regeneration was sparse. These site conditions are typical of *K. angustifolia* heath formation following harvesting in this region.

In spring 2000 we divided the main plots into three subplots (Fig. 1), each containing 96 seedlings, and randomly assigned to each subplot one of the three following fertilization treatments: (1) no fertilization (F0); (2) fertilization with Forest Paks (Reforestation Technologies International, Salinas, Calif.), a slow-release fertilizer contained in a "teabag", at time of planting (F1); and (3) fertilization with Silva Paks (Reforestation Technologies International), another form of slow-release fertilizer, at time of planting (F2). Each Forest Pak (F1) contains 10 g of fertilizer (15.0% total N; 15.0% P (P<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub>) available as phosphoric acid; 8.0% K (K<sub>2</sub>O) available as soluble potash; 8.20% S; 0.20% Zn; 0.41% Fe; 0.98% Mg; 0.10% Cu; 0.12% B; 0.22% Mn; and 0.09% Mo; this corresponds to 1.5 g elemental N, 0.6546 g elemental P, 0.66408 g elemental K, 0.8 g S, 0.02 g Zn, 0.041 g Fe, 0.098 g Mg, 0.01 g Cu, 0.012 g B, 0.022 g Mn, and 0.009 g Mo). N, P, and K sources are polymer-coated to supply 14.73% slow-release N, 6.64% slow-release P, and 5.49% slow-release potash. Each Silva Pak (F2) contains 10 g of fertilizer (26.3% total N; 12.0% P (P<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub>) available as phosphoric acid; and 6.0% K (K2O) available as soluble potash; this corresponds to 2.6 g elemental N, 0.5 g elemental P, and 0.5 g elemental K). N, P, and K sources are polymer-coated to supply 24.9% slow-release N, 5.3% slowrelease P, and 5.1% slow-release potash. In the F1 and F2 subplots we buried fertilizer bags 2 cm from the planting holes and 4 cm deep at time of planting.

Seedlings (2 + 0) germinated in 1998 (seed origin 48°20′N, 70°02′W) at the Centre sylvicole Forestville, a private nursery in Quebec, in containers consisting of 45 cells, each 110 cm<sup>3</sup>. Seedling production was completed in 1999–2000 at the Saint-Modeste provincial nursery. Each subplot (F0, F1, and F2) contained 24 seedlings at each of the following four levels of initial foliar N concentration randomly distributed (at the sub-subplot level): 1.43% (N1), 1.89% (N2), 2.33% (N3), and 2.76% (N4). P concentrations were 0.29%, 0.27%, 0.32%, and 0.32% for the N1, N2, N3, and

N4 treatment, respectively. K concentrations were 0.83%, 0.72%, 0.71%, and 0.73% for the N1, N2, N3, and N4 treatment, respectively. Concentrations are those measured at the nursery, before seedlings were shipped to the field (in May 2000). The N2 treatment corresponds to a standard foliar N concentration for seedling production in Quebec (Ministère des Ressources naturelles et de la Faune du Québec 2005). The desired foliar concentrations were obtained between August and October 1999 with use of the PLANTEC software (Girard et al. 2001), which calculates weekly fertilizer doses by integrating the evolution, over time, of seedling dry biomass, foliar N, P, and K concentrations, and substrate fertility. We planted seedlings in June 2000 at 1 m  $\times$  2 m spacing and tagged one seedling in every two  $(2 \text{ m} \times 2 \text{ m})$ for growth measurements. Extra seedlings were used for destructive sampling. A total of 8640 seedlings were planted.

#### **Seedling measurements**

Height (cm) and ground-level diameter (GLD; mm) were measured immediately after planting (Table 1) and in October of each of the first five growing seasons (2000–2004 inclusive). The survival rate was also noted each year. At time of planting (in June 2000) we randomly selected 60 seedlings with each initial foliar N concentration (N1–N4; a total of 240 seedlings) for determining initial aboveground biomass. Seedlings were kept frozen until they were oven-dried at 65 °C for 48 h. We separately weighed needles, twigs, and stems (Table 1). In October of 2000 (first growing season) and 2002 (third growing season) we sampled two seedlings in each scarification × fertilization × initial foliar N concentration combination for every block (a total of 720 seedlings) for determining aboveground biomass. Subsamples (~3.5 g) of dried needles used for biomass measurements in 2000 and 2002 were kept for measuring nutrient concentrations (see below). In 2004 we collected two composite foliar samples (one composite sample included needles from three seedlings) in each sub-subplot in every block. Foliar samples (2000, 2002, and 2004) were ground to 0.5 mm and subjected to Kjeldahl digestion (H<sub>2</sub>SO<sub>4</sub>/H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>/Se) (Walinga et al. 1995). Kjeldahl N was measured colorimetrically by spectrophotometry (FIA Quickchem, Lachat, Milwaukee, Wis.), and P, K, Ca, and Mg by inductively coupled plasma analysis (ICAP-9000, Thermo Instruments, Franklin, Mass.).

## **Vegetation assessment**

We carried out surveys of vegetation density (the number of individual stems protruding from the soil or humus) in mid-July of the first five growing seasons in one 0.8 m radius (2 m<sup>2</sup> ) permanent vegetation sampling plot within each main plot of every block. Surveys were not exhaustive, but were limited to species recognized as potential competitors in this region (Jobidon 1995).

## **Soil measurements**

From June to October of each study year (2000–2004 inclusive) we measured soil temperature at 10 cm depth in the centre of each main plot (S0, S1, S2) of six experimental blocks using thermistors (model 107 BAM, Campbell Scientific, Inc., Logan, Utah) and a data logger (CR-10, Campbell Scientific, Inc.). Temperature was measured at 15-min intervals and averaged hourly. We assessed soil nutrient concentrations in October of the second (2001), fourth (2003), and fifth (2004) growing seasons by sampling the surface mineral soil (0–10 cm) in the main plots of every experimental block. We took three samples per main plot; each sample was a composite of three subsamples. Soil samples were airdried down to 5% mass-based humidity at ambient temperature, and ground to pass a 2 mm mesh screen. Mineral N (NH4-N and NO3-N) was extracted with a 2 mol/L KCl solution and P, K, Ca, and Mg with Mehlich-3 solution (Sen Tran and Simard 1993). Chemical analyses were performed by spectrophotometry and inductively coupled plasma analysis.

#### **Statistical analyses**

Analyses of variance (ANOVA) for a split-split-plot design were conducted to assess treatment effects on survival (years 1, 3, and 5), height (year 5), GLD (year 5), aboveground biomass (year 3), and foliar nutrient concentrations (years 1, 3, and 5). Height, GLD, and biomass were naturallog-transformed prior to the analyses to meet normality and homoscedasticity requirements; for these variables we present back-transformed means and 95% confidence intervals after bias correction (Ung and Végiard 1988). All ANOVAs were performed with the MIXED procedure of the SAS® 9.1 system; survival data (binomial) were analysed with the macro procedure GLIMMIX (Littell et al. 1996). Given the high occurrence of experimental units without any mortality (19 out of 36), survival modelling was only possible by attributing a "dead" value to 1 sample seedling (out of 120) from each scarification × fertilization × initial N concentration combination for which it was the case. For all these variables we constructed a priori contrasts to answer specific questions: (*i*) is there an advantage to scarification, regardless of the method (S1 + S2 vs. S0)? (*ii*) if we scarify, is double-pass scarification better than single-pass scarification (S2 vs. S1)? (*iii*) is there an advantage to fertilization, regardless of the method (F1 + F2 vs. F0)? (*iv*) if we fertilize, is Forest Pak better than Silva Pak (F2 vs. F1)? (*v*) is the standard foliar N concentration better than a lower concentration (N2 vs. N1)? (*vi*) is there an advantage to using seedlings above the standard foliar N concentration (N3 + N4 vs. N2)? and (*vii*) if we use seedlings above the standard, is 2.76% foliar N better than 2.33% foliar N (N4 vs. N3)? An α value of 0.05 or less was considered significant.

Seedling height profiles over 5 years were compared among treatments using ANOVA for repeated measures. We performed these analyses with the REPEATED statement of the MIXED procedure (Littell et al. 1996) on natural-logtransformed data. A first-order autoregressive covariance structure (AR(1)) was selected. Given the relatively complicated covariance structure and the reasonably balanced design, we used the Kenward–Roger method for calculating denominator degrees of freedom (Schaalje et al. 2001). Treatment comparisons by contrasts were performed with Helmert transformations, which compare a level of a repeated measure with the mean of subsequent levels (SAS Institute Inc. 2004).

We used principal component analyses (PCA) to study relationships between the different species and the scarification treatments for each sampling year. The FACTOR procedure of SAS® was used to extract principal components. As stem density varies greatly from one species to another, PCAs were performed using correlation matrix, which forces all variables to have equal variances. Scores of the scarification treatments on axes 1 and 2 of each PCA were compared by means of ANOVAs after being assessed for agreement with standard parametric statistical assumptions. We used Fisher's protected LSD test to separate treatment means when ANOVAs showed significant effects (*p* < 0.05). Given the lack of new meaningful information brought with the analyses of data from the second, third, and fourth seasons, we only report results using the first- and fifth-year data.

The effects of scarification and its intensity on extractable soil nutrient concentrations in 2001 and 2003 were evaluated with ANOVAs for completely randomized block design. Data were natural-log-transformed to comply with ANOVA prerequisites. We compared soil temperature regimes against scarification treatments during the first, third, and fifth growing seasons using ANOVA for repeated measures; a spatial power covariance matrix was used (Littell et al. 1996).

## **Results**

#### **Seedling dimensions and survival rate**

Scarification and fertilization at planting significantly interacted to influence seedling height and diameter at age 5 and aboveground biomass after three growing seasons (Table 2, Figs. 2 and 3). In all cases, double-pass scarification (S2) resulted in taller and bigger seedlings than single-pass scarification (S1) (Table 3). Independently of scarification or initial N concentration, seedlings fertilized with Forest Paks (F1) had greater aboveground biomass than seedlings fertilized with Silva Paks (F2) (Table 3). The positive effect of scarification (either S1 or S2) on GLD and biomass was enhanced when seedlings were fertilized (with either F1 or F2) (Table 3, Fig. 3). Although significant in some cases (e.g., N2 vs. N1, *p* ≤ 0.002 for GLD and biomass), the effect of initial N concentration was weak compared with other treatment effects (Figs. 2 and 4).

## **Soil characteristics**

Scarification did not significantly affect soil extractable N-NH4, K, or Mg in either 2001 (year 2) or 2003 (year 4; *p* ≥ 0.101). In 2001, P concentrations were 30% lower in scarified plots than in control plots (*p* = 0.001); this effect was no longer significant in 2003. Scarification, either single- or double-pass, significantly enhanced soil extractable Ca concentrations in 2003 (*p* ≤ 0.001). For all treatments and in all years, extractable N-NO3 concentrations were below detection limits. Soil temperature profiles of the scarification treatments throughout the first, third, and fifth seasons were not parallel (scarification × time, *p* ≤ 0.001). From early June to early September of each year, the soil temperature was systematically higher in the S1 and S2 plots than in the S0 plots (Fig. 6). Between Julian Days 157 and 250, a mean difference of 3.2 °C was noted in the first year, 3.3 °C in the third year, and 2.8 °C in the fifth year. The differences be-

## **Discussion**

Scarification had a strong positive impact on seedling growth. The growth differences between the seedlings planted in scarified and unscarified plots appeared early in our study and increased over time. As a result, after 5 years, seedlings planted in scarified soil were almost twice the height and diameter of those planted in unscarified plots. The positive impact of scarification on seedling biomass was even greater, ranging from a 208% gain for F0 seedlings to a 369% gain for F2 seedlings. Such positive responses in seedling growth to mechanical soil preparation have been reported for northeastern sites in Canada invaded by ericaceous shrubs. Prévost and Dumais (2003) demonstrated the advantage of scarification for growth of *P. mariana* on boreal sites. A similar advantage of scarification was observed by Thiffault et al. (2005) 3 years after planting *P. mariana* and jack pine (*Pinus banksiana* Lamb.) on a *K. angustifolia* – *Vaccinium* spp. site in northwestern Quebec. On the other hand, the height-growth trajectories we observed in unscarified areas are typical of stunted seedlings (Mallik 2001; Titus et al. 1995), which will likely remain weak for several decades (Jobidon 1995; Mallik 2003).

Scarification was associated with the emergence of species that were not observed in control plots; the treatments favoured the emergence of intolerant, early-successional species and significantly reduced the abundance of ericaceous shrubs. The appearance of *P. pensylvanica*, a typical "seed bank" species (Marks 1974), was clearly stimulated by scarification. Based on results reported by Auchmoody (1979), we hypothesize that increased nitrification following soil disturbances (Lavoie and Bradley 2003) stimulated germination of buried seeds. On the other hand, seeds of *Salix* sp. are wind-dispersed and are known to germinate within 24 h when they land on well-lit, exposed mineral soils (Louter et al. 1993), as are found in scarified plots.

We did not measure any significant effect of scarification on soil extractable N after two or four growing seasons. Nevertheless, seedlings planted in scarified plots exhibit a higher foliar N concentration than those planted in control plots, even with the dilution effect of increased biomass (Timmer 1991). Similarly, the small but significant negative impact of site preparation on soil extractable P did not result in lower foliar P concentrations. Although all scarification × fertilization combinations led to P concentrations within the critical range reported by Morrison (1974) (0.14%–0.18%), seedlings in scarified plots had enhanced foliar P concentrations compared with seedlings in S0 plots. These results indicate that scarification somehow favoured nutrient absorption, most probably because of enhanced root growth (Nordborg and Nilsson 2003), stimulated by a higher soil temperature (Kaspar and Bland 1992). The sum of soil degree-days above 5 °C was 43% higher in 2002 and 40% higher in 2004 in scarified plots than in control plots (*p* ≤ 0.003). Such increases are similar to those reviewed by Örlander et al. (1990) for sites in Fennoscandia. However, our study does not take into account all the potential direct and indirect impacts of the treatments, such as on the or-

ganic nutrient pools (Hamel et al. 2004; Näsholm et al. 1998) or the N2-fixing potential of the feather moss layer (Houle et al. 2006).

Seedlings planted in plots subjected to double-pass scarification (S2) grew taller and bigger than seedlings planted in S1 plots. In terms of height, the advantage of S2 treatment over S1 was expressed during the first two growing seasons. Afterward, the difference between the two treatments remained constant. As available soil nutrients, soil temperature regime, percent organic matter, cation-exchange capacity, and sum of soil degree-days (data not shown) were all statistically similar for the two treatments, we believe this difference to be the result of a reduction in the direct influence of the ericaceous shrubs on the seedlings during the first two seasons. Both scarification treatments significantly modified the vegetation communities compared with the control, but the influence of S2 was stronger than that of S1. Double-pass scarification has also proved more effective than single-pass scarification in reducing *K. angustifolia* cover on a *K. angustifolia* – *Vaccinium* spp. site during the first growing season (Thiffault et al. 2005). In this study, the second pass of the scarifier also significantly increased the distance between the planted seedlings and the nearest *K. angustifolia* stem (Thiffault et al. 2005). The differences were no longer apparent after three growing seasons. Although we did not measure the ericaceous cover around each seedling in the present experiment, we suspect the scarification effects to have been similar. Yamasaki et al. (1998) have shown the distance-dependant relationship between planted seedlings and ericaceous shrubs. It thus appears that the larger "ericaceous-shrub-free" area around the seedlings created by the S2 treatment than by S1 was sufficient to explain the better height growth observed during the first 2 years.

Fertilization at the time of planting was beneficial to seedling growth, although some nutrient concentrations were slightly reduced (most probably following dilution). At the same time, scarification effects on seedling growth and fifthyear dimensions were enhanced when combined with fertilization. Fertilizer release from the polymer coating is partly governed by temperature (N. Anderson, Reforestation Technologies International, 1341 Dayton Street, Salinas, Calif., personal communication). We thus suggest that scarification and fertilization interacted as follows: as the warmer soil in the scarified plots promoted seedling root growth (Kaspar and Bland 1992), it also had a beneficial effect on fertilizer release, in turn promoting seedling nutrition and seedling growth. Brand (1991) also reported positive interactions between soil modification and addition of slow-release fertilizers, confirming that efficient use of a resource is a function not only of its availability but also of other environmental factors that affect a seedling's ability to use that resource. The 15N–15P–8K plus micronutrients slow-release fertilizer (F1) proved slightly more effective than the 26N–12P–6K formulation (F2) in terms of seedling growth (a fifth-year gain in height ranging from 11% in S0 plots to 33% in S1 and S2 plots), even if the foliar N concentration was initially better with the higher fertilization rate (until year 3). When they are limiting, responses to the addition of a very small amount of micronutrients can be striking (Binkley 1986); we measured enhanced Mn and Zn concentrations in F1 seedlings compared with F2 seedlings after one growing season. Fan et al. (2002) also reported increases in seedling growth with the use of a micronutrient fertilizer compared with macronutrient amendment only.

Our results concur with those from previous studies (Thiffault et al. 2004*a*, 2005) and further indicate that ericaceous shrub – conifer interactions rarely cause seedling mortality in the short term; the lowest survival rates were observed not in control plots (no scarification, no fertilization) but in plots where seedlings were fertilized. The F2 treatment affected seedling survival rapidly, that is within the first three growing seasons. Although in a lesser proportion than for F2, the F1 treatment also induced mortality during the first three seasons. Simpson and Vyse (1995), as well as Rose and Ketchum (2003), also reported an increase in mortality due to fertilization 7 and 4 years following planting, respectively. Osmotic shock induced by the fertilizer is probably one cause of the mortality that we observed (van Den Driessche 1991). Jacobs et al. (2004) confirmed that high fertilizer salt concentrations in the Douglas-fir (*Pseudotsuga menziesii* (Mirb.) Franco) seedling rhizosphere induce predawn xylem water potentials lower than those measured in unfertilized seedlings; water stress is the main cause of mortality during the first few years following planting (Burdett 1990; Margolis and Brand 1990). It is also possible that the high nutrient availability associated with fertilization at the time of planting promoted seedling growth beyond the usual period of frost hardening, thus exposing the newly planted seedlings to lethal frost damage. This interpretation is compatible with the fact that mortality was much higher in the F2 treatment (26% N) than in the F1 treatment (15% N).

Nursery × site preparation interaction trials are of particular interest in developing innovative reforestation strategies (South et al. 2001). Based on previous studies (e.g., Boivin et al. 2004), we expected seedling growth in the field to be significantly enhanced by the larger N reserves built up following intensive nutrient loading at the nursery. However, improvements in seedling performance related to increased fertility due to nutrition practices are expected to be shortlived (Grossnickle 2000). Our results support this hypothesis; the N3 and N4 seedlings showed a slight advantage over the seedlings with the standard foliar N concentration during the first 2 years, but their growth curves joined afterward. After five growing seasons, all seedling types had similar foliar N concentrations (0.85%). In comparison, natural seedlings collected in 2001 from control plots throughout the experimental site (*n* = 16) had foliar nutrient concentrations of 1.08% N, 0.18% P, and 0.59% K. As suggested by Salifu and Timmer (2001), the short-term growth response to nutrient loading can be expected to be enhanced on fertile sites compared with poor sites. Nevertheless, our study confirms the importance of foliar N concentration as a factor in seedling quality (Burdett 1990); seedlings below Quebec's standard foliar N concentration (N1 in our study) incur initial growth losses that are carried through years and possibly never recovered.

## **Conclusion**

We hypothesized that scarification benefits seedling estab-

lishment by enhancing the availability of critical environmental resources. In accordance with our hypothesis, the results illustrate a major microsite influence on early seedling growth, and how soil temperature and the proximity of ericaceous shrubs must be managed for the other treatments to be effective. Successful regeneration of many sites with ericaceous shrubs is conditional on periodic removal of the humus layer by wildfires (Mallik 2003). Scarification of these sites can be considered a way to emulate natural processes, as it provides the conditions necessary for resetting secondary succession where ecosystem retrogression is observed following *K. angustifolia* – *R. groenlandicum* invasion. Based on profile analyses, we demonstrated how typical exponential seedling growth is only expressed when the soil is scarified. Nutritional issues, addressed in our hypotheses relating to fertilization and initial foliar N concentration, were clearly revealed once the soil was scarified. Caution should be used in extrapolating these results to other ericaceousshrub-dominated sites, as interactions may occur between treatment effects and site characteristics. However, ongoing studies on other *K. angustifolia* sites should yield results on a broader scale.

## **Management implications**

We demonstrated that soil scarification is a necessary operation for successful growth of seedlings planted on a *K. angustifolia* – *R. groenlandicum* site. Moreover, our results concur with those reported by Prévost and Dumais (2003) and by Thiffault et al. (2004*a*), who pointed out that planting to increase natural regeneration stocking is not recommended without proper soil scarification. A small additional gain in height over 5 years can be obtained from a double-pass scarification treatment. However, because of its high cost and small benefit, such a treatment cannot be recommended without a demonstration of longer term benefits.

We identified distinctive vegetation communities in control and scarified plots, the latter being closely associated with early-succession species that were absent from the former. Increased species diversity is to be expected when soil preparation reduces but does not eliminate previously abundant late-successional species (such as *K. angustifolia*) and permits the appearance of early-successional species (such as *P. pensylvanica*) (Swindel et al. 1983, 1984). Mechanical site preparation created regeneration niches for disturbancedependent indigenous plants without accelerating non-native species invasion, an important characteristic of silviculture based on natural dynamics (Haeussler et al. 2002). A shift in species dominance following scarification is expected to influence the quality of future litter inputs and further modify soil processes (Bloom and Mallik 2004; Bradley et al. 1997).

When combined with soil scarification, spot fertilization at the time of planting provided an additional growth advantage. Based on survival rates, the Forest Pak product, which contains micronutrients and has less N, appears preferable to the Silva Pak for these sites. However, the economic advantage of such treatment on these sites needs to be demonstrated, based on longer term results. Finally, we do not recommend producing seedlings with a higher N concentration than the ones currently under production, the growth gains being short-lived.
