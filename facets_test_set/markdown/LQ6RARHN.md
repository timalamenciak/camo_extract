# RESEARCH ARTICLE

# Analysis of gene origin in the first adult returns to the Cultus sockeye salmon captive breeding program

Ruth E. Withler · Robert H. Devlin · Steve Latham · Chris C. Wood · K. Janine Supernault

Received: 16 September 2010/Accepted: 26 June 2011/Published online: 26 July 2011 © Her Majesty the Queen in Right of Canada 2011

**Abstract** Rigorous evaluation of the utility of captive breeding for the restoration of depleted wild salmonid fish populations has not been undertaken. In particular, little is known about the reproductive success of captively-bred individuals that are released back into an extant population and their capacity to assist in long-term population persistence. For the endangered Cultus Lake sockeye salmon population, we examined the potential genetic contribution of the first juvenile fish released from a captive breeding program upon their maturity in the natural Cultus Lake environment. Genetic analysis of 792 Cultus sockeye salmon that were spawned in captivity in 2004 and their adult progeny of 2007 and 2008 revealed a genetic bottleneck originating from 20 wild sockeye salmon hatcheryspawned at Cultus Lake in the previous generation. Pedigree analysis revealed that six of the 20 sockeye salmon spawned in 2001 (grandparents) gave rise to a majority of the hatchery spawners in 2004 (parents) and provided more than 30% of the genes in the progeny that survived to maturity in the wild. Allele frequencies and genetic diversity of the age three progeny that returned to Cultus Lake from their marine migration in 2007 reflected the bottleneck, but its genetic signature was faint among the more genetically diverse age four fish that returned in 2008. Two-generation analysis of gene origin among fish resulting from 2004 hatchery production indicated that they contained the genetic diversity expected from 36 effective ancestors.

#### Introduction

Populations greatly reduced in size during extended periods of low survival lose genetic diversity due to genetic drift and inbreeding, processes that may lead to reduced fitness, further reductions in survival and the possibility of an extinction vortex (Gilpin and Soulé 1986). Hatchery programs are in widespread use for Pacific salmonid populations but little assessment of their effectiveness for wild population recovery has been conducted (Waples et al. 2007). A fundamental question remains as to whether the immediate increase in abundance provided by hatchery production can offset the expected reduction in fitness for re-introduced hatchery individuals that breed in the wild (Araki et al. 2008; Fraser 2008). Captive breeding, in which individuals are removed from the natural environment for at least one entire life cycle to increase survival and reproductive capacity, can rapidly increase juvenile production in highly fecund species such as sockeye salmon. Pedigreed breeding programs can minimize the loss of genetic diversity due to inbreeding and random processes (e.g. O'Reilly and Harvie 2010), but pose their own challenges to maintenance of fitness in the wild environment (Fraser 2008). In this case study, we evaluate some genetic consequences of a program of captive breeding designed to recover an endangered sockeye salmon population in Cultus Lake, located in southern British Columbia (BC). The Cultus Lake population is a genetically distinctive member of the 'late run' group of Fraser River sockeye populations that are so-named because of their adult time of entry into the Fraser River (historically, mid September through October) on the spawning migration to natal tributaries. Average abundance of Cultus sockeye salmon declined from over 20,000 spawners from 1930 to 1960, to less than 10,000 from 1960 to 1990, and less than 4,000 since 1990. The Committee on the Status of Endangered Wildlife in Canada designated the population as Endangered in 2003 (COSEWIC [2003](#page-13-0)), and Fisheries and Oceans Canada established a recovery team in 2004 (CSRT [2009](#page-13-0)). Factors responsible for low survival have been identified in both the freshwater and marine environments occupied by Cultus sockeye salmon (CSRT [2009](#page-13-0)). Some of these factors are specific to the Cultus population whereas others also impact other Fraser River late run sockeye salmon populations (Cooke et al. [2006](#page-13-0); Miller et al. [2011\)](#page-13-0).

Even prior to the most recent declines in abundance, the Cultus sockeye population was characterized by unusual allele frequencies and low levels of diversity at allozyme and microsatellite loci relative to other Fraser River sockeye populations (Withler et al. [2000\)](#page-14-0). A primary objective of the conservation strategy developed for the Cultus sockeye population was to ''Ensure the genetic integrity of the population by exceeding a 4-year arithmetic mean of 1,000 successful adult spawners with no fewer than 500 successful adult spawners on any one cycle'' (CSRT [2009\)](#page-13-0). Conservation measures have been undertaken to improve both freshwater and marine survival, including annual reduction of Eurasian watermilfoil along the lakeshore, predator (northern pikeminnow) removal from the lake limnetic zone and harvest restrictions in the mixed-stock Fraser River sockeye salmon fisheries (Bradford et al. [2011](#page-13-0)). In addition, a two-generation (8 year) captive breeding program was initiated to protect a random sample of fish from the extremely low and unpredictable smolt-to-adult survival that threatened the population's persistence, and to promote the production of large numbers of juvenile fish for release into Cultus Lake or its outlet stream, Sweltzer Creek.

In this study, we use genetic methods to assign individuals that have returned from hatchery releases of Cultus Lake sockeye salmon to their parents and grandparents and estimate their potential contribution to the wild population. We establish a pedigree for the first generation of largescale hatchery production that will be used in this initial and ongoing assessment of the impacts of hatchery production on effective population size and coancestry among Cultus sockeye salmon that spawn in the wild. Our goal is to determine whether the hatchery program makes a beneficial contribution to wild population persistence, a contribution requiring the successful reproduction of hatcheryproduced fish in the natural environment of Cultus Lake.

The Cultus hatchery program, established in 2000, was founded with wild-born indigenous sockeye salmon. Spawning of broodstock has been conducted at the Cultus Lake laboratory with egg incubation and juvenile rearing undertaken at the nearby Inch Creek Hatchery (ICH) in the lower Fraser River drainage (Fig. [1\)](#page-2-0). Most of the juveniles from ICH were released into Cultus Lake the year following spawning (hatchery supplementation), but a subset of juveniles was transferred each year to Rosewall Creek Hatchery (RCH) on Vancouver Island for rearing until maturity and spawning (captive breeding). In addition, wild Cultus sockeye salmon smolts emigrating from Cultus Lake were captured in 2002 and 2003 and used to augment the fish transferred to RCH for the captive breeding. Once captive breeding at RCH started, juvenile progeny of captive breeders were transferred back to ICH, combined with the juveniles produced from supplementation and released to Cultus Lake or Sweltzer Creek as juveniles (Fig. [2\)](#page-3-0).

Large numbers of captively-bred progeny were first produced at RCH in 2004, a year in which fewer than 100 wild sockeye salmon returned to Cultus Lake itself, the lowest return on record (since 1925). The release of large numbers of captively-bred juveniles from a year in which few wild sockeye had entered Cultus Lake to spawn raised concern about the level of genetic diversity that would be present in the 2008 Cultus sockeye salmon return, expected to comprise predominantly hatchery-produced age four fish. In the current study we examined the level and sources of genetic variation in the hatchery-produced sockeye salmon that returned from the 2004 captive spawning to Cultus Lake in 2007/2008 to determine the total genetic contribution to the population from a single year of captive breeding. We performed the same analyses on all Cultus Lake sockeye salmon that returned to spawn in 2008 to determine the potential genetic impact of captive breeding on a single year of natural spawning when few wild fish returned.

A two-generation pedigree was established for the hatchery program with data from 14 polymorphic microsatellite loci. The microsatellite genotypes were used to determine parent-progeny relationships between the 2004 hatchery broodstock fish (generation G1) and their adult progeny that returned in 2007 and 2008 (G2). In addition, relationships among G1 genotypes were used to reconstruct the prior generation (G0) that produced them. ## Materials and methods

Sampling of Cultus Lake sockeye salmon in the wild

Cultus sockeye salmon spawn on gravel beds associated with the beaches and bays of Cultus Lake. Juvenile fish (smolts) emigrate through the lake outlet (Sweltzer Creek) and the lower Fraser River drainage to its the estuary in Georgia Strait (Fig. 1). The marine migratory route involves northward movement in coastal BC waters, offshore feeding in the Gulf of Alaska and a return trip undertaken as maturation approaches at age three, four or, less frequently, five (CSRT [2009\)](#page-13-0). Thus, generations overlap in the wild, with spawning fish in each year generally derived from at least 2 years of prior spawning.

# Hatchery spawning and sampling

Reared in captivity at RCH, the majority of Cultus Lake sockeye salmon (both male and female) mature at age three, with small proportions maturing at ages two and four. Thus, the transfer of juveniles to RCH for rearing until maturity and re-introduction of their progeny to Cultus Lake has increased the overlap of generations in Cultus Lake wild spawning. The hatchery- and wild-born fish spawned at RCH and Cultus Lake in 2004 comprised G1 of this study and were sampled for genetic analysis. At Cultus Lake, only 25 live wild-born fish (10 females and 15 males) were spawned, but cryopreserved milt from 36 additional wild-born males that had returned to Cultus Lake in 2002 was also used in egg fertilization. At RCH, 495 spawners were hatchery-born. They comprised 486 age three progeny of 20 G0 fish spawned at Cultus Lake in 2001 and nine age two progeny from the 2002 Cultus Lake crosses. Seventeen of the 20 fish spawned in 2001 were sampled for genetic analysis.

Wild-born G1 fish spawned at RCH in 2004 were 236 fish that matured from the wild smolt collections made in 2002 and 2003. Additionally, milt from 12 of the 15 live wild-born brood fish used at Cultus Lake in 2004 was also transferred to and used in crosses made at RCH. Crosses at RCH were made both within and between the hatcheryand wild-born parents, with parents of both sexes generally mated with two or three individuals. Only two-thirds of the hatchery-produced fish were marked, with all unmarked fish progeny of hatchery-born parents spawned at RCH between Nov. 18 and 23. At Cultus Lake, eggs of the 10 wild female broodstock were fertilized in semi-factorial fashion with milt from the 15 live males and cryopreserved milt from the 36 males from 2002. Virtually all ([95%) of the live and cryopreserved G1 parental spawners used at both RCH and Cultus Lake laboratory in 2004 were sampled for genetic analysis.

## Microsatellite analysis

Genetic analysis of archived tissue samples collected from fish sampled in the wild was begun in 2006. Analysis of archived samples from hatchery spawners was not undertaken until unusual allele frequencies were observed in the hatchery-produced fish that returned to Cultus Lake in 2007 (at age three) from captive breeding in 2004. All genetic analyses were conducted on a dataset of 14 polymorphic unlinked microsatellite loci, all of which display Mendelian single locus inheritance in the Cultus sockeye salmon population as described in Beacham et al. [\(2005](#page-13-0)). The loci were Ots2, Ots3 (Banks et al. [1999\)](#page-13-0), Ots100, Ots103, Ots107, Ots108 (Beacham et al. [1998;](#page-13-0) Nelson and Beacham [1999\)](#page-13-0), Oki1a, Oki1b, Oki6, Oki10, Oki16, Oki29 (Smith et al. [1998;](#page-14-0) Nelson et al. [2003](#page-13-0)), One8 (Scribner et al. [1996\)](#page-14-0), and Omy77 (Morris et al. [1996\)](#page-13-0). Samples scored for less than 10 loci were excluded. Allelic richness (AR) standardized to a sample size of 24 fish, gene diversity (HE) and pairwise FST values were calculated for samples using FSTAT (Goudet [1995](#page-13-0), [2001](#page-13-0)). The 'two parent' exclusion power of the microsatellite loci for Cultus sockeye salmon was determined (Jamieson and St. Taylor [1997](#page-13-0)).

## Parentage assignment and pedigree reconstruction

The two generation pedigree established for the progeny (G2 generation) that returned in 2007 and 2008 from the 2004 spawners (G1 generation) includes grandparents (G0 generation) that spawned in the wild between 1998 and 2001 as well as those that were hatchery-spawned at Cultus Lake in 2001 and 2002. No tissue samples were available for G0 grandparents that spawned in the wild. Instead, the identification of full- and half-sibling families among the wild-born G1 spawners, conducted by maximum likelihood (ML) analysis in the program COLONY (Wang [2004\)](#page-14-0), enabled their assignment to G0 parental fish. The fish collected as smolts in 2002 and 2003 were all assumed to have been emigrating at age one; therefore they arose from wild spawning that occurred in 2000 and 2001, respectively. The G1 broodstock fish spawned at Cultus were assigned to wild G0 parents that spawned between 1999 and 2001 based on their ages (3–5) as determined by otolith readings. Thus, wild fish spawned at Cultus in 2004 shared some G0 parentage from 2000 and 2001 with the smolts, collected from the wild, that matured and were spawned at RCH in 2004. Genotypes for 739 and 254 wild-born fish from the 2000 and 2001 spawning years were analyzed, respectively. Of these, the wild-born fish that were spawned at RCH and the Cultus Lake lab in 2004 were 223-fish and 30-fish subsets from 2000 and 2001, respectively.

The ML analysis conducted with COLONY (Wang [2004](#page-14-0)) consisted of the simultaneous examination of all individuals from each spawning year (a group-likelihood approach) in order to partition them into families of variable size based on Mendelian inheritance of alleles at unlinked loci. The program was run assuming parental polygamy and with both types of genotyping error set at 0.01 for all loci. Error types are class I (allelic dropout) and class II (all other 'errors' including miscalling of alleles, PCR artifacts and mutation). The assigned error rate was considered an upper bound for plausible values given that error rates in repeated genotyping of samples from this species in our laboratory are almost an order of magnitude lower. Re-genotyping does not reveal all sources of Mendelian 'error' (e.g. mutation, some PCR artifacts). For loci as polymorphic as the Cultus Lake sockeye salmon microsatellites of this study, the probability of including an unrelated individual in a fullsib family of five or greater was likely less than 0.01 (Almudevar and Field [1999](#page-13-0)). Since 70% of sampled wild-born fish from 2000 and 2001 were included in full sibships of five or more fish (unpublished data), the majority of the wild-born G1 fish were likely identified correctly to G0 family.

Tissue samples were available for most of the hatcheryborn grandparents spawned in 2001, but not 2002. Thus, all 495 hatchery-born spawners of 2004 except the nine born in 2002 were assigned to their parents by genotypic exclusion. Assignment of marked G2 fish to the large number of G1 parents, also by exclusion, was facilitated by the use of CERVUS (Kalinowski et al. [2007](#page-13-0)) and of hatchery records. Unmarked fish that returned from the 2004 spawning were also assigned to hatchery parents by exclusion if possible; only those that could not be assigned hatchery parents were classified as wild. A summarized G0–G2 pedigree was drawn with the assistance of the PedigraphTM program (Garbe and Da [2008](#page-13-0)).

#### Analysis of gene origin and effective population size

Age three and four fish that returned to Cultus Lake in 2007 and 2008 were the generation of interest (G2) in this study. Assessment of this generation enabled calculation of the effective potential genetic contribution from the first year of large-scale captive breeding to the Cultus sockeye population, with the contribution spread over 2 years (there was a negligible return of age five fish in 2009). An analysis of probable gene origins (Boichard et al. [1997](#page-13-0)) and estimation of effective population size (Ne) in the Cultus sockeye G1 and G2 generations was undertaken through pedigree analysis conducted with the program ENDOG 4.5 (Gutie´rrez and Goyache [2005](#page-13-0)).

Average relatedness (AR), the probability that an allele chosen randomly from the whole population in the

Ancestors in a pedigree are individuals who have made non-redundant genetic contributions; the numbers of total, effective and 'accountable for 50% of genetic variation' ancestors were calculated for both populations of interest (Boichard et al. 1997). Founders are ancestors whose parents are unknown and founder genome equivalents (FGE) are the number of founders required to produce the observed level of genetic diversity if all had contributed equally with no loss of alleles (Ballou and Lacy 1995). In ENDOG, FGE values are calculated as the inverse of twice the average coancestry of the individuals in the population (Caballero and Toro 2000).

Inbreeding effective population size,  $N_{eI}$ , of  $G_2$  was calculated in ENDOG using the increase in inbreeding  $(\Delta F)$  in each generation in the relationship  $N_{\rm eI} = 1/$  $2\Delta F$ . Note that because there were no known relationships among fish of  $G_0$ , no  $\Delta F$  took place in  $G_1$  by definition; hence the estimated values of  $N_{\rm eI}$  account only for the  $\Delta F$  that occurred in G<sub>2</sub>. Variance effective population size  $(N_{\rm eV})$  was calculated for  $G_1$  and  $G_2$  using the variance in progeny production according to:  $N_{\rm eV} = (\sum N_{ii})^2 / \sum N_{ii}^2$ where  $N_{ij}$  are the numbers of progeny produced by male and female parents. The  $N_{\rm eV}$  value over two generations was calculated as the harmonic mean of G<sub>1</sub> and G<sub>2</sub> values. Importantly,  $N_e$  values calculated were not  $N_e$  values for the Cultus Lake sockeye salmon population as a whole because, for a Pacific salmonid population,  $N_e$  is the sum of the effective number of breeders  $(N_b)$  in each year of the average generation time (Waples 2004).

The  $G_2$  returned to Cultus Lake over 2 years and constituted the majority of the fish that returned in 2008. The impact of the hatchery program on a single yearclass of the wild population was evaluated by extending the analysis of gene origin and effective population size to all adult sockeye salmon returns to Cultus Lake in 2008 (thus including 3-year old fish derived from 2005 spawning).

Calculation of contributions from 2005 wild and hatchery spawners were conducted in the same manner as described for 2004.

Maximum likelihood analysis of parental contributions

The restricted parentage, and resulting unusual allele frequencies, of the hatchery-born G<sub>1</sub> at RCH in 2004 enabled an independent estimation of their genetic contribution to the returning G<sub>2</sub> fish sampled in 2007 and 2008. Admixture of genes contributed by G<sub>1</sub> hatchery- and wild-born parents to G<sub>2</sub> was estimated from the microsatellite data using the likelihood estimation of admixture (LEADMIX) program (Wang 2003). This analysis is based on a model in which two or more source populations derived from a common ancestral population become admixed (hybridized) after secondary contact is re-established. Changes in allele frequencies through drift occur both in the source populations after their divergence from the ancestral population and in the admixed population derived from the source populations. The proportions of genes contributed by the source populations (and their 95% confidence intervals) are estimated based on an assigned minimum level of drift (0.00001 herein), with mutation ignored.

The total number of hatchery-produced G2 fish sampled in this study was 280, 62 at age three and 218 at age four (Table [1](#page-5-0)). In 2007, all 56 marked and all six unmarked age three fish that were sampled in the adult return were assigned by exclusion to 2004 spawners at RCH or Cultus Lake. Among the age four fish, all 198 marked and 20 of 36 unmarked fish were assigned to 2004 hatchery production. The unmarked fish assigned to G1 hatchery parents were members of the unmarked hatchery production from the 2004 spawning year. Of the 26 age three fish that were sampled in the 2008 return, only three were wild (unmarked). #### Allelic diversity

Genic diversity and allelic richness at the 14 microsatellite loci did not change in wild Cultus sockeye salmon sampled between 1992 and 2007 (Table 2). Hatchery-produced fish from 2002 and 2003 (primarily supplemental releases from ICH) had diversity values similar to those of wild samples (Table 2). However, G2 hatchery-produced age three fish sampled in 2007 (the captively-bred fish of this study) had lower levels of both genic diversity (HE) and allelic richness (AR). Allele frequencies of these fish were also unusual compared to other samples of naturally- and hatchery-spawned Cultus sockeye salmon. Whereas pairwise FST values between samples of wild fish and prior hatchery returns were less than 0.01 and seldom significant, pairwise FST values involving the captively-bred hatcheryproduced fish from 2004 with other groups were all significant (P\0.05) and ranged up to 0.02.

The reduced genetic variation observed in the hatchery-produced age three fish of 2007 prompted the genetic analysis of the 2004 G1 hatchery spawners that produced them. This analysis revealed that the hatchery-born spawners that dominated the RCH broodstock had also been characterized by low levels of diversity, skewed allele frequencies and a significant excess of observed heterozygotes compared with those expected (Table 2). These fish, progeny of crosses made at Cultus Lake in 2001, were primarily members of two large halfsib families (Families A and B), each resulting from the mating of a single female with two different males (Table [3\)](#page-7-0). The remainder were progeny of a factorial cross among five females (P–T) and five males (V–Z) (Table [3\)](#page-7-0). However, values of both AR and H<sup>E</sup> were higher in the age four G2 fish that returned in 2008 than in the age three G2 (Table 2).

# Reconstruction of G0 generation

Assignment of G1 fish (2004 hatchery spawners) to parents reconstituted the grandparental (G0) generation fish that spawned in the wild or were hatchery-spawned at Cultus Lake (Table 3; Fig. [3\)](#page-8-0). The 297 wild-born G1 spawners at RCH and Cultus in 2004 originated from 227 G0 parents that had spawned between 1998 and 2001, inclusive. The majority of them (207 females and 16 males) were age four fish that were among 739 fish arising from wild spawning in 2000 that were genotyped and assigned to 155 full-sib families. The 223 G1 spawners wild-born in 2000 included broodstock used both at Cultus and RCH and were assigned to 158 G0 parents (Table 3; Fig. [3\)](#page-8-0); each of these parents had spawned in the wild with as many as three contributing mates and individually produced up to 17 progeny in G1.

Similarly, the 30 age three wild-born G1 spawners used at RCH and Cultus were assigned to 55 G0 parents from 2001 and the eight age five Cultus spawners were assigned to ten G0 parents from 1999 (Table 3; Fig. [3\)](#page-8-0). The family structure of 36 males that contributed milt that was cryopreserved in 2002 and used at Cultus in 2004 was unknown, but only four of them produced progeny that returned in 2007/2008. These four males were arbitrarily assigned to two single pair families (four parents) from 1998 (Table 3; Fig. [3](#page-8-0)).

Assignment of hatchery-born G1 spawners used at RCH in 2004 to G0 crosses made with 20 parents in 2001 at Cultus Lake was straightforward due to the large number of G1 progeny involved. Four of the 20 G0 parents produced no G1 progeny that were spawned in 2004; these four parents have not been shown in the G0 (Table 3; Fig. [3](#page-8-0)). The remaining 16 G0 parents hatchery-born in 2001 are each indicated by a letter or letter-number combination (Table 3; Fig. [3\)](#page-8-0). The vast majority of hatchery-born G1 spawners were members of Families A and B, created in 2001 with six parents (Tables 3, [4;](#page-8-0) Fig. [3](#page-8-0)).

The nine G0 males from 2002 Cultus crosses were not sampled and therefore were arbitrarily assigned to three hatchery-produced families (three males to each pair of parents) (Table 3; Fig. [3\)](#page-8-0). Thus, the 495 hatchery-born spawners of G1 arose from a total of 22 G0 parents, 16 spawned in 2001 and six in 2002 at Cultus Lake. The hatchery-born spawners at RCH, especially Families A and B, provided most of the gametes in G1 hatchery production of 2004 (Table [4](#page-8-0)). Most G1 parents spawned at RCH and Cultus in 2004 contributed to progeny production in G1 although many did not contribute to subsequent G2 adult returns in 2007 and 2008.

## Effective population size

For all of the pedigreed G2 fish in the returns from the 2004 hatchery production, estimated NeI was 93 (Table [5\)](#page-9-0). NeI was larger for the more diverse age four returns of 2008 (125) than for the more inbred age three returns of 2007 (50). The NeI estimates were larger than those based on variance in progeny production (Table [5\)](#page-9-0) because of the shallowness of the available pedigree (which precluded an estimate of DF in G1) and the fact that fewer inbred G2 progeny returned than expected based on parental gametic contributions in 2004, especially in 2008.

The G1 NeV value of 35 revealed the effect of the predominance of fish belonging to Families A and B in G1 (Table [3](#page-7-0)). This number is the 'realized' NeV, because it is based only on G1 fish that reproduced successfully (i.e. parented a fish that returned in G2). In contrast, the NeV of 183 estimated for G2 (Table [5](#page-9-0)) has not yet been realized, in that it is based on fish that survived to reproductive age in 2007 and 2008, but have themselves not yet proven reproductively successful. The harmonic mean of NeV for G1 and G2 was 54.

Estimates of N<sup>e</sup> calculated for all fish in the breeding population of 2008 (Nb) were higher than those for the age four hatchery-produced fish due to the presence of 23 pedigreed hatchery-produced 3-year-old fish from spawning in 2005 (Table [4\)](#page-8-0). In addition, the estimates of NeV, but not NeI, for 2008 were increased by the presence of low numbers of wild age three and four fish. The wild fish, for which a two-generation pedigree was not available, were by definition founders and not inbred. Inclusion of the additional fish increased the NeV estimates for both G1 (44) and G2 (193), providing a harmonic mean value of 72 for the 2008 N<sup>b</sup> (Table [5](#page-9-0)). None of 23 pedigreed hatcheryproduced age three fish from 2005 were inbred. As a result, the presence of these fish reduced the overall level of inbreeding in the 2008 return and correspondingly increased the NeI estimate to 138 (Table [5](#page-9-0)).

#### Discussion

For a population of conservation concern, creation of a multigenerational pedigree enables better estimation of inbreeding and effective population size than most methods based on coancestry values derived from allele sharing estimation (Pemberton [2008\)](#page-14-0). Moreover, maintenance of a pedigree enables examination of heritability of traits and breeding structure during natural breeding (Szulkin and Sheldon [2008](#page-14-0); Grant and Grant [2008\)](#page-13-0) and optimization of parent choice and mating design (Pong-Wong and Woolliams [2007\)](#page-14-0) in hatchery breeding of the population under study. Development of the two-generation pedigrees for the Cultus Lake sockeye salmon that returned from hatchery spawning in 2004 and for the entire Cultus sockeye breeding population of 2008 enabled a realistic assessment of the amount and sources of genetic variation incorporated into the Cultus Lake sockeye salmon hatchery program and its potential impact in 2008. Maintenance of the pedigree for additional generations will enable assessment of kinship and the conservation value of individuals in the population as well as the reproductive success of both wildand hatchery-born individuals in natural spawning in Cultus Lake.

The two different methods (ML analysis of allele frequencies and individual parentage assignment) gave similar estimates of the contributions from hatchery- and wildborn parents to their progeny returning from the hatchery spawning of 2004. Parentage assignment allowed the estimation of the number of progeny returning to crosses made at the Cultus Lake lab because there was no significant difference in the allele frequencies of wild-born parents spawned at the two hatchery facilities. Ten of the 14 microsatellite loci employed in the study were highly polymorphic (possessed 10 or more alleles) even in the Cultus Lake population of sockeye salmon. Nevertheless, assignment of some progeny whose parents were both drawn from Families A and/or B to an exclusive pair of parents would not have been possible without use of the hatchery cross records to confirm that a pair of potential parents had actually been mated. For future generations of returning fish, ML estimation of parental contributions may not be possible because the more diverse groups of hatchery-born fish spawned at RCH since 2005 have not been characterized by such highly distinctive allele frequencies. On the other hand, parentage assignment for individual fish will be simplified by a vast reduction in the 'Aunt and Uncle effect' (Olsen et al. [2001](#page-14-0)) resulting from the limited numbers of eggs per family transferred each year from Cultus to RCH after 2001.

The pedigree captured known relationships among hatchery-born sockeye salmon transferred from spawning at Cultus to RCH for rearing, as well as inferred sibling relationships among wild-born fish spawned at both facilities. Most of the wild-born fish bred in 2004 were age four fish from natural spawning in 2000, a year in which 1227 sockeye salmon had entered Cultus Lake to spawn. Over

![](_page_10_Picture_12.jpeg)

700 fish born in the wild in 2000, primarily from the wild smolt collection of 2002, were available for estimation of full-and half-sib family structure. Recognition of the sibling relationships among the subset of these fish that was incorporated into the population by hatchery spawning prevented a significant overestimation of their contribution to genetic variation. The alternative of considering all 739 fish as unrelated would have required a parental population size of 1478, a number greater than the total return in the year 2000. Treating related individuals of unknown origin as founders (i.e. assuming that they were unrelated to each other and to other individuals in the pedigree) without correction could have potentially serious consequences for future conservation decisions based on kinship values (Oliehoek and Bijma [2009\)](#page-13-0).

ML estimation of single-generation family structure performed as well as pairwise allele-sharing methods in another sockeye salmon breeding program (Kozfkay et al. [2008\)](#page-13-0) when tested on known samples in which family size was limited to two. Moreover, larger sample sizes and larger sibships confer greater exclusion power to group ML methods and give them an advantage over pairwise methods in family identification (Almudevar and Field [1999](#page-13-0); Wang [2004\)](#page-14-0). This increased power improves identification of siblings that share relatively few alleles by chance and of unrelated individuals that share more alleles than expected by chance. Nevertheless, error in the sibships defined for the wild-born G1 fish lacking parental genotype information undoubtedly occurred. However, in the absence of these somewhat arbitrary sibships, the wild-born G1 fish would all have been founders in the pedigree and considered unrelated by definition. This assumption would have led to inflated estimates of N<sup>e</sup> in G2 and subsequent generations.

The pedigree has also been established to enable ongoing tracking of relationships in the Cultus sockeye population. Fish spawned at both facilities in years since 2004 have been related not only to each other but also to fish spawned in earlier and later years because of variation in sockeye salmon age of maturity. Moreover, the combined result of captive rearing and the difference in the predominant age of maturity between the natural (age four) and hatchery (age three) environments is that the fish released from supplementation at ICH in a given year are aunts and uncles to many of those released from captive rearing 3 years later. The pedigree established for the population, although by necessity an incomplete one, will enable longer-term assessment of genetic variation in the population as the consequences of small population size and captive breeding on coancestry, inbreeding levels and effective population size are realized.

The large difference between the total and effective number of ancestors for both G1 and G2 Cultus sockeye salmon revealed that contributions of the ancestors were highly unequal (Boichard et al. [1997\)](#page-13-0). This disparity was confirmed by the fact that only the 20 most influential ancestors out of a total of 142 were required to account for 50% of genes in G2. This imbalance largely resulted from the bottleneck imposed in the pedigree by the disproportionate representation of the small number of families created at Cultus in 2001. Not surprisingly, 11 of the 20 most influential ancestors were members of that lineage. Calculation of the number of ancestors for a population takes into account the loss of genetic variation that has occurred since founding due to bottlenecks, but not the loss due to segregation of parental alleles (Boichard et al. [1997](#page-13-0)), whereas all sources of gene loss are accounted for in estimation of the FGE for a population (Lacy [1989\)](#page-13-0). Unlike Ne, which decreases over time only if inbreeding or the variance in allele frequencies increases, FGE for a population declines over time due to the accumulating segregational loss of alleles each generation (Boichard et al. [1997](#page-13-0)). The FGE value of 32 for the Cultus sockeye G2 indicates that the equivalent of 32 complete founding genomes were still present. The similarity of this value to the NeV estimate of 35 for G1 suggests that, in fact, the greatest loss of diversity in the pedigree had arisen from the bottleneck rather from the segregational loss of alleles. This result reflects the shallowness of the pedigree (i.e. segregation had occurred only twice) and the highly variable contributions of founding families, both wild- and hatchery-born.

The captive breeding component of the hatchery program began producing large numbers of fish from spawning in 2004, a year in which only 53 wild fish spawned in Cultus Lake. Even if no fish had been removed from the return for hatchery spawning, only 90 sockeye salmon would have spawned in Cultus Lake. The number of effective breeders in a single yearclass of Pacific salmonids (Nb) is generally not greater than one-third of the adult census count (Waples [2004\)](#page-14-0). Therefore, in the absence of the hatchery program, N<sup>b</sup> for Cultus sockeye in 2008 would have been unlikely to exceed the value of 54 estimated for

![](_page_11_Picture_9.jpeg)

the hatchery-produced G2. However, the reproductive potential of the hatchery-produced fish under natural spawning conditions has not yet been assessed. Moreover, even in the absence of any fitness differential between hatchery- and wild-born fish, combined hatchery and natural rearing in a salmonid population generally is expected to reduce N<sup>e</sup> over time because of the increased the variation in family size (i.e., successful hatcheries produce larger families than would be produced by natural freshwater rearing). A long-term reduction in N<sup>e</sup> due to hatchery production will be avoided in Cultus sockeye salmon only if the hatchery-produced fish contribute to an increase in population size that is sustained after hatchery production ceases (Waples and Do [1994\)](#page-14-0).

The G1 fish spawned at Rosewall in 2004 were not typical of later captive broodstocks. The high rate of maturation at age three in a small set of families created at Cultus in 2001 likely had both genetic and environmental components, with maturation accelerated both by a high proportion of age three G0 parents and faster growth in captivity. Over 50% of G1 parents used at Rosewall and Cultus came from two families reared at Rosewall, each comprising the progeny of a single female mated with two different males. In subsequent years, the hatchery-produced broodstock at Rosewall has been derived from larger numbers of Cultus Lake parents, whose contributions in captive rearing have been largely equalized.

Analyses of the age four G2 fish confirmed the success of measures implemented to increase genetic diversity in the captive broodstock beyond that represented in the limited crosses made at Cultus in 2001. The substantial genetic impact of G1 parents derived from the wild smolt collections was primarily responsible for the normalization of allele frequencies and levels of genetic diversity in these fish. In addition, assignment methods indicated that about 10% of the age four G2 were progeny of Cultus crosses made with wild rather than captively-reared fish, although these crosses contributed less than 5% of the juveniles released. Thus, fish from the supplementation program tended to either mature a year later or survive better than those from the captive breeding program, or both. Unfortunately, differences in the rates of survival cannot be interpreted simply in terms of fitness because fish from the two hatchery sources were distributed unequally among release groups that were treated quite differently and subjected to different levels of predation during freshwater residence in Cultus Lake.

Inbreeding, which may cause loss of fitness in wild populations (Frankham [2010\)](#page-13-0), is of particular concern in re-introduced populations (Biebach and Keller [2010\)](#page-13-0). Any ability to manage the breeding structure of a threatened population that was afforded by captivity is likely lost upon re-introduction to the wild and inbreeding may accumulate rapidly while population size remains small (Ewing et al. [2008](#page-13-0)). In the case of salmonids, little is known about the extent to which, if any, individuals mating in the wild engage in inbreeding avoidance during mate selection (Hendry et al. [2004](#page-13-0)). Captive individuals from the Cultus program were released into an extant population of recent coancestry so that inbreeding may have occurred whether hatchery-produced individuals bred with each other or with wild-born individuals. Additionally, environmental or genetic factors other than inbreeding that reduce the reproductive capacity of hatchery-born animals in the wild (Araki et al. [2007a](#page-13-0); Schroder et al. [2008\)](#page-14-0) may impact 'hybrid' progeny resulting from the mating of hatcheryand wild-born parents, leading to a further decline in population productivity.

The 2008 return of 521 sockeye salmon was five times larger than that of 2004, with 40 fish likely produced from wild spawning. This can viewed as a successful outcome of the captive breeding program although the stated objectives of the conservation measures have not yet been achieved (Bradford et al. [2011\)](#page-13-0). The next significant genetic assessment of the hatchery-produced Cultus Lake sockeye salmon will involve their ability to spawn in the wild and produce progeny that themselves survive to spawn in the wild. A series of studies examining the spawning success of steelhead trout from a supplementation hatchery has produced variable results in which the reproductive capacity in the wild of hatchery-produced trout was estimated to decline by as much as 30% for each generation of hatchery rearing (Araki et al. [2007a,](#page-13-0) [b](#page-13-0)), a result that modelling indicated could be the result of domestication selection acting upon multiple traits (Araki et al. [2008](#page-13-0)). Assessment of the next generation indicated that the reduction in reproductive capacity extended to the wildborn fish that were produced by captive fish that spawned in the wild (Araki et al. [2009](#page-13-0)). Demographic and genetic analysis of sockeye salmon smolt production and adult returns at Cultus Lake over the coming years will enable evaluation of the reproductive capacity of hatchery-produced fish and perhaps reveal the ultimate consequences of the hatchery program on population persistence.
