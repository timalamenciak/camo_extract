Evaluating efforts to increase salmonid productive capacity through habitat enhancement in the low diversity/production systems of Newfoundland, Canada.

K. D. Clarke and D. A. Scruton

Fisheries and Oceans Canada, Science, Oceans and Environment Branch, P.O. Box 5667, St. John's, NF A1C 5X1 CANADA

#### **Abstract**

Habitat enhancement through the improvement of natural habitat and the restoration of degraded habitats has formed a significant component of salmonid management strategies in Newfoundland, Canada over the past 15 years. Projects have taken many forms with most being conducted on a small scale through local non-government organisations, mostly utilising methodologies developed in other jurisdictions, and with limited scientific evaluation. A small proportion of these projects have been conducted in conjunction with habitat researchers at the Canadian Department of Fisheries and Oceans. This sub-sample of projects, as well as directed research studies, and a few major restoration initiatives, form a substantial body of knowledge on habitat manipulations for our geographical area. These projects are reviewed under three broad categories: 1) Projects using instream structures for habitat enhancement in degraded streams; 2) Habitat improvement initiatives in natural systems, including targeted habitat manipulations and chemical additions; and 3) Major restoration or compensation projects conducted to offset habitat loses due to anthropogenic development. Each habitat enhancement methodology is discussed with respect to it's ability to increase the productive capacity of salmonid habitat under the low production conditions of Newfoundland systems.

#### Introduction

Freshwater systems in Newfoundland are usually dilute with low nutrient concentrations and a low primary production potential (Kerekes 1974, Knoechel and Campbell 1988, Scruton 1983, Ryan and Wakeham 1984). The fish communities themselves are simple with only hyposaline species recolonating the freshwaters of insular Newfoundland after the Wisconsian Glaciation (Scott and Crossman 1964). The most abundant and widespread of these are the Atlantic salmon (Salmo salar) and the brook trout (Salvelinus fontinalis). These two species make up the basis of the recreational fishery in Newfoundland and until 1991 a viable commercial salmon fishery was conducted around the island. The low species diversity has been hypothesized to promote an expansion of useable habitat for these species (Gibson et al. 1993). Thus a number of habitats that would not be traditionally regarded as good salmonid habitat are important production areas in many Newfoundland systems (Ryan 1986; Hutchings 1986; O'Connell and Ash 1989; O'Connell et al. 1990; Ryan et al. 1993; Erkinaro and Gibson 1997; Dempson et al. 1996).

A general decline in salmonid stocks in Newfoundland, coupled with increasing demands on salmonid resources, has focussed attention on maintaining and restoring salmonid habitat. As a result of the increased emphasis on fish habitat, a number of major habitat improvement and restoration programs have been undertaken in Newfoundland over the past fifteen years. Programs have included two major 5-year federal-provincial agreements; the Newfoundland Inshore Fisheries Development Agreement (NIFDA), Small Stream Component (1988 to 1992) followed by the Cooperation Agreement for Salmonid Enhancement and Conservation (CASEC), Habitat Improvement and Restoration Component (1992 to 1997). A number of other programs have

supported regional habitat restoration initiatives, including the Environmental Partner's Fund (EPF) of Environment Canada, Canada's Green Plan - Habitat Action Plan (HAP), Wildlife Habitat Canada, the Newfoundland Conservation Corps 'Green Teams', and others.

Ecological restoration is a process where an ecosystem is manipulated in order to return it to its original state after an external perturbation (Bradshaw 1996). Habitat improvement, on the other hand, generally refers to changes made in a natural ecosystem to improve the productive capacity of the entire ecosystem and thus benefit a target species and/or community. Both methods are widely used in aquatic ecosystem management, see papers in Kelso (1996) and references in Duff et al. (1995). Habitat 'enhancement' in this context covers both of these processes including a wide range of ecosystem manipulations, such as, chemical additions (Clarke et al. 1997, Lacroix 1996, Peterson et al. 1993) and physical habitat alterations (Geiling et al. 1996, van Zyll de Jong 1995, Elliot 1986) among others.

Most of the habitat 'enhancement' methodologies employed within insular Newfoundland were developed in other jurisdictions (Scruton et al. 1997) under very different species and productivity conditions. The few projects that were evaluated, under NIFDA and CASEC, tended to be the more complex restoration initiatives or those undertaken specifically for research purposes (Bourgeois et al. 1993; Clarke et al. 2001; Mitchell et al. 1998; Scruton et al. 1997; 1998; Van Zyll de Jong et al. 1997). These evaluations form a significant body of knowledge on the functionality and effectiveness of habitat manipulations within insular Newfoundland.

Many of these methodologies are currently being used and/or proposed as habitat compensation options under the Department of Fisheries and Oceans' "Policy for the Management of Fish Habitat" (DFO 1986). Under this policy any development that encroaches on fish habitat and harmfully reduces that habitat's 'productive capacity' must supply a compensation plan. These plans try to adhere to the 'no net lost' guiding principle of the policy and thus the proposed compensation should, in theory, replace or exceed the productive capacity lost due to the alteration or destruction of habitat by the development (DFO 1986, DFO 1998). This paper thus, reviews the knowledge gained through past habitat 'enhancement' practices within Newfoundland, against their ability to increase the manipulated habitat's productive capacity.

## Methods

This paper is a review of past work conducted within Newfoundland and most of the detailed methods can be found within the individual reports. The habitat projects conducted and evaluated over the past 15 years have had a wide geographical coverage (Figure 1) representing most of the different ecological conditions within the island portion of the province. For this discussion the projects have been sub-divided into three categories: 1) Projects using and/or evaluating instream structures as a habitat improvement/restoration strategy. 2) Projects where habitat manipulations have been conducted in a natural system to increase the productive capacity for a target species and/or life stage. 3) Major restoration projects that have included detailed habitat construction and/or rewatering of previously dry channels. The main objective of this report is to evaluate the ability of these various habitat enhancement methods to increase the productive capacity within Newfoundland systems. The surrogate measure of productive capacity used in this evaluation is salmonid biomass except in cases where this measure does not provide a good measurement against the projects stated objectives (e.g. Northeast Placentia River, Noel Paul Brook Experiments, see below).

Instream Structures:

Noel Paul's Brook Experiments

Two separate experiments were conducted in an experimental channel within Noel Paul's

Brook (Figure 1) on the habitat utilization of salmonids associated with differing instream structures. The first of these experiments was reported by Bourgeois et al. (1993) with the second suite of experiments forming the basis of a Master's thesis (Mitchell 1996, Mitchell et al. 1998). Bourgeois et al. (1993) tested the preference of Atlantic salmon and brook trout for two combined habitat treatments. The first consisted of a mid-channel treatment which was formed by a low head barrier followed downstream by a boulder cluster and the second was a stream bank treatment with wing deflector leading into a undercut bank structure. These treatments were tested with varying densities and under allopathic and sympathic conditions of the two test species (see methods in Bourgeois et al. 1993 and Scruton et al. 1997).

Mitchell (1996) built upon the earlier work of Bourgeois et al. (1993) utilizing the same habitat treatments in a slightly more complicated design including more replicates of habitat treatments and more variation in the spatial placement of the treatments within the experimental channel (Mitchell et al. 1998). This second set of experiments were conducted during 1994 and 1995 and focused on the distributional pattern and micro-habitat selection of Atlantic salmon and brook trout under varying densities and flow regimes both during the day and night.

## Joe Farrell's Brook

A multi-year scientific evaluation of various instream structures on salmonid populations and their habitat was conducted in Joe Farrell's Brook (Figure 1) from 1993 to 1995 (Van Zyll de Jong et al. 1997). Three types of stream structures were used: (i) boulder clusters, (ii) V-dam structures and (iii) half-log covers. Biological and physical habitat variables were sampled at eleven stations which included treatment sites (n=5), a sub-basin control site (n=1), and sites downstream of treatment locations (n=5). Stations were surveyed prior to the installation of structures (1993) and in each subsequent post-treatment year (1994 to 1996). Quantitative estimates of fish density and biomass were obtained by electrofishing following methods outlined in Scruton and Gibson (1995).

## Habitat Manipulations in Natural Systems:

## **Nutrient Additions**

Cole's Pond, a small (25.7 ha) oligotrophic lake, was fertilised continuously during the summer period (June-September) with nitrogen and phosphorus additions starting in late June 1991 and continuing through to 1996. Fertilisation rates with a calculated annual artificial loading rate have been reported in Clarke et al. (1997) and Knoechel et al. (1998). During the study detailed monitoring of biological responses were monitored including salmonid biomass. Population estimates for salmonids, primarily brook trout, were conducted by multiple mark recapture techniques follow methods outlined in Knoechel and Ryan (1994).

## Barrier Removal

Great Gull Brook, the largest tributary of the Northwest Gander River, had greater than ninety percent of it's almost 8700 units of available habitat previously inaccessible to anadromous Atlantic salmon (Traverse 1972). The waterfall that was blocking passage, located 2.5 kilometres upstream from the confluence with the Northwest Gander River, was blasted in 1996 in conjunction with the Gander River Management Association (GRMA). Two electrofishing stations above the waterfall and two located below were sampled by multiple pass removal in 1997 and 1998. Population estimates, density and biomass, were calculated and length frequency distributions were compared between sites to evaluate the remedial activities (see Clarke et al. 2001 for details).

#### Spawning Gravel Additions

A habitat survey of the Northeast Placentia River (Figure 1) identified a lack of good spawning habitat as possible deterrent for increased production of Atlantic salmon within this river system (Nicks 1994). Locations for gravel addition were selected from this survey and candidate

sites were then sampled for water depth and velocity to ensure they met criteria for preferred Atlantic salmon spawning habitat (e.g. Jones 1959, Pratt 1968, Beland et al. 1982). Subsequently, three sites met these criteria (Scruton et al. 1997). Rounded beach gravel was then sifted, cleaned, and sorted to size and proportion specifications (Porter 1975, Peterson 1978) as substrate material for addition to these sites. Gravels were subsequently transported to the pre-selected sites and manually added to the stream in 1995. Boulders and rock groins were added stabilise gravel additions at two of the sites (Scruton et al 1997).

Size distributions of juvenile salmonids were determined by semi-quantitative electrofishing (Scruton and Gibson 1995) of various sections of the river prior (1995) to addition of spawning gravel. These sites were resurveyed in 1996 one year after gravel additions. A total of six electrofishing sites were established; three in the proximity of spawning gravel additions, two where historically no spawning had occurred and no additions were planned and one in a known natural spawning location. Each site (30 m in length) was fished completely (one sweep) with a backpack electrofisher keeping the fishing time consistent between stations. Additionally, spawning surveys of the river were conducted in November of 1995 and 1996.

## Major Restoration | Re-watering:

## Seal Cove River

Seal Cove River was one of the first compensation projects conducted under DFO's habitat policy (Scruton 1996; Clarke and Scruton 2002). It was necessitated due to the destruction of 162 m reach of river for highway construction. The destroyed section of river was replaced with 195 m of compensatory habitat that was directed at producing older salmonids, specifically large brook trout. This directed approach involved the construction of 4 large pools with interspersed riffle sections, two of the pools had 'lunker' structures installed on the outside bend of the river channel to supply overhead cover.

Salmonid biomass estimates were determined via successive removal with a backpack electrofisher. These estimates were conducted in August of each year with baseline data being collected from the original stream reach and three upstream control sites in 1988 and 1989, post construction estimates were conducted in 9 stations within the new habitat and the three original controls stations. Post construction sampling was conducted in 1991 through to 1994 and 1999. Population estimates (density and biomass) were calculated via the Microfish 3.0 program developed by Van Deventer and Platts (1989), which employs a maximum likelihood (ML) estimator (Burnham formula, Van Deventer and Platts 1983).

#### Pamehac Brook

The restoration of Pamehac Brook entailed the re-watering of a 11 km section of river that was cut off from the mainstem to facilitate log driving activities in the early 1970's (Scruton et al. 1997, 1998). The project was conceived in 1989 as a partnership arrangement between the Environmental Resources Management Association (a local conservation group), Abitibi-Price Inc. (a pulp and paper company), the Environmental Partners Fund (of Environment Canada), and DFO.

The project underwent a scientific evaluation which included a quantitative assessment of juvenile fish populations before and after the project. Fish populations were sampled by quantitative electrofishing in 1990 (pre-project) and in 1991, 1992, and 1996 (post-project). A total of eight stations were electrofished, two above the diversion and six below the diversion in the newly restored habitat. Population estimates (density and biomass) were developed using the MicroFish 3.0 program.

#### Rose Blanche Compensation

This project is a recent compensation project and the monitoring is still ongoing. The

compensation was required due to a hydroelectric development on Rose Blanche Brook. As part of the compensation plan, a habitat channel with controlled flow was created (modified) to compensate for habitat losses associated with reservoir construction. This channel had existed prior to the project but was only wetted during high flow periods, primarily in the spring, during snow melt events. Further, this high flow channel was extensively scoured and contained primarily coarse substrates. The compensation works involved excavation and installation of a controlled flow culvert to ensure a constant flow over a suitable range of discharge, installation of dykes to protect the channel during high flow events, and the addition of an extensive amount of gravels to provide high quality spawning habitat within the channel.

Monitoring of the project began in the summer of 2000 two years after the opening of the compensation channel. Resident salmonids, Atlantic salmon and brook trout were not introduced to the channel but were expected to stray from the mainstem populations. Biological monitoring consists of quantitative electrofishing in 8 stations in the compensation channel and 3 additional sites within the mainstem. Sampling is conducted in the summer (late July) and fall (October) of each year. Fish greater the 1 year of age are marked with an individually number plastic fingerling Floy tag with younger fish being marked with an adipose fin clip. Information from this marking program will include individual production and growth estimates, information on habitat fidelity, and will give us some insight into the compensation channel's effect on production of salmonids within the entire Rose Blanche River.

#### Results

Instream Structures:

#### Noel Paul's Brook Experiments

Although not designed to evaluate the population responses, the experiments conducted at Noel Paul's Brook supplied important insights into the functioning of the instream structures tested. Bourgeois et al. (1993) concluded that there was no significant difference in preference for the two treatments tested between trout and salmon. Both species preferred the mid-channel treatment over the bank treatment over the control whether in conditions of allopathy or sympathy and increasing density displaced both species equally into the less preferred treatments. Mitchell (Mitchell 1996, Mitchell et al. 1998), however, showed that under natural densities, young salmon preferred the stream bank treatment while at higher densities (1.5 X natural), fish were displaced into the less preferred treatments.

Observations from these experiments at Noel Paul's Brook highlighted the importance of hydrologic conditions to the success of instream structures. The mid-channel treatment did not supply any benefit to salmonids under low velocity conditions but was the most preferred area when the velocity was increased. This suggests that these structures may not provide the needed cover and microhabitat areas required by salmonids during limiting low water periods such as those experienced in mid-summer and winter. These structures did however supply microhabitat refugia and cover, through turbulence, during increased velocity. This suggests that these structures would supply holding areas during high water events such as storms and spring runoff.

The stream bank treatment, specifically the wing deflectors, also did not produce the anticipated habitat features in these experiments. There was limited increased velocity and scouring around the wing deflector and instead of being selected for in high velocities, as hypothesized, this area was avoided due to the lack of protection from the current. This failure of the wing deflectors to provide the desired microhabitats was attributed to the artificial lack of diversity in discharge that occurred in the experimental channel by both Bourgeois et al. (1993) and Mitchell (1996). The wing deflectors did however provide cover during low flow experiments (Mitchell 1996) and were

preferred slightly by salmonids during these times. This observation indicates that although the wing deflectors did not work as hypothesized they supplied supplemental cover during low flow periods, that may have been as important to the salmonids as was their intended function.

The results from these experiments indicate that any direct effect on productive capacity due to the placement of any one of these structures would be extremely difficult to verify. The structures tend to be preferred at different hydrological conditions, thus a project attempting to increase habitat complexity should utilize several habitat structures within the project reach. Furthermore, any evaluation of productive capacity changes would then have to be conducted on a reach by reach basis as opposed to individual sites.

## Joe Farrell's Brook

Monitoring of the instream structures within Joe Farrell's Brook encountered many of the same problems as the experiments in Noel Paul's Brook with respect to hypothesised results (Van Zyll de Jong et al. 1997). Generally, the structures used in this system increased habitat complexity adjacent to the structure placement. There were also some positive fish density responses post treatment but these were not always as expected (Van Zyll de Jong et al. 1997, Scruton et al. 1997). The author's concluded that the structures increased production of the area by reducing competition through an increased habitat heterogeneity within the treated areas (Van Zyll de Jong et al. 1997).

Salmonid biomass within the treated sections of Joe Farrell's Brook exhibited inter annual variability such that, evaluating the effect of anyone of the structures would not be possible. On a reach basis, the average salmonid biomass increased approximately 2-fold in post treatment years as compared to pre-treatment (Figure 2). This increase was consistent in the post treatment years (1994-96) for the restored reach while no such trend was observed in the sub-basin control (Figure 2). It is important to note that only one sub-basin control was used in this experiment and it was an area with a naturally low productivity. This may have exasperated the differences between the sub-basin control and the experimental area.

The increased productivity observed in the restored reach was in part due to increases in density of particular salmonid cohorts (Van Zyll de Jong et al. 1997). There was also a trend towards increased condition (K) for juvenile Atlantic salmon in the restored reach over the course of the study. Condition factor (K) averaged 1.11 in 1993 as opposed to 1.61 in 1996. There was no increased condition observed for brook trout or within the sub-basin control. Thus, the combined effect of the various habitat structures throughout the restored reach appears to have increased survivorship, possible through reducing competition as suggested by Van Zyll de Jong et al. (1997) and increased growth, at least for the juvenile Atlantic salmon. It is important to note that the individual structure's effect on salmonid biomass was variable over time, which was consistent with the results of the Noel Paul's Brook experiments.

# Habitat Improvement of Natural Systems:

## Nutrient Additions

Nutrient additions in Cole's Pond have been shown to increase benthic invertebrate abundance (Clarke et al. 1997) which subsequently resulted in an increase of salmonid biomass (Figure 3). The increase in biomass was most pronounced after three years of nutrient additions as compared to the two control ponds with a peak of 8.25 Kg/ha recorded in September 1994 (Figure 3). Biomass in Cole's Pond was reduced in 1995 and 1996 as compared to the 1994 peak which coincided with an increased loon production, including fledged chicks, in this small oligotrophic lake (25.7 ha) (Knoechel et al. 1998). While the increased loon production was not an expected result, it highlights the need to consider the entire biological community when conducted habitat manipulations.

## Barrier Removal

Quantitative electrofishing conducted above and below the falls on the Great Gull River after remedial activities did not show any increase in salmonid biomass in the upper stations (Figure 4). Not only did salmonid biomass remain low in the upper stations post project but the length frequency distributions of fish in the two areas were also found to be dissimilar (Clarke et al. 2001). The lower stations had a distribution indicative of an anadromous salmon population, dominated by 0-3 year old fish, while the upper stations had more larger fish, few young of the year and a larger percentage of brook trout (see Clarke et al. 2001).

Spawning Gravel Additions

The pre-project electrofishing survey revealed that 96% of the juvenile salmon found in the known spawning area were fry (young-of-the-year or 0+) as compared to 8-67% at the other five sites (Table 1). The number and proportion of fry increased in all stations during 1996 (Table 1). These increases were most pronounced in the gravel addition sites with the exception of site 5 which also was observed to have a large increase in fry (Table 1).

Seven redds were observed in the newly added gravel in November 1995 confirming the newly placed gravel was selected for and used by spawning salmon. These seven redds constituted 4% of the total redds counted during the survey. In November 1996 a total of 39 redds were observed in the new gravel for 23% of the total. The most successful addition site was located approximately 500 m above the main spawning area. This site had 4 redds in 1995 and 23 redds in 1996, it also had the highest success of gravel retention, >90% as compared to approximately 50% in the other sites. This difference in gravel retention was most likely due to a large meandering steady, which reduces the hydrological power of the river at this location.

While the initial results of this project were very encouraging it was discontinued after 1996 highlighting the need for good follow up monitoring plans. It did however highlight several of the problems researchers encounter when conducting evaluations of habitat manipulations. When dealing with anadromous populations, adult sea survival will be a confounding factor that will affect freshwater habitat productive capacity. This may have been the cause of the increased young of the year numbers observed throughout Northeast Placentia River in 1996. Also, our knowledge of species interactions is not able to successfully predict the effect of increasing the young of year production of one species may have on habitat productive capacity. This makes this type of project almost impossible to evaluate against the requirements of the 'no net loss' guiding principle of DFO's habitat policy (DFO 1986).

# Major Restoration | Re-watering:

## Seal Cove River

Average salmonid biomass in the affected section of stream was always higher (pre and post construction) than that observed in the control sites and the majority of biomass in both areas was derived from brook trout 1+ and older (Scruton 1996, Clarke and Scruton 2002). Average biomass in the newly constructed habitat was back to pre-construction levels in the first year post construction, 1991 (Figure 5). There was then a period of three years (1991-1993) were average biomass increased within the system. This increase was checked in 1994 but was observed to be at similar levels in 1999 in the new habitat (Figure 5). These trends were similar in both the new and the control sites and was the for the most part a result of increased large brook trout biomass over this period. Although the trend was similar in both areas the difference between the average biomass observed in the new habitat compared to the control sites also increased over this period (1.8 times in 1991, 2.1 times in 1993 and 3.6 times in 1999).

Pools with 'lunkers' had on average 2.6 times the biomass of large brook trout than those without 'lunkers' over the post construction period (Clarke and Scruton 2002). This difference widen over time being 3.4 post 1992, which suggests that complete utilisation of these areas did not occur immediately after construction. The Seal Cove River compensation was successful in increasing the productive capacity, as measured by brook trout biomass. This was achieved through a design that considered both the hydraulic and biological habitat requirements of the target species.

# Pamehac Brook

There were no significant (P<0.05) changes in salmonid biomass (Figure 6) in the first year after restoration (1991) as compared to pre-restoration levels (1990). In the second year after restoration (1992) significant increases in density were observed for 0+ salmon (p=0.002) and older trout (>0+; p=0.012) (Scruton et al. 1998) but no significant changes in biomass were observed (Figure 6). The large increase in 0+ salmon was the major proportion of this density increase and was attributable to fry stocking efforts conducted in 1992. These stocking practices confounded the restoration experiment and were subsequently discontinued to allow populations to reach a natural equilibrium. Four years were allowed for the system to equilibrate after the stocking practices. Significant increases in biomass of older (>0+) trout and salmon (p=0.014 and 0.001, respectively) were observed in 1996 (Figure 6). These increases meant that total salmonid biomass was significantly greater (p=0.001) in 1996 as compared to 1992 and pre-restoration levels (1990). These results suggest that the habitat developed through the re-watering of Pamehac Brook was most suitable for the rearing of larger, older salmonids.

The survey and fish population data allowed estimation of the 'habitat gain' and the increase in productive capacity associated with this project. Pre-restoration fish biomass and available habitat suggested a production potential for the fluvial habitat in the watershed (for 1990) of 18.01 kg excluding standing waters and steadies. The average fish biomass and available habitat in 1992, two years after restoration, indicated a potential production of 51.46 kg, a 2.9 fold increase. The estimate for potential production in 1996 was 263.94 kg, a 14.7 fold increase from pre-restoration levels. The restoration of Pamehac Brook has thus resulted in a habitat gain of 215 units (30 % increase) and an increase in potential production of 246 kg from 1990 to 1996 (Scruton et al. 1998).

#### Rose Blanche Compensation

Preliminary results from the first two years of the project has indicated that the compensation channel is being predominately utilized by brook trout. Sampling has revealed a good mix of size classes which indicates that the habitat is supplying areas for all life stages of this species. Young of the year production was particularly strong for both brook trout and Atlantic salmon within the compensation channel for the 2001 year class (Figure 7). This year class strength was not evident in the main stem sampling sites (Figure 7), which may indicate differential survival for these areas. It will be important to follow this year class through it's evolution to evaluate the carry capacity of the compensation channel and if any additional production will be added to the mainstem populations. While these results are very preliminary they are encouraging and we expect to gain significant knowledge regarding the addition of this newly developed habitat to the salmonid population of Rose Blanche River.

#### Discussion

The habitat 'enhancement' projects conducted throughout Newfoundland over the past 15 years, for the most part, have successfully attained their main objective of increasing salmonid productive capacity. This supports the idea that the 'no net loss' guiding principle of DFO's habitat policy is an achievable goal. Projects utilising physical habitat manipulations to restored degraded habitat either through re-wetting a natural stream bed (e.g. Pamehac Brook) or restoring a natural

habitat (e.g. Seal Cove River) were best able to measure an increase in productive capacity. Projects that relied on instream structures tended to be more complicated and even produced some unexpected results. A series of instream structures appears to have increased biomass over a degraded reach of Joe Farrell's Brook but the relative importance of any one of the structures would be expected to change over time as environmental conditions (e.g. hydrology) changed within the stream.

The effect of physical habitat manipulations in natural systems on productive capacity was more difficult to quantify and would appear to require a system wide monitoring approach that may still fail to discern changes due to inter annual variability. The migration barrier removal on Great Gull River does not appear to provide a reasonable expectation for increasing productive capacity over the near term. The process of colonisation from natural straying would be expected to be erratic and subject to density dependent factors, which may require decades to reach equilibrium. Other projects of this type have been successful when adult and/or eggs/fry transfers have been made above the barrier to speed up the colonisation process (O'Connell and Bourgeois 1987, Mullins et al. 2002). The spawning gravel additions conducted in Northeast Placentia River, despite showing promise for utilisation, would also be very difficult to evaluate with respect to productive capacity. Even if these additions increased young of the year production there are no guarantees this would be translated throughout the population under natural conditions. Furthermore, our incomplete understanding of the competitive interactions for many species assemblages would subject these manipulations to even more uncertainty.

This uncertainty may be magnified in Newfoundland were the existing populations and the target species for 'enhancement' are both, generally, salmonids. The habitat utilisation patterns for many of our salmonid species have expanded from their norms to include most niches within the ecosystem (Gibson et al. 1993; Clarke et al. 1997). Thus it follows that an introduction of a new species or an 'enhancement' directed to benefit one species over the other could possibly adversely affect the production of the existing populations (see Cunjak and Green 1983). For example, it could be expected that the juveniles of anadromous and landlocked Atlantic salmon would have similar habitat requirements and any increase in the anadromous component may be at the expense of the existing landlocked component, especially if the habitat is already at its carrying capacity.

The one project conducted within a natural system that did observe increases in salmonid biomass was the nutrient additions in Cole's Pond. This project, however, was also confounded by an unexpected result, as it appears predators (i.e. loons) also benefited from the increased salmonid biomass (Knoechel et al. 1998). This highlights the need to carefully consider as many of the biological and physical attributes as possible when planning a habitat restoration, improvement or compensation project directed at a target species.

Several of these important physical and biological considerations were apparent throughout the course of the habitat 'enhancement' projects. These considerations although not specific to Newfoundland are important to the development of any future projects. The most prominent and possibly the most important physical consideration in fluvial habitat is that of local hydrological and geomorphological conditions (Newbury and Gaboury 1993). The failure of engineered instream structures has most often been attributed to the failure to consider hydraulic principles and the need to consider long term stability under hydraulic extremes (Hunt 1988; Frissell and Nawa 1992). Often stream habitats most in need of rehabilitation (e.g. channellized reaches) are least amenable to structural modification with stream enhancement technology (Frissell and Nawa 1992). Stream gradient may also be important in long term stability of instream structures with higher 'failure rates' associated with higher gradients. As well, certain stream rehabilitation structures (e.g. log weirs and dams) have higher rates of failure than others (e.g. boulder additions) (Frissell and Nawa 1992).

Experiments conducted at Noel Paul Brook (Bourgeois et al. 1993, Mitchell 1996) have highlighted how hydraulic regimes can affect the functioning of instream structures. In Mitchell's (1996) experiments the wing deflectors failed to provide the anticipated habitat characteristics during periods of high flow but did supply supplemental cover to fish during periods of low flow. The midchannel treatment did not supply any benefit to fish under low flow conditions but was the most preferred site during high flows. Both Bourgeois et al. (1993) and Mitchell (1996) cited the lack of natural variation in the controlled flume as a probable cause for these unexpected results. It is important to remember that many of the streams selected for improvement structures are not natural and channelization and/or control structures may often be a part of the watershed. In these cases an individual evaluation of the site in question would be warranted with follow up monitoring to ensure benefits are being created as expected. These results also suggest that although these structures did not always behave as anticipated they often supplied supplementary habitat features during limiting and stressful conditions (low flow - high flow). These unhypothesised habitat characteristics may have been just as important as those the structures were designed to create, thus it may be prudent to install several of these structures in conjunction when detailed hydrologic work cannot be performed. The benefit of this approach was observed in Joe Farrell's Brook.

As observed in our examples and others (Everest et al. 1991) habitat manipulations can provide benefits beyond the target species/age classes, which requires that their evaluation consider the entire biological community. Many habitat projects, while well intentioned, lack the solid biological basis for planning and implementation and often few allowances are made to allow for biological evaluation (Hunt 1988). In some instances, it is difficult to ascertain whether the initiative has resulted in increased production at the site or reach or whether fish have simply relocated to the rehabilitated reach with subsequent reductions in populations at other locations (see Riley and Fausch 1995). Other confounding factors can affect interpretation of evaluation studies. For example, anadromous salmonids experience natural population fluctuations unrelated to freshwater habitat conditions (e.g. sea survival rates of smolts) that can influence assessment of restoration initiatives. Consequently, there is a need for attention to experimental design when undertaking evaluation studies (Walters et al. 1989).

Important biological considerations include, but are not limited to, knowledge of the target species, an understanding of the limiting habitat factors, microhabitat preferences of species in their natural habitats, intra- and inter-specific interactions, seasonal and life-stage specific habitat requirements and availability of food. The design of habitat features in 'enhancement' projects must be based on the known habitat preferences of target species/age groups and methods that develop these features (Sedell and Beschta 1991). Although habitat is not the only determinate of productive capacity (see Van Zyll de Jong et al. 1997) it is often very important in explaining differences in low fertility areas such as those found in Newfoundland (O=Connell and Power 1976; Clarke and Scruton 1999).

Also, in Newfoundland, as in other northern locales, critical periods for resident salmonids in small streams are the low flow period in the warm part of the summer and overwintering periods. Consequently, strategies that provide habitat refugia during these limiting periods as well as during ecological extremes (e.g. droughts and floods) will be particularly beneficial (Thorpe 1994). Techniques intended to increase quantity (e.g. pool volume) and quality (e.g. substrate stability) of habitat during winter conditions may ultimately be more beneficial than those that provide microhabitat conditions during summer months (Power et al. 1993, Cunjak 1996). Additionally, habitat improvement methods that can increase summer growth and condition may improve overwintering success. Unfortunately, most evaluation studies have been limited to assessments conducted during the summer period with interpretations of overwintering benefits limited to comparisons of inter-annual survival.

A final consideration in evaluating habitat 'enhancement' projects is the time frame required for habitat features to stabilize. It may take additional time for fish populations to respond to these conditions. Thus, evaluation and monitoring of habitat projects must consider this temporal aspect and design assessments accordingly (Everest et al. 1991). Project sponsors must also plan for long term assessment and monitoring of projects and should develop contingencies for future modification and remedial work to maintain installations. Too often projects are undertaken with minimal short or long term follow-up as to effectiveness and/or structural stability.
