# Artificial reefs and reef restoration in the Laurentian Great Lakes

![](_page_0_Picture_8.jpeg)

Mathew McLean <sup>1</sup> , Edward F. Roseman ⁎, Jeremy J. Pritt, Gregory Kennedy, Bruce A. Manny

USGS Great Lakes Science Center, 1451 Green Road, Ann Arbor, MI 48105, USA

#### article info abstract

#### Article history: Received 27 May 2014 Accepted 30 October 2014 Available online 19 December 2014

Communicated by Tom Stewart

Index words: Artificial reefs Habitat Restoration Monitoring Spawning

We reviewed the published literature to provide an inventory of Laurentian Great Lakes artificial reef projects and their purposes. We also sought to characterize physical and biological monitoring for artificial reef projects in the Great Lakes and determine the success of artificial reefs in meeting project objectives. We found records of 6 artificial reefs in Lake Erie, 8 in Lake Michigan, 3 in Lakes Huron and Ontario, and 2 in Lake Superior. We found 9 reefs in Great Lakes connecting channels and 6 reefs in Great Lakes tributaries. Objectives of artificial reef creation have included reducing impacts of currents and waves, providing safe harbors, improving sport-fishing opportunities, and enhancing/restoring fish spawning habitats. Most reefs in the lakes themselves were incidental (not created purposely for fish habitat) or built to improve local sport fishing, whereas reefs in tributaries and connecting channels were more frequently built to benefit fish spawning. Levels of assessment of reef performance varied; but long-term monitoring was uncommon as was assessment of physical attributes. Artificial reefs were often successful at attracting recreational species and spawning fish; however, population-level benefits of artificial reefs are unclear. Stressors such as sedimentation and bio-fouling can limit the effectiveness of artificial reefs as spawning enhancement tools. Our investigation underscores the need to develop standard protocols for monitoring the biological and physical attributes of artificial structures. Further, long-term monitoring is needed to assess the benefits of artificial reefs to fish populations and inform future artificial reef projects.

#### Introduction

Habitat degradation is a global source of imperilment of fishes (e.g., [Jelks et al., 2008; Munday, 2004; Powels et al., 2000](#page-6-0)), and restoration of physical habitat is often used to mitigate losses and degradation of natural habitat [\(Bassett, 1994; Palmer et al., 2005\)](#page-6-0). In large aquatic systems such as the Laurentian Great Lakes, artificial reefs are often created to provide spawning and nursery habitat to benthic-spawning fishes (e.g., [Fitzsimons, 1996; Gannon, 1990; NOAA, 2007\)](#page-6-0). Artificial reefs have been used as a fishery management tool for over 40 years in the Laurentian Great Lakes and have often been championed as a successful means for improving recreational fisheries [\(Kelch, 2012](#page-6-0)). However, unequivocal evidence of improved fisheries resulting from habitat restoration projects is often lacking [\(Hughes et al., 2014; Jähnig et al., 2011; Whiteway et al.,](#page-6-0) [2010](#page-6-0)). Artificial reefs have been constructed in freshwater and marine systems (1) to attract fish ([Creque et al., 2006; Kelch, 2012](#page-6-0)) and (2) to provide spawning, nursery, and adult habitats, and increase fish abundance [\(Brickhill et al., 2005; Dumont et al., 2011; Kuhl, 1992; Pickering and](#page-6-0) [Whitmarsh, 1997](#page-6-0)). Artificial reefs have been proven to attract fish and increase catch rates in recreational fisheries [\(Bombace et al., 1994;](#page-6-0) [Brickhill et al., 2005; Kelch, 2012](#page-6-0)), but the ability of reefs to increase fish abundance is not well documented in freshwater and marine systems ([Baine, 2001; Pickering and Whitmarsh, 1997\)](#page-6-0). Several reports reviewed artificial reef projects in marine and freshwater systems and determined that reefs seldom increased fish population abundance. Problems arose from flawed study design [\(Brickhill et al., 2005\)](#page-6-0), lack of clear objectives for reef building ([Baine, 2001; Claudet and Pelletier,](#page-6-0) [2004\)](#page-6-0), and inadequate monitoring of reefs ([Baine, 2001\)](#page-6-0). Few studies concluded that artificial reefs can perform as well or better than natural reefs in terms of increasing fish abundance and richness at the reef site [\(Carr and Hixon, 1997; Koeck et al., 2014\)](#page-6-0). From an ecological perspective, whether artificial reefs can function as well as natural reefs remains unclear ([Perkol-Finkel et al., 2006](#page-6-0)). Some artificial reefs have produced fish communities that are different in composition from those in natural habitats ([Ambrose and Swarbrick, 1989; Flopp et al., 2013](#page-5-0)).

Despite careful planning, artificial reefs can be damaged or degrade over time. Damage to reefs can be caused by navigation and channelization, sedimentation, intrusion of groundwater, and changes in water levels [\(Rutherford et al., 2004](#page-6-0)). Other potential threats to reef quality and function over time include redistribution of reef materials by wave action, fouling by invasive species (e.g., Dreissenidae), and algae accumulation ([Marsden and Chotkowski, 2001\)](#page-6-0). These factors often limit the effectiveness of both artificial and natural reefs by filling interstices in the reef that protect fish eggs until they hatch. Most of these stressors do not pose an immediate threat, but they can develop over time. In order to quantify the contribution of man-made reefs to fish populations and provide a basis for comparison with natural reefs, routine standardized long-term monitoring protocols should be implemented ([Gannon, 1990](#page-6-0)).

Here we review available information about artificial reef projects in the Laurentian Great Lakes and synthesize information on reef construction, monitoring, and performance. Our objectives were (1) to inventory artificial reef projects in the Great Lakes and their connecting channels and tributaries, (2) to characterize the purpose and construction methods of artificial reef projects, (3) to determine the type and extent of physical and/or biological monitoring on Great Lakes artificial reefs, and (4) to determine whether artificial reefs have achieved their intended objectives.

### Review of artificial reef projects in the Great Lakes

We conducted a literature search using Google [\(https://www.](https://www.google.com/) [google.com/\)](https://www.google.com/), Google Scholar ([http://scholar.google.com/\)](http://scholar.google.com/), the U.S. Geological Survey Library ([http://rl3mq7xr4s.search.serialssolutions.](http://rl3mq7xr4s.search.serialssolutions.com/) [com/\)](http://rl3mq7xr4s.search.serialssolutions.com/), ISI Web of Knowledge ([http://go.isiproducts.com/cgi-bin/MPL/](http://go.isiproducts.com/cgi-bin/MPL/2_0_1/User/Logon.cgi) [2\\_0\\_1/User/Logon.cgi\)](http://go.isiproducts.com/cgi-bin/MPL/2_0_1/User/Logon.cgi), and the online search function of the Journal of Great Lakes Research (<http://www.sciencedirect.com/>). We paired location keywords, including "Great Lakes," "Lake Michigan," "Lake Erie," "Detroit River," etc., with subject keywords such as "artificial reef", "constructed reef," and "spawning habitat restoration". These terms were also searched without a specific location to obtain results for artificial reefs located outside of the Great Lakes. Lists of references in publications were searched forward (papers that cited the current paper) and backward (papers cited within) to find other relevant publications, including gray literature such as agency reports, theses, and news releases. For all relevant Great Lakes reef studies discovered, we obtained reef site name, location, year of construction, objectives for construction, materials used ([Table 1](#page-2-0)), construction design, monitoring scope, and timing/duration of monitoring. We also determined whether the objectives of each project were achieved, based on published accounts and personal communications with agency scientists familiar with respective projects.

### Purpose and design of artificial reefs in the Great Lakes

In our literature search, we found 38 artificial reefs constructed in the Great Lakes basin between the late 1800s and 2013. We found at least one project in each of the Great Lakes, as well as several in the Great Lakes connecting channels (St. Clair-Detroit rivers and St. Lawrence River) and tributaries [\(Fig. 1](#page-3-0)). Some projects consisted of multiple reefs, and unless individual reefs within a project were differentiated from one another by name, a project or site referred to all reefs constructed at that location. Lake Michigan (8 projects) and the connecting channels (9 projects) had the greatest number of reef projects, whereas Lake Superior had the fewest reef projects (2 projects; [Fig. 2](#page-3-0)). About 62% of manmade reefs have been the focus of biological monitoring but not physical monitoring, 24% of the reefs had both physical and biological monitoring, and about 14% had not been monitored ([Fig. 2\)](#page-3-0). Size and design of artificial reefs also varied on a project specific basis. Sizes of constructed structures ranged from less than 95 m2 [\(Marsden et al., 1995\)](#page-6-0) to over 33,000 m2 [\(Yauck, 2009](#page-7-0)). Physical designs also varied from a 3-m high wall of rock in East Tawas Bay, Lake Huron ([Foster and Kennedy, 1995\)](#page-6-0), to individual uniform beds of various materials to assess preference for rock type in the Detroit River [\(Roseman et al., 2011\)](#page-6-0) and large piles of rock in Lake Ontario ([Marsden et al., 1995](#page-6-0)).

The earliest artificial reefs were created incidentally, for purposes other than fish habitat [\(Fig. 3\)](#page-4-0). For example, the coal cinder reef in the North Channel of the St. Clair River is thought to have been formed in the late 1800s when ships discarded coal cinders while moored at the site ([Nichols et al., 2003](#page-6-0)). Other examples of incidental reefs include projects aimed at abating physical impacts of currents and waves, providing safe harbors, or to protect water intake pipes for industry and municipalities (i.e., [Liston et al., 1985; Marsden and Chotkowski, 2001;](#page-6-0) [Peck, 1986; Rutecki et al., 1985](#page-6-0)). Interest in building reefs to improve sport fishing and restore functional fish spawning habitat started after 1980 ([Fig. 3\)](#page-4-0). Structures designed to improve recreational fishing opportunities were most often placed near metropolitan areas where anglers could have access ([Bader, 1985; Kelch, 2012; Kelch et al., 1999;](#page-5-0) [Kevern et al., 1985; Stewart and Haynes, 1994; Vincent, 1995](#page-5-0)). For example, large reefs were built to attract fishes such as walleye and smallmouth bass near the cities of Cleveland, Ohio [\(Kelch, 2012](#page-6-0)) and Chicago, Illinois ([Creque et al., 2006\)](#page-6-0). These projects had social and economic objectives (i.e., increase angler effort and success) rather than to increase fish populations. Sport-fishing reefs have been constructed from natural materials, such as limestone and granite ([Creque et al.,](#page-6-0) [2006](#page-6-0)), and man-made materials, including cement, brick, and even old stadium rubble [\(Kelch et al., 1999\)](#page-6-0) [\(Table 1\)](#page-2-0). Hence, reefs aimed at improving recreational fisheries have often been sited to provide easy access to anglers and were constructed out of convenient, available materials, which may have limited the value of these reefs as fish spawning habitat.

More recently, the focus of artificial spawning habitat construction shifted to restoring and increasing functional spawning habitat in order to enhance fish populations (e.g., [Dumont et al., 2011; Fitzsimons,](#page-6-0) [1996; Manny et al., 2010; Roseman et al., 2011\)](#page-6-0) ([Fig. 3](#page-4-0)). Emphasis of these projects was on spawning habitat for imperiled or important species, such as lake sturgeon (e.g., [Dumont et al., 2011; Roseman et al.,](#page-6-0) [2011](#page-6-0)), lake trout ([Fitzsimons, 1996](#page-6-0)), and walleye ([Geiling et al., 1996\)](#page-6-0), or improving diversity of benthic-spawning fishes ([Manny et al., 2010\)](#page-6-0). Material used to construct fish spawning reefs has most frequently been broken limestone ([Roseman et al., 2011](#page-6-0)) but has also included igneous rocks [\(Geiling et al., 1996\)](#page-6-0) [\(Table 1](#page-2-0)). Substrate size also varied across artificial reef projects, ranging from boulders [\(Marsden et al., 1995\)](#page-6-0) to cobble and gravel ([Dumont et al., 2011](#page-6-0)). # Biological monitoring of reef projects

Biological monitoring of artificial reefs in the Great Lakes has focused on either the abundance or diversity of adult fish or on early life history stages to gauge the reproductive success of spawning fish. In general, biological monitoring of artificial reefs in the Great Lakes typically lasts for 2–7 years after reef construction (mean of 2.9 years across completed studies; [Table 1\)](#page-2-0). Many monitoring studies lack biological or physical assessment before reef construction to compare to post-construction conditions. Adult fish variables have included the abundance of target species, such as lake trout [\(Fitzsimons, 1996\)](#page-6-0), total fish abundance [\(Kelch et al., 1999](#page-6-0)), or species richness [\(Kelch et al., 1999](#page-6-0)). Sampling gears and methods for adult fish have varied across reef monitoring projects and have included underwater observation ([Environment Illimité](#page-6-0) [Inc., 2009, 2010; Kelch et al., 1999](#page-6-0)), gill nets ([Creque et al., 2006;](#page-6-0) [Dumont et al., 2011\)](#page-6-0), and trap nets ([Geiling et al., 1996](#page-6-0)). In addition to fish sampling, surveys of fisheries biologists [\(Geiling et al., 1996](#page-6-0)) and

Early life history variables have been used to measure fish production on artificial reefs and have varied among studies. # Physical monitoring of artificial reef projects

Most of the monitoring protocols on artificial reefs in the Great Lakes focused on monitoring the biological attributes of the reef, with the physical aspects being secondary or not measured at all [\(Table 1\)](#page-2-0). Studies that did monitor the physical properties of artificial reefs were typically qualitative observations, made by SCUBA divers, not standardized, quantitative methods. Underwater video and remotely operated vehicles (ROV) have been used to observe zebra mussel infestation of an artificial reef in Lake Huron [\(Foster and Kennedy, 1995\)](#page-6-0) and to evaluate sedimentation, algae, and mussel accumulation on a reef in the St. Lawrence River [\(Environment Illimité Inc., 2010](#page-6-0)).

Although qualitative habitat assessments have typically been used to monitor the physical aspects of Great Lakes artificial reefs, several examples of quantitative physical habitat measurements exist for artificial reefs in the Great Lakes. For example, divers have been used to map observations of a reef in Lake Michigan, including changes of reef shape over time [\(Binkowski, 1985\)](#page-6-0). [Dumont et al. \(2011\)](#page-6-0) quantified flow velocity, depth, and substrate on an artificial reef in the St. Lawrence River for 4 years via SCUBA. [Marsden and Chotkowski \(2001\)](#page-6-0) described a quantitative protocol for measuring zebra mussel accumulation on an artificial reef in the Port of Indiana over a 5-year period. In a similar study, [Rutecki et al. \(1985\)](#page-6-0) described methods for measuring algal colonization on reefs in Lake Michigan over time using SCUBA divers. Side scan sonar has also been used by researchers to monitor artificial reefs (e.g., [Collier and Brown, 2004\)](#page-6-0). Side scan sonar was used to evaluate the condition of a natural reef in Lake Huron ([Edsall et al., 1989,](#page-6-0) [1992](#page-6-0)), produce maps of artificial reefs in Lake Erie [\(Kelch, 2012\)](#page-6-0) and determine the shape of an artificial reef in Lake Michigan [\(Kevern et al.,](#page-6-0) [1985\)](#page-6-0). Side scan sonar, as well as underwater video cameras, PONAR grab samples, particle size analysis, and SCUBA diver observations were all used at a proposed reef site in the Detroit River during a preconstruction assessment of a constructed fish spawning reef [\(Manny,](#page-6-0) [2006\)](#page-6-0). However, these techniques have not been employed for postconstruction assessment work. Side scan sonar was used to determine the height of reefs in Lake Erie immediately after deployment; however, no record was made of follow-up measurements ([Kelch et al., 1999](#page-6-0)).

## Success of artificial reefs in the Great Lakes in attracting fish and increasing fish spawning

Stated objectives of artificial reef projects constructed to enhance sport fishing include attracting higher densities of adult fish and anglers (e.g. [Creque et al., 2006; Kelch et al., 1999](#page-6-0)). However, project objectives for fish spawning reefs are typically aimed at increasing fish population levels (e.g., [Dumont et al., 2011; Roseman et al., 2011](#page-6-0)). These population-level objectives of spawning reefs are often measured by monitoring the number of adult spawners, egg deposition, and larval fish production. Artificial reefs have been shown to attract congregations of adult fish sought by anglers [\(Creque et al., 2006; Fitzsimons,](#page-6-0) [1996; Kelch et al., 1999\)](#page-6-0). Moreover, fish egg deposition and survival of larvae produced have been shown to sometimes be greater on artificial reefs habitats than on natural habitats [\(Bouckaert et al., 2014; Dumont](#page-6-0) [et al., 2011; Fitzsimons, 1996\)](#page-6-0), indicating that artificial reefs could increase spawning success of Great Lakes fishes. Artificial reefs have been heavily used by numerous fish species throughout the Great Lakes and have increased fish feeding and spawning activity near reefs. Although artificial reefs have increased adult fish abundance and egg production at the site level (i.e., on reefs themselves), it is unclear whether artificial reefs have been successful in increasing year class strength of target fish populations. We found no studies that directly linked artificial reef projects to increased fish abundance at the population level (see [Dumont et al., 2011; Geiling et al., 1996](#page-6-0)).

Fish have been documented to use and spawn on incidental reefs that were not intended as fish habitat. For example, scientists have documented annual spawning for years by lake sturgeon at the incidental coal cinder reef in the St. Clair River ([Bouckaert et al., 2014](#page-6-0)) and lake sturgeon successfully spawned on gravel beds placed to prevent erosion of a power plant cooling water intake pipe in the St. Lawrence River [\(Johnson et al., 2006\)](#page-6-0). Similarly, lake trout have been shown to spawn <span id="page-5-0"></span>on man-made reefs in Lake Superior [\(Peck, 1986\)](#page-6-0), Lake Ontario [\(Marsden et al., 1995\)](#page-6-0), and Lake Michigan ([Fitzsimons, 1996\)](#page-6-0). Hence, reefs built to prevent erosion, such as those routinely employed by shipping and power generation industries may also serve as fish spawning habitat, presenting an opportunity for cooperation between industry and fisheries managers [\(Marsden and Chotkowski, 2001](#page-6-0)).

### Stressors and degradation of artificial reefs

Physical degradation of reefs can limit the effectiveness of artificial reefs as fish habitat. For example, SCUBA divers observed that silt, Cladophora spp., and dreissenid mussels had accumulated on a reef in the St. Lawrence River over the course of 4 years ([Johnson et al.,](#page-6-0) [2006\)](#page-6-0). Sedimentation and algal accumulation was also observed by divers on reefs in Lake Michigan [\(Kevern et al., 1985\)](#page-6-0), Lake Superior [\(Peck, 1986](#page-6-0)), and in the Detroit and St. Clair rivers (USGS unpublished data). Some reefs receive waves and currents that damage fish eggs. Others are prone to fouling by dreissenid mussels that may reduce habitat quality for fish spawning [\(Marsden and Chotkowski, 2001](#page-6-0)). As a result, physical stressors and degradation can limit fish egg deposition and larval fish production on artificial reefs [\(Johnson et al., 2006; Marsden](#page-6-0) [and Chotkowski, 2001\)](#page-6-0).

### Limitations of artificial reef monitoring projects

Although artificial reefs have been successful in attracting fish for feeding and spawning purposes (i.e., behavioral responses), the longterm effects of artificial reefs on fishes remain unclear. Often, the intent of reef building was to increase fish populations; however, reef assessments were typically not related to broader fish community goals and objectives or to fish population and fish community changes. Most artificial reef monitoring programs are short in duration (b5 years) and small scale (b1 km2 ). The intended objective of many recent artificial reef projects has been to increase the population abundance of one or more fish species (e.g., [Dumont et al., 2011; Fitzsimons, 1996; Roseman et al., 2011\)](#page-6-0). However, lake trout, lake sturgeon, walleye, and lake whitefish are often long-lived, late-maturing, and distributed over large areas. Observance of a population-level response, such as an increase in abundance, would take much longer than the duration of most reef monitoring programs and occur over a larger spatial extent. Moreover, stressors of artificial reefs, such as sedimentation, physical degradation by waves and water currents, and changes in water levels may occur over larger spatial extents and longer time periods than most reef monitoring programs. As a result, most recent artificial reef monitoring sampling designs are inadequate to measure long-term biological responses of fish populations to constructed spawning habitat.

To measure the biological effects and permanence of artificial reefs, long-term monitoring of physical and biological attributes of constructed reefs is needed ([Gannon, 1990; Hartig et al., 2009; NOAA, 2007](#page-6-0)). Long-term monitoring could potentially improve fish habitat restoration techniques and better focus limited management resources through adaptive management [\(Palmer et al., 2005](#page-6-0)). Inadequate monitoring of artificial reefs is common ([Smokorowski et al., 1998a](#page-6-0)) and problematic to understanding the results of artificial reef projects [\(Baine, 2001\)](#page-6-0). Long-term monitoring of man-made physical habitat was rare in the Great Lakes studies we reviewed. Furthermore, many of the reef construction projects that we reviewed lacked physical habitat monitoring or focused on a limited selection of physical habitat variables over a short time period (b5 years). The longest study that focused on the integrity of an artificial reef lasted 5 years but was narrowly focused on zebra mussel fouling [\(Marsden and Chotkowski,](#page-6-0) [2001\)](#page-6-0). Without longer-term monitoring plans, changes in the physical and biological attributes of artificial reefs are difficult to quantify, reducing understanding of their value as fisheries management tools.

We found that reef placement, size, design, and the size and type of parent materials used to construct artificial reefs varied greatly among the relatively small number of adequately monitored artificial reefs. Hence, our ability to discern the magnitude and significance of these confounding factors is limited. Future research is needed to determine the most effective materials and construction design needed to maximize the effectiveness of reef projects. Standardized monitoring protocols are also needed to evaluate trends in reef performance through time [\(Bortone and Kimmel, 1991; Murray, 1994; NOAA, 2007; Sheng, 2000\)](#page-6-0). Such standardized guidelines exist for reefs constructed outside of the Great Lakes and include the focal variables and duration of monitoring (e.g., [Bortone and Kimmel, 1991; Lindberg and Seaman, 2011\)](#page-6-0). Although sampling methods necessarily depend on the habitat and fish species of interest, the wide variety of biological variables used to assess artificial reefs may limit transferability across reef assessment studies and limit our understanding of artificial reefs as a fisheries management practice [\(Fitzsimons, 1996\)](#page-6-0). Historically, physical habitat monitoring of manmade reefs in the Great Lakes has typically consisted of qualitative underwater observations which may limit our ability to assess physical, temporal, and biological changes on reefs over time. More quantitative underwater observations (i.e., SCUBA divers) have been used to monitor physical attributes of man-made reefs outside the Great Lakes (e.g., Ambrose and Anderson, 1990; Wilding and Sayer, 2002). Standardized long-term monitoring of physical and biological variables on artificial reefs could improve the design and biological productivity of artificial reefs and allow fishery managers to better understand the benefits of reefs to fishery resources. We recommend that researchers focusing on Great Lakes reefs utilize quantitative methodology.

#### Conclusions

The use of artificial reefs as fisheries enhancement tools in the Great Lakes is widespread. Artificial reefs have been constructed in a wide range of Great Lakes habitats for many target species. Studies of artificial reefs in the Great Lakes using underwater cameras and side scan sonar advanced the monitoring of biological and physical characteristics of artificial reefs [\(Foster and Kennedy, 1995; Manny, 2006\)](#page-6-0). However, few of these methods have been incorporated into standardized, long-term monitoring programs.

We recommend the following practices for future research on the benefits of artificial reefs to fishery resources in the Great Lakes: (1) develop reef monitoring programs that better match the spatial and temporal extent of the biological responses and physical stressors; (2) monitor the physical, as well as biological changes in reefs; (3) standardize biological and physical monitoring and methodology to allow for comparisons among reefs and through time; and (4) measure quantitative response variables, not qualitative variables of the constructed reefs. Following these recommendations should better quantify reef efficacy and performance and improve our understanding of man-made fish spawning reefs.
