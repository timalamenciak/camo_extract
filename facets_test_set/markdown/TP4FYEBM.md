# Impacts of non-native plant and animal invaders on gap regeneration in a protected boreal forest

Jessica M. Humber · Luise Hermanutz

Received: 31 August 2010/Accepted: 25 June 2011/Published online: 9 August 2011 © Springer Science+Business Media B.V. 2011

**Abstract** In balsam fir (Abies balsamea)-dominated boreal forests of Gros Morne National Park, Newfoundland (Canada), non-native Cirsium arvense (Canada thistle) has invaded forest gaps. #### Introduction

Non-native plant invasions represent a severe threat to native species diversity, ecosystem structure and function, and human welfare, both locally and on a global scale (Vitousek et al. 1997; Levine et al. 2003). With the increased rate of movement of

![](_page_0_Picture_14.jpeg)

species throughout the world and high rates of disturbance, non-native plants are becoming a larger component of regional floras and are prompting conservation concern by managers of protected areas worldwide (MacDonald et al. 1986; Cowie and Werner 1993; Myers and Bazely 2003). Developing a better understanding of the impacts of non-native species on the ecological integrity of protected areas (Parks Canada Agency 2000) and devising methods to reverse the effects of invasive species (Hobbs and Norton 1996; Zavaleta et al. 2001; Coomes et al. 2003) are increasingly recognized as imperative goals of conservation management.

Many of Canada's National Parks contain hundreds of introduced plant species (Mosquin 1997). The large majority of these plants are found primarily in anthropogenically-disturbed areas such as roadsides, picnic areas, campgrounds, ditches, gravel pits, and hiking trails (Mosquin 1997; Rose 2002). These source populations of non-native plants may threaten ecosystem functioning and native species diversity if they become 'invasive' and successfully expand their range into adjacent native plant communities or highquality protected ecosystems that are remote from human activity (Burke and Grime 1996; Mosquin 1997; Rose and Hermanutz 2004; Hendrickson et al. 2005). When widespread invasion of natural areas occurs, controlling or eradicating the invader while minimizing damage to non-target native species and protecting surrounding natural communities can be a complex, and often impossible task (Myers and Bazely 2003). While invasive species control is often effective at reducing densities of non-native plants, accumulating evidence suggests that implementation of invasive alien plant control efforts in natural areas can have unexpected negative impacts on non-target native species and ecosystems (NRC 2000; Zavaleta et al. 2001; Smith et al. 2006). In fact, control and eradication practices such as chemical, mechanical, or biological control can result in more severe impacts on native species diversity and ecosystem function than by the non-native invader itself (Hobbs and Humphries 1995; Matarczyk et al. 2002; Pearson and Callaway 2003; Messing and Wright 2006) and may lead to severe environmental degradation (NRC 2000) and further exotic invasions (Smith et al. 2006).

How to remove exotic plants is a problem faced by managers of Gros Morne National Park of Canada (Newfoundand; GMNP) where Cirsium arvense (L.) Scop. (i.e. creeping thistle; Canada thistle), an herbaceous perennial alien plant in North America, has recently invaded widely-dispersed natural and anthropogenic boreal forest gaps on an extensive landscape scale. Surveys throughout the Park (1,805 km<sup>2</sup>) in 2005 indicated its presence in 55% of naturally-disturbed gaps and 41% of gaps formed by small-scale timber harvesting (Parks Canada, unpublished data) at densities up to 48 shoots/m<sup>2</sup> (Humber 2009). Many of these gaps are isolated within dense coniferous canopy and are located many kilometers from the nearest road. Despite its notoriety as a noxious weed in agricultural landscapes (Moore 1975; Donald 1990; Skinner et al. 2000), C. arvense invasion into GMNP forests was not anticipated since it is shade intolerant (Bakker 1960; Donald 1994; Nuzzo 1997; Edwards et al. 2000; Tiley 2010) and has not generally been considered a threat to boreal forests (Haber 1997). Forests act as a relatively continuous barrier to wind dispersal of alien plant seeds (Brothers and Spingarn 1992) and the low light availability, soil pH, and nutrient availability in undisturbed forest canopies, which often separate gap openings by great distances, are unsuitable for shade-intolerant herbs such as C. arvense (Bakker 1960; Haber 1997; Rose 2002). In GMNP, it appears that these obstacles to invasion have been overcome as a result of direct and indirect facilitation by the high density of non-native moose (Alces alces L.; Rose and Hermanutz 2004; Gosse et al. 2011).

Since the Park's establishment in 1973 and the onset of protection from hunting, moose populations in GMNP have increased from 0.14 moose/km2 in 1971 to densities of 5.0 moose/km<sup>2</sup> in preferred lowland forests in 2007 (Burzynski et al. 2005; Thompson 2007). In the highest density survey units, densities were as high as 12.0 moose/km<sup>2</sup> in 2007 (Thompson 2007)—representing some of the world's highest densities (McLaren et al. 2000). Browsing by these high density moose populations within GMNP's forests is having deleterious impacts on native tree species diversity and is threatening forest regeneration (McLaren et al. 2004; Burzynski et al. 2005; Forbes 2006). In winter, moose preferentially browse the advanced regeneration (i.e. seedling bank) of balsam fir (Abies balsamea (L.) Mill.), the dominant species in GMNP's forests and in all productive upland sites throughout

![](_page_1_Picture_6.jpeg)

Newfoundland (Hall and Richardson [1973;](#page-15-0) Burzynski et al. [2005\)](#page-14-0). The loss of the balsam fir seedling bank is highly detrimental to post-disturbance stand regeneration because it is the species' primary means of restocking gaps after disturbance (Duchesneau and Morin [1999\)](#page-15-0) and balsam fir seeds do not form a persistent seed bank (Morin and Laprise [1997](#page-16-0); Greene et al. [1999\)](#page-15-0). Densities of balsam fir seedlings within gaps are insufficient to restock forests (\1 individual per m<sup>2</sup> ), and where young balsam fir does occur, it is highly stunted from sustained browsing pressure and is unable to reach adult reproductive stages or form a canopy (Humber [2009](#page-15-0)).

Moose also facilitate and promote the invasion of non-regenerating gaps by alien plants such as C. arvense by maintaining disturbed conditions. After canopy opening, moose maintain gap susceptibility to invasion by focusing their browsing on the highly palatable species that revegetate early successional stands (Rose and Hermanutz [2004;](#page-16-0) Forbes [2006](#page-15-0); Gosse [2006;](#page-15-0) Gosse et al. [2011](#page-15-0)). This activity combined with trampling of native vegetation maintains high light availability and creates competitionfree, bare ground sites. Moose in GMNP also act as the primary conduits for alien seed dispersal into these favorable isolated habitat patches (Rose and Hermanutz [2004\)](#page-16-0) by transporting seeds on their hooves and fur along moose travel corridors.

Instead of investigating costly and potentially ineffective and harmful methods of directly controlling C. arvense in GMNP, this study takes a broader ecosystem-scale approach to management (Noss [1996\)](#page-16-0). It is yet unclear whether the accompanying widespread invasion of C. arvense into open boreal forest gaps in GMNP will continue to impede the expected successional trajectory to a closed canopy forest should moose undergo an eventual population crash or should human intervention be implemented to decrease moose populations (McLaren et al. [2004](#page-15-0); Forbes [2006](#page-15-0); McLaren and Tom-Dery [2007\)](#page-15-0). Under similar circumstances, Coomes et al. ([2003\)](#page-15-0) found that, when not combined with active replanting of native plant communities, culling programs aimed at controlling invasive deer populations in New Zealand have not resulted in recovery of the original forest to its pre-invasion composition; rather, introduced plants (e.g. exotic grasses) continue to impede reestablishment of native forest species independent of deer populations. Therefore, the primary goal of this study is to better understand if, and how, C. arvense affects balsam fir regeneration processes. The second goal was to determine if direct sowing of balsam fir seeds or planting of greenhouse-established fir seedlings would be successful in actively promoting native forest regeneration within thistleinvaded gaps. The final goal of this study was to identify how factors influencing balsam fir regeneration differ among gaps formed by natural or anthropogenic processes, as this knowledge could help guide management actions.

To achieve these outlined objectives, both a balsam fir seed-addition and a seedling-addition experiment were performed in naturally and anthropogenically disturbed forest gaps within GMNP and success of this native conifer (i.e. emergence, growth, herbivory damage, and survival) was compared among regions of each gap invaded with C. arvense monocultures, areas not containing C. arvense growth, and the adjacent forest edge. To our knowledge, this is the first study to experimentally examine native forest regeneration within adjacent areas concurrently containing or lacking an alien plant infestation. As balsam fir establishment success was hypothesized to be poor within C. arvense monocultures relative to uninvaded gap and forest edge areas, potential mechanisms by which C. arvense might impede the establishment of balsam fir were also explored. Balsam fir success in unaltered C. arvense monocultures was compared to success in plots where only thistle shoots were removed, excluding aboveground competition for light and potential aboveground allelopathic effects described for thistle shoots and leaves (Kazinczi et al. [2001;](#page-15-0) Glinwood et al. [2004;](#page-15-0) Humber [2009\)](#page-15-0) but maintaining belowground competition by roots, potential residual allelochemicals in soil, and any active allelochemical interference by the remaining roots (Kazinczi et al. [2001\)](#page-15-0); as described in other plant species, biomass removal can either stimulate chemical defenses (Siemens et al. [2002\)](#page-16-0) or result in a tradeoff between chemical defense and shoot regrowth (Hartley and Jones [1997\)](#page-15-0). It was hypothesized that success of fir would not be improved significantly by removing C. arvense shoots alone because new balsam fir emergents and older seedlings are shade tolerant (Hall and Richardson [1973](#page-15-0); Burns and Honkala [1990](#page-14-0)). Additionally, fir success was compared to plots where all competitive and potential direct allelopathic interference was removed by

![](_page_2_Picture_7.jpeg)

eliminating above- and below-ground thistle biomass (Bendall 1975; Kazinczi et al. 2001) but retaining any residual toxicity in the soil. It was hypothesized that fir success would be improved in this treatment but would remain lower than in uninvaded areas since studies of allelopathy in *C. arvense* have demonstrated potential for residual soil to exclude neighboring vegetation (Bendall 1975; Stowe 1979; Wilson 1981).

#### Methods

#### Study area

This study was conducted in the boreal forest of Gros Morne National Park, located on the western coast of the Great Northern Peninsula on the island of Newfoundland, Canada (49°30′ N, 57°50′ W). Our research was restricted to lowland forests (<150 m elevation) which comprises 43% of the park's total area (1,805 km²; Bouchard et al. 1991; Rose and Hermanutz 2004). These forests are dominated by balsam fir, white spruce (*Picea glauca* (Moench) Voss), black spruce (*P. mariana* (Mill.) B.S.P.), and white birch (*Betula papyrifera* Marsh). Soils in the park are primarily humo-ferric podzols characterized by poorly drained acidic, mineral soils and formed under the influences of the parent material, humid climate, and coniferous vegetation (Clayton et al. 1977).

All experiments were performed within 10 disturbed canopy gaps distributed widely throughout the Park's lowland forest (Online Resource 1). Forest gaps were identified using time-sequenced GISindexed aerial photographs (ArcGIS version 9.1, ESRI Corporation, Redlands, California) obtained from Parks Canada as well as with the aid of local foresters. All chosen sites were originally fir-dominated but have not yet recovered after disturbance. Gaps chosen were characteristic of the region; 44.5% of the disturbed lowland forest in GMNP (95.2 km<sup>2</sup>) contains 'severely impaired/failed' forest regeneration typical of the forest gaps chosen in this study, while another 24.1% of disturbed forest is considered 'vulnerable' to regeneration failure due to poor canopy development and varying success of understory regeneration (Parks Canada, unpublished data). Gaps were chosen based on their predominant disturbance regime: half of the gaps (n = 5) were formed naturally by insect defoliators including eastern spruce budworm (Choristoneura fumiferana Clem.) and hemlock looper (Lambdina fiscellaria fiscellaria Guenee) and half were formed by legal small-scale, traditional domestic harvesting by local residents (i.e. anthropogenic disturbance). As part of the Federal-Provincial Agreement for GMNP, local residents within the Park's seven enclave communities and two bordering communities are permitted to cut wood for personal uses within 12 Domestic Timber Harvest blocks comprising 129 km<sup>2</sup> of the 308 km<sup>2</sup> of lowland forest (Burzynski et al. 2005). Time since disturbance varied among gaps (ranging from 6 to 29 years; Online Resource 1), with all gaps having been formed within the past 30 years. Timber harvesting, or tree fall after succumbing to insect defoliation, often occurred over multiple years after the initial year of disturbance to eventually result in complete canopy removal. In addition to the predominant disturbance regime and time since disturbance, site selection was based upon the presence of C. arvense monocultures in gaps, accessibility of gaps (i.e. normally < 1.5 km of a road, with the exception of sites SPA and SPB which were reached by boat; Online Resource 1), approximate size of the gaps (i.e. between 1 and 4 hectares), and to achieve a wide distribution of gaps across the park's lowland forest area.

The undisturbed forest canopies surrounding the gaps were dominated by balsam fir mixed with subdominant white spruce and white birch; less abundant species included trembling aspen (*Populus tremuloides* Michx.), mountain maple (*Acer spicatum* Lam.), and black spruce. The ground vegetation in forests included a wide variety of herbs and mosses including *Pleurozium schreberi* (Brid.) Mitt., *Ptilium crista-castrensis* (Hedw.) De Not, *Hylocomium splendens* (Hedw.) B.S.G., *Rhytidiadelphus* sp., and *Dicranum* spp.

#### Seed addition experiment

In the first week of July 2006, five experimental plots  $(0.5 \text{ m} \times 0.5 \text{ m})$  were established within each forest gap (n = 50 total plots) for addition of balsam fir seeds. Each plot was located within a specific region of the gap (noted below) and was considered a specific 'treatment' in which success of seed addition could be determined:

![](_page_3_Picture_10.jpeg)

- (i) C. arvense monoculture (T): Dense, unmanipulated C. arvense monocultures with an average density of 30 shoots/m<sup>2</sup> . At the time of planting, thistle shoot height averaged 0.9 m among sites, although heights frequently reached [1.5 m later in the summer.
- (ii) C. arvense monoculture with aboveground biomass removed (T-A): Thistle monoculture as in i), but from which all aboveground C. arvense biomass was removed by cutting shoots at soil level immediately prior to planting of balsam fir seeds and continually throughout the growing season at 10 day intervals.
- (iii) C. arvense monoculture with above- and below-ground biomass removed (T-AB): Both aboveground and belowground C. arvense biomass were completely removed by digging up individual plants and their root systems from the soil immediately prior to planting balsam fir seeds; only aboveground thistle regrowth was subsequently cut at 10 day intervals to prevent damage to sensitive roots of balsam fir seedlings.
- (iv) Control—Uninvaded region of the gap (UG): A region within the gap that did not contain C. arvense and was located at least 15 m from the nearest thistle plants. To minimize variability among gap replicates, this treatment was always established within a seedbed of Cornus canadensis L., a native plant that is ubiquitous throughout the boreal forest, regularly cooccurs with balsam fir, and is known as a favorable seedbed for balsam fir establishment (Hall and Richardson [1973](#page-15-0); Burns and Honkala [1990](#page-14-0)).
- (v) Control—Uninvaded forest edge (UE): A plot randomly established 5 m into the adjacent, uninvaded forest edge; this treatment was established as a control for the gap itself, with which success of balsam fir seed addition in the UG treatment was compared.

Within a C. arvense monoculture, the three plot locations for the T, TA, and T-AB treatments (i–iii above) were randomly established within the same patch at a minimum separation of 1 m and treatment was assigned randomly to each plot.

Due to low local balsam fir seed crop in the year prior to study, all seeds were obtained from the National Tree Seed Centre (NTSC), Canadian Forest Service (Fredericton, NB). Seeds were taken from trees in two Newfoundland populations (Bay D'Espoir and Roddickton) and had documented germination rates of 50.0%. As per NTSC protocol, seeds were moist-stratified at 4C for 3 weeks prior to planting; the documented germination rates were confirmed through controlled germination tests of seeds in environmental chambers. As the target was to establish and monitor up to 16 fir seedlings per plot, 32 balsam fir seeds (i.e. with 50% germination rates) were planted into each 0.5 m 9 0.5 m plot by gently pushing them into the exposed mineral soil surface (n = 1,600 total seeds) immediately after plot preparation. Seeds were planted in pairs in a 4 9 4 grid, with 10 cm spacing between each group of two seeds and a 10 cm unplanted border on all sides. Immediately adjacent to each planted plot, an equally-sized (0.5 m 9 0.5 m) unseeded control plot prepared in the same manner was established to quantify natural balsam fir emergence and establishment.

Throughout the first summer (2006), plots were surveyed for newly emerged seedlings at 10 day intervals. Overall emergence rates were based on detection of the shoot above the soil surface. The timing of seedling emergence was observed to closely mimic the period of natural balsam fir emergence in the region. Newly-emerged seedlings were thinned to no more than one seedling per 10 cm 9 10 cm area by clipping at the base of the stem, for a maximum remaining density of 16 seedlings per plot. At 10 day intervals, seedlings were monitored for survival and occurrence of browse damage until the end of the field season on 29 August 2006, at which time the season's growth had ceased and the terminal bud had set. At this time, height of remaining seedlings was determined to the nearest 0.001 cm. All seedlings were re-monitored during mid-June of 2007 and late May of 2008, or approximately 1 and 2 years after planting, to track survival.

#### Seedling addition experiment

In mid-June of 2007, 432 balsam fir seedlings aged 15 months old were planted into four forest gaps within GMNP. Seedlings were obtained from Wooddale Provincial Tree Nursery, Newfoundland (Department of Natural Resources, Government of NL) and

![](_page_4_Picture_13.jpeg)

originated from balsam fir seeds collected from western Newfoundland. At planting, seedlings averaged 24.7 ± 0.2 cm (SE) in height. Gaps chosen for planting of seedlings were a subset of those having received seed addition. Two of the sites were naturally disturbed by insect outbreaks (i.e. sites BBA and SPB; Online Resource 1) and two were harvested sites (i.e. sites MBB and TKB). Each gap contained multiple C. arvense patches, together comprising approximately one third of the overall gap area. In each site, seedlings were planted into one of four locations corresponding to four of the seed-addition treatments (i, ii, iv, and v above), with the exception of the T-AB treatment due to limited seedling availability. Three replicate plots were established for each treatment and randomly positioned at a minimum separation of 1 m. Each plot measured 4 m 9 4 m, with a 1 m unplanted border on all four edges and with nine seedlings planted in a 3 9 3 grid at a distance of 1 m apart on all sides. A total of 48 plots and 432 seedlings were established among the four sites. Each seedling was identified using a small metal tag pinned to the ground near the base of the stem. Change in seedling height (to 0.1 cm), change in basal diameter (to 0.001 cm), presence of browsing or desiccation damage, and mortality were monitored near the end of the first summer growing season (18–20 August 2007) and again in late May of 2008 after almost 1 year in the field to determine changes over the autumn–winter period.

#### Abiotic soil properties

Soil samples were collected for soil moisture determination from each of the 10 gaps on: 14–16 July 2006, 3–5 August 2006, and 23–25 August 2006; and from eight of the gaps on 13–15 June 2007 (two of the anthropogenic gaps could not be sampled at this time due to the presence of black bears). In each gap, a 1m2 quadrat was randomly established in three treatments: C. arvense monoculture (T), uninvaded gap (UG), and uninvaded edge (UE). Soil cores to 10 cm depth were collected from the four corners of the quadrat and pooled in a tightly sealed sample bag at 4C until laboratory procedures to determine percent soil moisture could be performed. At this time, pooled soil samples from each quadrat were thoroughly mixed and three subsamples of 10 ± 0.1 g were removed, weighed, and dried in a 70C drying oven for 48 h before reweighing. Percent soil moisture was calculated as:

$$\%$$
 soil moisture = (wet mass - dry mass)/(dry mass)  $\times$  100

Soil temperature at 10 cm depth was also determined in the eight gaps on 13–15 June 2007 using a Barnant (Type K) Themocouple Thermometer (Barnant Company, Barrington, IL). As in the soil moisture procedure, in each gap a 1 m<sup>2</sup> quadrat was randomly established within three treatments (i.e. T, UG, and UE) and soil temperature (C) at 10 cm depth was measured at each of the four corners of each quadrat. Soil moisture and temperature data were used to compare environmental conditions among treatments within each gap.

## Statistical analyses

All statistical analyses were performed on SAS version 9.1 (SAS Institute, Cary, NC, USA) using generalized linear mixed modeling (GLMM; McCullagh and Nelder [1999](#page-15-0)). Where the response variable was a continuous variable with normal errors (i.e. height, basal diameter, soil moisture, or soil temperature), a mixed-model analysis of variance was performed using the MIXED procedure; random effects are specified in each model as outlined below. All test assumptions were met as determined through examination of residuals for normality, homogeneity, and independence. In general, where the response variable consisted of binary or proportional data (i.e. proportion of seedlings emerging, surviving, sustaining browse damage, or desiccating) with non-normal errors, mixed-model logistic regression was implemented in the GLIMMIX procedure with a logit link and binomial errors. The MIXED and GLIMMIX procedures have a comprehensive set of tools for working with random effects (Littell et al. [2002](#page-15-0)). Variance components were estimated by restricted maximum likelihood (REML) in the MIXED procedure and residual pseudo-likelihood in the GLIMMIX procedure. Denominator degrees of freedom were calculated using the Satterthwaite approximation described in Fai and Cornelius ([1996\)](#page-15-0) except where repeated measures were incorporated into the model design, in which case the Kenward-Rogers approximation was used instead to avoid inflated Type I

![](_page_5_Picture_9.jpeg)

errors (Littell et al. [2002\)](#page-15-0). Analyses were considered significant at a = 0.05.

Seed addition and seedling addition experiments

The main model for the seed and seedling addition analyses consisted of both fixed and random categorical variables. Fixed factors included treatment (T, T-A, T-AB, UG, or UE; excluding T-AB in the seedling addition), disturbance regime (natural or anthropogenic), and their interaction. The variable 'gap' was nested within disturbance regime and treated as a random variable to account for variation among individual gaps. To handle the non-independence of seed/new emergent clustering within plots in the seed addition experiment (i.e. spatially repeated measures), 'plot identity' was included as a repeated effect (equivalently, a 'residual' or 'R-side' random effect in GLIMMIX) and a compound symmetric (i.e. exchangeable) relationship was specified among the individuals within a plot to account for correlated errors (Horton and Lipsitz [1999\)](#page-15-0). In the seedling addition experiment, each seedling within a plot was considered independent as it was widely separated from other individuals by at least 1 m on all sides, however, a second random variable accounted for treatment replication within any gap (n = 3 plots per each treatment in each gap) and was nested within gap.

In the seed addition experiment, overall balsam fir emergence was analyzed based on the proportion of viable seeds planted (i.e. 32 seeds/plot 9 50% viability = 16 viable seeds/plot). Cumulative fir seedling survival was determined at three time periods: at the end of the first growing season (i.e. 'GS1'), at approximately 1 year (i.e. 'Y1'), and after about 2 years (i.e. 'Y2'). Seedling survival at each of these periods was expressed as the proportion of the total number of seedlings initially present in the plot (i.e. those remaining after post-emergence thinning). Fir survival was also determined between the first autumn–winter period (i.e. 'AW1', the period between GS1 and Y1) and between the first and second years (i.e. between Y1 and Y2). Cumulative browsing damage on seedlings (including lethal and non-lethal damage) and analyses of seedling height were determined at the GS1 and Y1 stages. In the seedling addition experiment, cumulative fir seedling survival was analyzed at GS1 and Y1, with autumn– winter survivorship between the GS1 and Y1 periods also determined. Desiccation and browsing damage (lethal and non-lethal combined) as well as change in height and basal diameter since planting were analyzed at the GS1 and Y1 stages.

In all analyses for both experiments, when the effect of treatment was significant a priori linear contrasts were used to compare means among selected treatments (Online Resource 2) and Bonferroni corrected for the number of comparisons performed (seed addition: a<sup>0</sup> = 0.0125; seedling addition: a<sup>0</sup> = 0.0167).

Abiotic soil properties

Fixed factors in the analyses of soil moisture and temperature using the Proc MIXED procedure consisted of treatment (T, UG, or UE), disturbance regime, and their interaction. The random variable 'gap' was nested within disturbance regime. In the analysis of soil moisture in 2006, potentially correlated errors resulting from repeated measures over the field season were accounted for by adding 'sampling period' as a repeated effect with a firstorder autoregressive structure to account for equal periods between re-sampling (Littell et al. [2002](#page-15-0)). Soil moisture was sampled once in 2007 and was analyzed separately. When the effect of treatment was significant, three linear contrasts were used to compare among each pair of treatment means and were Bonferroni corrected for three comparisons (a<sup>0</sup> = 0.0167).

In all statistical analyses performed in this study, where the interaction between treatment and disturbance regime was significant, the main effects could not be examined within this model (Sokal and Rohlf [1995\)](#page-16-0) and thus the effect of 'treatment' was examined separately within each disturbance regime. We report only significant interaction terms. Results are expressed as mean ± standard error of the mean.

Again, in natural gaps the fir emergence within C. arvense monocultures was not significantly increased by removal of thistle biomass (T-A: P = 0.1113; T-AB: P = 0.1214); however, all emergence of balsam fir seedlings in thistle treatments (T, T-A, T-AB) occurred within just two of the five naturallydisturbed gaps (SPA and SPB; Online Resource 1). No emergence occurred in T, T-A, and T-AB treatments in the other natural gaps.

Survival, browse damage, and height growth

The impacts of C. arvense monocultures on fir seedling survival, height, and browse damage could not be determined within anthropogenic gaps due to extremely low emergence. In naturally disturbed gaps, survival of newly emerged seedlings over the first summer growing season ranged from 32.6 to 72.2% among treatments (Fig. [2](#page-8-0)). The lowest survival was within the uninvaded gap (UG) treatment whereas the highest survival rates were in thistle monocultures (T) and were 2.25 times higher than in UG. In T-<sup>A</sup> and T-AB treatments, mean fir survival rates were also 1.62 and 1.80 times higher than in UG, respectively. By the following spring however (Y1), this pattern of higher survival within thistle areas was no longer evident (Fig. [2\)](#page-8-0). After 2 years in the field (Y2), no seedlings remained in any thistle treatment while 21.7 and 33.3% of seedlings were still alive in UG and UE. However, none of these survival rates differ statistically.

Seedling herbivory was attributed primarily to voles because the stem was clipped cleanly; the remaining herbivory was attributed to slugs which leave characteristic slime trails. Herbivory was the main cause of mortality in the UG, UE, and T-AB treatments; as no trace could be found of 59 and 44% of the seedlings within the T and T-<sup>A</sup> treatments,

## Abiotic parameters

In 2006, soil moisture levels at seedling rooting depth did not differ among treatments (F2,42 = 0.36, P = 0.7023) but there was a significant difference in percent soil moisture content among disturbance regimes (F1,8 = 9.33, P = 0.0159). Soil of naturally disturbed gaps was considerably drier than anthropogenic gaps (natural: 1.24 ± 0.42% moisture; anthropogenic: 3.06 ± 0.42%); with one exception, soil moisture content was higher in all anthropogenic gaps than in natural gaps. In 2007, soil moisture levels in each treatment was determined separately for each disturbance regime due to a significant interaction (F2,57 = 8.72, P = 0.0005). In anthropogenic gaps, there were marginally significant differences among treatments (F2,22 = 3.37, P = 0.0531), with % soil moisture content in T (3.26 ± 0.49%) marginally higher than in UE (2.79 ± 0.49%; P = 0.0290) and UG (2.83 ± 0.49%; P = 0.0432) treatments. In natural gaps, soil moisture content among treatments was significantly different (F2,36 = 14.38, P\ 0.0001) with the highest moisture levels in UE (1.72 ± 0.28%), less moisture in T (1.10 ± 0.27%), and very low moisture levels in UG (0.51 ± 0.27%); all treatments were significantly different at a<sup>0</sup> = 0.0167 when Bonferroni-corrected for three comparisons. In 2007, soil in the five natural gaps was generally drier than the anthropogenic gaps (see Humber [2009](#page-15-0) for gap-specific data).

Soil temperature at 10 cm depth, as measured during the 2007 growing season, was significantly different among treatments (F2,64 = 44.22, P\0.0001) but not among disturbance regimes (F1,5 = 0.00, P = 0.9702). Soil temperatures in T (13.65 ± 0.33C) were significantly higher than temperatures in UG (12.61 ± 0.33C; P = 0.0008) and UE (10.78 ± 0.34C, P\0.0001).

## Discussion

Invasion of non-native C. arvense into boreal forest gaps negatively affects balsam fir regenerative processes at early stages of emergence and survival, and thus may impede natural balsam fir regeneration and the formation of a closed canopy forest independent of future moose densities. However, the success of balsam fir within thistle-invaded boreal forest gaps is dependent upon numerous factors including the life stage in which fir is planted (i.e. as seeds or seedlings), the way in which success is measured (i.e. emergence, survival, growth), the history of forest disturbance, and the distinctive abiotic conditions specific to individual gaps. Older seedlings transplanted into C. arvense patches were not disadvantaged over the first year and thus may be a viable management strategy to encourage regeneration in invaded gaps. This knowledge is highly valuable in helping to identify appropriate ecosystem management actions in thistle-invaded boreal forests.

Balsam fir emergence in natural gaps was three times lower in C. arvense monocultures than in uninvaded areas of gaps. Because physical removal of either aboveground C. arvense biomass or the entire plant did not significantly improve fir emergence, the results suggest that C. arvense does not limit fir recruitment through direct competition, but may be the

result of allelopathy or numerous other potential soil properties and microsite conditions (e.g. nutrient availability, water availability, level of compaction). Although fir seedling survival in thistle monocultures was low over the first 2 years, this exotic invader facilitated fir survival over the first growing season such that newly emerged seedlings experienced less than one fifth of the level of browsing, and had over twice the survival rate, than seedlings in uninvaded areas of the gap. This finding is biologically significant since conifer seedlings are at greatest risk of predation during the first 10–16 weeks post-germination (Duchesneau and Morin [1999](#page-15-0)). During the growing season, the sharp spines on C. arvense apparently deterred herbivores and offered protection to neighboring fir from early seedling consumers. Removal of aboveground, or above- and belowground, C. arvense biomass was not beneficial as it caused increases in the level of browsing on fir and decreased survival. During the autumn–winter period, C. arvense shoots die back and likely offer less protection to fir: browsing incidence on seedlings in all three thistle treatments (T, T-A, and T-AB) increased during this period but damage remained the lowest in the thistle monoculture (T) after 1 year. This facilitative effect was unexpected although similar positive interactions between neighboring plants occurring under conditions of strong consumer pressure have been elsewhere described (Bertness and Callaway [1994](#page-14-0); Brooker et al. [2008\)](#page-14-0). A tradeoff may therefore exist for newly emerged seedlings growing amongst C. arvense: there may be a net benefit on seedlings at early stages when the seedling is most vulnerable to herbivory, but over time, negative influences on survival could outweigh any benefits. The steady, complete decline in fir survival over 2 years which was observed in the thistle-invaded areas differs from patterns in uninvaded areas of the gap and the forest edge as well as patterns described in other seedling mortality studies (Burns and Honkala [1990;](#page-14-0) Simard et al. [2003\)](#page-16-0), where initial seedlings losses were the greatest during the first few months but declined only minimally thereafter.

Herbivory by small mammals and slugs was the main cause of newly emerged seedling mortality in uninvaded treatments as well as in the entire C. arvense biomass removal treatment; at least 80% of deaths in these treatments could be attributed to either herbivory or desiccation. This early herbivory was likely by nonnative species, as only one species (Deroceras laeve Mu¨ller) out of ten species of slugs established in GMNP is native (Moss and Hermanutz [2010\)](#page-16-0) and over 90% of small mammal captures in the forests of GMNP have been non-native species (Burzynski et al. [2005](#page-14-0)). In unaltered thistle monocultures and where thistle shoots were removed, a large proportion of seedlings disappeared without a trace and thus cause of death could not be determined. The seedlings may have been buried and smothered by senescing thistle shoots in autumn, or their mortality increased due to growing under dense cover (Duchesneau and Morin [1999](#page-15-0)) or allelopathic effects of thistle (Kazinczi et al. [2001](#page-15-0); Glinwood et al. [2004](#page-15-0); Humber [2009\)](#page-15-0).

Compared with newly emerged seedlings, larger nursery-derived seedlings (aged 15 months at planting) experienced low levels of herbivory (\6%) over the first growing season as they were not browsed by small mammals and slugs and they largely avoided moose herbivory until the autumn–winter period, when moose begin to rely heavily on balsam fir (Des Meules [1962\)](#page-15-0). Despite low levels of herbivory during this growing season, there was some evidence ofC. arvense protecting these older fir seedlings from herbivory. Over the duration of 1 year, there was no evidence of any negative impact of transplanting nursery-derived balsam fir seedlings into C. arvense monocultures. Levels of desiccation were lower, and percent soil moisture contents higher, in thistle-invaded areas of the gap than in native gap vegetation. As moisture is not normally a major limiting factor for C. arvense and it commonly grows in very dry soil such as sand dunes and sandy fields (Moore [1975](#page-16-0); Nuzzo [1997\)](#page-16-0), this pattern is most likely explained by a favorable moist microclimate created by the dense herbaceous C. arvense canopy. The results suggest that older, nurseryderived balsam fir seedlings are capable of growing among dense C. arvense monocultures without experiencing significant consequences to survival or growth. Removal of aboveground C. arvense shoots by cutting appeared to be slightly disadvantageous to fir seedling success. However, as the success of these transplanted seedlings could be followed for only 1 year, it is not certain whether these benefits will persist over longer time periods. Allelopathic effects of other plant species can cause the failure of tree regeneration (Fisher [1987\)](#page-15-0) therefore it is important to better understand the potential allelopathic interactions between C. arvense and native boreal tree species (Humber [2009](#page-15-0)).

![](_page_12_Picture_6.jpeg)

This study revealed that success of balsam fir emergence and establishment differs greatly between disturbance regimes. Transplanted nursery seedlings experienced minimal height growth and increased rates of desiccation-related mortality in natural gaps compared with anthropogenic gaps, yet fir emergence was ubiquitously low throughout all treatments in anthropogenic gaps. Seedbed quality and dominant plant communities in insect-disturbed gaps and harvested gaps in GMNP are known to be very similar (Humber [2009\)](#page-15-0) and seeds were always planted within a consistent seedbed. Further research is needed to investigate if the ubiquitously low emergence within anthropogenic gaps may potentially be the result of higher levels of seed predation in this disturbance regime (Simon et al. [1998](#page-16-0); Sullivan et al. [1999](#page-16-0)), as remnants of predated seeds were occasionally observed within experimental plots.

## Management implications

Augmenting thistle-invaded anthropogenic forest gaps of GMNP with balsam fir seeds is not a viable approach to encouraging regeneration due to extremely low emergence. Although emergence was much more successful in natural gaps, sowing is likely still an inefficient management approach because of high rates of loss to early seedling mortality, unpredictable success among gaps, and negative impacts of C. arvense on emergence and survival. Frequently-used methods of mechanical thistle control in small infestations (i.e. hand cutting or digging up plants; Trumble and Kok [1982;](#page-16-0) Donald [1990;](#page-15-0) Dock Gustavsson [1997](#page-15-0)) did not improve the success of balsam fir emergence and establishment. Moreover, the sowing protocol involved selectively planting individual seeds at a favorable position slightly under the soil surface, yet large-scale restoration normally involves either broadcast sowing, hydro-seeding, or in very large-scale projects, aerial seeding from lowflying aircraft (Davy [2008](#page-15-0)). Large-scale sowing would, by necessity, be much less selective than in the present study and previous research indicates that the majority of seedbeds existing within these gaps are unfavorable for conifer recruitment (Humber [2009\)](#page-15-0). For these same reasons, the results suggest that even in the event of significantly decreased moose densities, non-regenerating boreal forest gaps in GMNP (especially those disturbed by timber harvesting) are not likely to regenerate on their own from seed.

Where barriers to the success of sown seeds exist, restoration processes may be greatly accelerated by planting long-lived perennial species to rapidly reestablish populations of important or keystone species (Davy [2008\)](#page-15-0). The results of this study indicate that planting balsam fir seedlings could be a viable approach to actively promoting native forest regeneration and natural successional processes within gaps, regardless of whether they are invaded by C. arvense or not. However, the variability of fir seedling success among gaps suggests that the response of balsam fir to C. arvense invasion is complex and highly dependent upon individual gap characteristics. For this reason, any restoration program would greatly benefit from initial small-scale trials in target gaps and a detailed evaluation of the suitability of local environmental conditions. Such an ecosystem approach to management, where ecosystems are the conservation targets rather than individual species (Noss [1996\)](#page-16-0), may be much more effective in this system than targeting control efforts towards C. arvense alone since the problem of invasion is rooted in large-scale changes to ecosystem processes and disturbance regimes (Chapin et al. [2000](#page-15-0); Myers and Bazely [2003\)](#page-16-0).

Even if single-species control of C. arvense is desired, any attempts at achieving and maintaining this control in large or widespread infestations may not be feasible or financially realistic (Mosquin [1997;](#page-16-0) Rejma´nek and Pitcairn [2002](#page-16-0); Smith et al. [2006](#page-16-0)). Aggressive chemical or mechanical weed control in protected natural areas can threaten sensitive nontarget native species and stimulate further alien plant invasion (Hobbs and Humphries [1995](#page-15-0); Zimdahl [2004;](#page-16-0) Smith et al. [2006\)](#page-16-0). By shifting the management focus towards re-establishing a native forest canopy, control of non-native, shade-intolerant ruderal plants may also be achieved as they are shaded out over time (Mosquin [1997\)](#page-16-0). C. arvense is shade intolerant: growth of its seedlings is considerably reduced in 60–70% of full daylight levels (Bakker [1960](#page-14-0); Edwards et al. [2000](#page-15-0)) and even mature plants experience reduced vigour, vegetative spread, and flower development when growing under taller vegetation or in closed canopy woodlands (Bakker [1960;](#page-14-0) Trumble and Kok [1982](#page-16-0); Donald [1990;](#page-15-0) Tiley [2010](#page-16-0)). This

![](_page_13_Picture_7.jpeg)

<span id="page-14-0"></span>shading principle has been successfully practiced in abandoned farmland and old fields in eastern Canada to form dense closed conifer canopies which shade out alien grasses and forbs over many years (Fisher [1987;](#page-15-0) Mosquin [1997](#page-16-0)). This less 'invasive' option to combat large or widespread invasive weed populations holds great potential for the management of sensitive natural areas.

Ultimately however, no restoration program initiated to encourage balsam fir regeneration and canopy formation in Gros Morne National Park will be successful unless moose densities are first lowered. Although there were no negative impacts of C. arvense on transplanted fir seedlings over their first year, seedlings in all treatments experienced very high levels of moose herbivory and significant height suppression (see Humber [2009](#page-15-0)). Allocating valuable resources towards gap regeneration will be futile as long as moose continue to suppress canopy development; similarly, any attempts to control or prevent C. arvense invasions will be unsuccessful in the long term if the root cause of the invasion (i.e. overabundant moose) remains. Restoration actions that combine moose reductions with a seedling planting program to restore natural regenerative processes (McLaren et al. [2009\)](#page-15-0) may be the best first step for managers of Gros Morne National Park. In Point Pelee National Park (Canada), culling of overabundant white-tailed deer herds combined with reintroductions of mid-successional trees and shrubs, passive restoration (i.e. encouraging natural colonization), and modifications to topography and hydrology have reduced browsing suppression of Carolinian forests (McLachlan [1997;](#page-15-0) Buckley et al. 2008). As a result, native herb and shrub communities are gradually increasing and are shading out non-native ruderal plants in some areas (McLachlan [1997](#page-15-0); Buckley et al. 2008). Until the overabundant moose population is lowered in Gros Morne National Park, either naturally or through human intervention, these charismatic non-native animals will remain the greatest inhibitors to natural forest regeneration and active restoration alike and will continue to redefine the community compositions of this protected forest landscape.
