# **Efects of Emergent Vegetation Patterns on Flow Velocity, Turbulence, and Erosion Around River Banks**

**Zhongyu Wang1,2 · Huayong Zhang1 · Weigang Xu3 · Hualin Wang1 · Hai Huang1**

Received: 15 April 2016 / Accepted: 9 November 2018 / Published online: 19 November 2018 © Shiraz University 2018

#### **Abstract**

The efects of two diferent emergent vegetation patterns on the fow velocity and turbulent kinetic energy (TKE) along the vegetated banks are experimentally investigated with natural reed stems. The results reveal that vegetation patterns can signifcantly alter the distributions of fow velocity and TKE. At locations for the maximum TKE, closed concentric circles named recirculation cells are observed. The location for the maximum TKE which may induce potential soil erosion for partly vegetated pattern moves forward 1 m in comparison with the vegetated pattern. An empirical function is found to describe the variations of TKE along the fow direction. Gentler distributed fow velocity and lower TKE at the rear part of the vegetation section for partly vegetated pattern suggest that vegetation pattern is an important factor for aquatic vegetation recovery in rivers. The fndings of the research may provide some guidance for the engineering practices of river ecological restoration with aquatic vegetation.

**Keywords** Vegetation pattern · Flow velocity · Turbulence kinetic energy · Rigid unsubmerged vegetation · Soil erosion · Ecological river restoration

# **1 Introduction**

As an integral part of aquatic ecosystem, vegetation provides many positive efects in habitat restoration, water pollutants purifcation, and bank soil erosion (Sand-Jensen [1998\)](#page-10-0). In many countries, aquatic vegetation planting has become a popular technique in aquatic ecological restoration (Callow [2012\)](#page-9-0). With this concern, numerous research works have been devoted to the comprehensive understanding of the role of vegetation in river ecosystems (Wu et al. [1999;](#page-10-1) Musleh and Cruise [2006](#page-10-2)).

In open-channel fow, fow velocity is an indicator measuring the transportation ratio of suspended particles, especially for non-scouring and non-silting velocities (Chow [1959](#page-9-1); Abrari et al. [2015](#page-9-2)). As well recognized by researchers in the feld, vegetation existence in river can alter the water surface profle and the distribution of fow velocity (Vanoni [2006](#page-10-3); Neary et al. [2012](#page-10-4)). For the research focusing on the vegetation efects on fow structure in vegetated channel, many experimental studies have been conducted in laboratory fumes (Wu et al. [1999](#page-10-1); Musleh and Cruise [2006\)](#page-10-2). In recent years, to realize the mechanism of the interactive efects between aquatic vegetation and water fow, more attention has been paid to the research of the evolution of hydraulic roughness, biomechanics and streamlining of vegetation, and turbulent structure (Nepf [1999;](#page-10-5) Merritt and Cooper [2000;](#page-10-6) Zhang et al. [2015a](#page-10-7)). Bank vegetation increases the efective roughness height of the boundary, which has the efect of reducing the forces of drag and lift acting on the bank surface (Thorne [1990\)](#page-10-8). Considering vegetation resistance is a function of vegetation size, structural properties, and location in the channel and local hydraulic conditions of water fow, Green pointed out that there is a nonlinear relationship between channel resistance and the proportion of the channel occupied by vegetation (Green [2005a\)](#page-9-3). With the efects of vegetation resistance, aquatic plants can convert kinetic energy into TKE, which is an indicator measuring the development and recession of turbulence. Based on this relationship of energy transformation which should be responsible to wake generation and turbulence intensity, Nepf developed a model describing the drag, turbulence, and difusion for fow through emergent vegetation (Nepf [1999](#page-10-5)).

In vegetated channels, the stems of rigid unsubmerged vegetation might cause local acceleration of fow velocity and therefore turbulence moving in the wake zone (Thorne [1990\)](#page-10-8), where detachment and entrainment usually occur (Leeder [1983\)](#page-10-9). Turbulence can promote the exchange of momentum, energy, and matter; its efects are under high frequency and small scale (Cliford [1993\)](#page-9-4). As well recognized, emergent vegetation in aquatic ecosystem can profoundly infuence the turbulent structure and therefore alter the pattern of soil erosion and stream morphology (López and García [2001](#page-10-10); Neary et al. [2012;](#page-10-4) Zhang et al. [2015b](#page-10-11)). The vegetation effects of different forms on turbulence characteristics are therefore becoming a research hot spot (Velasco et al. [2003](#page-10-12); Afzalimehr and Dey [2009\)](#page-9-5). Experimental tests were conducted by Velasco et al. [\(2003](#page-10-12)) using plastic plants seeded in a gravel bed to measure the turbulent regimes within and above fexible submerged vegetation. McBride et al. [\(2007](#page-10-13)) observed that TKE shows signifcant diferences between forested and non-forested runs. Yagci et al. [\(2010](#page-10-14)) experimentally investigated the turbulence and kinetic energy patterns behind single erect vegetation, by which an empirical equation was proposed regarding the relationship between the kinetic energy components of fow. Neary et al. ([2012](#page-10-4)) examined the interactions and feedback between fow, vegetation, and stream morphology with a variety of macrophyte patterns in riverine environments, and revealed that the spatial variability of the fow, momentum transfer, turbulence transportation are factors afecting the morphological processes in rivers.

In natural rivers, vegetation cover increases the irregularities of water fow and makes the fow systems partially continuous (Zhang and Dai [2009\)](#page-10-15). The interactive efects among the water fow, aquatic vegetation type, distribution pattern, and soil erosion increase the difculty of the research in the feld (McBride et al. [2007;](#page-10-13) Baghlani and Talebbeydokhti [2013;](#page-9-6) Johari and Nejad [2015\)](#page-10-16). Particularly, computations of experimental values of TKE are difcult to obtain due to a large number of measuring locations that need to be obtained (López and García [2001](#page-10-10)). Above all, little regard is given to the spatial afecting process of vegetation on fow velocity, turbulence, and erosion, especially for the local efects in open channel with diferent vegetation patterns.

This research intends to investigate how emergent vegetation patterns alter the transportation of fow velocity and TKE around the vegetated channel banks. With series experiments conducted in a laboratory channel which is, respectively, planted with vegetated and partly vegetated patterns, the distributions of fow velocity and TKE along the vegetated banks will be investigated. The research will also try to establish an empirical approach for predicting the extent of the TKE alternations. The primary goals of the research are to explore the mechanism and empirical relationships of emergent vegetation patterns on TKE in vegetated open channel and provide some guidance to the soil erosion control in ecological restoration practices for river banks.

# **2 Laboratory Experiments and Analytical Methodology**

# **2.1 Experimental Setup**

The primary goal of the research is to study the fow velocity and turbulent kinetic energy and further analyze the erosion possibility near the vegetated wall of the experimental channel under vegetated and partly vegetated patterns. From this point, a series of experiments are conducted in a laboratory channel whose bed and walls are all made of concrete. The internal dimensions of the experimental channel are 30 m in length, 1.3 m in width, and 0.8 m in depth, and the bed slope is fxed at 0.0055 m/m. In this experimental channel, water fow is circularly supplied by a piping centrifugal pump (Model: 200-200(I) A) which has a maximum fow discharge of 99.4 l/s. In this research, the fow discharge is select as 90 l*/*s to fully measure the internal fow and turbulent structure. In order to stabilize the water fow and control the fow discharge, a HQC-2 water level-discharge controller, a thin-walled rectangle weir, a stilling basin, and an energy dissipation fence are equipped in front of the main channel. As shown in Fig. [1,](#page-2-0) the 6-m-long test section is located between the *y*=4 m and 10 m locations away from the channel head, and there is a 4-m-long vegetation section within this test section.

Natural reed stems are used to simulate the rigid unsubmerged vegetation to make the results of the research closer to reality. The experimental stems are cut from the bottom of natural reed stalks with a uniform length of 0.6 m and a mean diameter of 0.007 m. For the vegetation planting, a 0.2-m-deep pool is built concave downward the channel bottom in the vegetation section (*y*=5 –9 m). Then, the pool is flled with fne sand and covered with punched plates on which several holes with a uniform diameter of 0.01 m are drilled in a uniformly stagger array. As such, stems can be uniformly staggered and frmly planted into these holes according to the experimental requirements. To get the experimental conditions of vegetated and partly vegetated patterns, the experimental stems are planted at the bottom of the channel by two arrays. As shown in Fig. [2a](#page-2-1), the vegetated pattern can be formed by planting reed stems in the whole 4-m-long section (*y* = 5–9 m). The area within the red pane in Fig. [1](#page-2-0) will not be planted which means the reed stems are only planted in half of the 4-m-long section (*y*=5*–*9 m), and a partly vegetated pattern can be obtained (see Fig. [2](#page-2-1)b). In this research, the vegetated and partly vegetated experimental conditions are, respectively, defned as P1 and P2. As the fow velocity can be highly reduced by dense vegetation, higher vegetation densities are generally used in the practices of river ecological restoration (Nikora et al. [2013](#page-10-17)). In this research, the reed stems are planted with a density of 202 stems/m<sup>2</sup> for both of these vegetation patterns.

# **2.2 Determinations of Hydraulic Parameters**

Reynolds Number and Froude Number along the vegetated banks for vegetated and partly vegetated patterns are frst calculated to determine the fow conditions in the channel. The Reynolds Number can be obtained at every measure point all over the channel, the calculating formula is

$$Re = \rho v R / \mu = \rho Q / (\mu (B + 2h)) \tag{1}$$

<span id="page-3-1"></span>where *𝜌* = 0.9991 × 10<sup>3</sup> kg/m3 is the water density at 15 °C, *v* is the streamwise fow velocity along the *y* direction, *𝜇* = 1.1404 × 10<sup>−</sup><sup>3</sup> Pa s is the dynamic viscosity of the water at 15 °C, *Q* =90 l*/*s is the fow discharge, *B*=1.3 m is the width of the experimental channel. The hydraulic radius *R* and the water depth *h* in Eq. ([1\)](#page-3-1) can be, respectively, calculated with

$$R = Bh/(B+2h) \tag{2}$$

and

$$h = Q/vB \tag{3}$$

![](_page_3_Picture_15.jpeg)

![](_page_3_Picture_16.jpeg)

In this research, the Reynolds Numbers for all the measuring points belong to the ranges of 43, 555-47, 136, 46, 806-49, 142 for the vegetation patterns of P1 and P2, respectively. All Reynolds numbers are much higher than the critical value 500 for open-channel fow, which illustrates that the fow conditions for the experiments are fully developed turbulent.

The formula used for the calculation of the average Froude number is

$$\overline{Fr} = \overline{v/(gh)^{0.5}} \tag{4}$$

where *g* = 9.81 m/s<sup>2</sup> is the gravitational acceleration. And the Froude Numbers for the vegetated and partly vegetated experiments are 0.21 and 0.32, respectively. For both of the two experimental conditions, the Froude numbers are below 1.0, which attests that the fows in the channel are the state of the gradually varying fow.

As mentioned above, the 3-D fow velocities along the banks of vegetated and partly vegetated patterns are measured in the experiments, with which the total fow velocities can be determined with

$$V = (u^2 + v^2 + w^2)^{0.5}$$
 (5)

where *u*, *v* and *w* are the transverse (along the channel weigh), longitudinal (along the channel length) and vertical (along the channel length) component of fow velocity, respectively.

As the mean kinetic energy per unit mass associated with eddies in turbulent fow, the turbulence kinetic energy (TKE) is calculated and analyzed to investigate the infuence of vegetation patterns on turbulent characteristics. With the instantaneous fow velocities measured in the experiments, the TKE can be calculated at each measure point for both vegetated and partly vegetated cases. In turbulent fow, TKE is an isotropic parameter characterized by measured root-mean-square velocity fuctuations. For each measurement point, TKE can be quantifed by the mean of the turbulence normal stresses as below:

TKE = 
$$(\overline{u'^2 + v'^2 + w'^2})/2$$
 (6)

where *u*� = *u* − *u*, *v*� = *v* − *v*, and *w*� = *w* − *w* represent the fuctuations of the transverse, longitudinal, and vertical component of fow velocity *u*, *v*, *w*, which means the deviations of instantaneous measurements are diferent from the average, respectively (Hiscoxa et al. [2015](#page-9-8); Zhang et al. [2015b](#page-10-11)). *u*, *v*, *w* are the average values of *u*, *v*, *w*, and the average processes above can be made by averaging the values of 3000 samples measured by ADV, accordingly. In addition, the relative turbulent kinetic energy which is defned as TKE*<sup>r</sup>* = TKE/TKEmax will also be analyzed regarding the longitudinal locations for diferent vegetation patterns.

# **3 Results and Discussion**

# **3.1 Bank of Vegetated Pattern (P1)**

#### **3.1.1 Distributions of Water Depth and Flow Velocity**

Before investigating the variations of TKE along the channel banks, the distributions of fow velocity and water depth are frst analyzed in this research. In Fig. [4,](#page-4-0) the longitudinal distributions of relative fow velocity and water depth along the bank of vegetated pattern are plotted. It should be noticed that the variation trends for fow velocity and water depth are not completely negatively correlated as the measurement points are near the channel bank.

As can be seen in the fgure, for the vegetated pattern, vegetation block can raise the water depth and reduce the fow velocity before the vegetation section. In natural rivers, reduction in fow velocity and therefore erosion power will reduce the erosive efects on river banks and may induce suspended particle deposition although the non-scouring velocities are diferent depending on the boundary materials (Chow [1959](#page-9-1)). These efects are benefcial to the control of bank soil erosion, which have promoted the aquatic vegetation planting and management to be a common technique for river ecological restoration, especially for the soil erosion control (Zhou et al. [2006](#page-10-18)). However, deposition of suspended particles will occur, and the entrance of vegetation section may also be silted up if the fow velocity is too small, which is similar to the results reported in previous studies (Nagle [2002](#page-10-19); Butt et al. [2010\)](#page-9-9). Planting the vegetation stems with various densities may be a possible way to uniformize the fow velocity along the fow direction.

In the vegetation section, due to the raising of water level at the entrance of vegetation section, fow velocity along the channel bank in this case increases all along and reaches a maximum value at the exit of the vegetation section. Although the fow velocity presents a decreased tendency after the vegetation section, the values of fow velocity

#### 3.1.2 Vertical Profile of TKE

The vertical profiles of TKE along the vegetated banks are selected to characterize the turbulent structure around river banks with different vegetation patterns. For the bank along the vegetated pattern, the contours of TKE are drawn in Fig. 5 using the inverse distance weighting method. As can be found in the figure, the variation of TKE along the flow direction is significantly affected by vegetation block and can be divided into largely three sections. In addition, the interfaces among these three sections are clear and almost upright.

Firstly, the measured TKE values are small and stable in the section y=4 m (1 m before the vegetation block) to y=7.2 m (near the middle of the vegetation block), which forms a low-TKE zone. Within this zone, the TKE values are relatively higher at the area near the bottom of y=5 m location, where the TKE values increase from 53.0 to 154.9 m²/s² and form several closed circles reflecting the complex interaction between the vegetation and water flow. This phenomenon may be caused by the higher momentum exchange at the entrance of vegetation section where the water flow breaks against the vegetation block. Outside this region, the TKE in the section y=4-7.2 m is mostly lower than  $53.0 \text{ m}^2/\text{s}^2$ , indicating that the turbulence variation is very small. Considering that the flow velocity will be reduced at the forepart of the vegetation section due to the vegetal

blocking effects (Green 2005b), the potential risk of soil erosion should be lower in this section. The probability of potential soil erosion should therefore be very small (Toorman et al. 2002).

Secondly, in the section of y = 7-9.9 m, the TKE values increase rapidly along the flow direction from 53.0 m<sup>2</sup>/s<sup>2</sup> and reach the maximum value 562.5 m<sup>2</sup>/s<sup>2</sup> at the location of y=9 m (the exit of the vegetation section). After y=9 m, the TKE values begin to decrease until 78.4 m<sup>2</sup>/s<sup>2</sup> at y = 9.9 m location. At y=9 m location, there are four regions along the water depth where TKE are much higher and form several closed concentric circles. Among these regions, the largest one is near the water surface although the water depth at this location should be very small, and the highest value of TKE is 562.5 m<sup>2</sup>/s<sup>2</sup>. At the centers for the other three areas, the TKE values are around 200 m<sup>2</sup>/s<sup>2</sup>. This phenomenon is in good agreement with the results reported by Curran (Curran and Tan 2014). In their research, such circles were named as recirculation cells, and the calculated TKE revealed that the clusters increased turbulence and induced the formation of recirculation cells. Similar to the y=5 m location, y=9 m location is also a boundary of the vegetation block, while the difference is that the TKE values are much higher than that of 5 m location. As reported in previous studies, the flow velocity varies rapidly at the interfaces of vegetation block in open channel and therefore momentum exchange, especially for the end part of vegetation section (Nikora et al. 2013). Much higher TKE in this section demonstrates higher momentum exchange and turbulence variation, the erosive effects on the bank can therefore be amplified due to the higher turbulence intensity (McBride et al. 2007). Along with the decrease of the vegetal blocking effects, the probability soil erosion should be higher as the flow velocity increases in this section.

At last, after y = 9.9 m location, the TKE values decrease toward the level of low-value zone.

The results above illustrate that the TKE transportation is much higher at the exit of the vegetation section, and may promote the potential soil erosion risk at this area.

In the section *y*=8.9–9.5 m, the TKE values are relatively low and distributed stably.

After *y*=9.5 m location, an interesting phenomenon can be found is that the variation of TKE increases slightly. That can be explained by the velocity mixing when the vegetation array disappears. As mentioned above, the velocity diference between the vegetated and unvegetated side will decrease rapidly after the partly vegetated reach which makes the water fow mix dramatic. The mixing of fow velocity brings higher momentum exchange and therefore higher turbulence. This phenomenon suggests that longer area after the vegetation section should be noticed comparing with the whole vegetated pattern if the partly vegetated pattern is used for the river restoration.

# **3.3 Vegetation Patterns with Velocity Distribution**

The distributions of flow velocity along the vegetated banks of vegetated and partly vegetated patterns are plotted in Fig. [8](#page-7-0) to compare the efects of vegetation patterns. As shown in the fgure, in the before part and fore part of vegetation section (before *y*=8 m location), the fow velocities for vegetated pattern are lower compared with the partly vegetated pattern, and higher for the remaining part which is 1 m before and after the exit of vegetation block. Although the lower fow velocities in the forepart may be benefcial to the soil erosion control, the higher velocities around the exit of vegetation section are undesirable. In comparison with vegetated pattern, the variation of fow velocity for the partly vegetated channel is more stable, not too low at the entrance and not too high at the exit of vegetation section. Comprehensively, partly vegetation pattern may be more suitable for vegetation recovery in rivers, according to the present experimental conditions.

# <span id="page-7-0"></span>**3.4 Vegetation Patterns with Average TKE**

As recognized by researchers in this feld, it is necessary to average the measurements taken in the vegetated channel on space scale to obtain meaningful observations (López and García [2001\)](#page-10-10). In this research, the depth-averaged TKE for each measurement point is calculated and plotted in Fig. [9](#page-8-0) to quantitatively investigate the variation of TKE along the vegetated banks afected by two diferent vegetation patterns.

As shown in Fig. [9](#page-8-0), the curves of average TKE for two different vegetated banks seem unchanged or changed slightly before *y*=6 m location. Same as the above results obtained for the vegetated banks of vegetated and partly vegetated patterns, the average TKE begin to increase after *y*=6 m location and reach their maximum values at *y*=8 m and *y*=9 m locations, respectively. From the perspective of comparison, the values of average TKE for the bank of vegetated channel are mostly higher than that of the vegetated bank of partly vegetated channel. To some extent, this may indicate that the vegetation planting is not the more the better in the engineering practices of river ecological restoration. The planting pattern is also an important factor afecting the efects of the restoration.

Based on the results above, a further quantitative analysis is performed with OriginPro 9.0 in expecting to fnd a suitable method to describe and predict the variations of the average TKE along diferent banks. Firstly, it can be obviously observed from the fgure that both the variations of the average TKE along the vegetated banks under vegetated and partly vegetated patterns present single peak forms. Secondly, the functional forms as well as the ftting results are compared between diferent functions to fnd the best one. Thirdly, the relevant parameters as well as the coefcients

The results demonstrate that the variations of TKE along the vegetated banks of vegetated and partly vegetated patterns can be expressed with Eq. (7) which is an amplitude version of Gaussian peak function. In this function, TKE<sub>0</sub> is the intercept value of each fitting curve,  $y_c$  represents the location of the peak where TKE is the highest, and the parameters a and b are constant coefficients.

TKE = TKE<sub>0</sub> + 
$$a * \exp(-(y - y_c)^2/2b^2)$$
 (7)

In the present research, the parameters in the function are TKE<sub>0</sub> = 36.94, 37.69,  $y_c$  = 8.85, 7.79, a = 247.21, 71.54 and b = 0.53, 0.57 corresponding to the vegetated banks of vegetated and partly vegetated patterns. (8), (9), and (10), respectively.

$$R^{2} = 1 - \sum_{j=1}^{m} (TKE_{j} - \widehat{TKE}_{j})^{2} / \sum_{j=1}^{m} (\widehat{TKE}_{j} - \overline{TKE})^{2}$$
 (8)

$$RMSE = \sqrt{(1/m) * \sum_{j=1}^{m} (TKE_j - \widehat{TKE_j})^2}$$
 (9)

BIAS = 
$$(1/m) * \sum_{j=1}^{m} (\widehat{TKE}_j - TKE_j)$$
 (10)

where m is the number of measurements,  $TKE_j$  and  $\widehat{TKE_j}$  are the jth measured and predicted turbulent kinetic energies, respectively, and  $\overline{TKE}$  is the mean TKE for all the measurements.

<span id="page-8-1"></span>For the fittings with Eq. (7), the values of RMSE are 9.95 and 3.96 corresponding to the vegetated banks of P1 and P2, while the BIAS values are  $1.00 \times 10^{-4}$  and  $-4.90 \times 10^{-4}$ , respectively. Based on the comprehensive considerations above, it can be concluded that these two fitting results show remarkable performance, which means that the fitting results demonstrate relatively high precisions to some extent. As can be found, the values of the parameter  $y_c$  where TKE is highest demonstrate agreement with the analysis above. For the coefficient b, the values are around 0.55 for the vegetated banks, this illustrates that the values for coefficient b may be an approximate value under a determined hydraulic condition. As such, Eq. (7) may be used to predict the TKE values along the vegetated banks for the similar vegetated and hydraulic conditions, if it can be verified and calibrated with more experimental results under different flow discharges, channel slopes, etc.

For a certain condition, a dimensionless equation should be more conducive. For the relative turbulent kinetic energy  $TKE_r$ , Eq. (11), which is a dimensionless form of Eq. (7), is utilized for the nonlinear fitting. That is

$$TKE_r = TKE_{r0} + a' * \exp(-(y - y_c)^2 / 2b'^2)$$
 (11)

It can be found that the values for  $y_c$  and b' are same as that of Eq. (7). In addition, the values of p and  $R^2$  of these fitting lines are also the same as those of the fittings with Eq. # **4 Conclusions**

For the vegetated banks under vegetated and partly vegetated patterns, the distributions of fow velocity and TKE are experimentally investigated in a laboratory open channel. Natural reed stems are used to simulate the rigid unsubmerged vegetation in the experiments. The following conclusions can be obtained:

- 1. The distributions of fow velocity and TKE can be signifcantly altered by the vegetation patterns in open channel. For the vegetated banks of vegetated and partly vegetated patterns, similar variation trends were observed for fow velocity although the variation for partly vegetated pattern was found gentler than that of vegetated pattern. Flow velocities are reduced before vegetation section where siltation may be induced, and then increased until the maximum value at the exit of vegetation section, where erosion may be induced.
- 2. It can be found that TKE is relatively lower and stable in the before and fore part of vegetation block. In the rear part of vegetation section, TKE is forced to increase rapidly and reach their maximum values near the exit of vegetation block. At the maximum location for TKE, a lot of closed concentric circles named recirculation cells are observed in the contour map. In addition, the maximum TKE for partly vegetated pattern which may induce larger potential soil erosion is moved forward 1 m compared with the vegetated pattern.
- 3. An empirical function TKE = TKE0 + *a* ∗ exp(−(*y* −*yc*) <sup>2</sup>∕2*b*<sup>2</sup>) is found to describe the variations of the average TKE along the fow direction, with which the TKE values may be predicted. The coefcients of determination are calculated as 0.995 (*p*=0.0001) and 0.691 (*p*=0.0127) corresponding to the vegetated banks under vegetated and partly vegetated patterns.
- 4. Vegetation pattern should be considered an important factor in the restoration of ecological waterways. In the present research, partly vegetated pattern is found better due to the gentler distributed fow velocity and lower TKE at the rear part of vegetation section.

In actual situations, more interactive factors afecting the efects of vegetation patterns on water fow should be considered, and it will be interesting and necessary to further consider the vegetation patterns and hydraulic conditions together. With the fndings of the research, the comprehensive understanding on the efects of vegetation patterns on turbulence and erosion may be improved, and some inspirations to the practices of river ecological restoration may be provided.
