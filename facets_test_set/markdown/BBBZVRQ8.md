#### ABSTRACT

Northern peatlands store approximately one third of the global soil carbon (C) stocks. Peat extraction for horticulture changes C fluxes turning these soils from sinks into large sources of C emitted to the atmosphere as greenhouse gases. Restoring hydrological conditions may not be enough for returning the C sink function of these ecosystems and a recovery of the original Sphagnum-dominated vegetation cover could be necessary. Here we tested the effects of the moss layer-transfer technique (MLTT) on the CO2 and CH4 fluxes three years after restoration of a minerotrophic cutover peatland in Quebec, Canada. Gas emissions were compared to nearby undisturbed plots, and plots with restored hydrology but without vegetation transfer. The main drivers for the observed emissions were inferred from the relationships with relevant environmental variables (vegetation cover, water table depth and temperature). Restoration of the extracted minerotrophic fen through the MLTT produced significant changes in the exchange of CO<sub>2</sub>, bringing these fluxes closer to undisturbed plots than plots with restored hydrology only, which remained C sources throughout the study. Methane emissions were generally low across all the restored plots and far from natural levels, with only a slight increase at the MLTT restored plots observed during the third year. Even though the MLTT failed to recover the original bryophytedominated vegetation, the observed changes in the emissions of CO2 and CH4 at the restored plots were strongly associated with changes in vegetation cover (i.e. increase in vascular plants), supporting the use of this restoration technique to recover the C sink function of harvested minerotrophic peatlands.

#### 1. Introduction

Peatland ecosystems constitute one of the largest terrestrial sinks of soil carbon (C) and play a key role in climate regulation with soil carbon stores of approximately 550 Pg of C globally, and over 400 Pg in northern peatlands (Loisel et al., 2014), equivalent to one third of the total terrestrial soil C stocks (Frolking and Roulet, 2007). In these ecosystems storage of C is possible because of the combination of ecohydrological factors such as the prevalence of anoxic waterlogged conditions, and the presence of peat-accumulating plant species, that slow down decomposition rates. As a result, the dominant process driving C accumulation is the primary production by peat-accumulating vegetation, while C outputs through decomposition remain lower (Vitt, 2006).

Peat extraction for horticulture dramatically changes ecohydrological conditions having a direct impact on C fluxes. In Canada,

most peat is extracted using the vacuum-harvesting technique, where the peatland is first drained through the construction of ditches, and vegetation is removed for the subsequent extraction of the peat using a tractor-pulled vacuum. Vegetation removal virtually eliminates the C inputs, and aeration of the peat profile after drainage increases decomposition rates, which results in the annual release of massive amounts of  $CO_2$  (300–400 g C m<sup>-2</sup>; Waddington and McNeil 2002). Thus, peat extraction transforms peatlands from sinks into net sources of C, contributing to global warming (Strack and Waddington, 2012; IPCC, 2014).

After extraction, spontaneous revegetation of the peatland might occur, particularly in minerotrophic environments, increasing photosynthetic C uptake and CH $_4$  efflux (Graf and Rochefort, 2009; Mahmood and Strack, 2011) potentially bringing these ecosystems to their original C sequestration function in the long term. Moreover, spontaneous revegetation alone is unlikely to result in plant communities similar to undisturbed, reference peatlands as bryophyte cover remains lower post-extraction [\(Graf and Rochefort, 2008a,b\)](#page-8-2).

Fens are minerotrophic peatlands that receive water from precipitation, surface and/or groundwater, as opposed to ombrotrophic peatlands or bogs that receive only water from precipitation. This leads to differences not only in peat chemistry, as some fens can have a higher pH and cation content, but also in plant composition with a higher abundance of vascular plants and brown mosses in fens [\(Charman,](#page-8-3) [2002; Vitt, 2006](#page-8-3)). Bogs may also be transformed into fens after extraction of the peat and exposure of the underlying minerotrophic peat ([Graf et al., 2012; Bérubé et al., 2017](#page-8-4)).

Even though extensive research on fen restoration has been carried out in Europe, this has little applicability in North America because of the different restoration priorities and challenges [\(Graf and Rochefort,](#page-8-2) [2008a,b; Lamers et al., 2015\)](#page-8-2). The main challenges for restoration in Europe are the scarcity of pristine fens and eutrophication because of agricultural use, which hampers the collection and establishment of the original plant community. So the goal is to achieve a semi-natural fen state that will allow recovery of biodiversity and preservation of certain endangered plant species [\(Ehrenfeld, 2000; Lamers et al., 2015\)](#page-8-5). In North America, where one of the major causes of peatland degradation is peat extraction, the main challenges for restoration are the absence of a seed bank and the recovery of hydrological conditions, while the focus is on the recovery of the ecosystem functions such as C sequestration ([Rochefort et al., 2003\)](#page-9-5). Here, the existence of larger extent of non-degraded fens, allows for the collection of representative original plant material to be used as diaspores and transferred into degraded areas.

Previous fen restoration experiences in North America have prioritized the vascular plant component because of their larger and faster biomass accumulation potential ([Cooper and MacDonald, 2000](#page-8-6)). However, because of their low decomposition rates and plant structure, mosses also play a key role in peat accumulation, structure and hydrological conditions ([Graf and Rochefort, 2009\)](#page-8-1). The moss layertransfer technique (MLTT) approach to peatland restoration [\(Quinty](#page-9-6) [and Rochefort, 2003\)](#page-9-6) aims at recovering the peat accumulation and C sink function of bog ecosystems by re-establishing the original Sphagnum-dominated vegetation cover. This involves the introduction of donor vegetation material from a nearby-undisturbed site, protection of the introduced moss propagules with straw mulch and application of a light dose of phosphate fertilizer ([Rochefort et al., 2003](#page-9-5)). Because of the key role of water availability and peat moisture for Sphagnum growth, reclamation plans include necessarily the restoration of hydrological conditions of the peatland by blocking the drainage ditches to raise the water table [\(Rochefort et al., 2003](#page-9-5)).

The MLTT has been successfully applied in the restoration of ombrotrophic peatlands, or bogs, where it has shown to have significant effects in C fluxes, efficiently shifting them from growing season C sources into sinks shortly after restoration ([Waddington et al., 2010;](#page-9-7) [Strack et al., 2014](#page-9-7)). However, research on the efficiency of this technique for fen restoration is still scarce. While it has been observed that it can be effective in recovering the peat-forming vegetation layer in extracted fens [\(Graf and Rochefort, 2008a,b](#page-8-2)), albeit with low bryophyte cover when applied mechanically [\(Rochefort et al., 2016\)](#page-9-8), it is not yet clear whether this is accompanied by a recovery of the C sink function and what the impact of this restoration technique is on peat gas exchange to the atmosphere. Therefore, the objective of this study was to assess the effects of MLTT restoration on the fluxes of CO2 and CH4 in the short term, three years after restoration of a minerotrophic cutover peatland in Quebec, Canada. Furthermore, we aimed to understand the main environmental controls on these fluxes. The specific hypotheses to be tested were: (i) that fen restoration using MLTT has a significant short-term impact on the  $\rm CO_2$  flux, reducing emissions and increasing photosynthetic uptake from the atmosphere; (ii) restoration increases  $\rm CH_4$  emissions to levels similar to natural conditions; and (iii) changes in  $\rm CO_2$  and  $\rm CH_4$  fluxes can be explained through changes in water table depth and vegetation abundance.

#### 2. Material and methods

#### 2.1. Study site

This study was conducted at the Bic-Saint-Fabien (BSF) peatland (48°18'N, 68°52'W), 25 km away from Rimouski, Quebec, Canada (Fig. 1). The undisturbed portion of the site is a moderately rich fen, with sparse forest cover and an average peat depth of 4.5 m. The natural vegetation is dominated by Eastern White Cedar (Thuja occidentalis) in the forested part, with the moss layer dominated by brown mosses including Campylium stellatum, Drepanocladus spp., and Tomenthypnum nitens. Peat extraction started at this site in 1946 and continued until 2000, when it was abandoned. To date, a total of 22 ha has been extracted. The area extracted was originally a bog, but extraction of surface layers has exposed peat layers indicative of a poor fen (Bérubé et al., 2017). Restoration activities on this area started in 2008 and were completed in fall 2009. The purpose was to create favorable conditions for the development of a moss layer and the recovery of the C sink function. Briefly, restoration included modification of the extracted peatland hydrology through the blocking of the drainage ditches and construction of dykes to retain snowmelt and raise the water table. In addition, moss diaspores mechanically harvested from a nearby moderate-rich riverine fen were spread in a 1:10 ratio (1 ha of collected material over 10 ha of restored area), and subsequently covered with straw mulch (see Rochefort et al., 2016 for more details).

Six sampling plots were established in the restored site (restored) arranged in three groups of two plots each along three original neighboring peat fields. There is a general slope to the peatland and thus these plots were arranged to capture the gradient in hydrologic conditions along this slope. Another set of six sampling plots was established in the nearby natural undisturbed peatland, three of them on hummocks (undisturbed hummock) and three on hollows (undisturbed hollows). Additionally, in order to quantify the effects of vegetation cover after restoration on CO<sub>2</sub> and CH<sub>4</sub> baseline fluxes, four plots where bare peat was exposed were monitored at the restored area (restored bare). Carbon fluxes in the different plots were monitored from early May until late August in 2010 and 2011 and and early May until early September 2012. Fluxes were assessed biweekly in 2010 and weekly in 2011 and 2012.

## 2.2. Assessment of CO2 exchanges

Net ecosystem exchange of CO2 (NEE) was determined at each plot through the closed chamber method. Chambers consisted of a square collar (60 cm  $\times$  60 cm) left in place throughout the study period plus a  $60\,\text{cm}\times60\,\text{cm}\times30\,\text{cm}$  transparent acrylic lid, except at the bare peat site, where chambers consisted of a round (30 cm diameter) collars with a groove for water sealing. Chambers placed on these round collars were 30 cm high. The sleeve of the collars extended 20 cm in soil, which is usually below the rooting zone (Laiho and Finér, 1996). Total chamber height from the soil surface ranged between 48 and 30 cm (depending on the depth of collar insertion) and was used to estimate the exact air volume within each chamber. CO2 concentration in the chamber headspace was monitored at 15 s intervals for 105 s with a portable infrared gas analyzer (EGM-4, PP Systems, Massachusetts, USA). A battery operated fan mixed the headspace during flux measurement. Flux was determined as the linear change in CO2 concentration over time correcting for chamber volume and ambient temperature as recorded with a thermocouple inserted into the chamber. Together with flux measurement, photosynthetically active radiation (PAR) was measured with a quantum sensor connected to the infrared gas analyzer. NEE was measured under a variety of light levels created using shades and ecosystem respiration (ER) determined under an opaque tarp and it is expressed in positive values to indicate  $\rm CO_2$  emissions to the atmosphere. Gross ecosystem photosynthesis (GEP) was calculated as the difference between NEE and ER ad its expressed in negative values to indicate  $\rm CO_2$  influx from the atmosphere into the vegetation. At the bare peat plots, only ER was measured, as no vegetation was present.

## 2.3. Assessment of CH<sub>4</sub> fluxes

Methane fluxes were assessed in the same collars by using an opaque  $60\,\mathrm{cm}\times60\,\mathrm{cm}\times30\,\mathrm{cm}$  chamber equipped with a battery-operated fan to mix the headspace air. Headspace gas samples were collected at regular time intervals (7, 15, 25 and 35 min) after chamber deployment onto the collars through a three-way valve sampling port. Samples (20 mL) were stored in 12 mL pre-evacuated Exetainers (Labco Ltd., UK), and further analyzed for  $\mathrm{CH_4}$  concentration by using a gas chromatograph equipped with flame ionization detector (Varian 3800, Varian Canada).

Chamber gas concentrations were converted to mass per volume units assuming ideal gas relations using chamber air temperature values. Fluxes (mg  $CH_4$  m $^{-2}$  d $^{-1}$ ) were calculated from the linear change in chamber  $CH_4$  concentration over time, taking into account chamber volume and soil surface area. Instantaneous fluxes were calculated on a per minute basis and then extrapolated to daily fluxes, assuming that  $CH_4$  flux at the time of sampling was representative of daily average flux. If the pattern of concentration did not consistently increase or decrease over time or jumped suddenly indicating potential ebullition, the flux value was not used except in cases where the slope was not significantly different from zero indicating a non-detectable, low flux. Other than these low fluxes, values were accepted if the  $R^2$  values of the linear regression between sampling time and sample concentration were larger than 0.75. This resulted in loss of 13, 18 and 14% of the data in 2010, 2011 and 2012 respectively.

## 2.5. Data analyses

All statistical analyses were carried out with SPSS v.23.0 and SAS (SAS Institute, Cary, NC, USA) software programs. Differences in average CH<sub>4</sub> flux, GEP, NEE and ER between treatments (undisturbed hummocks, undisturbed hollows, restored and restored bare) were evaluated using linear mixed models. The interaction between 'plot' and 'DOY' was entered in the model as random factor whereas 'treatment', 'year' and the interaction between the two were entered as fixed factors.

Environmental controls (vegetation volume, soil temperature and water table depth) on these variables were also evaluated using Pearson correlations. ## 3.2. Carbon dioxide fluxes

Mean growing season GEPmax was significantly greater (more negative values) in undisturbed than in restored plots, with more uptake measured in hollows than in hummocks (Treatment: F = 72.73, p < 0.001; [Fig. 4\)](#page-6-0). In addition, undisturbed plots showed larger GEPmax values in 2010 as compared to 2011 and 2012, suggesting large interannual variability at these plots. A different trend was observed in restored plots, where GEPmax increased significantly in 2012 as compared to 2010 (Treatment\*Year: F = 7.69, p < 0.001; [Fig. 4](#page-6-0)). As no plants were present at the restored bare plots GEP was assumed to be 0.

Ecosystem respiration (ER) was significantly greater on undisturbed hummocks and hollows, followed by restored moss-transfer and bare plots (Treatment: F = 86.44, p < 0.001; [Fig. 4\)](#page-6-0). Respiration rates decreased over the three years of study in undisturbed and restored bare plots, while they increased slightly in the moss-transfer restored plots (Treatment\*Year: F = 13.83, p < 0.001, [Fig. 4\)](#page-6-0).

#### 3.3. Methane flux

We observed large variability in the  $CH_4$  flux, both between treatments and years (Treatment\*Year: F=14.86, p<0.001). Undisturbed hollows were the largest sources of  $CH_4$  particularly in 2010, although flux decreased significantly in the following years. Methane flux was very low in undisturbed hummocks and restored plots, with negative values in some of the years indicating  $CH_4$  consumption (Fig. 5). Restored MLTT plots increased their  $CH_4$  fluxes slightly over the three years, although no significant differences were detected. Throughout treatments and years  $CH_4$  flux was significantly and positively correlated with vegetation volume (r=0.22, p<0.001). Otherwise, no significant correlations were found with water table depth (r=0.03,

p=0.45) or soil temperature at 5 or 20 cm depth ( $r=0.02,\,p=0.62$  and  $r=0.03,\,p=0.41$ , respectively).

Undisturbed hollows emitted between 66 and  $859 \,\mathrm{mg}$  CH<sub>4</sub>-C m<sup>-2</sup> based on seasonal estimates, whereas undisturbed hummocks and restored bare plots had very small fluxes either being a source or a sink of CH<sub>4</sub> depending on the year (Table 3). Restored MLTT plots, consumed relatively large amounts of CH<sub>4</sub> one year after restoration, and became a small source of CH<sub>4</sub> in the years following (Table 3).

#### 4. Discussion

Carbon accumulation in northern peatland ecosystems is mostly driven by low decomposition rates due to the prevalence of water-logged and anoxic conditions (Limpens et al., 2008). However, results of this study show that restoration of hydrological conditions does not seem to be enough for the recovery of the C sink function in extracted peatlands, highlighting the need for an active restoration of the vegetation cover to increase C sequestration, at least in the short term. Here we observed that, while bare plots were sources of C consistently over the three years of the study, restoration of the extracted minerotrophic

<span id="page-7-1"></span>![](_page_7_Figure_12.jpeg)

Fig. 5. Average methane flux ( $\pm$  s.e.) recorded at the undisturbed hummocks and hollows, restored-bare and restored plots where MLTT was applied, in the three years of the study.

fen through the MLTT produced significant changes in the exchange of CO2, bringing these fluxes closer to natural conditions than plots with restored peatland hydrology but no revegetation. These changes were observable already three years after the restoration was put in place. At the restored MLTT plots, changes in CO2 were clearly correlated to the increase in vegetation cover, whereas bare plots remained sources of CO2 consistently through the three years of the study. Environmental conditions such as water table depth, soil temperature and PAR, had also a strong influence on C fluxes, explaining the variability observed between years, particularly at the undisturbed site. Similar results have been observed across other northern peatlands in Canada where the same restoration technique was applied [\(Strack et al., 2016\)](#page-9-14).

Methane emissions were generally low across all the restored plots and far from natural levels, with only a slight increase at the MLTT restored plots observed during the third year. In previous studies increases in CH4 emissions at other restoration sites using the MLTT, have shown to be controlled mainly by WT depth (i.e., hydrological conditions, [Strack et al. 2016\)](#page-9-14). Yet, in our study, CH4 fluxes were more strongly associated to plant cover, with the MLTT restored plots, becoming net sources three years after restoration, when vegetation cover was the highest. Due to the more complex biogeochemical properties of the minerotrophic peat layers as compared to bog peat [\(Zoltai and Vitt,](#page-9-15) [1995\)](#page-9-15), usually vascular plants dominate in early stages of secondary succession after restoration, whereas bryophyte establishment is minor ([Mahmood and Strack 2011; Rochefort et al., 2016](#page-9-4)). The presence of vascular plants has shown to increase CH4 production in anoxic layers of the peat, most likely through the supply of fresh C substrates either through litter deposition or root exudation ([Mahmood and Strack,](#page-9-4) [2011, Bergman et al., 2000, Strack et al., 2017](#page-9-4)). In addition, aerenchymatous plant species may also increase CH4 fluxes by venting the CH4 produced in anoxic peat layers (e.g., [Shannon and White, 1994](#page-9-16)).

It is unclear whether secondary succession will lead the recovery of the original bryophyte-dominated vegetation, even with active restoration ([Jones et al., 2018\)](#page-9-17). One of the main issues to assess the success of restoration in the recovery of the original plant community is the scarcity in long term studies on secondary succession of plant communities in degraded peatlands. Alternatively, restoration success can be assessed through the recovery of ecosystem functions such as biogeochemical cycles [\(Gorham and Rochefort, 2003](#page-8-10)). Changes in C fluxes observed in this study showed a shift towards natural-undisturbed plots, showing the success of the MLTT technique. As the present study focused on only warm season fluxes, annual C balance of the restored plots was not determined. As non-growing season fluxes are not negligible ([Treat et al., 2018\)](#page-9-18), future studies should include wintertime C exchange measurements in restored peatlands to quantify annual fluxes and improve our understanding of C biogeochemistry in cold soils.

The undisturbed sites, including hummocks and hollows, acted as net sources of C in 2010 and 2012, and were weak sinks in 2011, indicating the existence of interannual variability in natural background emission levels from peatlands (e.g., [Roulet et al., 2007; Adkinson et al.,](#page-9-19) [2011\)](#page-9-19). Most of the variability in C fluxes was associated to changes in ecosystem respiration and CH4 efflux, both larger in 2010. In addition, clear differences in ecological functionality and biogeochemical cycling were found between the two microtopographical features studied in the undisturbed site. In the case of hummocks, they were consistently sources of CO2 resulting in a positive C balance, in spite of their role as a sink of CH4. Undisturbed hollows were also sources of C in 2010, emitting large amounts of both CO2 and CH4. Across microtopographic forms, high respiration rates were responsible for the weak C sequestration capacity of the natural site. These high respiration rates are most likely associated to the presence of trees in the vicinity of the measurement plots [\(Epron et al., 1999; Hanson et al., 2000](#page-8-11)). Tree biomass at this site was 8418 g m−<sup>2</sup> and, while our plot-scale fluxes most likely include tree root respiration, they fail to include the GEP of the overstorey vegetation therefore underestimating the C sequestration of the whole ecosystem [\(Strack et al., 2016\)](#page-9-14).

## 5. Conclusion

Restoration of the hydrological conditions did not reduce the C emissions at this minerotrophic peatland at sites that remained unvegetated. The moss layer transfer technique, even though it failed to recover the original bryophyte-dominated vegetation, produced a significant increase in the cover of vascular plants. This increase in vegetation cover caused significant changes in the C fluxes (CO2 and CH4) as compared to bare plots, bringing them closer to natural conditions than just restoring the peatland hydrology. More research is needed on the potential of this technique, particularly when donor material is collected and spread mechanically for the recovery of the native bryophyte-dominated vegetation cover found in fens with the associated C cycling, or the effects of alternative, secondary succession pathways on ecosystem-level C fluxes.
