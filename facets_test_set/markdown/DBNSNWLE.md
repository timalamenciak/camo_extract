## Abstract

The purpose of this report is to provide practising silviculturists in north-central British Columbia with critical knowledge required 10 improve regeneration success ratcs in subalpine forests. High elevation forestry in this region is relatively new. An insufficient understanding of the environmental conditions in subalpine forests and the changes in those conditions when forests arc harvested has frequently contributed to problems in regeneration. This report highlights lhe current knowledge of these conditions. their effects on tree survival and growth, some of lhe rorest practises that can be used to modify adverse conditions, and the silvieal characteristics of the affected tree species. Specific environmental conditions that are discussed include air temperature, soil temperature, and impacts of competing vegetation and snow. Forest practices such as canopy removal, site preparation, planting and vegetation management arc discussed.

#### 1.0 INTRODUCTION

High-elevation forestry in north-central British Columbia is relatively new. Until the mid 1980s. logging in subalpine stands in the Prince George. #### 2.0 THE ESSF ZONE

The biogeoclimatic ecosystem classification system used in British Columbia provides an excellent basis for delineating areas within which there are similar management-related environmental concerns. The province is divided into 14 biogeoclimatic zones, each zone being a large geographic area with a broadly homogenous macroclimate (PojaT ct al. 1991). High elevation foresls within much of the interior of British Columbia lie within the Engelmann Spruce - Subalpine Fir (ESSF) biogeoclimatic zone (Fig. I). In north-central British Columbia, this zone occurs above the Sub-boreal Spruce (SBS). #### 3.1.1 Soil Temperature3

Soil temperature has dramatic effects on transpiration and photosynthesis (Fig. 4). Low soil temperatures reduce water flow through the soil-plant-air continuum by limiting conductance across root cell membranes and by increasing water viscosity (Goldstein et al. 1985; Billings 1973; Carter et al. 1988). Water deficits within the plant may then result, even where soil moisture is not limiting. This in turn promotes stomatal closing, reduces gaseous exchange, and reduces photosynthesis. In the spring, plant moisture deficits can limit shoot and needle expansion, thus reducing the photosynthetic surface of the plant for the entire growing season. Later in the season, temperature induced moisture deficits may affect the onset and depth of dormancy, potentially leading to increased damage from winter frosts (see Section 3.1.2).

Soil temperature also affects root and stem growth (Stathers et al. 1990). Minimum soil temperatures for root growth range from 0 to 7°C, and the optimum temperature ranges from 10 to 25°C depending on tree species (Sutton 1991). Figure 5 shows the impact of soil temperature on root and stem growth for Soots pine and Norway spruce. The behavior of Norway spruce is similar to what has been observed or implied for white and Engelmann spruce (Endean 1972; Heninger and White 1974; Coates et al. 1991; Brand and Janas 1988). Engelmann spruce root growth is thought to dramatically decline below 8-10°C (Coates et al. 1994).

Excessively high soil surface temperatures have also been reported to cause problems, resulting in reduced growth or seedling mortality (Heninger and White 1974; Cochran 1969; Hungerford 1979). Such effects are most evident on slopes with south aspects, dry soils, and where surface materials have a low thermal conductivity (i.e., a dry duff layer) or an extremely low albedo (i.e., a freshly burned site). In north-central British Columbia, where a lower sun angle and generally moister and cooler conditions occur than at more southerly latitudes, high temperatures do not appear to be a problem. Here, the most successful plantations in terms of both survival and early growth occur on well drained soils on south aspects, i.e., on those soils most likely to have the

#### 3.1.2 Air Temperature<sup>5</sup>

Low but non-freezing air temperatures have effects similar to those of soil temperatures on translocation, photosynthesis and growth. Such low air temperatures normally only occur consistently early in the growing season when soil temperatures are also very limiting, and the relative effects of the two factors are difficult to distinguish (Smith et al. 1987). However, daily average air temperatures increase faster through spring and summer than do soil temperatures, leaving soil temperature as the main growth limiting factor throughout most of the growing season.

More damaging than non-freezing temperatures are frosts. Sub-zero air temperatures can have a range of impacts on seedlings, from growth losses to mortality. While light growing-season frosts in the range of 0 to -4°C have been observed to cause slight to moderate and reversible reductions in

photosynthesis for Engelmann spruce and other species, usually without visible symptoms, temperatures below approximately -4°C cause tissue damage, resulting in greater and longer lasting photosynthetic losses or in severe cases mortality (DeLucia and Smith 1987; Örlander et al. 1990). Such a relationship between photosynthesis and air temperature the previous night is illustrated in Figure 6, where the effect of frost on photosynthesis is further compounded by cold soil temperatures.

The physiological condition of seedlings is an important factor in susceptibility to frost damage. Lundmark and Hällgren (1987), for example, found that frost susceptibility was greater for seedlings of Norway spruce grown in full sunlight than for those that were partially shaded. Nienstaedt (1985) found that the fastest growing white spruce seedlings in his trial were the most susceptible. Both papers suggest that physiologic adaptations for maximizing growth will adversely affect resistance to tissue freezing. Frost damage also occurs more frequently in the year of planting than in subsequent growing seasons (Coates et al. 1994). This is thought to be the result of an imperfect acclimation of the seedlings to the field environment and has often been blamed for plantation failures.

Summer frost<sup>6</sup> is one of the most critical factors affecting early survival and growth of tree seedlings throughout the interior of British Columbia (Stathers 1989; Steen et al. 1990). In the ESSF zone, cool air and soil temperatures result in a higher frost risk than in other zones. Steen et al. (1990) list the ESSF subzones of the Cariboo Forest Region within the two highest risk groups out of eight. There is some indication, however, that the risk of frost in the wetter ESSF subzones prevalent in the north central interior of British Columbia may be lower than in the drier subzones which occur further south (P. Sanborn<sup>7</sup> unpub. data; R. Butson<sup>8</sup> unpub. data; pers comm. A. Vyse<sup>9</sup>), presumably due to a higher incidence of cloud cover.

There are two causes of frost: radiative cooling which occurs in situ, and advection or down-slope movement of cold air (Stathers 1989). Radiative cooling typically occurs on clear nights when energy from the ground is radiated back to the cold night sky. The risk of this type of frost increases with elevation. Advective frosts, by contrast, occur after air is cooled either by radiative cooling or by conductive cooling, typically over a snow field or glacier. Advective frosts occur on a wide range of scales, from the micro (such as small pockets of cold air trapped behind a log or within a trench on a clearcut) through the meso (such as ponds of cold air dammed up behind the down slope edges of clearcuts or in meso scale depressions) up to the macro (such as large frost "lakes" which settle into and occupy the entire extent of large valleys). On the micro and meso scales, the risk of advective frosts in the ESSF will be similar or slightly higher (due to increased cooling) than at lower

elevations, while at the macro scale the risk may be lower as cold air bodies drain down out of the mountains into major valleys. Either type of frost can occur in any month during the growing season in the ESSF zone. A site-specific methodology for assessing frost risk has been developed for the Cariboo Forest Region (Steen et al. 1990).

The risk of damage to seedlings is greatest when seedlings are small (less than 1 m). Damaging radiative frosts are more likely near the ground (Figure 7). As seedlings grow, a higher proportion of the photosynthetic surface enters into air strata further from the ground where the risk of frost damage is less. This is also important for micro and meso scale advective frosts, where seedlings may eventually grow out of the zone of frost influence. In larger frost collecting areas, however, the depth of the frost layer may exceed the heights of mature trees.

#### 3.3 Impacts of Snow

Snow packs have a large effect on thermal and moisture regimes. late melting snow packs delay the start of the growing season by inhibiting soil wanning, and in many cases by providing excessive amounts of soil moisture which. through its high volumetric heat capacity, inhibits soil warming. Alpine snow packs will also contribute to subalpinc thermal regimes by cooling local air masses, resulting in colder climatic conditions and in some cases advective frosts.

Snow also causes damage to seedlings. Leaphan et al. (1972) identified six classes of damage12• the worst of which is stem failures. Snow damage is often broken into the categories of "snow press" and "snow creep". Snow press is a phenomenon which normally occurs in conjunction with overtopping vegetation, and results in seedlings being crushed to the ground (Figure 8a). Brush species which commonly contribute to snow press in north-central British Columbia include fireweed, blucjoint reedgrass, and slide alder. Damaged secdlings are often found pinned under mats of dead vegetation, and may trail along the ground for most of their stem length.

Snow creep occurs when all or a portion of the snow pack moves down the slope (Figure 8b). In some areas, particularly in steep mountainous terrain. snow creep may be one of the largest contributors to plantation mortality. One contributing factor to this type of damage is the surface character of the site. Snow moves easiest over steep, smooth surfaces, and glides particularly well over grass swards (Scagel et al. 1989) or fireweed mats. Snow may also slide along sheer planes, which are caused by the formation of layers within the pack of different densities and crystal structures. This type of movement is particularly prevalent in Skeena and Bulkley drainages of the Prince Rupert Forest Region, whcre alternating surges of dry cold interior air masses and warm wet coastal air masses contribute to the formation of the appropriate snow conditions. The risk of snow creep also increases with slope and on more southerly aspects.

Snow is also generally believed to have a large effect on the pattern of stems in matijfC forests near timberline. #### 3.4 Environmental Conditions at Timberline

It is useful to understand the environmental conditions that cause the formation of timberlines and the changes in stand structures near timberlines, as they may represent extremes of conditions that will affect forest plantations. particularly in the higher reaches of the ESSF zone. Several theories for the location of limberlines have been proposed. essentially all of which relate to temperature. Goldstein et al. (1985) suggested that white spruce tree lines in Alaska are at least partially related to the impacts of cold soils on photosynthesis and growth, while Becwar et al. (1981) found that a -40"C threshold for winter stem tissue damage to many conifer species corresponded to the minimum environmental temperature predicted for many Colorado timberlines. A more comprehensive theory, however, is based on a phenomenon known as frost-drought (Hadley and Smith 1983; Ogilvie 1976).

Frost-drought occurs in winter, and results in the desiccation of foliage with insufficient resistance to water vapor loss through the cuticle. Cuticle fonnation may be retarded by summer frost or cold soils. or fonnation of the cuticle may be halted due to the short growing season. Cuticle damage as the result of abrasion by wind-borne snow or dust may also be a factor. During winter. when trees have essentially no ability to translocate water, there is no way to replace moisture lost through the cuticle, and tissue death may result.

A major factor in the occurrence of frost-drought is wind. Wind exposure strongly affected needle water relations and mortality in *Piua enge/manii* and *Abies lasiocarpa* krummholtz in winter in Wyoming (Hadley and Smith 1983). Water content, xylem pressure potential and needle viability were all lower on the windward needles than on leeward ones. Similar wind effccts may occur on clearcuts near timberline. As exposure to dry cold air and wind is required for this phenomenon. there will be no frost droughlon trees below the snow pack.

#### 5.2 Site Preparation

Site preparation methods in the ESSF are similar those used in other biogeoclimatic zones of the Province. Options for site preparation and their methodologies are well described in other publications (Orlander et al. 1990; Von der Gonna 1992; Haeussler 1991; Lavender et al. 1990).

Soils are warmed mainly by the absorption of direct solar radiation. The main factors affecting absorption are vegetation, duff cover, and slash. which in turn are affected by site preparation treatments. Vegetation layers shade the soil (see section 5.3), which limits the amount of energy available for absorption. Duff layers and slash act as insulating blankets, which inhibit mov'ement of energy into the soil profile. Vegetation, slash and duff cover can be significantly reduced by appropriate site preparation. with corresponding heat sum increases, either singly or in combination, of up to 100% (Stathers and Spiltlehouse 1990).

Soil temperature is fUrlher affected by the creation of raised microsites. This type of mechanical site preparation increases the absorptive surface area

of exposed mineral soil, improves the slope of the absorptive surface relative to the incipient radiation, and decreases soil moisture by providing improved drainage. The first two factors will increase absorption of solar energy, while the last will lower the heat capacity of the soil and thus provide larger temperature increases per unit of added energy. Macadam (1991) found the greatest early season soil temperatures increases in inverted mounds, substantially less warming with patch scalping on fresh sites, and essentially no warming from patch scalping on moist and wet sites. Bassman (1989), in a high elevation (1550 m) study near Williams Lake, found significantly greater root and diameter growth resulting from the higher soil temperatures in mounds compared to scarified patches and controls, but found that height growth was not increased until the third growing season when the roots were able to access greater water reserves outside of the·mounds 17• Lctchford and Hawkins (1994), reporting on lO-year results of trials established in the Bowron Valley, found that trees on mounded microsites did not show consistently better results than controls until after seven growing seasons, at which time both height and stem diameter benefits could be observed. In general, raised microsites will provide the highest heat sums of all site preparation treatments, with mounding and plowing, which is seldom applicable in the mountainous terrain and stony soils of the ESSF zone, providing the greatest benefits.

The orientation of raised sites is also important: Drlander et al. (1990) noted a significant increase in hcat sums (degree days) on mounds in which the mound/trench interface had a southern exposure as opposed to a northern one. Another study (M. OsberglS unpub. datal9) found higher heat sums on the south aspect of disc trench furrows as compared to the north aspect, which had lower heat sums than even the untreated control.

Prescribed burning also provides conditions which enhance soil warming beyond that provided by vegetation and duff reduction. The blackened surface of a burned area increases absorption of incoming radiation, which increases surface temperature up to 10·C (Stathers and Spittlehouse 1990). The conductance of this heat down into the soil profile, however, is dependent on a sufficiently reduced duff layer.

The benefits of site preparation relative to frost are closely related to soil temperature. Tree seedlings are protected from frost at night by long-wave radiation and conductive heating of the air from the wann soil (Stathers 1989). Many of the same factors which enhance soil warming will help with frost avoidance, most particularly the exposure of mineral soil. Re-radiation of soil heat for seedling protection has been most effective for seedlings planted in the bottom of trenches where the seedling is exposed to a large radiating surface area, but this planting position will seldom be suitable in the ESSF as this position will in most cases be excessively wet and cold. Frost protection is also afforded by the raised micro-site of large mounds, which may partially lift seedlings out of shallow advective frost ponds near the ground (Steen et al. 1990).

Options for site preparation in many areas in the ESSF will be limited by terrain and site conditions. Steep slopes will frequently preclude the use of mechanical methods, and conditions dry enough for broadcast burning may be rare, particularly on wetter sites and on north aspects. Such limitations and the risks of not achieving the desired environmental modifications will decrease the options available for silviculture prescriptions.

**In** a survey of 65 sites in the ESSFdk subzone within the Nelson Forest Region, , with the exception of submesic sites, survival and growth generally increased with increased levels of duff reduction and mineral soil exposure (Butt 1990). Such high impacts appeared critical on the wettest sites. This agrees with personal observations made during a tour of subalpine forestry operations in the summer of 1992. Site preparation, particularly at the higher levels of duff reduction and mineral soil exposure, will contribute to both soil warming and frost protection during much of the growing season. One must be cautious, however, to prevent excessive or inappropriate disturbance which may lead 10 increases in bulk density and nutrient depletion (Ballard 1988). #### 5.3 Planting

Artificial regeneration is recommended over natural regeneration wherever feasible in the ESSF Zone (Butt and Vyse 1992). While several cases of successful natural regeneration were noted during the 1992 field tours, most notably on a series of blocks harvested just prior to an exceptional seed year in 1978, this method is more often unreliable, as documented by Alexander (1969, 1984) in Colorado and Butt (1990) in southern British Columbia<sup>20</sup>. Contributing factors include poor seed crops (pers. comm. J Revel<sup>21</sup> 1993), low germination rates — particularly on undisturbed soils, and poor survival (Cui and Smith 1991) - particularly on exposed

microsites. Stock production and planting practices for artificial regeneration of Engelmann spruce and lodgepole pine are well established and reasonably successful, while artificial regeneration using subalpine fir is relatively new and unproven. Given suitable planting stock, however, the principles for planting subalpine fir should be similar to those for spruce.

Many plantation failures in the ESSF have been attributed to poor adaptation of seedlings to their environment in the first field season. This results from newly planted trees being out of synchronization with the timing of the growing season. Spring planting in the ESSF must be delayed until the late snow packs have cleared and soils have begun to warm, usually in late June or even early July. Seedlings are then unable to achieve adequate frost hardiness before damaging frosts occur in mid to late August.

late spring planting in the ESSF zone also necessitates cold storage periods of up to 8 months, increases risk of infection by damaging molds (Hocking and Nyland 1971) and reduces carbohydrate reserves (Hocking and Nyland 1971; Revel et al 1990), which in tum typically reduces root growth capacity - particularly at low soil temperatures (Camm and Harper 1991). When finally planted. both root growth and water uptake are reduced by cold soils while tops are actively respiring, and this further reduces carbohydrate reserves and stresses the plant.

Many operational foresters have now at least partially abandoned the practice of spring planting in the ESSF zone in favour of summer planting, particularly for Engelmann spruce. Summer planting is done with "hot-lifted" stock which is not coldstored. Summer planting is best done during a brief period in the physiological development of the seedling between the cessation of shoot growthlbud set and the onset of late season root growth (Figure 10). In this condition. all shoot tissues have completed active growth for the year and may be less susceptible to frosts during the growing season. Additionally, the seedlings become well established on the site because soils are warm and favourable for late summer root growth, which in tum allows the seedling to take maximum advantage of site resources the following spring. Pitfalls of this planting method include the difficulty of coordinating nursery regimes, seedling transport and planting crews to minimize damage to the non-dormant seedlings, the potential of missing the critical window between the cessation of top growth and the beginning of root growth, and the risk of finding soil conditions that are too dry for planting, although this final factor is of far less significance in the wetter ESSF subzones of north-central British Columbia than at lower elevations. Revel et aI. (1990) also cautioned that although seedlings have set buds. they are not yet completely frost resistant and may still be damaged by summer frosts. Late summer planting is therefore discouraged.

Micro-site selection for planting in the ESSF is also important for seedling survival. Planting on raised, well drained micro-sites, preferably with exposed mineral soil. will provide the warmest soil conditions, and some protection from frost and snow press. On sites with undulating terrain, relaxing spacing requirements to concentrate seedlings on higher ground will improve overall stocking and growth. Planting near large thermal masses such as stumps and logs will help to protect seedlings from frost and winter desiccation. Planting near stumps will also protect seedlings from snow creep, with the up hill side being the preferred location.

An additional caution for planting in the ESSF zone is warranted, as it is anywhere that summer frosts occur, with respect to frost heaving (Orlander et al. 1990; Revel 1989). Frost heaving is most prevalent on silt, clay loam and silt loam soils, and the risk is increased by mechanical site preparation. The risk of frost heaving will be reduced by maintaining at least a thin duff cover, and by timing planting operations so that roots are expanding into the surrounding soil before frosts become more prevalent late in the summer.

#### 5.4 Brushing and Weeding

Herbaceous and shrubby vegetation on cut blocks can both help and hinder planted seedlings. The positive effects can include maintaining populations of mycorrhizal fungi (perry et al. 1987, 1992) and frost protection, the former being particularly true for shrubby vegetation such as *Rhodo<kndron albiflorum* and *Vaccinium* spp., the latter for tall shrubs such as alder. The negative effects include contributions to snow press and snow creep, and shading of the soil. Prescriptions for vegetation control should carefuUy balance the relative impacts of these factors.

Opportunities for using vegetative canopies to protect seedlings from frost within a c1earcut may be rare. Unless a tall vegetation canopy is present at the time of planting when seedlings are the most vulnerable to frost, it will have little impact on overall plantation survival.

Decreasing ground cover of subalpine shrubs and herbs has been shown to improve soil temperature regimes (Coatcs et al 1991). This can markedly improve early seedling performance (Endean 1972; Brand and Janas 1988; Heninger and White 1974; Hellmers et aJ. 1970). Once seedlings are well established with good access to site resources, a reduced vegetative layer may be of less importance. Because soil temperatures are of critical importance in the ESSF, it is important that any anticipated brushing treatments be carried out sooner rather than later.

Some of the more commonly applied brushing and weeding techniques may be difficult to apply in some parts of the ESSF. Steep terrain will in some cases limit the use of livestock and manual brushing methods. Livestock use may also be limited by territorial connicts with grizzly bears and concerns about the risk of transferring diseases to wild ungulate populations. #### 6.0 DEVELOPING EFFECTIVE REFORESTATION PRESCRIPTIONS

A good silviculture prescription selects a set of site and stand management activities designed to meet the best compromise between sometimes conflicting forest, watershed and stand management objectives (Moss 1991). These objectives define the desired short-tcrm and long-term products of stand establishment and development based on the social, economic and political needs of the land owner or manager. Thc degree to which these objectives arc satisfied will depend at least partially on the environmental (site) and biological (stand) limits imposed by the existing conditions at the location of interest. The identification, interpretation and integration of limiting factors and their impacts on physiological and chemical processes within tree seedlings is considered key to developing the biologically feasible alternatives which will satisfy all or some of the management goals (Margolis and Brand 1990; Hadley et a!. 1990).

Treatments to address one particular limiting factor will sometimes worsen the effect of another. Just as various prescription alternatives must be weighed to achieve the best compromise between conflicting objectives. various combinations of treatments must be evaluated to achieve the best amelioration of antagonistic limiting factors. #### 7.0 CONCLUSION

Reforestation can be successful in the ESSF zone of north-central British Columbia. This is not to say, however, that there will not be problems. While reforestation success rates on the warmer south aspects and particularly at lower elevations within the zone can be quite high, environmcntal conditions on north aspects at higher elevations will continue to challenge silviculturists, with poor seedling performance and marginal success rates. In this report I have summarized the critical environmental factors which affect seedling establishment within the zone, outlined some of the conditions under which they might be found, and suggested methods of overcoming them.

The application of the infonnation in this report will help silviculturists to develop biologically feasible options for reforestation in subalpine forests. The final choice of options, however, is outside the realm of the sciences describing seedling microclimate and physiology. This is where socioeconomics takes over, for the final prescription must fit within stand, watershed and forest level objectives. The ultimate outcome of the prescription must fit the needs of various resource requirements such as the maintenance'of water quality, the protection of critical wildlife habitat, or the preservation of scenic quality. Many potential solutions to reforestation problems will not fit these higher level objectives, and will therefore be invalidated.

Regrettably, one of the shortcomings of this report is a lack of detailed information on the application at high elevations of silvicultural systems other than those based on c1earcutting. Unfortunately, little work on this topic has been completed to date, particularly with respect to the modification of critical environmental parameters. There is, however, silviculture systems research currently under way by the British Columbia Ministry of Forests' research groups in the Kamloops, Cariboo and Prince George Forest Regions which will al least start to address this problem. Research on seedling responses 10 various environments, interactions of conifers with herbaceous and shrubby vegetation, and methods of identifying and ameliorating adverse environmental conditions also is being continued by several agencies. Results of these studies and others yet to come will continue to add to the knowledge base required for low risk entries into subalpine forests in the future.
