## SOMMAIRE

La foret mature d'epinette rouge *(Picea rubens* Sarg.) a subi un grand declin dans Test du Canada et des Etats-Unis, entre autres a cause de la pollution atmospherique, des depositions acides et de la deforestation. Avec 1'abandon des terres agricoles et forestieres, le potentiel de regeneration de cette foret doit etre evalue. Le taux et le patron de la regeneration dependront du potentiel de dispersion des propagules et de la capacite de ces propagules de s'etablir dans un nouveau site. L'humidite, la temperature et la teneur en nutriments dans le sol ainsi que la luminosite des sites ou les graines sont dispersees sont les caracteristiques influencant ce succes d'etablissement. De plus, la competition avec les autres especes deja etablies peut influencer ce succes de retablissement.

Le Pare national de l'lle-du-Prince-Edouard du Canada est un fragment d'ecosysteme qui a autrefois ete amenage de diverses facons et qui est isole au milieu de terres agricoles. Bien que plusieurs pensent que la foret d'epinette rouge ne pourrait pas se regenerer naturellement sur les anciennes terres agricoles, a cause de manque de propagules, aucune etude n'a ete faite pour tester cette hypothese. Le but de mon etude etait d'examiner le potentiel de dispersion et de regeneration de l'epinette rouge dans des forets jeunes et matures et dans des terres agricoles abandonnees a l'interieur du Pare national de l'lle-du-Prince-Edouard. Cette etude avait pour objectifs specifiques 1) d'inventorier, le long de transects, les especes deja presentes; 2) d'evaluer la banque de graines et la dispersion aerienne des semences d'epinette rouge; 3) et de tester la viabilite de ces propagules dans les sols des sites d'origine et dans des sols d'anciens champs agricoles avoisinants sous differentes conditions de luminosite, d'humidite et de temperature. Mes hypotheses de recherche etaient; 1) parce que les graines de l'epinette rouge ne sont viables que pour un an, la dispersion aerienne des semences est le seul mecanisme naturel permettant la regeneration de l'epinette rouge sur les terres agricoles abandonnees, la banque de graines etant non existante, 2) parce que l'epinette rouge est une espece d'habitat relativement humide et ombrage et parce que ses graines, dispersees par le vent, devraient tomber plus pres des parents, le taux de

regeneration sera plus el eve a l'interieur de la foret et diminuera vers l'interieur des champs et 3) parce que l'epinette rouge peut croitre sur plusieurs types de sol, mais les semis sont particulierement sensibles a la secheresse, la germination dependra principalement de l'humidite au niveau du sol.

Les resultats montrent qu'a travers la regeneration naturelle, les sites sont deja en voie de regeneration. Bien qu'il n'y ait pas, d'une maniere significative, d'epinettes rouges matures sur les terres agricoles abandonnees, la presence de jeunes epinettes et de semis dans deux des champs etudies indique que cette espece est en train de les coloniser. Par contre, la flore forestiere herbacee ne montre aucun signe de retablissement. II n'y avait que 39 graines d'epinette rouge dans la banque de graines (N = 160 echantillons de 80 ml de sol), presque toutes en voie de decomposition. La regeneration dans les sites etudies dependra done de la dispersion aerienne, qui etait plus abondante, particulierement a l'interieure de la foret. Le taux de germination des graines collectes dans les sites (0,004%) etait trop faible pour y tester les effets des differentes conditions environnementales. La regeneration d'epinette rouge dans les champs etudies sera plus lente que celle observee suivant les coupes forestieres avec peu d'impacts sur le sol. Un suivi continu permettrait de determiner s'il est necessaire d'intervenir et d'augmenter l'effectif. Mon projet contribue a evaluer les possibilites de regeneration de l'epinette rouge sur les terres agricoles abandonnees dans le Pare national de l'lle-du-Prince-Edouard et a en comprendre les mecanismes. Cette etude s'inscrit dans le cadre de la recherche reliee au maintien et a la restauration de Pintegrite ecologique des pares nationaux.

Mots cles : *Picea rubens,* regeneration, champs abandonnes, banque de graines, dispersion aerienne

#### SUMMARY

Mature red spruce forests have declined in eastern Canada and the United States of America as a result of atmospheric pollution, acid deposition and deforestation. With the recent abandonment of agricultural fields, the opportunity has risen to evaluate the potential for red spruce forests to regenerate on these lands. The pattern and rate of red spruce regeneration will depend on the ability of the trees to disperse their seeds and on the ability of the seeds to grow in the new site. Soil humidity, temperature and nutrient availability, as well as light availability at the growing site will influence establishment success. In addition, the ability of red spruce seedlings to compete with the already established vegetation will affect their success.

Prince Edward Island National Park, which has a diverse land use history, is now an ecological island bordered by agricultural land to the south and by the Gulf of St. Lawrence to the north. Although there are doubts that red spruce could naturally regenerate onto the old fields because of a lack of propagules, no one has ever tested this hypothesis. The overall objective of my project was to examine the dispersal and potential for establishment of red spruce in young and mature forests and in adjacent old fields in Prince Edward Island National Park. My specific objectives were 1) to survey the vegetation present along transects from the forest interior out into adjacent fields; 2) to evaluate the abundance of the red spruce seed bank and seed rain; and 3) to test seed viability in forest and field soils from the study sites and under various light, humidity and temperature conditions. My hypotheses were: 1) because red spruce seeds are viable for only one year, there would be no red spruce seed bank and the seed rain would be the only source from which red spruce could naturally regenerate in the study sites, 2) because red spruce habitat is typically humid and shaded and because its wind-dispersed seeds should fall close to the parent trees, the regeneration rate would be higher inside the forest and diminish with distance into the field, and 3) because red spruce can grow in many soil types but its seedlings are particularly sensitive to drought, soil humidity would be the variable most affecting red spruce seed germination.

Results show that the studied old fields are already beginning to naturally reforest with red spruce saplings. On the other hand, none of the forest ground flora has invaded the fields. With only 39 red spruce seeds collected in the seed bank (N = 160 samples of 80 ml of soil), most of which were already decaying, the seed rain is the only source from which red spruce can naturally reforest the old fields. Red spruce seed rain was more abundant, particularly in the forest. From the germination tests, we inferred a germination rate of 0.004%; therefore, we could not test the effects of different soils and environmental conditions on red spruce seed germination. Red spruce will regenerate more slowly in the fields we studied than has been observed after forest harvests with little soil disturbance. Continual monitoring in our sites will be needed to determine whether additional red spruce trees should be planted. My project contributes to the Prince Edward Island National Park's understanding of the potential and mechanisms for red spruce regeneration in their old fields. This study fits within the research framework of maintaining and restoring the ecological integrity of national parks.

Key words: *Picea rubens,* regeneration, old field, seed bank, seed rain

## INTRODUCTION

L'lle-du-Prince-Edouard, comme plusieurs regions de 1'Amerique du Nord, possede des terres agricoles qui ont ete exploiters depuis le debut de la colonisation, mais dont certaines ont recemment ete abandonnees. Dans certains ecosystemes, des etudes (Bazzaz 1996, Dieffenbacher-Krall 1996, Dibble et al. 1999, Yamasaki et al. 1998, Marrs et al. 2000, Mallik 2001, Pickett et al. 2001, Reyes 2002, Moola et Vasseur 2004) ont montre que le potentiel de regeneration de la foret originale peut etre limite par la presence de certaines especes competitives, telles les *Rubus* et les ericacees, surtout dans des sites perturbes. Bien que plusieurs etudes aient examine le potentiel de regeneration de la foret acadienne apres une coupe a blanc ou toute autre pratique forestiere (Dibble et al. 1999, Reyes 2002, Prevost et Pothier 2003, Reyes et Vasseur 2003, Moola et Vasseur 2004), et que plusieurs etudes aient examine la regeneration de la foret d'epinette rouge *(Picea rubens* Sarg.; eg.: Pauley 1989, Smallidge et Leopold 1994, Hughes et Betchel 1997, Archambault et al. 2003, Fortin et al. 2003), aucune etude recente a evalue le potentiel de regeneration de cette foret apres une exploitation agricole de longue duree.

Les mecanismes de regeneration d'une foret, apres une exploitation a long terme, sont en partie connus et comprennent: 1) la persistance des semis deja presents sur les sites perturbes; 2) la colonisation des sites perturbes par les especes forestieres a partir de semences conservees dans la banque de graines du sol; 3) la colonisation des sites au moyen des semences arrivant par dispersion aerienne (heliochorie) ou animale (zoochorie) (Primack et Miao 1992, Matlack 1994, Bazzaz 1996, Greene et Johnson 1996, Hughes et Bechtel 1997, Baskin et Baskin 1998, Greene et al. 1999, Beckage et al. 2000, Roy et al. 2000, Prevost et Pothier 2003). Cependant, ce potentiel peut etre affecte par les conditions environnementales du site d'etablissement (Primack et Miao 1992, Smallidge et Leopold 1994, Beckage et al. 2000, Pickett et al. 2001, Ramovs et Roberts 2003); cela inclut la temperature, les teneurs en nutriments et les autres substances chimiques, la disponibilite en eau et en lumiere (Bazzaz 1996) ainsi que la communaute deja presente (Myster et Pickett 1994, Marshall 2000, Van der Putten et al. 2000, Meiners et al. 2002).

L'lle-du-Prince-Edouard est exploitee depuis longtemps pour l'agriculture et constitue un site ideal pour examiner le processus de regeneration de la foret acadienne qui, actuellement, origine principalement de l'exploitation forestiere et de la deforestation pour l'agriculture (Loo et Ives 2003). Cette possibility est d'autant plus reelle depuis la creation du Pare national de l'fle-du-Prince-Edouard, en 1937, et son expansion en 1998 a Greenwich. Meme si la caracterisation de la communaute vegetale de ce Pare reste a completer, les observations preliminaires (Brown, Cavallin, Lajeunesse, Vasseur) effectuees en automne 2002, montrent qu'il existe des forets matures qui n'ont pas ete exploiters pendant une longue periode de temps (>80 ans). Ces forets sont cependant isolees et representent des sources de propagules d'especes de foret mature comme l'epinette rouge, qui pourraient permettre la regeneration de la foret sur une certaine proportion des terres agricoles abandonnees avoisinantes. La capacite des especes de ces forets matures a disperser leurs semences, combinee avec le potentiel des semences a germer et a croitre en individus matures dans les environnements et dans les voisinages ou ils sont deposes, determineront le succes et la vitesse de regeneration des forets ainsi que la composition future de ces communautes vegetales (Smallidge et Leopold 1994, Bazzaz 1996, Greene et al. 1999, Pickett et al. 2001).

Le but de mon projet de maitrise etait d'examiner le retablissement de la foret acadienne, plus particulierement la regeneration de l'epinette rouge, ainsi que les mecanismes de ce retablissement apres l'abandon des terres agricoles. Les **objectifs**  specifiques de mon projet etaient: 1) d'inventorier les especes deja presentes et d'examiner la distribution spatiale de l'epinette rouge a partir de forets matures vers les champs; 2) de determiner la presence de banque de graines de cette espece a partir de la foret vers les champs; 3) d'evaluer la dispersion des graines d'epinette rouge a partir de la foret mature vers les terres agricoles abandonnees; 4) de tester enfin la viabilite et le potentiel de croissance de graines de cette espece dans les sols des forets et des terres avoisinantes sous differents regimes environnementaux. Ce projet faisait partie d'une etude plus large qui avait pour but de mieux comprendre Pintegration d'habitats precedemment geres par les communautes locales (comme les champs agricoles) dans un plan de gestion de l'integrite ecologique. Cette etude se deroule dans quelques pares

nationaux des provinces des Maritimes et examine les facteurs a considerer et ce surtout dans le cadre de la protection de la foret acadienne. L'epinette rouge etant une espece d'importance ecologique et economique pour la region, il a paru important pour le pare d'examiner plus en details celle-ci.

La capacite de colonisation des especes depend en partie du mode de dispersion de leurs semences (Bazzaz 1996, Yao et al. 1999). Les semences dispersees par le vent ont tendance a tomber pres des plantes-meres (Bazzaz 1996), et ce, avec un declin suivant une distribution exponentielle negative en s'eloignant des parents (Greene et Johnson 1996). Les semences dispersees par les animaux sont souvent deposees plus loin de leurs parents avec une distribution plus aleatoire en grappe (Bazzaz 1996), comme e'est le cas des caches d'ecureuil. Le mode de dispersion agit en combinaison avec l'emplacement et la fecondite des parents, avec l'heterogeneite de l'environnement et du microenvironnement, avec les utilisations anterieures des terres, ainsi qu'avec la predation et le deperissement des semences (Myster et Pickett 1994, Bazzaz 1996, Greene et al. 1999, Pickett et al. 2001, Meiners et al. 2002). Tous ces facteurs influencent le patron de dispersion. L'arrivee de nouvelles semences, la germination d'autres semences ainsi que leur enfouissement et leur mortalite peuvent aider a maintenir une banque de semences en flux dynamique pour certaines especes. Cette banque (souvent appelee reserve) peut aussi varier en fonction de la viabilite et de la duree de vie des graines (Archibold 1989, Bazzaz 1996).

La germination des semences et l'etablissement des semis dependent des conditions environnementales specifiques, telles que la teneur en nutriments et la concentration de d'autres substances dans le sol, la disponibilite en eau et en lumiere ainsi que la presence ou l'absence d'une certaine microfiore du sol (Bazzaz 1996, Baskin et Baskin 1998, Yamasaki 1998, Greene et al. 1999, West et Jones 2000, Catovsky et Bazzaz 2002, Catovsky et al. 2002). Par exemple, dans des conditions plus ensoleillees, telles que les trouees du couvert forestier, les especes pionnieres croissent rapidement (Catovsky et al. 2002) et ont done une plus grande chance de survivre et de s'etablir (Greene et al. 1999). En revanche, les sites de germination plus ombrages privilegient la croissance (Catovsky et al. 2002) et la survie (Greene et al. 1999) des especes de forets matures (de succession plus avancee). Les especes pionnieres et

celles de forets matures different aussi dans leurs reactions a la temperature et a l'humidite de leurs sites de germination (Burton et Bazzaz 1991). Dans les sites forestiers, les graines sont a l'abri de la secheresse et des temperatures extremes que Ton retrouve dans les sites ouverts (Matlack 1993). Dans ces conditions, les semis de l'epinette rouge, qui tolerent tres bien 1'ombre, peuvent persister longtemps parmi la vegetation de sous-bois. Cependant, en milieu ouvert, ils craignent la secheresse et les temperatures excedant 33° C (Blum 1990).

La survie des semis depend de Peffet combine de la composition de la vegetation environnante et des conditions environnementales. Ainsi, certaines especes peuvent empecher l'etablissement d'autres especes a travers la competition ou Pinhibition. Par exemple, les *Rubus,* dont les semences restent viables longtemps dans le sol (Lautenschlager 1991), et les ericacees (Yamasaki et al. 1998, Mallik 2001) genent a la croissance des semis d'epinette et peuvent retarder leur etablissement pendant longtemps. Par contre, la presence de certaines especes pourrait encourager l'etablissement d'autres especes (facilitation), particulierement dans des environnements hostiles, en aidant indirectement les disperseurs, en augmentant la quantite de litiere au sol, en ombrageant davantage le microenvironnement (Bazzaz 1996, Pickett et al. 2001) et/ou en ameliorant la qualite du substrat (Marshall 2000).

Moola et Vasseur (2004) ont montre que la diversite d'especes typiques de la foret mature acadienne decline apres la coupe forestiere en raison de l'ouverture accrue de la canopee, et que cette diversite ne se retablit pas necessairement, meme apres 54 ans de succession secondaire. Quels sont les impacts que 1'agriculture a eus sur le potentiel de regeneration des forets acadiennes? Les impacts causes par une activite agricole (ou les sols sont tres perturbes) ne sont pas les memes que ceux generes par la coupe sylvicole (ou les sols sont moins perturbes) sur un environnement forestier. En ce sens, les especes qui envahissent les deux types d'environnements perturbes ne sont pas necessairement les memes non plus (Ramovs et Roberts 2003). Par consequent, les patrons de regeneration peuvent differer pour les deux types de perturbation. Selon l'etude de Reyes (2002), la contribution la plus importante a la regeneration de l'epinette rouge apres la coupe a blanc vient de la survie de la reserve de semis deja presents sur le site coupe. Ce mode de regeneration n'etant pas possible sur les terres

agricoles abandonnees, la regeneration doit se faire a partir de la banque de semences ou de la dispersion aerienne des semences des forets avoisinantes.

D'apres Reyes (2002), plus de 80% des semences d'epinette rouge arrivant sur les coupes a blanc par dispersion aerienne tombent a 40 metres de la lisiere de la foret, vers l'interieur de la coupe (courbe leptocurtique). A une distance de 75 metres de la lisiere, la densite des semences arrivant par dispersion aerienne n'est pas superieure a 6% par rapport a celle de l'interieur des forets matures adjacentes a la coupe. De plus, il semble que les semences d'epinette rouge perdent deja une bonne partie de leur viabilite apres un an (Blum 1990). En supposant que la foret d'epinette rouge puisse se regenerer sur les terres agricoles abandonnees, au moyen des seuls mecanismes possibles sur ces terres, la regeneration de la foret devrait y etre plus lente et devrait y avancer plus graduellement a mesure que Ton s'eloigne de la lisiere si on la compare avec celles des coupes forestieres ou est conservee une reserve de semis.

Dans les sols agricoles, les labours amenent une homogeneisation des 15 a 20 premiers centimetres du sol. Cela implique que la banque de graines pourrait aussi etre plus uniformement distribute dans le sol (Bazzaz 1996). Par contre, la distribution des graines dans le sol est infiuencee par le type d'instrument utilise pour labourer la terre (Baskin et Baskin 1998). Plus les semences seront enfouies profondement, moins elles auront de chance d'atteindre la surface du sol avant de consommer toutes leurs reserves. De plus, en atteignant la surface plus tard que les plants germes (situes plus haut dans la couche de sol), les survivants des plants germes en profondeur auront une capacite de competition inferieure a celle des plants plus precoces (Bazzaz 1996). Toutefois, certaines graines restent en dormance quand elles sont enfouies profondement. En stimulant la germination des graines en reserve, la perturbation des sols reduit cette reserve (Baskin et Baskin 1998) et peut meme l'eliminer (Ramovs et Roberts 2003). Par consequent, les sols forestiers devraient contenir plus de semences viables que les sols agricoles, surtout dans le cas d'especes forestieres.

## **Ecologie de l'epinette rouge**

L'epinette rouge *(Picea rubens* Sarg.) se reproduit seulement par semence lorsque la luminosite au niveau de la couronne est adequate. Son potentiel de reproduction atteint son maximum environ 15 ans apres le debut de la production de graines. Cette espece produit de grandes quantites de sentences periodiquement a tous les 3 a 8 ans (Sullivan 1993). Entre ces annees de grande production, elle en produit de petites quantites. Les cones de l'epinette rouge sont pollinises par le vent au printemps apres la formation de leurs bourgeons et murissent l'automne suivant (Blum 1990). Les semences, dispersees par le vent et par la pluie, tombent des arbres peu apres que les cones murissent. La plupart des semences germent au printemps; peu germent le meme automne qu'elles tombent. II semble que les semences ne sont viables que pour un an (Blum 1990). Les semences d'epinette rouge germent sur plusieurs types de sol ou le pH varie de 4,0 a 5,5 (Sullivan 1993). Elles sont sensibles a la chaleur et a la secheresse, auxquelles elles sont sujettes quand elles germent dans de l'liumus epais. Des temperatures entre 20°C et 30°C sont favorables pour la germination des semences d'epinette rouge. L'humidite influence fortement aussi la germination (Blum 1990). Les semis d'epinette rouge peuvent s'etablir avec aussi peu que 10% de la lumiere du soleil, mais leur croissance optimale est a 50% de la lumiere du soleil. La secheresse et le gel sont les causes principales de la mortalite des semis de premiere annee. Les racines principales des semis ne penetrant pas loin dans la litiere et dans l'humus ou l'eau et les nutriments seront principalement absorbes (Blum 1990).

L'epinette rouge est un des arbres dominants des forets matures coniferiennes de la region de la foret acadienne. Elle vit, en moyenne, de 250 a 350 ans et atteint, en moyenne, 26 m de hauteur (Atlantic Forestry Centre 1999). Cette espece tolere tres bien l'ombrage et peut persister longtemps dans le sous-bois. II est possible qu'une epinette rouge dans le sous-bois qui ne mesure qu'un metre et demi soit agee de 50 ans. Des que le couvert forestier s'ouvre, l'epinette rouge profite de la lumiere pour croitre (Blum 1990).

L'epinette rouge fournit un habitat et de la nourriture pour des oiseaux et des mammiferes. La gelinotte se nourrit des aiguilles et des rameaux de l'epinette rouge.

Les souris et les campagnols se nourrissent des semences. Des oiseaux, des poresepics, des ours, des lievres et parfois des chevreuils mangent les bourgeons terminaux. L'ecureuil roux *(Tamiasciurus hudsonicus)* coupe les rameaux et les bourgeons terminaux, et mange les graines et les bourgeons (Steele 1998, Sullivan 1993). Les insectes n'attaquent les epinettes rouges que quand elles sont matures et incluent: *Choristoneura fumiferana, Dendroctonus rufipennis, Diprion hercyniae, Pikonema alaskensis etAdelges abietis* (voir Sullivan 1993 pour une revue de ce sujet). L'exploitation et la deterioration environnementale ont reduit l'aire de repartition de Pepinette rouge a 15% a 20% de sa distribution originale (White et Cogbill 1992, Mosseler 1998). La pollution atmospherique, le depot acide (Johnson et al. 1992, McLaughlin et al. 1987) et les stress climatiques (Webster et al. 2004) ont reduit sa croissance et augmente sa mortalite (Sullivan 1993).

Selon les analyses de pollens fossiles d'Anderson (1980) dans des tourbieres a rile-du-Prince-Edouard, les epinettes sont arrivees a Pile il y a a peu pres 10 000 ans, peu de temps apres le retrait du glacier Wisconsinien. La concentration en pollen d'epinette sur Pile etait a son maximum il y a 8000 a 9000 ans. Ensuite, pendant Pintervalle Hypsithermal, les pins *(Pinus* L.), les pruches *(Tsuga canadensis* (L.) Carr.), les bouleaux arborescents (Betula sp.), les hetres a grandes feuilles *(Fagus grandifolia* Ehrh.) et les chenes *(Quercus* L.) ont apparus sur Pile a leur tour. Depuis 3000 ans, avec Paugmentation de Phumidite et le declin de la temperature Pepinette a retabli son importance. Neanmoins, les pollens de toutes les especes arborescentes ont diminue pendant la periode de colonisation des Europeens sur Pile entre les annees 1830 et 1880. Selon Anderson (1980), nous voyons aujourd'hui une resurgence d'epinettes rouges, qui sont aptes a coloniser les ouvertures partielles dans les forets feuillues sur les terres hautes. Selon Rowe (1972), les epinettes rouges croissent aux bas des pentes sur PIle-du-Prince-Edouard et les epinettes blanches *(Picea glauca*  (Moench)Voss.) colonisent les terres agricoles abandonnees. Selon Environnement Canada (2005), ce sont soit les epinettes blanches ou les epinettes rouges qui colonisent les anciennes terres sur Pile.

La foret acadienne couvre la plupart des provinces maritimes du Canada. Elle est situee entre la foret boreale au nord et les forets caducifoliees au sud. Elle est composee de divers types de forets caducifoliees, aciculifoliees, et mixtes (Loo et Ives 2003). #### **Abstract**

Red spruce forests have declined considerably throughout their range in the past decades. As agricultural fields are abandoned and land becomes available for reforestation, the possibility arises for red spruce forests to expand onto them. This study addresses the potential for red spruce forests to expand onto adjacent old fields in Greenwich, Prince Edward Island National Park, Canada. We examined plant species diversity, red spruce distribution and abundance, and changes in community composition along a gradient from the interior of red spruce forests out towards the center of adjacent old fields. Red spruce regeneration is limited to the forest stands in our sites where the fields have been abandoned more recently, but it is extending into and it is more vigorous in the sites with the older fields. Although species diversity varied from forest to field only for the tree and shrub layers, important changes in ground species composition occurred. There is no evidence yet that the herbaceous species present in the forest stands will colonise the old fields. The results suggest that both environmental differences among sites and length of time since the fields were abandoned explain red spruce regeneration patterns. To more accurately assess the potential for red spruce regeneration in old fields, long term monitoring of the production, dispersal and viability of red spruce seeds from adjacent forests and of the constraints to seedling establishment and survival in old fields will be needed.

## **Introduction**

Descriptions of the patterns and processes of succession abound in the ecological literature; however, since successional trajectories are contingent upon site-specific conditions, generalisations from one site cannot necessarily be applied to another (Pickett et al. 2001). For example, agricultural and logging disturbances can have similar initial impacts but the regeneration dynamics following them can differ significantly. This is because successional patterns depend on the nature of the disturbance, on the life history strategies of each species involved and on the outcomes of the interactions among the species and their environment (Pickett et al. 2001). This study focuses on red spruce *(Picea rubens* Sarg.) regeneration in agricultural fields after their abandonment.

Agricultural practices such as tilling, flattening, clearing and planting greatly disturb the soil layer, resulting in a field ground environment that differs considerably from a forest ground environment. Soil mycorrhizal colonisation (Barni and Siniscalco 2000), organic matter and nitrogen content (Richter et al. 2000), nutrient availability and the condition of the surface horizon (Pickett et al. 2001) change significantly in agricultural soil. In most cases, the conversion of forested land to agricultural land leads to the elimination of the forest herbs (Ramovs and Roberts 2003). Additional practices that influence succession include spreading manure (Pickett et al. 2001), fertilisation (Richter et al. 2000) and ploughing (Pickett et al. 2001, Ramovs and Roberts 2003), as well as post-agricultural management such as pasturing (Howard and Lee 2002).

Recolonisation, whether in forest or agricultural ecosystems, depends heavily on: 1) the reproductive strategies of the species in question, such as its mode of dispersal (Bazzaz 1996, Yao et al. 1999), 2) the spatial features of the available site, such as its size and its distance from the seed source (Bazzaz 1996, Hughes and Bechtel 1997, Yao et al. 1999, Pickett et al. 2001, Battaglia et al. 2002, Meiners et al. 2002), 3) the environmental characteristics of the new site (Facelli 1994, Bazzaz 1996, Pickett et al. 2001, Howard and Lee 2002, Cogliastro et al. 2003) and 4) the interactions between species (Bazzaz 1996, Ostfeld et al. 1997, Li and Wilson 1998, Meiners et al. 2000, Pickett et al. 2001, Battaglia et al. 2002).

Red spruce is an eastern North American tree species that regenerates from wind-dispersed seed. Although its seedlings and saplings can persist upwards of fifty years in the shade, its seeds are seldom viable for longer than one year (see Sullivan 1993 for a review of red spruce ecology). In a forest environment that is not too disturbed, red spruce seedlings and saplings can be preserved as advance regeneration during logging and dominate forest regeneration later (Greene et al. 1999, Reyes and Vasseur 2003). Conversely, in an agricultural setting, seed dispersal from sufficiently stocked, nearby sources of reproductive trees will be necessary for natural colonisation.

Red spruce is an important but declining component of eastern North American forests and it is a species characteristic of mature Acadian forests (Loo and Ives 2003). Atmospheric pollution, acid deposition (Johnson et al. 1992, McLaughlin et al. 1987) and deforestation (White and Cogbill 1992) are the principal causes of its decline throughout its range. Late-successional species, including red spruce, have declined in abundance and age in Prince Edward Island as a result of selective forest exploitation and clearing for agriculture. Consequently, the forest composition has shifted to higher frequencies of balsam fir *{Abies balsamea* (L.) P. Mill.), red maple *(Acer rubrum* L.), white spruce *(Picea glauca* A. Dietr.), white birch *(Betula papyrifera* Marsh.) and trembling aspen *(Populus tremuloides* Michx.) (Loo and Ives 2003 describe the presettlement and current Acadian forest in detail). For these reasons, it is important to monitor and protect red spruce in Prince Edward Island.

This study assesses the species composition along a gradient from red spruce forests to adjacent, recently abandoned agricultural fields in order to better understand the structure of the red spruce population and its regeneration dynamics. Our study examines 1) spatial variation of red spruce abundance and density from the forest into the field, 2) the influence of adjacent forests on red spruce abundance in the old fields, and 3) the influence of historical disturbance on species composition.

## Methodology

Study sites

The study sites were located in Prince Edward Island National Park (hereafter PEINP). In 1996, the park acquired new land, including several agricultural fields (Rennie et al. 1997). The cessation of agricultural disturbances (as of 2001 or earlier) on these fields raised the possibility for adjacent red spruce forests to expand onto them and thus raised the possibility to study the potential and mechanisms of such regeneration.

Using aerial photographs from 1997 (scale 1:4 000, provided by PEINP, flight line A31757, No. 51-52 and 80-83), all the sites in the Greenwich section of the park where forests and old fields occurred adjacent to one another, unseparated by barriers (such as roads or footpaths) were located. These potential sites were then visited to determine whether they met the following criteria: 1) red spruce is present in the tree layer; 2) the soil drainage and texture are similar in all sites; 3) the forest edge of each site is oriented in roughly the same direction; and 4) the forest stand is large enough for a full vegetation survey (105 m along the edge, > 50 m deep). In addition, sites with high presence of white spruce were avoided.

Four sites were selected. They were all on well drained Orthic Humo-Ferric Podzols of the Charlottetown map unit (Soil classification map provided by PEINP, Greenwich, 2003). The topography at the sites is gently rolling. The forest edges at all sites are oriented roughly east-west. The mean annual temperature of PEI is about 5.5 °C; the summer mean is 15 °C and the winter mean is -3.5 °C (Environment Canada 2005). The frost-free period averages 130 days per year between late May and early October (Atlantic Climate Centre 2004). The mean annual precipitation ranges from 900-1150 mm (Environment Canada 2005).

The four sites differ in their land use histories and in some associated characteristics (Table 1). Just before they were abandoned, the old field portions of the four sites were used to grow hay, especially timothy *(Phleum pratense* L.). Prior to that, they were also used to grow potatoes (Lajeunesse 2004, pers. com.). Site 3 is the only one where red spruce is not the most dominant species in the tree and sapling layers of

the forested component; in this site, red maple (55%) dominates the tree layer and a combination of *Amelanchier spp.* (35%) and red maple (31%) dominate the sapling layer.

#### Species identification

Red spruce was differentiated from white and black spruce *(Picea mariana* (P. Mill.) B.S.P.) morphologically, mostly using twig hair characteristics. Since red spruce was far more abundant than white and black spruce in the tree and sapling layers of the study sites and their surroundings, seedlings were assumed to be red spruce. Because white spruce saplings sometimes have sparse twig hair (Jablanczy 1964), it is possible that some red spruce saplings in this study might have been misidentified. It is important to note that trees identified as red spruce in our sites were most likely red spruce x black spruce hybrids (Mosseler 2004, pers. com.); morphological features are not reliable for distinguishing pure spruce from hybrid spruce (Nkongolo et al. 2003). For simplicity, and because we do not know the degree of hybridisation of individual trees or stands, in this study we refer to the probable red spruce x black spruce hybrids simply as red spruce.

#### Survey plot design

Each of the four sites was 105 m wide (parallel to the forest edge) and 110 m long (perpendicular to the forest edge). We defined the forest edge as the conspicuous boundary between forest and field, regardless of the age of the trees. The width included a 20 m buffer zone on each side. The sites comprised five transects oriented perpendicular to the forest edge and spaced 15 m apart (Figure 1). From a zero point at the forest edge, each transect extended 50 m into the forest and 60 m into the field. Eight 5 m x 5 m quadrats, each spaced 10 m apart, lined the west side of each transect at the following distances from the forest edge: (1) +45 to 50 m, (2) +30 to 35m, (3) +15 to 20 m, and (4) 0 to +5 m in the forest; (5) -10 to 15 m, (6) -25 to 30 m, (7) -40 to 45 m and (8) -55 to 60 m in the field. These distances are termed positions in the remainder of

this paper; the positions are identified by the above number in brackets, which designates the distance that follows it. Each 5 m x 5 m quadrat contained two embedded 1 m x 1 m quadrats, one centered against the west edge and one centered against the east edge.

## Data collection

In each 5 m x 5 m quadrat, we surveyed the mature trees (DBH > 10 cm), the saplings (DBH< 10 cm, height > 1 m) and the shrubs (height > 1 m). In the 1 m x 1 m quadrats, we surveyed the herbaceous layer, including tree seedlings and shrubs shorter than one metre. For the trees and saplings we counted the number of stems of each species; for the shrubs and herbs, we estimated the percent cover for each species. The survey began in July 2003 and ended in September 2003. This survey design is adapted from the Ecological Monitoring and Assessment Network's Terrestrial Vegetation Biodiversity Monitoring Protocols (Roberts-Pichette and Gillespie 1999) and from Reyes (2002) and Reyes and Vasseur (2003). All species were identified using Gleason and Cronquist (1991) and Roland (1998) and species names were updated according to the Integrated Taxonomic Information System database (2005, [http://www.itis.usda.gov/\).](http://www.itis.usda.gov/)

#### Statistical analyses

*Univariate analyses.* Three diversity indices (species richness (R), the Shannon-Weiner index (HP) and Simpson's index (D)) for mature trees, saplings, shrubs and herbs, as well as red spruce tree and sapling count were calculated at the position level (the average of the data from the five quadrats at the same distance from the forest edge within a site). Species for which there were only one or two observations might have skewed the data so we removed them from the data set, except in the shrub layer where most species occurred only once or twice. The data were not normally distributed according to Kolmogorov-Smirnov and Shapiro-Wilk tests and they could not be transformed to meet the conditions for normality; therefore, they were tested for

significant differences among the positions and among the sites using Kruskal-Wallis tests with a = 0.05. To locate the differences when the null hypothesis was rejected, a *post priori* multiple comparisons test designed to follow Kruskal-Wallis tests was used (Post Hoc for Kruskal; macro available on StatSoft website, 2006). All univariate analyses were performed using SPSS 11.0, except the nonparametric multiple comparisons which were performed using STATISTICA, Version 6 (StatSoft, Inc. 2001).

We used 2x 2 contingency tables to analyse relationships between red spruce seedling, sapling and tree occurrences. Chi-square tests were followed with contingency and uncertainty coefficient calculations. Contingency coefficients measure the degree of association between the variables. Uncertainty coefficients measure the proportional reduction of error in the prediction of one variable when another is known. Quadrat level correlations between red spruce tree and sapling abundances were examined using Spearman rank correlation tests.

*Multivariate analyses.* To assess the similarities in forest and field species composition in our sites, community level changes in vegetation composition across the forest - field gradient were examined with detrended correspondence analysis (DCA) using CANOCO 4.02. "Species" data were stem counts for the tree and sapling analysis and percent cover for the herb and shrub analysis. The "downweighting of rare species" option was selected to prevent infrequently occurring species form exerting too strong an influence on the analysis (ter Braak and Smilauer 1998). Position relative to the forest edge was entered as "environment" data, but because DCA is an indirect method of gradient analysis, this environmental variable did not influence the ordination axes. The calculation of its values as an ordination axis was supplementary. The actual DCA axes represent hypothetical gradients along which the maximum variability in species composition is explained (ter Braak and Smilauer 1998).

#### Results

In the four sites surveyed, there were a total of seven species present in the tree layer (DBH  $\geq$  10 cm), nine species in the sapling layer (DBH  $\leq$  10 cm and height  $\geq$  1 m), eight species in the shrub layer (height  $\geq$  1 m) and 71 species in the herbaceous layer. The relative abundances of each tree and sapling species are presented in Table 2. Only red spruce and balsam fir saplings were present in the field portions of the sites, and only two of the sites had saplings in their fields. Trees and saplings of all other species were present only in the forest portions of the sites.

Five diversity variables varied significantly along the distance gradient relative to the forest edge (Kruskal-Wallis, N = 32, df = 7): R trees ( $X^2 = 25.353$ , P = 0.001), H' trees ( $X^2 = 25.477$ , P = 0.001), D trees ( $X^2 = 14.896$ , P = 0.037), R shrubs ( $X^2 = 17.220$ , P = 0.016) and H' shrubs ( $X^2 = 17.896$ , P = 0.012) (Table 3). The results suggest that the significant variation lies between forest and field. As expected, nearly all the trees were located in the forest. Shrub layer species diversity was highest at the forest positions nearest the edge (Table 3). Only D for the sapling layer differed significantly among sites ( $X^2 = 18.419$ , df = 3, P = 0.001), with sites 1 and 3 and sites 2 and 4 as two homogenous subsets (Table 3).

#### Red spruce distribution

Red spruce tree ( $X^2 = 90.561$ , df = 7, P < 0.001) and sapling ( $X^2 = 18.664$ , df = 7, P = 0.009) abundance differed significantly with distance from the forest edge (Figure 2). Mean red spruce tree stem density was 1 100 stems / ha in the forest and 0 stems / ha in the field; maximum density was 1 600 stems / ha at position 2. Mean red spruce sapling stem density was 1 600 stems / ha in the forest and 700 stems / ha in the field; maximum density was 4 700 stems / ha at position 4 and it rapidly declined to 1 200 stems / ha by position 6.

Sites differed significantly in red spruce tree abundance ( $X^2 = 16.466$ , df = 3, P = 0.001), with subsets defined as sites 1, 2, and 3 together and sites 1, 2, and 4 together. Testing site differences using only forest data ( $X^2 = 34.476$ , df = 3, P < 0.001) instead

defined three subsets, grouping together sites 1 and 2, sites 2 and 4, and leaving site 3 separate. The number of red spruce saplings also varied significantly between sites *(X<sup>2</sup>* = 39.951, df = 3, P < 0.001). Sites 1 and 3, sites 1 and 4, and sites 2 and 4 formed three homogenous subsets. Saplings have not begun to colonise the fields of sites 1 and 3. Despite their similarities in abundance, sites 2 and 4 differed in sapling distribution; it was more even in the field of site 4 and patchier in the field of site 2. Red spruce regeneration was most abundant in site 2 (Figure 2).

Red spruce trees occurred in 37.5% of the 160 surveyed quadrats and red spruce saplings occurred in 40.6% of the quadrats. There is a relationship between red spruce tree and sapling occurrence (Pearson *X<sup>2</sup>* = 17.621, df = 1, P < 0.001); however, the association is not very strong (contingency coefficient = 0.315; symmetric uncertainty coefficient = 0.083, P < 0.001). The abundances of red spruce trees and saplings were also significantly correlated (Spearman rank correlation coefficient (rs): 0.299, P < 0.001).

Red spruce seedlings (height < 1 m) occurred in 16.3% of the 160 quadrats, always where red spruce sapling abundance was highest. The relationship between red spruce seedlings and saplings (Pearson *X<sup>2</sup> =* 29.452, df = 1, P < 0.001; contingency coefficient = 0.394, symmetric uncertainty coefficient = 0.173, P < 0.001) was stronger than the one between saplings and trees. There was no significant relationship between seedling and tree occurrences (Pearson *X<sup>2</sup> =* 2.755, df = 1, P = 0.097). Separating the occurrence data into forest and field quadrats gives the following stocking rates (percentage of quadrats with at least one occurrence): in the forest, 18.8% for seedlings, 53.8% for saplings and 72.5% for trees; in the field, 13.8% for seedlings, 27.5% for saplings and 2.5% for trees.

#### Vegetation gradient

DCA results exposed the strong field/forest distinction in the species composition of our study sites, as well as the distinction between the species composition of site 3 and that of the other sites (Figures 3 and 4). In the tree and sapling layer ordination, the four samples belonging to the forest portion of site 3 are clustered

on the left of the diagram near the abundance optima for red maple, trembling aspen and *Amelanchier sp.* All other forest samples are positioned between the abundance optima for balsam fir and red spruce trees, with no particular order in the positions. All the field samples (that were not empty of saplings) are clustered around the optimum for red spruce saplings on the right side of the diagram. The eigenvalues of the first two axes in this ordination were 0.850 and 0.259, respectively; the cumulative percentage of variance in the species data explained was 35.4 % by the first axis and 46.2 % by the second. The sum of all eigenvalues was 2.399. The eigenvalue calculated for position relative to the forest edge as a supplementary environmental variable was 0.344.

In the herbaceous and shrub layer ordination, all of the forest species and samples are positioned on the left of the diagram, while all the field species and samples are positioned on the right of the diagram. Forest positions and species are loosely ordered from forest interior to edge from the left of the diagram to the center, except for the four samples belonging to site 3 at the far left. Species exclusive to or most abundant in site 3 include *Kalmia angustifolia L., Vaccinium angustifolium* Ait., *Gaultheria procumbens* L., and *Nemopanthus mucronatus* (L.) Loes. Among the field species, grasses are scattered at the top of the diagram. There is no apparent ordering within the field positions. The eigenvalue of Axis 1 was 0.943 and that of Axis 2 was 0.361. The cumulative percentage of variance in the species data explained was 24.4 % by the first axis and 33.8 % by the second. The sum of all eigenvalues was 3.858. The eigenvalue calculated for position relative to the forest edge as a supplementary environmental variable was 0.702.

#### **Discussion**

Red spruce populations appeared to be capable of expanding into at least some of the abandoned fields at a slow colonisation rate. Saplings, mostly of red spruce, have begun to colonise the fields of the two sites with the older forests, but species in the herbaceous layer did not overlap between forest and field. The fields of the two sites with the younger forests had no saplings the year of our survey. This suggests that the time since abandonment has been too short for significant change in the fields. Even

after 54 years of succession following clearcutting in an Acadian forest, Moola and Vasseur (2004) found no evidence that pre-harvest forest species composition was returning. Among the species which they found to have the greatest difficulty recovering after clearcutting, the following are also present in our forest sites: *Aralia nudicaulis, Coptis trifolia, Maianthemum canadense, Monotropa uniflora* and *Trientalis borealis.* 

Red spruce regeneration density in our sites was lower than in studies conducted on harvested forest stands. For example, Roy et al. (2000) inventoried red spruce regeneration three years after clearcutting (80 m x 120 m stripcuts) with protection of advance growth in southern Quebec and recorded densities between 900 to 5 000 stems / ha; their stocking rates were 13% to 75%. Prevost and Pothier (2003) measured a mean of 200 stems / ha in unscarified clearcuts and 17 000 stems / ha in scarified sites. In Nova Scotia, Reyes (2002) found very high red spruce regeneration densities with means of 34 000 seedlings / ha as far as 110 m into the cut and 76 000 seedlings / ha between 0 and 20 m from the forest edge three and four years after the cut. These differences confirm that recovery of forest stands on agricultural lands cannot be compared to forest regrowth after logging.

The species diversity indices suggest that, other than for the tree layer, diversity did not vary significantly from forest to field. Nonetheless, the pattern we observed in herbaceous species richness approached the one in Meiners and Pickett (1999), where species richness increased across the forest - field gradient to a peak 40 m into the field and then declined. Despite the continuity in species diversity, species composition greatly differs between habitats.

Red spruce was the dominant regenerating tree species along the forest-to-field gradient in all sites except site 3. A significant cover of both hardwoods and *Kalmia angustifolia* in site 3 could explain the competitive difficulty for red spruce seedling establishment. Ericaceous species, especially *Kalmia angustifolia,* can be inhibitory to spruce establishment (Yamasaki et al. 1998, Mallik 2001). Hardwoods also can compete considerably with spruce seedling growth (Lautenschlager 1991, Roy et al. 2000, Prevost and Pothier 2003). Site-specific information about past disturbances, other than the logging and farming visible in the aerial photos, was not available, but it is possible

that the currently forested portion of site 3 received a different treatment from the other sites in the past and thus is undergoing a different successional path. Soil fertilisation, forest clearing and burning all create conditions favouring *Kalmia* growth and spread (Mallik 1994, 1996).

Moreover, the dominance of red spruce trees in the other three sites provides a greater seed source for maintaining the species in the forest and for reforesting the fields. Dibble et al. (1999) regarded the presence of seed-bearing red spruce trees as a very important indicator of red spruce regeneration habitat. We indeed found the presence and abundance of red spruce saplings to be significantly correlated with those of mature red spruce.

In conclusion, our results suggest that red spruce forests have the potential to serve as sources for reforesting adjacent old fields. However, this regeneration will be slow, raising two choices concerning park management: the fields can be left to reforest slowly and naturally or they can be artificially reforested to speed up the process. Red spruce can be grown naturally in fields from seedlings (Beaulieu et al. 1989), but its susceptibility to drought and winter desiccation can limit its success (Beaulieu et al. 1989, Blum 1990). Further monitoring would be needed to better understand this option. An assessment of red spruce seed abundance, dispersal and viability contributes to further explaining the regeneration dynamics of red spruce forests in Greenwich, PEINP (Cavallin and Vasseur, in press).

# **Potential for red spruce** *(Picea rubens* **Sarg.) establishment from natural seed dispersal in old fields adjacent to forest stands**

## **Resume**

Dans la collecte de graines arrivees par dispersion aerienne, il y avait 224 graines d'epinette rouge, dont 213 etaient certainement non-viables. Elles etaient distribuees dans 29.4% des quadrats, majoritairement dans la foret. Les differences temporelles en abondance n'etaient pas significatives. **Keywords: regeneration, red spruce, old fields, dispersal, seed bank, seed rain** 

#### **Abstract**

Red spruce can establish on abandoned agricultural land only from seed, unlike in logged environments where red spruce forests can establish from seedlings that survived the harvest. In this study, we collected seeds from the seed rain and from soil cores to examine the abundance and distribution of the red spruce seed rain and the seed bank along a gradient from the forest interior to adjacent old fields in Greenwich, Prince Edward Island National Park (PEINP), Canada. In addition, we examined the temporal abundance and distribution of the seed rain through the seed-fall season. We subsequently tested the germination potential of red spruce seeds from the forest portion of our sites. Because red spruce seeds are usually viable for only one year, we expected the seed bank to be either very small or non-existent. We found 39 red spruce seeds in the seed bank, distributed over 13.1% of the sampled area; 37 of these seeds were definitely nonviable. From the seed rain, we collected 224 red spruce seeds, distributed over 29.4% of the sampled area; 213 of these seeds were definitely nonviable. Nearly all of the red spruce seed rain fell within the forest; differences in abundance among collection dates were not statistically significant. Our seeds had a low germination rate of 0.004%, in part because red and black spruce commonly hybridise in our study area. Despite the low rates of red spruce seed dispersal and viability, the presence of red spruce saplings in our two longest-abandoned fields shows that the species is able to slowly colonise some old fields in PEINP.

## **Introduction**

Red spruce *(Picea rubens* Sarg.) reproduces almost exclusively by seed; very few studies on red spruce ecology reported the species as propagating vegetatively (Blum 1990). Therefore, the ability of red spruce to establish in nearby old fields will depend on the ability of its seeds to disperse, to germinate, to compete and to survive. Seedlings can establish in two ways: they can germinate from freshly dispersed seeds or they can germinate from seeds stored in a soil seed bank (Bazzaz 1996). Red spruce seeds are mostly wind-dispersed and are seldom viable for more than one year (Blum 1990), so the seed rain should be the principal source of new red spruce establishment.

The dispersal of red spruce seeds might vary across a gradient from forest interior to adjacent old fields because seed distribution patterns are contingent upon the interactions of the location and fecundity of the parent trees, patterns of predation and decay and the heterogeneity of the physical environment (Bazzaz 1996, Smallidge and Leopold 1994, Hughes and Bechtel 1997, Pickett et al. 2001, Battaglia et al. 2002, Meiners et al. 2002, Reyes and Vasseur 2003). Edges create particularly marked differences in the physical environment (Matlack 1993, Meiners et al. 2002). Such a variation in red spruce seed dispersal is found along a gradient from the interior of intact forest out into adjacent land where the trees were recently harvested. For example, Randall (1974) and Reyes (2002) found that because of the dispersal ability of spruce seeds, seed abundance in the harvested land decreased with distance from the forest edge.

Biological factors that could affect the dispersal of red spruce seeds, and the establishment of seedlings as well, include the composition and structure of the surrounding plant communities. Vegetation patches create particular microsite conditions, facilitating the establishment of some species and inhibiting others. Patches of young trees in clearings become important seed sources and sites for the activities of seed-dispersing animals; they also shade their immediate environment and affect temperature, moisture and soil properties, possibly aiding the establishment of more trees (Bazzaz 1996, Li and Wilson 1998). Conversely, patches of species such as red raspberry *(Rubus idaeus* L.), bracken fern *(Pteridium aquilinum* (L.) Kuhn) and sheep

laurel *(Kalmia angustifolia* L.) can delay tree establishment (Lautenshlager 1999, Marrs et al. 2000).

Once dispersed, and given favourable environmental conditions, seeds can only be considered a source for regeneration if they are able to germinate. This is a particularly important consideration for red spruce because viable seed yield is highly variable for this species. Over five Maritime red spruce populations, Mosseler et al. (2000) reported that an average of 16 to 94% of ovules were aborted (i.e. the seed coats did not develop). They reported an average of 4 to 74% of empty seeds and 0 to 65% of filled seeds. Of the filled seeds which they had sewn in a greenhouse, 70 to 80% had germinated after 28 days. Nicholas et al. (1992) reported that a range of 54 to 100% of red spruce seeds were empty (the seed coats did not contain an embryo) and 0 to 10% were viable (the seed coats contained an embryo capable of germinating) in their Great Smoky Mountain plots between 1985 and 1990.

This study examines the potential for red spruce to establish in old fields in Greenwich, Prince Edward Island National Park (PEINP) in Canada. We tested for the presence of a red spruce seed bank and seed rain and analysed their distributions along a forest to field gradient, among sites and, with the seed rain, among collection dates. Furthermore, we tested the germination potential of red spruce seeds collected from Greenwich under controlled temperature, moisture and soil origin. Because 1) red spruce regeneration on old fields must rely on seed dispersal and establishment, 2) open growing conditions can challenge red spruce establishment and 3) there is high yearly variability in viable red spruce seed production, we expect red spruce regeneration to be slow in old fields compared to logged sites. We have also observed a few red spruce saplings in our two older fields (Cavallin and Vasseur, submitted). We therefore hypothesized that 1) the seed bank, if present, will be small and decrease with distance from the forest, 2) the seed rain will be more abundant closer to the forest edge than towards the centre of the fields, 3) the seed rain will be more abundant in sites where the forest is older and red spruce is more abundant, and 4) seed germination potential (under controlled conditions) will be low, reflecting a slow process of regeneration of the red spruce forest in the PEINP old fields. For the park's managers, there is a need to know whether the local viable seed source is abundant enough for natural regeneration to

occur; otherwise, planting could be considered as a means of increasing the red spruce population.

## Study area

PEINP was created in 1937 and currently covers 27.25 km<sup>2</sup> of land along a 40 km stretch of PEI's north shore. Most of this ecosystem is composed of sand dunes, beaches, ponds, freshwater and saltwater marshes and several forest communities (MacQuarrie et al. 1999, Parks Canada 1999). In 1996, the PEINP acquired new land, including fields that had once been farmed (Rennie et al. 1997). The mean annual temperature of Prince Edward Island is about 5.5 °C; the summer mean is 15 °C and the winter mean is -3.5 °C (Environment Canada 2005). The frost-free period averages 130 days per year between late May and early October (Atlantic Climate Centre 2004). The mean annual precipitation ranges from 900-1150 mm (Environment Canada 2005).

Prince Edward Island's forests belong to the Acadian Forest Region (Rowe 1972), or to the Maritime Atlantic Ecozone (Environment Canada 2005), and were largely cut down during European colonization (1600-1920). Land clearing for agriculture had peaked in 1911; since then, many farmlands have gradually been abandoned and left to return to forest (Glen 1998). In 2000,45.7% of the island (263 207 hectares) was forested. Red spruce crowns occupied 1.4% of that area. This is a reduction in forest and red spruce cover from 1980, when 47.5% of the island (273 593 hectares) was forested and red spruce crowns occupied 5.3% of that area (Department of Agriculture, Fisheries, Aquaculture and Forestry 2003). In Prince Edward Island National Park, 35% of the land is forested and 10% is clear land (not forested but no longer in agricultural use) (Island Nature Trust 2002). Typically, it is expected that abandoned fields on Prince Edward Island first grow into white spruce *(Picea glauca(Moench)* Voss) or red spruce forests (Environment Canada 2005) and eventually hardwood species become important in the species composition of the old field forests (Glen 1998).

In PEINP, red spruce is known to have hybridised with black spruce *(Picea mariana* (P. Mill.) B.S.P.) (Mosseler 2004, pers. comm.). This phenomenon is neither recent nor rare; there is genetic evidence of red and black spruce hybridisation from

trees older than 100 years in New Brunswick. Red and black spruce from different provenances can be crossed successfully, indicating that neither reproductive barriers nor ecological isolation mechanisms keep red and black spruce separate in the Acadian region (Nkongolo et al. 2003). Red and black spruce hybrids produce fewer viable seeds than do pure red spruce; Gordon (1976) found that hybrid seeds had only 0.7 to 2.9 % the viability rate of pure red spruce seeds. The red spruce trees in this study were identified morphologically; therefore, the degree of hybridisation and the effect of this hybridisation on germination are unknown. Although a hybrid index based on morphological characteristics does exist (Manley 1973), Gordon (1976) and Nkongolo (2003) have shown that the extensive overlap in these characteristics is too great between the species for hybrids to be reliably identified based on their morphology.

### **Methodology**

### Site selection

Using aerial photographs from 1997 (scale 1:4,000, provided by Prince Edward Island National Park, flight line A31757, No. 51-52 and 80-83), all the sites in the Greenwich section of the park where forests grew adjacent to old fields, unseparated by barriers (such as roads or footpaths) that could interrupt the flow between the two were located. These potential sites were then visited to determine whether they met the following criteria: 1) red spruce is present in the tree layer; 2) the soil drainage and texture are similar in all sites; and 3) the forest edge of each site is oriented in roughly the same direction. In addition, there were few or no white spruces in or near the selected sites.

The four selected sites were on well drained Orthic Humo-Ferric Podzols of the Charlottetown map unit (Soil classification map provided by Prince Edward Island National Park, Greenwich, 2003), which "have developed on strongly acid fine sandy loam glacial till or residual material" under hardwood or mixed forests (MacDougall et al. 1988). Charlottetown soils have thin organic surfaces. The topography at the sites is gently rolling. The forest portions of the sites have pit and mound micro topography,

whereas the field portions have fairly even surfaces. The forest edges at all sites are oriented roughly east-west. The strongest wind gusts come from the Gulf of St-Lawrence, north of the sites (Environment Canada 2002).

Site 3 is the only one where red spruce does not dominate the tree and sapling layers; in this site, red maple *(Acer rubrum* L.) (55%) dominates the tree layer and a combination of *Amelanchier spp.* (35%) and red maple (31%) dominate the sapling layer. Of the four sites, sites 2 and 4 now have the more mature forests and old fields; sites 1 and 3 have the younger forests and old fields. Exact years in which agriculture ceased on each field are not known, but at the time of our survey, agriculture had ceased as of less than three years in site 3 and over 6 years in the other sites. The sites are described in greater detail in Cavallin and Vasseur (submitted).

#### Survey plot design

Each of the four sites is 105 m wide (parallel to the forest edge) and 110 m long (perpendicular to the forest edge). We defined the forest edge as the conspicuous boundary between forest and field, regardless of the age of the trees. The width includes a 20 m buffer zone on each side. Each site comprises five evenly spaced transects oriented perpendicular to the forest edge. Forty 5 m x 5 m quadrats are evenly distributed among the transects at the following distances from the forest edge: 0 to +5 m, +15 to 20 m, +30 to 35m and +45 to 50 m (position #1) in the forest; -10 to 15 m, -25 to 30 m, -40 to 45 m and -55 to 60 m (position #8) in the field (5 transects x 8 positions). The term 'forest' refers to positions 1 to 4; within this, 'forest interior' refers to positions 1 to 3 and 'forest edge' refers to position 4. The term 'field' refers to positions 5 to 8.

#### Data collection

During a vegetation survey of the four sites (Cavallin and Vasseur, submitted), two soil cores were collected in each 5 m x 5 m quadrat for seed bank analyses. A total of 320 soil cores were collected and stored at -4°C until analysis.

Seed traps were installed in August 2003 and checked until November 2003. The seed traps were plastic cups with holes pierced into the bottom for drainage. A coffee filter was suspended from the top of each cup to catch seeds and hardware cloth was folded around the edge of the cup to keep the coffee filter in place and to keep out predators larger than the 1 cm mesh openings. One seed trap was placed in each of the 5 m x 5 m quadrats.

We collected and replaced the filters from the seed traps every two to three weeks for a total of 4 collections (all in 2003). The first collection was September 6 and 7; the second collection was September 27 and 28; the third collection was October 25 and 26; and the fourth collection was November 11 and 12. All filters were stored in plastic bags in a refrigerator at 4°C.

### Soil seed bank

The soil cores were thawed and left to air dry, and then the two cores from each quadrat were combined and mixed. The number of red spruce seeds with developed (enlarged) seed coats in 80 ml of sifted soil was recorded for each quadrat. Red spruce seeds that disintegrated when lightly rolled between index finger and thumb were certainly nonviable (Baskin and Baskin 1998) and thus they were recorded as empty. Those which remained intact were recorded as filled. Seeds recorded as filled were not tested for viability.

## Seed rain

The seed trap filters were left to air dry, and then all developed seeds were counted and recorded as "red spruce" or "other species". Red spruce seeds were identified morphologically. Like with the soil seed bank, red spruce seeds (with developed seeds coats) that disintegrated when lightly rolled between index finger and thumb were recorded as empty; those which remained intact were recorded as filled. Viability was not tested.

# Seed germination

The germination experiment was designed to test the germination potential, under various conditions, of red spruce seeds collected from the study sites. Freshly fallen cones were collected and stored in the refrigerator for a stratification period of four months. In February 2004, the seeds were extracted from the air-dried cones by shaking and tapping. The filled seeds were sorted from the empty seeds and returned to the refrigerator in Petri dishes. The number of seeds with developed coats and the number of filled seeds were counted in 466 of these cones. The germination experiment began in March 2004 and ended in June 2004.

The germination experiment included 18 different treatments (environmental condition combinations) and 10 repetitions (pots) for each treatment. Only 503 seeds from the cones were filled, limiting the number of seeds we could sow in each pot to two to five. The variables were two ambient temperature combinations, three soil humidity levels and soils from three different origins. Growth chambers were used to keep a controlled environment. The first temperature combination was 24°C for the day and 21°C for the night. The second temperature combination was 15°C for the day and 12°C for the night. Light levels were set at full light for the day, lasting 15.5 hours and no light for the night, lasting 8.5 hours. These temperature and light hour combinations were calculated using meteorological data for Prince Edward Island (temperatures: Environment Canada 2002; light hours: Herzberg Institute of Astrophysics 2004) for the months of May to August, when red spruce seeds in their natural environments are most likely to germinate (Blum 1990).

The volume of water the pots received every two to three days controlled the soil humidity levels. Level 1 pots received 25 ml of water to moisten but not saturate the soil; level 2 pots received 50 ml of water to saturate the soil; level 3 pots received 75 ml of water to supersaturate the soil. The soils were 1) forest soils and 2) field soils collected from the study sites, and 3) a commercial potting soil serving as the control soil.

## Statistical analyses

Soil seed bank and seed rain data could not be transformed to meet conditions for normality. Differences among positions relative to the forest edge, among sites and among collection dates were analysed by Kruskal-Wallis tests followed by a multiple comparison test designed to follow Kruskal- Wallis tests (Post Hoc For Kruskal; macro available on Statsoft website, 2006). This post-hoc test uses the absolute value of the differences in mean ranks. It compares difference values for all pairs with a critical value, which is calculated using a normal approximation with alpha adjusted to account for multiple comparisons. Correlation tests were performed with seed data from this study and seedling, sapling and tree data which are presented in Cavallin and Vasseur (submitted). Seedling data were binary (presence / absence) data. Correlations with seedling presence were done with 2x 2 contingency tables for which analyses were Pearson chi-square tests, after which contingency coefficients were calculated to measure the degree of association between the variables and uncertainty coefficients were calculated to measure the proportional reduction of error in prediction of one variable when another is known. Correlations among other variables were examined using non-parametric Spearman rank correlation tests. All analyses were performed with SPSS 11.0, except the nonparametric multiple comparisons which were performed using STATISTICA, Version 6 (StatSoft, Inc. 2001).

## **Results**

#### Seed bank and seed rain

The red spruce soil seed bank was, as hypothesised, extremely small. There were a total of 39 red spruce seeds in our collection, distributed over 13.1% of the 160 quadrats. Only 2 of these seeds were filled. The prevalence of zeros in our data considerably reduces the power of our statistical analyses and the results are to be interpreted with caution. The distribution of red spruce seeds in the soil seed bank along a gradient of distances from the forest edge did not vary significantly (Kruskal-Wallis: N = 160, *X<sup>2</sup>* = 10.390, df = 7, P = 0.168). On the other hand, seed distribution did vary significantly among sites (N = 160, *X<sup>2</sup> =* 15.776, df = 3, P = 0.001).

Red spruce seeds were more abundant in the seed rain than in the soil seed bank. Over 12 weeks, we collected a total of 224 red spruce seeds; they were distributed over 29.4% of the 160 quadrats. Only 11 of these seeds were filled; they were not tested for viability. Differences among collections in red spruce seed abundance were not statistically significant (Kruskal-Wallis: N = 640, *X<sup>2</sup> =* 1.563, df = 3, P = 0.668), but differences in abundance of seeds of other species were significant (N = 640, *X* = 22.968, df = 3, P < 0.001; subset 1 = collections 1 - 3, subset 2 = collections 3 - 4). Most of our red spruce seed rain data consisted of zeros and results are to be interpreted cautiously. The greatest percentage of red spruce seeds, relative to seeds of other species, was 22.9% red spruce seeds in the first collection (September 6-7). Most of the filled seeds were from this collection as well. In the other collections the traps caught between 1 and *6.6%* red spruce seeds. Seeds were mostly of white birch in the forest traps and of the Asteraceae and Poaceae families in the field traps; edge traps had a mix of those three types of seeds. Seeds of non-spruce species increased in abundance in the traps as the season progressed. Exceptionally high values in the forest occurred when birch catkins got caught in the traps.

The differences in abundance of red spruce seed rain among the positions of the forest - field gradient were statistically significant when all collections were treated together *(X<sup>2</sup> =* 62.482, df = 7, P < 0.001). The only clear distribution pattern along this gradient is that nearly all the seeds were collected in the forest positions and nearly none were collected in the field positions (Figure 1). The greatest percentage of red spruce seeds relative to other seeds in the traps were in positions 2 (29.6%), 3 (32.4%) and 4 (33.8%). In all other positions, 0 - 5.4% of the collected seeds were red spruce.

Differences in abundance of red spruce seed rain among sites were also significant *(X =* 17*.769,* df = 3, P < 0.001). Seed count was most abundant in site 1 (Figure 2). The percentage of red spruce seeds relative to other seeds caught in the traps was greatest in site 2 (9.8%); it was 5.7% in site 1, 2.7% in site 4 and 1.5% in site 3. Combining all the data, 5.1% of the seeds trapped were red spruce.

For seeds other than red spruce, the main differences in abundance were found between forest positions and field positions (N = 640, *X<sup>2</sup> =* 169.971, df = 7, P < 0.001) and between site 1 and all other sites (N = 640, *X<sup>2</sup> =* 38.621, df = 3, P < 0.001). Seeds other than red spruce were more abundant in the field than in the forest (Figure 1); they were also most abundant in site 1 (Figure 2).

There is a statistically significant association between the presence of red spruce seeds in the soil seed bank and the presence of seedlings (Pearson *X* = 8.476, df = 1, P = 0.004). A contingency coefficient of 0.224 (P = 0.004) (symmetric uncertainty coefficient = 0.052, P = 0.008) indicates that this association is rather weak. Seeds are present in the seed bank in 8 of the 26 quadrats (=31%) where seedlings are present. The presence of red spruce seeds in the seed rain and the presence of seedlings are not significantly associated (Pearson^ = 0.411, df = 1, P = 0.521). They are present in 9 of the 26 (=35%) quadrats where seedlings are present. This demonstrates that the numbers of seeds (and seedlings) in our data are too few for statistical testing to be satisfactorily powerful.

Non-parametric Spearman rank correlations indicate significant, although weak, associations between the distribution in abundance of seeds in the seed bank and the distribution of seeds in the seed rain as well as the distribution of red spruce trees (DBH > 10 cm) (Table 1). The distribution in abundance of seeds in the seed rain is significantly associated with the distribution of saplings and, more strongly, of trees. Red spruce saplings occurred in 40.6% of the surveyed quadrats and trees occurred in 37.5% of the quadrats.

### Germination tests

Out of 503 sown red spruce seeds, only one germinated. This single seed germinated after 16 days. We were therefore unable to determine how moisture, temperature and soil types affected germination. However, this gives us an estimate that about 0.2% of filled red spruce seeds were viable in Greenwich, PEI in 2003. Seeds were counted in 466 of the collected cones. There were 2,074 seeds with developed seed coats in these cones, averaging about 4 developed seeds per cone. Only 2% of the

developed seeds were filled, which equals 1 filled seed per 10 cones. We can extrapolate that only 0.004% of the total seed sample was viable; in other words, only one seed in 250,000 cones was viable.

#### **Discussion**

Our results suggest that low rates of seed production and dispersal might constrain the rate and potential of red spruce regeneration in Greenwich, PEINP. As we expected, the red spruce soil seed bank was practically non-existent. Red spruce seeds were more abundant in the seed rain, but most of these were nonviable as well. This high proportion of nonviable seeds might not be out of the ordinary, since smaller cone crops are expected to produce lower numbers of filled seeds (Greene and Johnson 2004). In fact, our germination experiment inferred an extremely low viability rate. A wide range of red spruce seed production and viability rates has been recorded in other studies: Hughes and Bechtel (1997) recorded 14-17 viable red spruce seeds per m<sup>2</sup> in a montane clearcut, 25-30 m from the forest edge. Nicholas et al. (1992) reported a range of zero to millions of red spruce seeds per hectare. Randall (1974) also recorded much higher volumes of spruce seed rain than we did in his clearcut sites (white and red spruce seeds were not differentiated but tree cover in the adjacent forest was mostly red spruce).

Our low values might have resulted from the small size of the seed crop and from hybridization in these populations. Mossier (pers. comm.) suggested that most red spruce trees in PEI are hybrids. Hybridisation between red and black spruce reduces seed viability (Gordon 1976). Low seed viability may also have resulted from a variety of other causes. Among these are inbreeding, pollination failure, unfavourable weather (cool and wet conditions during the pollination period), insect damage and fungal damage (Bramlett et al. 1977, Mosseler et al. 2000).

Nonetheless, established saplings and trees confirm that regeneration has been successful in other years. Our sites have a mean of 1100 red spruce tree stems and 1600 red spruce sapling stems per 100 m2 in the forest and 700 sapling stems per 100 m<sup>2</sup> in the field (Cavallin and Vasseur, submitted). Establishment of new seedlings will rely in

part on the position of the mother trees as our results showed that seed rain distribution is correlated with sapling distribution and, more strongly, with tree distribution. It follows that nearly all of the red spruce seeds were trapped in the forest positions, mostly between the forest edge and 30 m into the forest, and nearly none were trapped in the field positions. Seeds were trapped 10 m into the field only in the site where saplings occurred up to 30 m into the field. In contrast, Randall (1974) trapped abundant seed rain as far as five chains (approximately 100 m) from the forest edge into a clearcut. Seed rain decreased rapidly within the first chain (approximately 20 m) then decreased more gradually and exponentially after that. Reyes (2002) found that red spruce seeds dispersed evenly under mature canopy, but seed rain into clearcuts declined with distance from the forest edge. He collected 80% of seeds within 40 m of the forest edge, and continued to collect seeds up to 110 m from the edge.

Our hypothesis that red spruce seed rain would be more abundant in sites where the forest stands have an older and more abundant red spruce composition was not supported. The highest seed count was in site 1, which is the second youngest site and where the red spruce regeneration is still limited to the forest. However, red spruce seed rain was least abundant in the site with the youngest forest, the lowest abundance of red spruce trees and the most recently abandoned field. The abandoned fields we studied are exposed to ocean winds which might limit the dispersal of seeds into the fields.

Despite dispersal limitations, presence of red spruce saplings in our two longestabandoned fields shows that the species is able to slowly establish in these fields. Given the low dispersal distance we found, collecting red spruce seeds from the forest and sowing them in the fields might encourage a faster establishment of a red spruce forest. A seeding experiment could also be an opportunity to assess the constraints to red spruce germination, growth and survival in PEINP old fields. Very likely, in these open fields, establishment constraints such as winter desiccation (Beaulieu et al. 1989) combine with dispersal constraints in the slow regeneration dynamics of red spruce.

## DISCUSSION GENERALE

Le but de cette etude etait d'evaluer le potentiel de retablissement de la foret acadienne, en particulier de la pessiere rouge, sur les terres agricoles abandonnees dans le secteur Greenwich du Pare national de l'lle-du-Prince-Edouard. Plus precisement, je voulais examiner les mecanismes de retablissement d'une espece importante, soit l'epinette rouge, apres l'abandon des terres agricoles.

Mon hypothese concernant l'abondance et la densite de l'epinette rouge plus elevees dans les milieux forestiers les plus matures et son declin vers les champs n'a pas ete rejetee. La distribution de l'epinette rouge a ete erudiee sur trois strates : les arbres matures (DHP > 10 cm), les arbres jeunes (DHP < 10 cm, hauteur > 1 m) et les semis (hauteur > 1 m). Les arbres dans les champs n'ont pas encore atteint la maturite, a 1'exception de trois individus dans le champ le plus avance en succession. Dans les deux sites de succession plus avancee, les jeunes arbres etaient presents sur tout le long du gradient etudie, de l'interieur de la foret jusqu'a 60 m dans les champs. Ces deux sites avaient effectivement une plus haute abondance d'epinettes matures dans la foret. Dans un de ces champs, les jeunes arbres etaient distribues plus densement dans les premiers 30 m de la lisiere, formant des rangs serres. Dans l'autre champ, les jeunes epinettes etaient distributes plus uniformement et moins densement. Les causes de cette difference en distribution pourraient comprendre l'interaction avec les autres especes, 1'interaction entre le vent, l'eau, les herbivores et les granivores, et la microtopographie, ou la capacite des epinettes matures a disperser leurs graines (Bazzaz 1996, Whisenant 1999). Les semis d'epinette rouge se trouvaient ou les jeunes arbres etaient plus abondants.

La presence de jeunes epinettes et de semis dans deux des champs indique que cette espece est en train de les coloniser. Certains des jeunes arbres et semis dans ces champs peuvent etre des epinettes blanches; cependant, sans la possibilite d'analyser genetiquement tous les semis, il est impossible de determiner leur proportion. Les especes associees a l'epinette rouge, telles *qa'Aralia nudicaulis, Maianthemum canadense,* et *Trientalis borealis,* ne la suivent pas encore. Meme si la diversite en especes ne variait pas, sauf pour les arbres et les arbustes, de l'interieur de la foret vers

les champs, la flore de la foret etait distincte de celle des champs; aucune des especes herbacees forestieres ne se trouvaient dans les champs. Les especes forestieres n'ont peut-etre pas ete capables de disperser leurs propagules jusqu'aux champs ou elles n'ont peut-etre pas pu s'etablir dans les tres differentes conditions environnementales des champs (Primack et Miao 1992, Matlack 1994). De plus, les champs avaient ete abandonnes pas plus que 13 ans avant mon inventaire. Ceci est tres peu de temps pour la colonisation des champs par des especes forestieres (Matlack 1994). II est possible que certaines especes herbacees typiques de la foret acadienne mature ne se retablissent pas, comme l'ont rapporte Moola et Vasseur (2004), meme 50 ans apres des perturbations intenses de l'habitat.

Une analyse des correspondances redressee des especes herbacees et des arbustes, en plus de separer les especes forestieres de celles des champs, a separe les especes du plus jeune site de celles des sites plus avances. Certaines especes particulieres trouvees dans le plus jeune site etaient des Ericacees, connues pour inhiber la croissance des epinettes (Yamasaki et al. 1998, Mallik 2001). De plus, ce site etait le seul ou l'epinette rouge n'etait pas parmi les especes dominantes dans la strate d'arbres matures comme dans la strate de jeunes arbres. Probablement par manque d'un effectif suffisant d'epinettes rouges matures, la dispersion aerienne de graines etait tres faible dans ce site. A cause d'un manque de graines et d'une competition interspecifique elevee, il n'est pas certain que la population d'epinette rouge augmentera sans intervention dans ce site.

Les graines d'epinette rouge etaient arrivees par dispersion aerienne dans la foret de tous les quatre sites, mais elles etaient presentes dans les champs seulement dans les deux sites ou il y avait deja des jeunes epinettes rouges. Parmi ces deux sites, le site 2 avait la plus grande proportion de graines d'epinette rouge par rapport au nombre de graines de d'autres especes attrapees dans les pieges (9.8%); cela pourrait expliquer pourquoi ce site avait presque 10 fois plus de jeunes epinettes que le site 4, ou seulement 2.7% des graines provenaient d'epinette rouge. Neanmoins, ce site le plus mature avec le plus grand nombre d'epinettes rouges et la plus forte proportion de graines d'epinette rouge (site 2) n'etait pas celui ou nous avons recolte le plus grand nombre de graines. Elles etaient plus abondantes dans le jeune site 1 ou l'abondance d'epinettes rouges matures etait environ 50% inferieur a celle des deux sites les plus matures. II n'est pas

clair pourquoi l'epinette rouge n'avait pas encore envahi le champ du site 1. L'ecureuil roux *{Tamiasciurus hudsonicus),* qui mange les graines d'epinettes (Steele 1998), pourrait jouer un role dans la dispersion des graines en cachant des reserves dans son territoire (Hurly et Lourie 1997). L'activite de l'ecureuil roux pourrait contribuer aux differences en distribution des jeunes epinettes rouges dans les champs.

Les graines d'epinette rouge etaient peu abondantes dans les pieges. Cependant, les cones etaient abondants dans la foret. Cela suggere que la faible abondance en graines etait causee par soit l'endogamie, par l'hybridation, par les dommages causes par les insectes ou les champignons, ou par une periode de pollinisation avec peu de succes (Gordon 1976, Bramlett et al. 1977, Mosseler et al. 2000). La periode de pollinisation de l'epinette rouge est courte, elle dure seulement quelques jours en printemps (Blum 1990). Des conditions trop humides et fraiches pendant cette courte periode empechent la pollinisation (Mosseler et al. 2000). II est attendu que le changement climatique apportera une plus haute frequence de pluie et de tempetes (IPCC 2001). Dans ces conditions, il est probable que la pollinisation de l'epinette rouge soit reduite davantage et que la reproduction de cette espece soit compromise. La predation des graines ne devrait pas avoir affecte les pieges, car elles etaient recouvertes de grilles pour empecher leur predation par les ecureuils roux, les campagnols ou autres granivores. Par contre, des petits insectes auraient pu y entrer. Le nombre total de graines d'epinettes rouges dans la banque de graines etait negligeable.

La recolte de graines d'epinette de 2003 dans le Pare national de l'lle-du-Prince-Edouard avait un taux de germination tres bas, soit de 0.004%, ce qui est correspond a une graine viable pour 250 000 cones. D'autres etudes sur la germination des graines d'epinette rouge (non hybride) ont deja trouve de zero a des millions de graines germees par hectare (Nicholas et al. 1992). De plus, il fallait s'attendre a ce que les taux de viabilite des graines soient plus faibles, considerant que les epinettes rouges de l'lle-du-Prince-Edouard sont hybrides avec l'epinette noire (Mosseler, com. pers.; Gordon 1976). A cause de ce tres faible taux de germination, il a ete impossible de tester mon hypothese selon laquelle la temperature ambiante, 1'intensite lumineuse et surtout l'humidite du sol expliqueraient la plupart de la variance dans la germination de

l'epinette rouge. II a ete egalement impossible de tester l'effet du type de sol (forestier ou des champs) sur la germination des graines et la croissance des semis.

Mon etude confirme que la regeneration naturelle de l'epinette rouge dans les champs agricoles abandonnes depend de la dispersion aerienne des graines et que ces graines doivent arriver d'une foret adjacente ou l'epinette rouge est une espece dominante. La regeneration naturelle semble etre possible sur trois des sites etudies. Par contre, cette regeneration sera beaucoup plus lente que celle observee dans des coupes forestieres (eg. Roy et al. 2000, Reyes 2002, Prevost et Pothier 2003).

L'epinette rouge est une espece caracteristique de la foret acadienne de valeur historique (Loo et Ives 2003) et ecologique (Sullivan 1993) qui a subi un important declin (McLaughlin et al. 1987, Johnson et al. 1992, White et Cogbill 1992). Elle est peu abondante dans le Pare national de l'lle-du-Prince-Edouard. Par consequent, il est important de faire des suivis sur son retablissement et de la proteger. II serait aussi avantageux de continuer la recherche portant sur sa regeneration dans le Pare en etudiant les limites imposees par l'environnement, incluant les interactions interspecifiques, et sur sa capacite a coloniser de nouveaux sites. Une synthese de la presente etude portant sur la dispersion de l'epinette rouge et d'une recherche portant sur sa capacite a coloniser des champs permettrait de concevoir un plan de conservation efficace. De la, il serait possible d'encourager les conditions favorables a la regeneration naturelle des forets d'epinette rouge sur les anciennes terres agricoles. Un suivi continu permettrait de determiner s'il est necessaire d'intervenir et d'augmenter l'effectif. La regeneration naturelle est preferable aux plantations parce que les forets etablies de facon naturelle ont une plus grande complexite ecologique (Ramovs et Roberts 2003), ce qui a une valeur sociale prisee (Carey 2006). Une gestion favorisant le meilleur retablissement possible de la foret mature d'epinette rouge sur les anciennes terres agricoles au Pare national de l'lle-du-Prince-Edouard concorderait avec l'objectif des pares nationaux de restaurer et maintenir leur integrite ecologique.
