# Initial distance to *Kalmia angustifolia* as a predictor of planted conifer growth

Nelson Thiffault · Geneviève Picher · Isabelle Auger

Received: 28 October 2011/Accepted: 3 April 2012/Published online: 22 April 2012 © Her Majesty the Queen in Right of Canada 2012

**Abstract** In the Canadian boreal forest, conifer plantations are mainly used to overcome poor natural regeneration following harvesting or wildfires. However, competitive interactions can impair the successful establishment of forest plantations, especially in the presence of ericaceous species, such as Kalmia angustifolia, that are good competitors for soil resources. We used data from a silvicultural trial established in northwestern Quebec (Canada) to test the hypothesis that *Kalmia*'s effect on seedling growth is an asymptotic nonlinear function of proximity to Kalmia at time of planting. Our main objective was to derive species-specific thresholds of Kalmia-proximity tolerance at the time of planting for boreal sites prone to Kalmia invasion following harvesting. Using nonlinear quantile regressions (90th percentile), height, diameter and growth of planted Picea mariana and Pinus banksiana seedlings over 8 years was regressed against the initial distance to the nearest Kalmia stem at time of planting. We also investigated the relation between annual growth and the distance to nearest Kalmia stem during any given year between age 2 and 8-years. Finally, we evaluated how mechanical soil scarification influenced Kalmia recovery over time by looking at its rate of spread, estimated from mean distance to planted seedlings over 8 years. Silvicultural treatments increased the intercepts and asymptotes of all variables for *Pinus*. However, asymptotic values for *Picea* could not be identified, thus precluding threshold assessment for initial distance to Kalmia at planting for this species. For *Pinus* planted on scarified sites, a *Kalmia*-free radius of 60 cm would lead to  $\sim$  80 to 85 % of the 90th percentile of maximum potential height at age 8 years. Mechanical scarification created Kalmia-free microsites that were rapidly re-invaded by spread of Kalmia; planting immediately following soil preparation of these sites is therefore important for ensuring successful plantation establishment.

#### Introduction

Forest plantations are an important component of sustainable forest management (Evans 2009). They can be used to meet a wide variety of social, economic and environmental objectives and to provide key ecosystem services (Paquette and Messier 2010), such as increasing species diversity and cover (Ganatsas et al. 2012). In the Canadian boreal forest, conifer plantations are most often used when natural regeneration following harvesting or wildfires is inadequate and hence prescribed regeneration standards are not met (Thompson and Pitt 2003). As a result,  $\sim 605$  M seedlings are planted each year across Canada, with 75 % planted in Quebec, Ontario and British Columbia (Canadian Council of Forest Ministers 2009).

Reforestation success largely depends on the management of competitive interactions between the planted species and surrounding vegetation (Newton 2006). Although facilitation interactions can be observed and promoted in some situations, disturbances usually favour the establishment and growth of opportunistic, fast-growing species that capture resources at the expense of planted trees (Balandier et al. 2006). In the boreal forest of northeastern Canada, ericaceous species compete with conifers on many regenerating sites. *Kalmia angustifolia* L. is a shade-intolerant, low understory shrub that primarily reproduces through vegetative growth from buried rhizomes and is hence well adapted to site conditions caused by wildfires and harvesting activities (Mallik 1993). *Kalmia* affects regenerating conifers by altering humus quality (Joanisse et al. 2009), by direct competition for nutrients (Thiffault et al. 2004b), and through potential allelopathic effects (Yamasaki et al. 2002; Zhu and Mallik 1994). Hence, conifers outplanted on *Kalmia*-invaded sites often exhibit symptoms of growth check and chlorotic foliage (de Montigny and Weetman 1990; Yamasaki et al. 1998).

As observed with other ericaceous species such as *Rhododendron flavum* Don. (Yildiz et al. 2010), *Kalmia* can be controlled effectively after application of chemical herbicides (Moroni et al. 2009). However, mechanical scarification is also an appropriate alternative to chemical control for promoting seedling growth on *Kalmia*-dominated sites (Thiffault et al. 2010); it is thus the preferred silvicultural tool for management of these ecosystems in Quebec, where the use of herbicide is banned on public forest lands ( $\sim 90$  % of the provincial forest land base in Quebec; Thiffault and Roy 2011).

Trench scarification equipment creates ditches of exposed mineral soil, flanked by a berm of mixed organic-mineral material (Prévost 1992). Specific planting locations (microsite planting) are chosen to ensure the best local conditions for the planted conifers; on mesic sites, tree planters are thus directed to position seedlings at the trench-berm interface (Burton et al. 2000; Heineman 1998). Although both the humus layer and *Kalmia* plants are severely disrupted by scarification, typical treatments leave intact bands of forest floor and vegetation about 2–3 m wide between trenches (Sutherland and Foreman 1995). Planted black spruce (*Picea mariana* (Mill.) BSP) seedlings growing within one meter of *Kalmia* plants have reduced foliar P concentration, are more often infected by pseudomycorrhiza and exhibit reduced growth, compared to seedling growing farther away (Yamasaki et al. 1998). Thus, recent concerns have arisen concerning the optimum planting position on scarified sites relative to *Kalmia* abundance. Moreover, long-term

![](_page_1_Picture_8.jpeg)

results from field trials suggest that seedling sensitivity to the presence of Kalmia is species-specific; species with high potential rates of resource capture, such as jack pine (Pinus banksiana Lamb.), would be less affected by Kalmia as compared to species adapted to low-resource environments, such as black spruce (Thiffault et al. [2004a;](#page-18-0) Thiffault et al. [2010\)](#page-18-0). Thus, the best planting microsite relative to Kalmia distribution is expected to vary with the planted species, and general guidelines must be adapted accordingly to favour optimum seedling growth.

We thus hypothesized that the effect of Kalmia on planted jack pine and black spruce growth over 8 years and dimensions at age 8-years is a monotonically increasing, nonlinear asymptotic function of the distance to the nearest Kalmia plant at the time of planting. This hypothesis was inspired by the work of Yamasaki [\(1999](#page-19-0)), who tested a nonlinear asymptotic function using the 7th-year growth increment of black spruce seedlings and distance to Kalmia during that same year. However, this relationship has yet to be evaluated to derive species-specific thresholds of Kalmia-proximity tolerance at the time of planting. Our objective was thus to test the initial (time of planting) distance to Kalmia as a predictor of seedling dimensions and growth over 8 years, and potentially provide foresters with an additional criteria for microsite selection.

To do so, we analyzed data from an experimental plantation established in boreal Quebec using nonlinear quantile regressions. We focussed our analytical efforts on the seedlings that were most likely to become dominant or codominant in the future stands. Annual growth data were also regressed against the corresponding distances to Kalmia, to better understand the distance effect over time. We also evaluated how mechanical site preparation influenced Kalmia recovery over time by looking at the evolution of the distance between Kalmia and planted seedlings over 8 growing seasons. We expected black spruce to be more sensitive to Kalmia proximity than jack pine. We also posited that an increase in soil perturbation intensity (double-pass scarification at 90 to each other) would be more effective in sustaining the presence of Kalmia-free microsites over time relative to single-pass scarification.

# Materials and methods

## Site description

The study site was located in the Abitibi region of northwestern Quebec (4829<sup>0</sup> 37<sup>00</sup>N, 7655<sup>0</sup> 40<sup>00</sup>W) within the balsam fir (Abies balsamea (L.) Mill.)–white birch (Betula papyrifera Marsh.) bioclimatic domain described by Saucier et al. ([2009\)](#page-18-0). The regional climate is subpolar subhumid continental, with a mean annual temperature of 2.6 C, a growing season of 150–160 days, and a mean annual precipitation of 950 mm, of which 30 % falls as snow (Robitaille and Saucier [1998](#page-18-0)). The previous stand was 70-year-old and composed of black spruce and jack pine. It was clearcut-harvested during the summer 1999. The soil is a humo-ferric podzol (Soil Classification Working Group [1998](#page-18-0)), and characterized by an 8-cm deep mor humus. It developed on a moderately well-drained fluvioglacial deposit with a loamy-sand texture (74 % sand, 22 % silt, 4 % clay) in surface mineral horizons. The corresponding ecotype is RE21, based on the provincial classification system (Saucier et al. [2009\)](#page-18-0). Ground vegetation at time of planting (June 2000; see below) was dominated by Kalmia, Vaccinium angustifolium Ait., V. myrtilloides Michx. and ground lichens (Cladina spp.) (Thiffault et al. [2005\)](#page-18-0).

# Experimental design and treatments

We established an experimental plantation as a complete block split-plot field trial, with treatments randomly applied within 10 replicate blocks (see Thiffault et al. [2005](#page-18-0) for details). In summary, we applied one of the 3 following scarification treatments in October 1999 within each block (30 9 66 m), to each of 3 main plots (18 9 30 m and separated by 6-m buffers): (a) no scarification (S0); (b) single-pass scarification with a TTS passive disk trencher (S1); and (c) double-pass scarification, with the second pass perpendicular to the first (S2). We planted 72 black spruce seedlings and 72 jack pine seedlings in the hinge position (trench-berm interface) in each main plot in early June 2000. In control plots (no trench), seedling plugs were completely inserted in the mineral soil (the root-collar located at the mineral/organic interface). Seedlings were grown over 2 years in containers of 45 cells of 110 cm3 each. For each species, we planted seedlings 1-m apart in 3 rows of 24 seedlings each, with 2 m between rows. Main plots were split longitudinally into 2 subplots; one subplot was chosen at random as a control (F0) and the other for fertilization (F1) at the time of planting with a slow-release fertilizer package (26–12–6 Silva Pak; Reforestation Technologies International, Salinas, CA, USA). Silva Paks were buried 5-cm deep, about 2-cm away from the seedling root plug. One seedling out of 2 was tagged (alternate seedlings) for long-term growth measurements (i.e. 18 seedlings per experimental unit). We used untagged seedlings for destructive sampling during the first 3 years (Thiffault et al. [2005](#page-18-0)); the resulting plantation was thus spaced at 2 9 2 m.

# Seedling measurements

We measured seedling height (cm) and ground-level diameter (GLD; mm) immediately after planting (June 2000) and 1, 2, 3, 5, 6 and 8 growing seasons following planting (October 2000, 2001, 2002, 2004, 2005 and 2007). In June 2000, black spruce seedlings were 28.5 ± 4.6 cm tall (mean ± SD) and 3.2 ± 0.5 mm in GLD; jack pine seedlings were 31.5 ± 5.6 cm tall, and 4.3 ± 0.6 mm in GLD (Thiffault et al. [2005](#page-18-0)). Absolute growth rate in height (AGRH) and GLD (AGRD) over 8 growing seasons (June 2000 to Oct. 2007) was calculated as (heightOctober 2007 or GLDOctober 2007–heightJune 2000 or GLDJune 2000)/8. Annual absolute growth rate in height (AGRH-annual) was calculated as well, using data from consecutive measurements. The distance between each tagged seedling and the nearest Kalmia stem ([ 0.1 m in height; within a 3-m radius) was measured at the time of planting (June 2000) and 1, 2, 3, 5, 6, and 8 growing seasons following planting.

## Model parameterization and statistical analyses

Based on Yamasaki [\(1999](#page-19-0)), we assumed that (a) seedlings' height, diameter and growth are not expected to increase infinitely with increasing initial distance to Kalmia, and (b) seedlings in close proximity to Kalmia are expected to present positive values of height, GLD and growth (intercept[0). Using the NLIN procedure of SAS 9.2 (SAS Institute Inc. [2010\)](#page-18-0), we thus performed preliminary analyses and tested functions with horizontal asymptote and non-zero ordinate-intercept (Pienarr and Turnbull [1973](#page-18-0); Richards [1959;](#page-18-0) Schumacher [1939](#page-18-0); Yamasaki [1999\)](#page-19-0). All analyses were performed separately for jack pine and black spruce. Based on early results obtained on the same site (Thiffault et al. [2005](#page-18-0)), data from S1 and S2 plots were pooled for all the analyses of seedling dimensions and growth. Values of the model coefficients were related to the scarification 9 fertilization

Model selection was based on the goodness of fit with the following criteria: root mean square error (RMSE), coefficient of determination (R<sup>2</sup>), and on graphical evaluations. With these criteria, Yamasaki's equation (Eq. 1) was retained:

$$Y_i = a + a_1 I_1 + a_2 I_2 + a_3 I_3 + b (1 - c^{\text{DIST}_i})$$
(1)

where,  $Y_i$ : Height in 2007, GLD in 2007, AGR<sub>H</sub> or AGR<sub>D</sub> over 8 years, for the ith seedling,  $I_1: \begin{cases} 1, & \text{if S0F0 plots} \\ 0, & \text{otherwise} \end{cases}$ ,  $I_2: \begin{cases} 1, & \text{if S0F1 plots} \\ 0, & \text{Otherwise} \end{cases}$ ,  $I_3: \begin{cases} 1, & \text{if S1F0 or S2F0 plots} \\ 0, & \text{Otherwise} \end{cases}$ ,  $a_i$ ,  $a_2$ ,  $a_3$ : coefficients related to the intercept, b: coefficient associated to the length from the intercept to the horizontal asymptote, c: coefficient related to the amount of curvature in the fitted curve, DIST $_i$ : initial distance from the ith seedling to the nearest Kalmia stem >0.1 m in height.

Height, GLD, AGR<sub>H</sub> and AGR<sub>D</sub> over 8 years are expected to be influenced by an array of factors, such as soil temperature, soil water availability, and mineralization rates (Margolis and Brand 1990). As growth and yield models for plantations in Quebec are based on dominant and codominant trees (Prégent and Végiard 2000), we focussed on determining the influence of the initial distance to Kalmia on the 90th percentile expected responses of the planted seedlings (the seedlings that will likely become dominant or codominant). Focusing on the upper zone of the response variables plotted as functions of one independent variable, such as the initial distance to *Kalmia*, enables to identify the maximum response when other variables are not limiting (Jarvis 1976). As an example, Grossnickle and Arnott (1992) used boundary line analyses to model western hemlock (Tsuga heterophylla (Raf.) Sarg.) maximum photosynthesis and stomatal conductance as a function of photosynthetically active radiation or vapor pressure deficit. Similarly, quantile regression provides the opportunity for estimating models for conditional quantile functions (Koenker 2005), without the need to arbitrarily segment samples into subsets defined according to the conditioning covariates (Koenker and Hallock 2001). Like ordinary regression, all observations in quantile regression participate equally in determining the coefficients (Koenker 2005), except that conditional quantile is modelled instead of conditional mean. Thus, we estimated the superior decile of height, GLD, AGR<sub>H</sub> and AGR<sub>D</sub> over 8 growing seasons (2000-2007) for jack pine and black spruce using nonlinear quantile regressions based on Eq. 1, with the nlrq function of the quantreg package available in R (Koenker 2009; R Core Team 2011). When an horizontal asymptote was not reached (i.e. parameter bof Eq. 1 not significant), linear quantile regression was used with the following equation:

$$Y_i = a + a_1 I_1 + a_2 I_2 + a_3 I_2 + b \text{DIST}_i \tag{2}$$

where parameters are as in Eq. 1, except for b, which is related to the slope.

We used a linear mixed model (MIXED procedure of SAS; Littell et al. 2006) to model the annual AGR in height (AGR<sub>H-annual</sub>) for jack pine and black spruce as a function of the distance to nearest *Kalmia* stem during the corresponding growing season. Model coefficients (intercept and slope) were related to the scarification treatments (S0, S1–S2), fertilization treatments (F0, F1), time since treatment application (growing seasons 2, 3, 4–5, 6 and 7–8), and their interactions. We based the model selection on a backward method: starting with the full model and eliminating non significant effects (p > 0.05) one at a time, beginning with the less significant. Random effects (block, block × scarification and block × scarification × fertilization) are normally distributed and uncorrelated. We took

![](_page_4_Picture_9.jpeg)

into account the covariance pattern of the repeated measurements on the same tree by specifying the structure of the residual matrix (Brown and Prescott [2006](#page-16-0)). Residual plots were used to check normality of the random effects and to identify potential outliers. A simulation-based approach was used to test the effect of the distance to the nearest Kalmia (a = 0.05).

The planted species and fertilization treatments were not expected to influence Kalmia dynamics (Moroni et al. [2009;](#page-17-0) Thiffault et al. [2010\)](#page-18-0). A generalized linear mixed model (GLIMMIX procedure of SAS) was thus used to model the distance from the nearest Kalmia stem as a function of the scarification treatments (S0, S1, S2) and time since treatment application. This approach was selected to take into account the lack of normality of the response variable and the hierarchical structure of the data with the inclusion of random effects in the model. We used a gamma distribution with a log link, as suggested for positive continuous variables that are highly skewed to the right (SAS Institute Inc. [2007\)](#page-18-0). In this type of model, random effects (block and block 9 scarification) are normally distributed and uncorrelated (Brown and Prescott [2006](#page-16-0)). The covariance pattern of the repeated measurements was taken into account by specifying the structure of the residual matrix (Brown and Prescott [2006\)](#page-16-0). Residual plots were thus used to check the normality of the random effects and to identify potential outliers. A simulation-based approach was used to separate treatment means when ANOVAs showed significant effects (p\0.05).

## Results

## Jack pine

We were able to model jack pine height, GLD, AGRH and AGRD using nonlinear quantile regressions (Table [1\)](#page-6-0). Fertilization alone at the time of planting increased the model intercept by 29, 38, 31 and 47 % for jack pine height, GLD, AGRH and AGRD, respectively, compared to the control (unscarified and unfertilized) conditions (Figs. [1](#page-7-0), [2\)](#page-8-0). At the closest distance to Kalmia, the model predicted 36–57 % increases in jack pine 90th percentile height, GLD, AGRH and AGRD when planted in scarified (unfertilized) plots compared to unscarified plots. The combine effect of fertilization and scarification led to a 55 % increase in minimal (intercept value) 90th percentile jack pine height, and increased the expected minimal (intercept value) 90th percentile GLD by 71 %, compared to control conditions (Figs. [1](#page-7-0), [2\)](#page-8-0). When used alone, fertilization and scarification had similar impacts on the maximum predicted 90th percentile height, GLD, AGRH and AGRD (asymptotes), with increases ranging from 15 to 22 %, compared to the absence of treatment. The maximum predicted height increased by 28 % (83 cm) in scarified—fertilized plots, compared to control conditions (Fig. [1\)](#page-7-0).

Scarification, fertilization and time since treatment application influenced the effect that distance to the nearest Kalmia stem had on jack pine yearly growth (significant interactions in Table [2](#page-9-0)). In non-fertilized plots (with or without scarification), AGRH-annual was linearly related to the distance of the nearest Kalmia stem during growing seasons 2, 3 and 4–5 (Table [3](#page-10-0), Fig. [3](#page-11-0)). The AGRH-annual was linearly related to the distance of the nearest Kalmia stem during growing seasons 3 and 4-5 in fertilized—non-scarified plots. In scarified—fertilized plots, no significant relation was detected between the distance to nearest Kalmia stem and AGRH-annual.

## Black spruce

Black spruce dimensions and growth were adequately modelled using linear quantile regressions (Table 1; Figs. [4,](#page-12-0) [5\)](#page-13-0), since asymptotic values could not be identified (parameter b of Eq. [1](#page-4-0) not significant). After 8 growing seasons, the black spruce seedlings that were planted in control plots in close proximity to Kalmia stems had a 90th percentile of 72 cm in height, 13 mm in GLD, 5 cm year-<sup>1</sup> in AGRH and 1 mm year-<sup>1</sup> in AGRD. Fertilization alone increased these values by 13, 41, 32 and 45 % for height, GLD, AGRH and AGRD, respectively. The 90th percentile of black spruce seedlings planted near Kalmia stems in scarified but unfertilized plots was 43 % higher for height, 65 % higher for GLD, 90 % higher for AGRH and 77 % higher for AGRD, compared to control conditions.

As expected, growth responses were species specific: (a) we observed a non-linear relationship between jack pine growth in height and diameter and dimension, and Kalmia proximity at the time of planting, and (b) black spruce seedlings responded linearly to the increased distance of the nearest Kalmia. The lack of an asymptotic response for black spruce indicates that Kalmia influence was still significant at relatively great distances, at least within the radius at which we measured the closest competitor. This result contrasts with those of Yamasaki [\(1999\)](#page-19-0), who successfully identified a maximum range for Kalmia influence on black spruce leader growth for a number of sites in Newfoundland (Canada). However, Yamasaki [\(1999](#page-19-0)) reports on Kalmia and spruce data that were collected concomitantly (during the 7th growing season), whereas we regressed dimensions at age 8 and growth over 8 growing seasons against initial Kalmia presence (year 0) to generate planting recommendations. Yamasaki's results (1999) thus potentially reflect the direct and indirect effects of Kalmia on black spruce free growth during a particular year i.e. the elongation of the stem's unit formed during the months when growth occurred (Grossnickle [2000](#page-17-0)). In contrast, ours integrate the global effect of Kalmia over time as a function of its initial position relative to planted seedlings. We indeed detected significant relations between annual growth in height and the corresponding distance to Kalmia (Table 2), but the relations varied from year to year, and were greatly influenced by the silvicultural treatments.

Relative to spruce, pine has a higher potential rate of N uptake by unit root length (Reich et al. [1998](#page-18-0)), and exhibit an higher N retranslocation efficiency than spruce (Bothwell et al. [2001\)](#page-16-0). We thus hypothesise that this enhanced capacity of pine to absorb and use nutrients limits the extent of Kalmia influence on this species relative to black spruce. Moreover, jack pine rooting habits differ greatly from those of black spruce. We believe that this trait might enable jack pine to extend its root into an environment that is relatively free of the influence of Kalmia. Whereas jack pine has a strong tap root with most of its root system located at 15–25 cm below the mineral soil surface, black spruce rarely explores the soil below 10 cm (Bannan [1940\)](#page-16-0) and a majority of its roots spread laterally at the moss-humus interface (Viereck and Johnston [1990\)](#page-18-0). Black spruce also shows adventitious root formation in the organic soil (Krause and Morin [2005\)](#page-17-0), a zone heavily colonized by the vegetative progagules of Kalmia (Mallik [1993](#page-17-0); Thiffault et al. [2004b](#page-18-0)), and which presumably contains high concentration of phenolic compounds known to reduced primary root growth (Zhu and Mallik [1994](#page-19-0)). Yamasaki ([1999\)](#page-19-0) has indeed evidenced the relationship between humus depth and the intensity of the Kalmia effect on planted black spruce. #### Management implications

Our main objective was to test if the initial distance to the nearest *Kalmia* stem (an easily-assessed criterion) could be used to identify preferred planting locations on reforestation sites. We acknowledge that other microsite characteristics and competitive interactions are likely to have a significant impact on planted seedling growth during the establishment phase, and that these characteristics and interactions can be expected to vary from site to site (Burney et al. 2007; Delong et al. 1997; Jonsson 1999; Peltzer and Wilson 2001). As an example, when planting jack pine on scarified (non-fertilized) sites, a Kalmia-free radius of 60 cm would lead to \*80 to 85 % (between 277 and 294 cm) of the 90th percentile of maximum potential height at age 8 years for this treatment (Table [5](#page-16-0)). The use of a fertilization treatment at the time of planting can reduce by 5–11 cm the Kalmia-free radius required to reach a given percent of full potential height.

Height is preferred to diameter for these calculations, as the model parameters (Table [1](#page-6-0)) confirm that apical growth is more sensitive to Kalmia proximity than radial growth. In other words, distance criteria would be reduced if based on diameter rather than on height (e.g. 97 cm to reach 90 % of the full AGRD potential in S0F0 plots, as compared to 134 cm to reach 90 % of the full AGRH). Diameter growth is indeed a sensitive indicator of competitive interactions, especially in contexts of competition for light (Jobidon [2000](#page-17-0)). But, previous studies conducted on the same site (Thiffault et al. [2004b](#page-18-0), [2005\)](#page-18-0) have confirmed that both height and diameter growth are affected in these conditions, as competition for nutrients and allelopathic interactions (rather than light competition) are the main drivers of seedling performance in this context.
