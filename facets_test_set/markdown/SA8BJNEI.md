# RESEARCH ARTICLE

# **Divergent Pathways of Successional Recovery for In Situ Oil Sands Exploration Drilling Pads on Wooded Moderate-Rich Fens in Alberta, Canada**

Richard T. Caners1,2 and Victor J. Lieffers3

## **Abstract**

**Peatlands in northern Alberta, Canada, are being rapidly impacted by oil sands activities, with potentially long-term consequences for their recovery. In situ oil sands extraction requires exploration of oil resources on a dense network of drilling pads across the landscape. This study examined the recovery of wooded moderate-rich (WMR) fens 10 years after abandonment of these sites with minimal restorative measures. Bryophyte and vascular plant diversity, site microtopography, and water chemistry were assessed on drilling pads and in adjacent areas of undisturbed reference habitat. WMR fens affected by drilling activities were divided a priori into two groups based on strongly divergent trends in their successional development. One group represented the majority of WMR fens observed on the land base; at these sites hummock-forming mosses including minerotrophic** *Sphagnum* **species were infrequent and tree** **recruitment was almost absent. The other group was dominated by** *Sphagnum* **species, had** *Picea mariana* **and** *Larix laricina* **recruitment, and appeared to recover more quickly. Both groups had high abundance of wetland sedges, notably** *Carex aquatilis***. Further, drilling pads belonging to the first group had a high water table, limited elevated microsites, and had surface flooding over a portion of the growing season, in contrast to** *Sphagnum***-dominated sites. Development of the aquatic, bryophyte-poor wetland type is comparable to early stages of wetland succession and these systems will recover relatively slowly, likely from decades to more than a century. Restoring part of the vertical distribution of microhabitats before abandonment of these pads could stimulate the successional recovery of vegetation.**

**Key words: boreal, brown moss, disturbance, liverwort, microtopography, peatland,** *Sphagnum***, vascular plant.**

## **Introduction**

Northern Alberta, Canada, is being increasingly altered by the oil and gas sector, especially in the bitumen deposits of the Athabasca oil sands region. This region contains large areas of boreal forest dominated by peatlands (Vitt et al. 1997), most of which reached their current distribution 2–6K years ago (Halsey et al. 2000). Peatlands are most abundant in regions with cold-temperate boreal climate, and Canada has about 1.24 × 10<sup>6</sup> km2, or approximately 30%, of total global peatlands (Vitt 2006). With intensifying industrial activity (*>*150 projects are currently operating in Alberta; Government of Alberta 2013), northern landscapes and its peatlands are becoming progressively disturbed and fragmented (Turetsky & St. Louis 2006; Koropchak et al. 2012). Extensive areas (approximately 93,000 km2) of deeply buried bitumen deposits are extracted through in situ methods (Government of Alberta 2013).

In situ oil sands exploration (OSE) requires dense networks of temporary drilling pads to explore the bitumen deposits. Thousands of OSE drilling pads have been established over the past decade, and these are associated with forest cut lines and winter hauling roads for access. Lee and Boutin (2006) suggested that lack of microtopography was the reason for poor recovery of seismic exploration lines in wooded peatlands. However, there have been no detailed studies of recovery mechanisms for disturbed OSE systems. Peat harvesting operations that leave sites nearly devoid of viable seedbank and propagules have been extensively studied in North America (Campbell et al. 2003; Poulin et al. 2005; Chirino et al. 2006; Graf & Rochefort 2008, 2010; Pouliot et al. 2011, 2012). In those systems recovery is more like primary succession than what might be expected on temporarily disturbed OSE sites.

Along the peatland continuum from rich fen to bog, moderate-rich fens were chosen for study as these are the predominant peatland type in western Canada (Vitt et al. 1997). These fens have surface waters with pH ranging from 5.5 to 7.0, with low to moderate alkalinity and base cations, and are often dominated by brown mosses with lower abundances of minerotrophic *Sphagnum* species (Vitt 1994, 2006). Moderate-rich fens have high plant species richness, driven largely by the diversity of microhabitats present

Our preliminary observations suggest that OSE pad preparation modifies banks of vegetative propagules and seeds, exposes lower layers of non-decomposed peat, and reduces variation in microtopography and microhabitats available for bryophyte and vascular plant species. We hypothesize that OSE surface flooding based on visual assessments and loss of vertical microhabitats will have a large influence on bryophyte and vascular plant species recovery after disturbance.

Our preliminary ground-based surveys of OSE pads approximately one decade after disturbance revealed that most sites were dominated by wetland sedges, and few sites exhibited the bryophyte and woody plant flora typical of adjacent undisturbed areas. These contrasting phases of wooded moderate-rich (WMR) fen recovery form the basis of comparison for this study. Our objective is to assess the characteristics of OSE pads distinguished by bryophyte-poor versus bryophyte-dominated vegetation to inform managers to improved ecological restoration. In our study, where each OSE pad was paired with an immediately adjacent intact reference peatland, we (1) characterize the effects of OSE activity on bryophyte and vascular plant species composition and diversity, surface microtopography, and water chemistry; (2) assess if greater variation in habitat microtopography following OSE disturbance is associated with increased species diversity; (3) summarize the potential trajectories of WMR fen successional development following OSE disturbance; and (4) recommend management options that may be required to stimulate the restoration of disturbed habitat.

### **Methods**

#### Study Area

This study was conducted within a  $10 \times 10 \,\mathrm{km^2}$  area south of Conklin, in northeastern Alberta (centered on 55°32′N, 110°54′W). The area is situated in the Central Mixedwood Natural Subregion of the Boreal Forest Natural Region (Natural Regions Committee 2006). Climate is continental with mean daily temperature of 1.7°C and mean total precipitation of 426.6 mm (Environment Canada 2013). Wetlands are often extensive and are black spruce-dominated (*Picea mariana* (Mill.) B.S.P.) fens and bogs. Some discontinuous permafrost is present in northern portions of the subregion (Natural Regions Committee 2006).

#### Site History and Site Selection

Ten OSE pads were selected from an initial set of approximately 50 fens that we visited during spring in 2011. Drilling pads were approximately 0.75 ha in size and constructed during winter months, using winter roads. Trees on drilling pads were removed and mechanically chipped and dispersed on site; the fen surface was bladed to remove most tree roots and stumps to produce a level working surface for machinery and to increase frost penetration (Fig. 1a & 1b). The bladed fen surface was windrowed along the edges of the site. Water was hauled to each site from nearby lakes and sprayed to create a frozen, level

surface. Each site was occupied by drilling equipment for a few days, after which the equipment was removed, windrows of peat were redistributed, and the pad was left to regenerate naturally.

The following criteria were used to select 10 sites: (1) sites had to be within 5 km of roadways for foot access; (2) reference habitats (sensu Society for Ecological Restoration International Science & Policy Working Group 2004) immediately adjacent to drilled OSE areas had to be classified as WMR fens using Alberta Vegetation Inventory (AVI) data and on-site assessments of peat depth, surface water pH and electrical conductivity, and the presence of moderate-rich fen indicator bryophytes, including minerotrophic Sphagnum species, Tomentypnum nitens, Hamatocaulis vernicosus, and Brachythecium mildeanum (National Wetlands Working Group 1997; Vitt 2006); (3) densities and heights of black spruce and tamarack (Larix laricina (Du Roi) K. Koch) trees adjacent to each pad were similar among sites; and (4) disturbance dates corresponded to the OSE pads from 2000 to 2002 (Devon Energy Corp., Canada). For sites with these matching criteria, OSE pads selected for study also had to represent the two dominant manifestations of WMR fen recovery on the landscape. Sites of each group were selected using an a priori classification in the field. The first group, labeled "bryophyte-poor," had OSE pads that were dominated by wetland sedges, had standing water above and throughout the peat surface based on visual estimates when visited in mid-June, and had low abundance of regenerating bryophytes with minimal (≪1%) Sphagnum cover (Fig. 1c & 1e). The second group, labeled "bryophyte-dominated," also had high cover of wetland sedges, but did not have standing water in mid-June, and had high abundance of woody plants and regenerating bryophytes with Sphagnum cover of greater than 50% (Fig. 1d & 1f). As only five bryophyte-dominated sites that matched the above criteria were encountered, this limited sample size of each recovery group to five.

#### **Vegetation Sampling**

At each bryophyte-poor (n=5) and bryophyte-dominated (n=5) site, we used a paired vegetation sampling design. Sites were sampled between 3 July and 20 August 2011. At each site one 20 m transect was established on the drilling pad, 15 m from and parallel to the pad edge. A second 20 m transect was established in the adjacent reference habitat, 15 m from the treed edge and parallel to the first transect.

Five points were randomly located along the length of each transect; points had to be greater than or equal to 2 m apart. At each point a 10 m transect was established perpendicular to the main transect (5 m on either side of the main transect). The highest and lowest elevations of the 10 m transect relative to the water table were determined by self-leveling Johnson Cross Line Laser (Johnson Level & Tool Inc., Canada). In each high and low point, a  $0.5 \times 0.5 \,\mathrm{m}^2$  plot was established and we visually assessed the percent cover of each plant species less than  $0.5 \,\mathrm{m}$  in height, peatland surface substrates, and open water. The  $0.5 \times 0.5 \,\mathrm{m}^2$  plot captured the vegetation community of the selected elevation. Thus, at each 20 m transect 10 plots were established: five at high

#### Site Measurements

At 0, 10, and 20 m along each 20 m transect we assessed tree layer structure: total canopy cover by convex spherical densiometer; height of the tallest tree per species; and total basal area using a #2 wedge prism. Peat depth was measured at each point by inserting a 170 cm steel rod into the peat until it reached mineral soil or was fully inserted. We present the mean value of each measure per transect.

Water samples were obtained along each transect from 17 to 19 August 2011, under consistent weather conditions. Samples were collected in 1L Nalgene bottles from depressions in the peat substrate or from wells excavated to 0.3 m depth. Samples were packed on ice in the field and frozen within 6 hours of collection. Samples were analyzed by the Natural Resources Analytical Laboratory (NRAL) at the University of Alberta for sodium (Na+), potassium (K+), magnesium (Mg<sup>2+</sup>), calcium (Ca<sup>2+</sup>), iron (total Fe), total phosphorous (TP), total organic carbon (NPOC), dissolved nitrogen (DN), ammonium (NH<sub>4</sub><sup>+</sup>-N), alkalinity (HCO<sub>3</sub><sup>-</sup>), carbonate (CO<sub>3</sub><sup>2-</sup>), chloride (Cl<sup>-</sup>), nitrate (NO<sub>3</sub>-N), phosphate (PO<sub>4</sub>-P), and sulfate (SO<sub>4</sub>-S). Analytical methods are available on the NRAL website http://www.rr.ualberta.ca/Research/ResearchLaboratories/ NaturalResourcesLab.aspx. pH and electrical conductivity ( $\mu$ S) readings were taken along each transect after correcting for water temperature using a Fisher Scientific Accumet AP85 meter. (Thermo Fisher Scientific Inc., Canada).

Microtopography was estimated in relation to water table position along each 20 m transect between 5 to 8 July 2011. Light-gauge fishing line was strung above the peat surface between wooden posts (inserted into the peat at 0, 10, and 20 m) and leveled by laser. The water table position was determined by the distance between the fishing line and water surface (open pools or excavated wells in closest proximity to each wooden post). Similarly, the peat surface was measured every 0.25 m to within  $\pm$  0.5 cm and subtracted from the reference water table position to provide surface elevations.

#### **Statistical Analyses**

#### **Effects of OSE on Peatland Environment**

Environmental parameters were compared between disturbed OSE and reference habitats, separately for bryophyte-poor and bryophyte-dominated sites. Water chemistry, tree layer structure, surface microtopography, and peat depths were compared using paired-samples *t* tests (SPSS 20.00.2).

#### Effects of OSE on Vegetation Composition and Diversity

Separate analyses were conducted for bryophytes and vascular plants using mean species cover per transect. Vegetation composition was compared between OSE (n = 10) and reference (n = 10) habitats using Principal Coordinate Analysis (PCoA: Legendre & Legendre 2012). PCoA was performed using log-transformed (ln + 1) species cover data and Hellinger resemblance (Legendre & Legendre 2012) in Canoco 5 (ter Braak & Šmilauer 2012). Differences in species composition among habitats were assessed by permutational multivariate analysis of variance (PERMANOVA 1.6; Anderson 2005). Tests were performed using 9,999 unrestricted permutations of raw data and assessed with Monte Carlo asymptotic p values (Anderson 2005). When F tests were significant, pairwise comparisons were conducted using 9,999 unrestricted permutations of raw data, and examined after sequential Bonferroni correction (Holm 1979) of Monte Carlo p values. PERMANOVA is sensitive to differences in dispersion among the groups of samples in multivariate space. Rejection of the null hypothesis may result from differences in either composition or dispersion of samples, or both. We used permutational analysis of multivariate dispersions (PERMDISP; Anderson 2004) to assess influence of sample dispersion on results obtained from PERMANOVA.

Species richness of bryophytes and vascular plants, and mean total cover of sedges, were compared among habitats and microtopographic positions using two-factor ANOVA (SPSS 20.00.2) and sequential Bonferroni adjustment for multiple tests. Mean cover of *Carex aquatilis* and *Picea mariana* per plot were compared between OSE habitats using Kruskal Wallis tests. Indicator Species Analysis (ISA; Dufrêne & Legendre 1997) was employed to identify species associated with a particular habitat. ISA was conducted in PC-ORD 5.06 (McCune & Mefford 2006) using mean species cover within the 10 sample plots per site and significance tested by 9,999 permutations.

## **Effects of OSE on Vegetation Composition and Diversity**

A total of 79 bryophyte and 70 vascular plant taxa were documented across sites (Table S1). Mean total bryophyte cover per plot was similar for bryophyte-dominated (95.9%) and bryophyte-poor (96.8%) references, and for bryophyte-dominated OSE habitat (93.3%), but was lower for bryophyte-poor OSE habitat (52.2%). For total bryophyte species richness, there was a significant interaction between habitat type (reference and OSE habitats) and microtopographic elevation (highest vs lowest plots) for both bryophyte-poor and bryophyte-dominated sites (bryophyte-poor Wald <sup>2</sup> =46.77, *p<*0.001; bryophyte-dominated Wald <sup>2</sup> =10.30, *p*=0.001; Fig. 3). Namely, for bryophyte-poor sites, bryophyte richness in low elevation plots was greater than high elevation plots for reference habitats, but this trend was reversed for OSE habitats. For bryophyte-dominated sites, bryophyte richness in low elevation reference plots was greater than high elevation reference and also high and low elevation OSE plots. For total vascular plant species richness there was no interaction between reference and OSE habitats and microtopographic elevation for either bryophyte-poor or bryophyte-dominated sites (mean species richness per plot ranged from 13.2 to 14.6 species). However, mean total sedge cover per plot was significantly higher in OSE habitat (bryophyte-poor=44.7%; bryophyte-dominated=37.6%) than references (bryophytepoor=3.1%; bryophyte-dominated=5.6%) (bryophyte-poor Wald <sup>2</sup> =116.35, *p<*0.001; bryophyte-dominated Wald <sup>2</sup> = 28.81, *p<*0.001). Sedge cover was dominated by *Carex aquatilis* for both bryophyte-poor (25.8%) and bryophytedominated (24.5%) OSE habitats, but the difference was non-significant.

## **Discussion**

This study characterizes two post-disturbance pathways of vegetation development following in situ OSE on WMR fens. The strongest differences between reference and OSE habitats were microtopography, and plant species richness and composition. There was little difference in water chemistry between the regenerating bryophyte-dominated and bryophyte-poor OSE habitats, or between the OSE and reference habitats, approximately one decade following drilling. This contrasts with previous studies where peat harvesting resulted in short-term changes to water chemistry in relation to adjacent unharvested habitat (Wind-Mulder et al. 1996). However, the microtopographic variation in peatland water chemistry (Karlin & Bliss 1984) was not examined in this study. Prolonged flooding of microsites likely has a major influence on vegetation development. As flooding in summer is detrimental to most boreal forest trees (Grossnickle 2000), tree seedlings are unlikely to survive in the bryophyte-poor OSE habitat. Flooding has also been shown to affect the establishment of some moss species negatively (Granath et al. 2010), and is expected to greatly increase the length of time it takes for WMR fens affected by OSE drilling to reestablish vegetation typical of undisturbed habitat.

The development of an aquatic bryophyte-poor wetland type on these OSE pads is similar to early stages of wetland succession (MacDonald 1987; Kuhry et al. 1993). In continental western Canada, macrofossil analysis of peat cores has shown that early peatland development on mineral soil is frequently dominated by herbaceous vegetation, especially Cyperaceae, with low abundance of bryophytes (Kuhry et al. 1993; Koropchak et al. 2012). Sedges will probably dominate the disturbed OSE habitats until the sedge peat accumulates sufficiently to develop elevated microsites. However, high substrate surface temperatures in bryophyte-poor OSE habitat (R. Caners, unpublished data) could potentially accelerate residual peat decomposition (Karofeld et al. 2008), substantially limiting peat accumulation. The transition from bryophyte-poor to bryophyte-dominated habitat will depend on internal (autogenic) processes including peat accumulation and *Sphagnum* establishment, and external (allogenic) factors including regional climate and local and larger scale hydrology (Kuhry et al. 1993; Halsey et al. 2000; Grootjans et al. 2012).

Different plot elevations in reference and OSE habitats affected bryophyte species richness and composition, presumably through fine-scale moisture and nutrient gradients. Reference habitats were characterized by a wide range of hummock-hollow elevations, with strong differences in bryophyte richness between highest and lowest microtopographic positions. Higher bryophyte richness in low versus high elevation reference plots likely resulted from more prolonged substrate moisture. In bryophyte-poor OSE habitat, higher bryophyte richness at high versus low elevations suggests that minor elevational differences influence species distribution patterns (Triisberg et al. 2014). Although hummock-hollow systems are capable of developing within one or two decades following active restoration of harvested peatlands (Pouliot et al. 2011), the typical vegetation associated with these microhabitats may take much longer to develop. Indeed, despite the apparently strong development of microtopography in bryophyte-dominated OSE habitat, plant composition remained significantly different from adjacent references. Future research should examine how species assemblages develop in relation to microtopographic formation (Pouliot et al. 2011, 2012).

The reason for differences in post-disturbance microtopography between bryophyte-poor and bryophyte-dominated habitats requires further investigation. Reference habitats did not differ for any measured biotic or abiotic parameter within the scope of this study. The divergent recovery pathways could be related to severity of mechanical disturbance in winter during OSE pad construction, influenced by differences in equipment, machine operators, and/or site preparation techniques. Further, snow depth and moisture content of the peatland surface may have varied over winter months and years of construction, affecting depth of frost penetration and impact of machine traffic needed to "freeze-in" pads before drilling. Machine traffic can compress soils and reduce microtopography (Kotowski et al. 2013), and may have greatest impacts when frost penetration is reduced in warmer winters. Minimizing disturbance severity during OSE site preparation may limit the need for active restoration measures. The moss *Polytrichum strictum* was a strong indicator of bryophyte-dominated as compared to bryophyte-poor OSE habitat and may facilitate establishment of *Sphagnum* and other plant species at those sites (Groeneveld et al. 2007). Once established, *Sphagnum* can influence the establishment of other species (Malmer et al. 2003), and both *Sphagnum* and vascular plant species can interact to influence hummock-hollow patterns (Malmer et al. 1994). Although some WMR fen sites appear to recover more quickly following OSE disturbance (i.e. the a priori selected bryophyte-dominated sites), these less-common sites were still significantly different from adjacent intact habitat in species richness and composition.

In terms of facilitating restoration after in situ OSE, transition to a hummock-hollow system might be stimulated by excavating with machinery, large intact mounds or blocks of peat after drilling operations in the winter when the peat surface is frozen. These could create microsites that are elevated above the water table surface during high water levels, for colonization of woody plants and hummock-building mosses (R. Caners, unpublished data). A number of studies have examined restoration measures for North American bogs following peat harvesting (e.g. Campeau et al. 2004; Chirino et al. 2006) but research on fen restoration is more limited (Cobbaert et al. 2004; Graf & Rochefort 2008, 2010). The *Sphagnum* transfer method is recommended in North America for restoration of harvested (i.e. cut-away) bogs and fens (Graf & Rochefort 2008), but these systems differ substantially from surface-disturbed OSE habitat. OSE-drilled peatlands can reestablish via persistent propagule banks through secondary succession (Graf 2009), but harvested peatlands have no bank of viable propagules, necessitating recovery through a more primary successional pathway (Campbell et al. 2003).

Many OSE pads appear to be following a post-disturbance developmental trajectory that will take an extended time to recover to the bryophyte and woody vegetation that characterize undisturbed WMR fens. The speed of restoration of these wetlands has further potential implications for those wildlife species that are sensitive to habitat edges and fragmentation. Our characterization of bryophyte-poor OSE pads having high water table and minimal microtopography suggests that active creation of microtopography, perhaps coupled with the reintroduction of vegetative propagules, are needed to hasten the recovery of these systems, but such approaches require testing.

## **Implications for Practice**

- Loss of vertical microtopography and prolonged surface flooding after OSE operations were associated with major shifts in vegetation composition away from reference habitat conditions and will need to be managed.
- Removal of extant vegetation by blading prior to drilling in winter should be minimized to increase fen surface height and to retain plant reproductive fragments and diaspores for regeneration.
- Mechanical site preparation after drilling that increases surface microtopographic variation would provide elevated microhabitats above the water table for tree germination and establishment of flood-intolerant vegetation. Removing large blocks of frozen peat during pad abandonment in winter and dispersing these on-site could be a simple, cost-effective technique, warranting investigation.
