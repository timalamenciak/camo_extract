# Monitoring effects of remediation on natural sediment recovery in Sydney Harbour, Nova Scotia

Tony R. Walker & Devin MacAskill & Theresa Rushton & Andrew Thalheimer & Peter Weaver

Received: 29 May 2012 / Accepted: 28 February 2013 / Published online: 20 March 2013 # Springer Science+Business Media Dordrecht 2013

Abstract Chemical contaminants were assessed in Sydney Harbour, Nova Scotia during pre-remediation (baseline) and 3 years of remediation of a former coking and steel facility after nearly a century of operation and historical pollution into the Sydney Tar Ponds (STP). Concentrations of polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons (PAHs), polychlorinated biphenyls, metals, and inorganic parameters measured in sediments and total suspended solids in seawater indicate that the overall spatial distribution pattern of historical contaminants remains unchanged, although at much lower concentrations than previously reported due to natural sediment recovery, despite remediation activities. Measured sediment deposition rates in bottommoored traps during baseline were low (0.4–0.8 cm year−<sup>1</sup> ), but during dredging operations required for construction of new port facilities in the inner Sydney

Harbour, sedimentation rates were equivalent to 26– 128 cm year−<sup>1</sup> . Measurements of sediment chemical contaminants confirmed that natural recovery rates of Sydney Harbour sediments were in broad agreement with predicted concentrations, or in some cases, lower than originally predicted despite remediation activities at the STP site. Overall, most measured contaminants in sediments showed little temporal variability (4 years), except for the detection of significant increases in total PAH concentrations during the onset of remediation monitoring compared to baseline. This slight increase represents only a short-term interruption in the overall natural recovery of sediments in Sydney Harbour, which were enhanced due to the positive impacts of large-scale dredging of less contaminated outer harbor sediments which were discharged into a confined disposal area located in the inner harbor.

# Introduction

Sydney Harbour, Nova Scotia has long been subject to effluent and atmospheric inputs of contaminants, including metals, polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons (PAHs), and polychlorinated biphenyls (PCBs), from a large coking and steel plant which operated

![](_page_0_Picture_12.jpeg)

in Sydney from 1901 until it closed in 1988 (Lambert and Lane [2004\)](#page-16-0). Contaminants comprised of coal tar residues were discharged from coking ovens into Coke Ovens Brook, which in turn were discharged into the Sydney Tar Ponds (STP) (Furinsky [2002\)](#page-16-0). The STP forms a small tidal tributary, Muggah Creek, located in the South Arm of Sydney Harbour. Thousands of tons of metal and PCB- (derived from electrical equipment used in the steel plant) and PAH-contaminated sludge were released into Muggah Creek via an aquatic pathway into the receiving marine environment of Sydney Harbour (Lambert et al. [2006](#page-16-0)). These particle-reactive contaminants bind to fine-grained organic-rich harbor sediments and become the main pathway of exposure and endpoint for contaminants in the marine environment, particularly for benthic organisms (Chapman [1989;](#page-16-0) Stewart et al. [2002](#page-17-0); Leipe et al. [2005\)](#page-17-0).

Numerous studies have linked these effluent and atmospheric contaminants to local ecological and human health impacts in the area (e.g., O'Leary and Covell [2002](#page-17-0); Tay et al. [2003\)](#page-17-0). Within Sydney Harbour, the highest chemical contaminant concentrations in sediments were historically reported near Muggah Creek, with concentrations decreasing towards the outer harbor (e.g., Stewart et al. [2001](#page-17-0); Lee et al. [2002\)](#page-16-0). Additionally, elevated chemical contaminants previously detected in the digestive glands of American lobster (Homarus americanus) resulted in the closure of the commercial lobster fishery in the inner harbor in 1982 (Hilderbrand [1982;](#page-16-0) King et al. [1993\)](#page-16-0).

Since the closure of coking operations, there have been several attempts to remediate this former industrial site (Campbell [2002](#page-16-0)). Finally, in May 2004, the governments of Canada and Nova Scotia committed to remediating the STP to reduce potential ecological and human health risks to the environment. A federal and provincial Environmental Impact Statement (EIS) and public Joint Review Panel concluded that remediation would be "unlikely to cause significant negative environmental impacts with the implementation of appropriate and stringent mitigation" (AMEC [2005](#page-15-0)). A follow-up monitoring study was established to determine the effectiveness of mitigative measures on potential adverse environmental effects during remediation and to verify environmental effect predictions made in the EIS in accordance with the Canadian Environmental Assessment Act. Monitoring was designed to assess changes (both "positive" and "negative") in the surrounding ecosystem (including groundwater, aquatic, and marine), potentially attributed to remedial activities (Dillon Consulting Limited [2010](#page-16-0)). This study in Sydney Harbour comprised a subcomponent of the larger monitoring study.

Remediation at the STP site began in 2009 and included in situ remediation using solidification and stabilization (S/S) by mixing contaminated tar pond sediments with Portland cement, transforming it into a durable, solid, low-hydraulic conductivity matrix to reduce contaminant leaching rates into the surrounding environment (ITRC [2011\)](#page-16-0). Although the S/S technology is relatively new, several temporal studies of S/S performance at former industrial sites revealed that environmental conditions have improved to within acceptable limits for many contaminants (Paria and Yuet [2006](#page-17-0); ITRC [2011\)](#page-16-0).

The pollution legacy of contaminants in Sydney Harbour sediments is well documented (e.g., Lee et al. [2002\)](#page-16-0) and reached their maxima between 1960 and 1980 coinciding with peak production of coking operations (Barlow and May [2000](#page-16-0)), but a study by Smith et al. ([2009\)](#page-17-0) used radionuclide tracers to predict the time required for natural recovery (or "capping") of historical contaminants. Our study included ground truth monitoring of current contaminant levels in Sydney Harbour sediments to assess monitored natural recovery (MNR) and to confirm modeled predictions of natural sediment recovery rates reported by Smith et al. [\(2009](#page-17-0)). The technique of MNR can have profound economic, human, and ecological health benefits by avoiding sediment removal (or "capping") activities (Magar and Wenning [2006](#page-17-0); Wenning et al. [2006\)](#page-18-0), and benthic organisms respond positively by bioaccumulating less contaminants in their tissues (Leipe et al. [2005\)](#page-17-0).

The general aim of this study was to address potential impacts of remedial activities on surface sediments (currently undergoing natural sediment burial), during baseline and remediation activities at the STP. Specifically, the objectives were (1) to monitor potential disturbance artifacts (either positive or negative) of remedial activities by assessing results of sediment and water quality indicators in Sydney Harbour and (2) to assess contaminant concentrations using MNR techniques to confirm earlier modeled predictions of natural sediment recovery reported by Smith et al. [\(2009](#page-17-0)).

# Materials and methods

# Monitoring stations

Sydney Harbour, Nova Scotia has modest freshwater inputs from Sydney River resulting in estuarine conditions with tidal ranges of 0.9–1.4 m (Gregory et al. [1993;](#page-16-0) Petrie et al. [2001\)](#page-17-0). Four "areas of assessment" and 11 marine monitoring stations (9–11 m deep) were sampled by boat to monitor variables throughout this study during 2009 baseline (pre-remediation) and 3 years of remediation (i.e., year 1, 2010; year 2, 2011; year 3, 2012) (Fig. 1): area 1—near-field (three stations), area 2—mid-field (three stations), area 3 far-field (two stations), and area 4—Sydney River Estuary (one station). Geographic positions were recorded using hand-held and boat Garmin GPS units.

# Water sampling and analysis

Baseline marine water quality samples for total suspended solids (TSS) were collected monthly between April and August 2009 using a hand-held 2-L

Fig. 1 Marine monitoring stations and assessment areas in Sydney Harbour

water sampler (Hoskin Scientific®). Remediation phase water samples were collected monthly from September through October each year, except for a 3-month lapse during winter ice cover. Monthly three-point composite samples were collected 1 m below surface (shallow) and 1 m from the bottom (deep) at each station. Composite sampling improves spatial and temporal coverage of stations and reduces contaminant variability (Correll [2001](#page-16-0)). Care was taken during sampling to prevent collection of surface detritus and bottom sediments. Samples were preserved and analyzed using U.S. Environmental Protection Agency analytical methods based on 160.2/2540-D (APHA [1998;](#page-16-0) US-EPA [2005](#page-17-0)). Samples were analyzed by Maxxam Analytics Inc. (Maxxam), a Canadian Analytical Laboratory Association (CALA) certified laboratory.

### Sedimentation rates

Bottom-moored sediment traps were deployed for 3 months to measure sediment deposition rates during baseline between April 21 and July 21, 2009 (91 days)

![](_page_2_Figure_11.jpeg)

![](_page_2_Picture_12.jpeg)

and again prior to dredging activities in Sydney Harbour between August 31 and December 9, 2011 (100 days). Sediment traps consisted of PVC pipes (15 cm×122 cm) secured to plastic milk crates bolted to concrete patio stones. Trap dimensions conformed to ratios recommended by Bloesch [\(1994\)](#page-16-0) for minimizing potential sediment losses due to resuspension (see Nedwell et al. [1993;](#page-17-0) Walker [2005a;](#page-17-0) Walker et al. [2008a](#page-18-0), [b\)](#page-18-0). Depth of sediment accumulation was measured to determine sediment deposition following trap retrieval. Sedimentation rates from stations within the same assessment area were pooled and presented as means for each area (±SE, n=3).

### Sediment sampling and analysis

Sediment samples were collected using an Ekman grab (15×15 cm) (Wildco®). Care was taken to allow surface seawater in the grab to drain away to minimize disturbance of surface sediment before subsampling (Walker and Grant [2009](#page-17-0)), to retain highly flocculent surface "fluff" which reflect the most recent changes in sediment contaminant concentrations (Milligan and Loring [1997](#page-17-0)). Subsamples were collected from the top 0–1-cm horizon to capture the most recently deposited sediment material in consideration of low deposition rates in Sydney Harbour (Lee et al. [2002](#page-16-0)). Three grab samples were collected to create representative composite samples at each station during baseline and remediation (Correll [2001](#page-16-0)), although triplicate sediment samples were collected in 2011 (year 2) to provide a better understanding of potential variation in chemical concentrations at each site.

Sediment samples were analyzed for grain size, total organic carbon (TOC), PAHs (comprising 18 individual PAH compounds), PCBs, metals (As, Cd, Cu, Pb, Hg, Zn), porosity, sulfides, ammonia, acid volatile sulfides, and simultaneously extracted metals (AVS/SEM ratios). Sediments were sampled during baseline (2009) and 3 years of remediation (2010, 2011, and 2012).

Sediment grain size was analyzed by sieve and pipette based on MSAMS-1978 method. TOC was determined by treating an aliquot of dried sediment sample with sufficient hydrochloric acid (HCl) (1:1) to remove inorganic carbon prior to analysis on dried sediments at 105 °C using a LECO CR-412 Carbon Analyzer (Schumacher [2002](#page-17-0)). Analysis for low level PAH and aroclor PCB concentrations was based on US-EPA 8270C and US-EPA 8082, respectively.

![](_page_3_Picture_7.jpeg)

### Quality control

New powder-free nitrile gloves were worn during each sample collection to minimize potential cross contamination. Sediment and seawater samples were transported to the laboratory within 6 h in coolers on ice. Blind field duplicates were collected for every ten samples. Analytical test methods were based on US-EPA ([2005\)](#page-17-0), unless otherwise stated.

### Data analysis

Excel and Minitab were used to perform standard statistical analyses. Wilcoxon matched-pair signed ranks tests were used to determine significant differences between baseline and remediation unless otherwise stated. In our sampling design, baseline sampling data represents "before" and were compared with subsequent annual monitoring events during remediation, representing "after" treatment. US-EPA ([2006](#page-17-0)) was used to calculate 95 % upper confidence limits (UCL95) for TSS concentrations based on combined shallow and deep water data for each assessment area (including TSS data with below detection limit observations). Concentrations of PAHs and PCBs were compared to the National Oceanographic and Atmospheric Administration criteria for effects range-low (ER-L), corresponding to background concentrations below which the presence of contaminants has little chronic or acute effect on benthic organisms; and effects range-medium (ER-M), above which organisms are very likely to be negatively affected by the presence of contaminants (Jones et al. [1997](#page-16-0)). Metal and PCB concentrations were compared to Canadian Council of Ministers of the Environment (CCME) Canadian Water Quality Guidelines for the Protection of Aquatic Life probable effect levels (PEL) and the interim sediment quality guidelines (ISQG) (CCME [2007](#page-16-0)).

#### Results and discussion

#### Water quality indicators

Mean monthly TSS concentrations were generally higher in deep water  $(3.7\pm0.4 \text{ to } 27.3\pm6.2 \text{ mgL}^{-1})$ compared to shallow water stations  $(3.0\pm0.1 \text{ to } 7.0\pm$ 0.5 mgL<sup>-1</sup>) (Fig. 2). Higher TSS concentrations at depth may be related to sampling equipment disturbance artifacts or normally higher concentrations encountered at the sediment/water interface from tidal or current resuspension (Sanford et al. 2001). Variations in surface and deep water TSS concentrations were significantly different between monitoring years, remediation activities. An environmental assessment for dredging required for construction of a new marine container terminal in Sydney Harbour reported TSS concentrations of  $10 \text{ mgL}^{-1}$  (surface) and  $50-100 \text{ mgL}^{-1}$  (bottom), which were higher than those measured during this study (Jacques Whitford 2009). #### Sedimentation rates

Measured sediment deposition rates in bottommoored traps during baseline were low in all areas (0.4–0.8 cmyear<sup>-1</sup>) (Fig. 4). The greatest deposition occurred near Muggah Creek and Sydney River (areas 1 and 4). According to Petrie et al. (2001), flocculation is the most important deposition mechanism in these areas and these low sedimentation rates were comparable to previous estimates made in Sydney Harbour (0.2–2 cmyear<sup>-1</sup>) (Stewart et al. 2001; Lee et al. 2002; Smith et al. 2009). Deposition rates were also similar to those reported for Halifax Harbour  $(0.1-1.0 \text{ cmyear}^{-1})$  by Buckley et al. (1995) and Cranston (1999), although sedimentation rates measured during dredging operations in Sydney Harbour were equivalent to 26–128 cmyear<sup>-1</sup> at stations 2–2 and 2-3 representing huge quantities of sediment for "capping."

Area 1 stations had the lowest AVS/SEM ratios which serve as predictors of toxic effects of organic enrichment and anoxic conditions in sediments (Magar and Wenning [2006\)](#page-17-0). Ratios >1 have excess sulfide available to form insoluble metal sulfides which are relatively nonbioavailable for uptake by benthic organisms, thereby reducing potential toxicity; however, metals can become potentially toxic and bioavailable to benthic organisms with ratios <1 (McGrath et al. [2002\)](#page-17-0).

Although some of the lowest AVS/SEM ratios (<1) were measured at stations closest to Muggah Creek and elsewhere within area 1, there were also low ratios detected in the far-field reference area, so interpretation of contaminant bioavailability may not be entirely related to sulfide availability alone. However, stations in area 2 had AVS/SEM ratios >1, suggesting that metal sulfide contaminants would be relatively nonbioavailable to benthic organisms (Walker [2005b\)](#page-17-0). Redox potential was highly variable both spatially and temporally, but did not indicate impacts related to remediation activities as they were within baseline ranges.

Likewise, there was some interannual variation in TOC concentrations, with year 1 showing significantly higher TOC concentrations than baseline (Wilcoxon signed rank test, nine matched observations, z=2.310, P=0.0104, one-tailed); in contrast to baseline versus year 2 which showed no significant differences (z=1.066, P=0.1432) (Fig. 6). Except for stations in area 2, results from sediments collected in the inner harbor are consistent with earlier studies (e.g., Stewart et al. [2001](#page-17-0); Lee et al. [2002](#page-16-0)), suggesting that comparison of sediment chemistry between years is unaffected by temporal changes in substrate type.

Concentrations of Cu measured during year 1 was not significantly different from baseline (z=0.533, P=0.2970), but decreased significantly during year 2 (z=1.836, P=0.0332). Mercury concentrations decreased significantly during remediation compared to baseline (e.g., year 1, z=2.201, P=0.0139; year 2, z=2.380, P=0.0086) and continued to decrease during year 3 (Figs. 7 and 8).

Almost all sediment metal concentrations were below CCME PEL concentrations across all stations, except for Pb at stations close to Muggah Creek, Cu at station 1–4 in 2011, and Zn at stations 1–2 and 1–4

during 2011. The PEL concentration for Pb ( $112 \mu g g^{-1}$ ) was exceeded at stations 4–1, 1–1, 1–2, and 1–3. Most stations exceeded CCME ISQG concentrations for other metals. Only Cd concentrations were below ISQGs ( $0.7 \mu g g^{-1}$ ) at all but stations 4–1, 1–3, and 1–2 (Fig. 7).

Despite these results, metal concentrations in Sydney Harbour are lower than other industrial harbors in North America (e.g., Tay et al. 2003), and several were below background concentrations reported for coastal sediments in Nova Scotia (Loring et al. 1996). Historical inputs of metal contaminants were likely derived from atmospheric sources from coal combustion when the coking and steel facility was operating (J. Smith, personal communication, March 17, 2011), which is also supported by the sediment metal concentration data which tend to decrease less sharply from Muggah Creek. For example, elevated Pb and Zn concentrations in area 1 stations were consistent with Pb and Zn

Overall, ranges of total PAH concentrations in surface sediments measured in Sydney Harbour between 2009 and 2012 (1.4–73.8  $\mu g g^{-1}$ ) were at least an order of magnitude lower than ranges determined by Smith et al. (2009) from geochronologies of sediment cores representing concentrations from the 1960s to the 1980s (200–500 µgg<sup>-1</sup>). Their study also predicted that total PAH concentrations in surface sediments would eventually decrease to levels between 5 and 80 μgg<sup>-1</sup> by 2010 (comparable to ranges observed in our study), as a result of natural containment by less contaminated sediments. Another study by Tay et al. (2003) in Sydney Harbour reported total PAH concentrations in sediments collected in 1999 were an order of magnitude higher  $(17-246 \mu g g^{-1})$  than those measured in our study. Furthermore, total PAH concentrations in our study were comparable to those in Halifax Harbour (20–27  $\mu g g^{-1}$ ) (Gearing et al. 1991; Tay et al. 1992). Total PAH concentrations in our reference stations  $(1.4-4.5 \mu g g^{-1})$  were also comparable to background levels measured in the Gulf of Maine (1  $\mu g g^{-1}$ ) by Laflamme and Hites (1978).

The increase in total PAH concentrations in sediments during remediation could have occurred because of onsite releases from remedial activities, but the expected signature from a point source such as the STP would have resulted in a more localized increase in concentrations (i.e., centered on area 1 stations) rather than at all stations as evidenced in our results. One possible explanation for the increase in concentrations across all stations (specifically during year 1) could have been caused by resuspension of contaminated sediments as a result of major storms. For example, there were five major storms and hurricanes between 2009 and 2010 (Dillon Consulting Limited 2012). Also, results during baseline could have been "unusually" low due to burial from less contaminated sediments during major storm activity. However, absolute total PAH concentrations during year 1 (2010) were low compared to historical levels. Subsequent measurements of PAHs in 2011 and 2012 revealed that overall concentrations were lower than 2010, but still significantly different from baseline concentrations, although the observed changes in grain size at area 2 stations support the possibility of storm-induced resuspension.

In order to help explain the increase in total PAH concentrations in surface sediments across all stations during remediation, the flux of PAHs leaving the STP

site via Muggah Creek was estimated (Dillon Consulting Limited 2012). The total efflux was estimated as the product of total water outflow multiplied by the average PAH concentration in surface water measured at the mouth of Muggah Creek (1.1  $\mu$ gL<sup>-1</sup>). Approximately 100 kg year<sup>-1</sup> of PAHs was estimated to have left Muggah Creek during year 1 and only 20 kg year<sup>-1</sup> during year 2. These estimated effluxes are considerably lower than the 777 kgyear<sup>-1</sup> estimated in 2001 by JDAC (2002). Analysis by Lee et al. (2002) at that time also indicated that the concentration of PAHs in the harbor was, in fact, decreasing. Therefore, an increase of PAH concentrations in harbor sediment stations based on a flux 20-100 kgyear<sup>-1</sup> would not be expected. Further monitoring of total PAH concentrations and fluxes relative to distance from Muggah Creek may lead to a better understanding of the actual transport mechanism.

A further complicating factor in the variation in total PAH concentration may be the presence of coal dust observed in some sediment, particularly those close to the bulk coal unloading wharf at Whitney Pier, located 400 m from the mouth of Muggah Creek. Sediments sampled in 2011 near the coal loading facility revealed PAH concentrations were approximately double the maximum values measured in this study (i.e., 137.8 μgg<sup>-1</sup>) (Dillon Consulting Limited 2012). Coal dust (or "black carbon") contamination in marine sediments may be responsible for high PAH and TOC concentrations, but may not result in pollution impacts (i.e., negative biological effects) (Chapman et al. 2009; Yan et al. 2009). Black carbon (e.g., soot, coal, coke) has a high binding affinity for PAHs and even small amounts in bottom or suspended sediments can dramatically reduce bioavailability and toxicity of PAHs. For example, Paine et al. (1996) reported that PAHs associated with coal and coke from an aluminum smelter had limited bioavailability and, hence, low toxicity. Although the specific causes for significant increases in PAH concentrations in sediments during remediation compared to baseline remain uncertain, the absolute concentrations are low compared to historical levels. This slight increase represents a short-term interruption in the overall natural recovery of sediments in Sydney Harbour, but the detection of these changes attests to the effectiveness of MNR (Smith et al. 2009; Walker et al. 2013a).

On the Pacific coast, concentrations of PCBs have been observed in contaminated sediment as high as 8.4  $\mu g g^{-1}$ , from industrial sources in British Columbia (Environment Canada 1994), whereas maximum PCB levels of 0.04  $\mu g g^{-1}$  measured in sediment cores from San Francisco Bay (Venkatesan et al. 1999) are more typical where PCB inputs are related to general urbanization rather than to specific industrial inputs.

Assessment of remediation activities and confirmation of modeled predictions of natural sediment recovery

Ongoing recovery of sediments may have been partially enhanced following recent channel and berth dredging activities (October 2011 to January 2012). Dredging was required for a new marine container terminal in Sydney Harbour, which will accommodate large Post-Panamax size ships (Jacques Whitford 2009). While these types of dredging and infilling operations often result in major sediment disturbances, such as resuspension, remobilization, and enhanced bioavailability of historical contaminants (Eggleton and Thomas 2004), it was found that these dredging operations remobilized less contaminated sediment from the outer harbor which increased rates of natural sediment recovery near Muggah Creek (Walker et al. 2013a).

Our sediment trap data collected during dredging indicated enhanced recovery of sediments. For example, measured individual PAH concentrations were at least an order of magnitude lower at station 2–3 following dredging activities compared to pre-dredging (e.g., phenanthrene and pyrene concentrations were 0.81 and 0.98 µgg<sup>-1</sup> pre-dredging and 0.069 and 0.071 µgg<sup>-1</sup> post-dredging, respectively).

The primary recovery processes for MNR are natural sediment burial and contaminant transformation to less toxic forms (Magar and Wenning 2006). Several MNR techniques may be used to demonstrate natural recovery processes including measurement of sediment stability, contaminant source control, contaminant burial, contaminant transformation, ecological recovery, and long-term risk reduction (Wenning et al. 2006).

Sediment stability is affected by many factors such as porosity, organic content, grain size, and bioturbation (e.g., Walker et al. 2008b; Walker and Grant 2009). Therefore, quantifying sediment transport and erosion thresholds in coastal systems becomes an important predictive tool in order to understand fluxes of organic matter, particulates, and associated contaminants (Walker and Grant 2009). The apparent attenuation of contaminants and primary recovery processes has been the result of natural sediment burial and contaminant transformation (e.g., microbial degradation in the case of PAH compounds) and weathering to less toxic forms.

Therefore, if natural containment is sufficiently rapid and effective, it may not be necessary to

undertake large investments in capital and environmental resource management to clean harbor sediments, such as the use of more aggressive remedies such as capping, dredging, and the use of amendments. Natural recovery processes are likely to provide the most cost-effective solution for sediments in Sydney Harbour and MNR would support future management decisions (Walker et al. 2013a).

Interestingly, Smith et al. (2009) projected concentration ranges of Pb, PAH, and PCBs for 2010 that were comparable to those measured here and by Walker et al. (2013a). For example, they reported a projected range of concentrations of Pb in surface sediments by 2010 (25–110  $\mu g g^{-1}$ ), following natural sediment containment, which compared favorably to the minimum and maximum range of measured Pb concentrations of 31–210  $\mu g g^{-1}$  in our study (Fig. 7). A similar pattern also emerged with PAH concentrations between predicted (5–80  $\mu g g^{-1}$ ) and measured values (1.4–73.8  $\mu g g^{-1}$  [this study, Fig. 9a]), and similar with PCB concentrations between predicted (0.02–0.4  $\mu g g^{-1}$ ) and measured values in this study, except for 3.8  $\mu g g^{-1}$  measured at station 1–3 in 2010 (Fig. 10a).

As the harbor sediments become less contaminated, they become more hospitable for benthic infauna, as evidenced by our subtidal benthic community indicator monitoring (Dillon Consulting Limited 2010). This resulted in greater abundance and more diverse benthic communities (species richness) in Sydney Harbour, when compared to studies in similar areas in 1999-2000 (Stewart et al. 2002). Increasing concentrations of benthic infauna may result in higher rates of bioturbation and the reworking of oxygen, nutrients, and contaminants from deeper sediments to surface sediments, thereby potentially slowing the rates of natural remediation (Nedwell and Walker 1995; Simpson et al. 1998). Measurement of ecological recovery over time can be much more challenging than measuring sediment recovery over time, because ecological recovery tends to be slower and more difficult to quantify. Increases or decreases in benthic communities may not necessarily indicate changes in surface sediment chemistry (e.g., Stewart et al. 2002). Instead, they may indicate changes in exposure-mediated environmental loadings not related to sediments (Magar and Wenning 2006). In other words, decreases in environmental loadings due to natural recovery may not be immediately reflected in decreased biological concentration trends, if these trends are overshadowed by other influences

![](_page_14_Picture_11.jpeg)

<span id="page-15-0"></span>such as sewage effluent or reduced oxygen concentration (Stewart et al. 2002).

Assessments of the time-dependent performance of S/S at remediated sites on metals, PCBs, PAHs, and coal tar constituents concluded that former industrial sites improved environmental conditions to within acceptable limits for most contaminants (Paria and Yuet 2006; ITRC 2011). This study presents data in support of the positive impacts (for most parameters) on Sydney Harbour sediments as a result of stringent onsite mitigative measures implemented during S/S remediation activities at the STP site and of natural sediment recovery predictions made by Smith et al. (2009).

While sediment contaminant concentrations are lower than those previously reported, most of the contaminant inventory remains intact below the sediment surface, with the potential risk for future contaminant disturbance or resuspension (Wenning et al. 2006). The greatest risk of potential sediment erosion would be expected during high-energy conditions, including storms, flood events, and human disturbances, especially ship wake from cruise ships, which can be significant in Sydney Harbour.

Despite several regulatory exceedances for sediment contaminants, there were no comparable effects observed in marine organisms. For example, there was a lack of chemical signature in annual tissue assessments of blue mussels (Mytilus edulis) and rock crabs (Cancer irroratus) (Walker et al. 2013b, c). Contaminants measured in blue mussels were below the reported Canadian Food Inspection Agency levels (Walker et al. 2013c) and rock crab contaminant concentrations were much lower than those reported by Chou et al. (2002) in rock crabs analyzed from stations in the inner Bay of Fundy (Walker et al. 2013b). Other studies have shown similar ecological patterns during periods of natural attenuation of historical contaminated sediments. For example, Leipe et al. (2005) found that PAH and metal-enriched sediments in the Western Baltic became less toxic to the benthic ocean quahog (Arctica islandica) if left undisturbed during natural attenuation and decrease in bioavailability of contaminants. Historical contaminant levels that resulted in the closure of the commercial lobster fishery in the inner Sydney Harbour in 1982 (Hilderbrand 1982; Uthe and Musial 1986) now appear to be present at much lower concentrations. For example, PAH concentrations in rock crab hepatopancreas tissue was undetectable ( $<0.05 \mu gg^{-1}$ ) and are much lower than those reported in lobster by Ernst et al. (1999) which were up to 13  $\mu$ g g<sup>-1</sup> (Walker et al. 2013b).

![](_page_15_Picture_6.jpeg)

The annual monitoring of most marine sediment parameters in Sydney Harbour has not detected evidence of substantial contaminant releases into the marine environment as a result of remediation activities at the STP site. However, total PAH concentrations in sediments increased significantly during remediation compared to baseline monitoring, but absolute concentrations were lower compared to historical levels. This slight increase represents only a short-term interruption in the overall natural recovery of sediments in Sydney Harbour despite the land-based remediation activities at the STP site. These recent ground truthed results of sediment chemistry support the modeled predictions made by Smith et al. (2009) of natural containment of the main inventory of contaminants deposited during the 1960s-1980s by layers of cleaner surficial sediments. The continuing trend of natural sediment containment may have also been enhanced as a result of recent dredging activities in Sydney Harbour. Further monitoring of these ecological indicators becomes increasingly important, whether the aim is to assess impacts of industrial activities (e.g., remediation of a former industrial sites or harbor dredging activities) or to gauge the rates of natural sediment recovery using MNR.
