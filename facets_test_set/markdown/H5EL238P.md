However, changes in measures such as species abundance or diversity are driven by cumulative responses of individuals to disturbance, and may take time to detect. Quantifying individual responses could allow us to foresee and mitigate future population declines resulting from industrial activities, while providing ecologically informative indices to assess quality of reclaimed land. We examined life-history and phenotypic traits of mountain bluebirds (Sialia currucoides) and tree swallows (Tachycineta bicolor) breeding on reclaimed copper mine lands in Canada over two years in comparison to a nearby undisturbed reference area. Bluebirds feed on terrestrial invertebrates, whereas swallows feed on adult forms of insects with aquatic larvae, allowing us to assess quality of both reclaimed terrestrial and aquatic systems as habitat for insectivorous birds. Supplemental feeding of bluebirds also was used to experimentally assess nutritional limitation of birds feeding on terrestrial invertebrates. Bluebirds on reclaimed land initiated clutches later, and in one year had lower fledging success compared to birds on the reference area. Tree swallows also bred later in the season on reclaimed land, but were otherwise comparable to or exceeded performance of birds on the reference area. Annual differences in responses of nestling bluebirds on the mine to supplemental feeding revealed an apparent switch in lifehistory strategy of parents between years, from brood reduction to brood survival, suggesting greater annual fluctuations in ecological conditions within terrestrial systems on reclaimed land. Sex differences in response of nestling bluebirds to food supplementation additionally suggested high within-brood competition for food on reclaimed land. #### 1. Introduction

Studies assessing ecological impacts of industrial activities on wildlife typically examine population- or community-level responses, such as species abundance or diversity. However, these measures are ultimately driven by the cumulative responses of individual organisms to environmental disturbance or degradation, and it may take time before effects at the population or community level are detected. For example, female northern goshawks (Accipiter gentilis) may skew brood sex ratios toward less-costly sons in food-limited habitats (daughters are larger, requiring more resources to produce; [Ingraldi, 2005\)](#page-7-0); the long-term population-level effect of this response, if sustained over multiple breeding seasons, may be a reduction in the number of breeding females, and hence population productivity, in future years. Anthropogenic noise has been associated with localized changes in age structure and pairing success of males [\(Habib et al., 2007](#page-7-0)), and with alterations in song ([Francis et al., 2011](#page-7-0)), and reproductive output [\(Halfwerk et al., 2011\)](#page-7-0) of several avian species. Birds may be more susceptible to parasites or pathogens in disturbed environments; for example, nests of tree swallows (Tachycineta bicolor) on reclaimed oil sands wetlands harbored more parasitic blow flies compared to nests on reference areas, and this was associated with reduced body condition of nestlings [\(Gentes et al., 2007\)](#page-7-0). Individual responses such as these, if detected early, may allow us to foresee and mitigate future wildlife population declines resulting from industrial activities [\(Clutton-Brock and](#page-7-0) [Sheldon, 2010\)](#page-7-0).

Conducting individual-level studies may be deemed impractical for assessing land reclamation success, given the time and financial constraints associated with industrial environmental assessments. There is increasing evidence, however, that some measures of individual quality or condition are especially sensitive to environmental degradation, and so may respond quickly to changes in habitat quality. For example, the quality of condition-dependent traits which have evolved through sexual selection may decline rapidly under poor environmental conditions [\(Cockburn et al., 2008](#page-7-0)). Some of the best demonstrations of this are found in studies of plumage coloration in male birds, and these ornamental traits can therefore be important indicators of environmental health [\(Hill, 1995; Eeva et al., 2008\)](#page-7-0). Identification and measurement of sensitive individual traits, however, has rarely been considered in the context of land reclamation or industrial impact assessment.

In this study, we investigated individual life-history and phenotypic traits of mountain bluebirds (Sialia currucoides) and tree swallows breeding in nest boxes on reclaimed mine tailings and waste rock within the Highland Valley Copper (HVC) operating area in British Columbia, Canada over two years. Mountain bluebirds feed primarily on insects gleaned from vegetation or the ground, whereas tree swallows are aerial insectivores, feeding predominantly on adult forms of invertebrates with aquatic larval stages [\(Power and Lombardo, 1996; McCarty,](#page-7-0) [1997; Winkler et al., 2011\)](#page-7-0). By including both species in this study, we were able to assess the capacity of reclaimed tailings, waste rock and tailings ponds to support a variety of wildlife species, across a wider trophic range than would be possible by studying a single species. For comparison with birds on the HVC area, we also studied birds breeding on a nearby reference (REF) area, which has not historically been affected by mining operations. To experimentally investigate food availability as a specific factor affecting reproductive investment and offspring quality in mountain bluebirds on each study area, we additionally incorporated a food supplementation manipulation over both years. The goals of this study were to use measures of avian life-history and phenotypic traits to assess the effectiveness of existing mine reclamation protocols and to identify biologically relevant and informative indices of avian reproductive investment and phenotypic traits that can be costeffectively measured by industry to assess land reclamation success.

#### 2. Methods

### 2.1. Study areas and field procedures

The Highland Valley Copper mine is a ~7000 ha open pit copper and molybdenum mining operation in south-central British Columbia, Canada. This study was conducted within the HVC Bethlehem Tailings and Reclaim area (elevation 1300–1550 m above sea level), which consists of reclaimed tailings and waste rock that have been enriched by application of biosolids and seeded with a grass-legume mix ([Gardner,](#page-7-0) [2004\)](#page-7-0). Patches of conifer forest are present in waste rock areas, and the margins of the site border on mature second growth mixed-wood forest. Several reclaimed tailings ponds and pit lakes are also present on the site ([Hamaguchi et al., 2008](#page-7-0)). Mining on the study area occurred from the 1960s to the 1980s, and most of the land reclamation has been carried out since 1990 [\(Jones, 2001\)](#page-7-0). The primary end land use goal for the site is cattle and wildlife grazing and forage production ([Freberg and](#page-7-0) [Gould Gizikoff, 1999\)](#page-7-0).

Nest boxes were installed on fence posts throughout the HVC study area in 2001, and have been annually monitored to record nesting activities of mountain bluebirds (MOBL) and tree swallows (TRES). Reproductive data such as clutch size and overall nest success suggest that reproductive output for both species is comparable to that of populations breeding at similar elevations in undisturbed habitats ([Howie,](#page-7-0) [2006; Highland Valley Copper, 2011](#page-7-0)). However, there is currently no comprehensive information on the reproductive investment or success of birds that are attracted to reclaimed lands on HVC, or how offspring quality (e.g. size at fledging) compares to that of birds breeding in areas not affected by mining operations.

The REF area consisted of established nest box trails located 10– 30 km from the HVC site, at elevations of 1100–1400 m above sea level. Average ambient temperatures in nest boxes on the REF area in 2013 were approximately 1 °C higher throughout the breeding season compared to boxes on HVC, but minimum temperatures did not differ between areas (unpublished data). Nest boxes on the REF area were mounted on fence posts or snags in habitat types varying from clearcuts, agricultural crop and grazing land as well as open grasslands bordered by mature mixed-wood or conifer forests. We monitored a total of 165 boxes on HVC and 194 boxes on the REF area during 2012 and 2013. On both study areas, boxes were typically mounted in pairs approximately 5 m apart, to allow both MOBL and TRES access to nest sites with minimal interspecific competition. Beginning in early May, we checked boxes every day to determine clutch initiation date and clutch size. Completed clutches were subsequently checked daily beginning 12 days after clutch completion to determine hatching date (designated nestling day 0) and hatching success of each egg in the clutch. Once all fertile eggs in a clutch had hatched, we weighed each nestling using an electronic balance (on day 1 or 2 for MOBL, day 2 for TRES, hereafter called hatching mass). When nestling MOBL were 15 days old and TRES were 16 days old, we measured mass (nearest 0.125 g, using a spring balance), and lengths of the longest primary wing feather (eighth in MOBL, ninth in TRES; nearest 0.5 mm with a ruler) and of the combined head and bill (as a measure of structural size; nearest 0.01 mm with digital calipers).We used a non-toxic pen to mark the tarsus of each nestling with a unique color combination when they were weighed after hatching, and refreshed marks throughout the nestling period so we were able to match each bird measured on day 15 or 16 to their hatching mass (see [Section 2.3](#page-2-0) (Data Analysis)). The phenotypic <span id="page-2-0"></span>measures we assessed are important indicators of avian fitness and may reflect diet quality. For example, body mass of songbirds prior to fledging is positively associated with recruitment and reproductive success [\(Perrins and McCleery, 2001](#page-7-0)), flight feather length predicts age at fledging ([Michaud and Leonard, 2000](#page-7-0)), and structural size is influenced by dietary minerals such as calcium ([Dawson and Bidwell, 2005\)](#page-7-0). We also determined the sex of nestling MOBL at day 15 based on color of the primary wing feathers [\(O'Brien and Dawson, 2009\)](#page-7-0). Adult birds were captured in nest boxes with swing-door traps while they fed nestlings, and we documented age of female TRES as second year (SY) or after second year (ASY) using plumage coloration [\(Hussell, 1983](#page-7-0)). Nests were then visited once nestlings had fledged to determine the proportion of the brood that fledged.

#### 2.2. Food supplementation experiment

Once nest construction had neared completion, breeding pairs of MOBL were assigned to either a food supplement or control treatment. Supplemented birds were provided with 12 g of live mealworms and waxmoth larvae daily, representing approximately 50% of the daily energy requirements of an adult bluebird during breeding [\(Mock, 1991;](#page-7-0) [Merkle and Barclay, 1996](#page-7-0)), which we have previously shown to affect reproductive investment ([O'Brien and Dawson, 2011, 2013\)](#page-7-0). Food was provided in a small dish installed within 1 m of the nest box, and feeding continued until nestlings were 17 days old. Control boxes had dishes installed in the same manner and were visited daily but were not provided with additional food. MOBL defend territories of up to 5 ha [\(Power and Lombardo, 1996\)](#page-7-0), and birds in neighboring territories would therefore not have had access to supplemental food.

#### 2.3. Data analysis

We examined clutch initiation date and clutch size as measures of primary reproductive investment by female birds. For all subsequent analyses, we excluded nests that were depredated because we were interested in the ability of birds to fledge young within ecological constraints other than predation. We examined hatching and fledging success, respectively, to test whether overall success of birds breeding on reclaimed mine lands was driven by processes occurring specifically at the incubation or offspring rearing stage of breeding. Measures of hatching success included probability of hatching at least one egg, and for nests in which at least one egg hatched, we also investigated proportion of the clutch that hatched. We similarly examined fledging success of nests with young by first assessing the probability of at least one young fledging, and then for broods that fledged at least one young, we analyzed the proportion of the brood that fledged.

All analyses of binary data used logistic regression, while continuous variables were analyzed using general linear or linear mixed models (LMM). Initial models included study area (HVC or REF) and year (2012 or 2013) as fixed factors. Analyses of reproductive success also included food supplementation treatment (MOBL) or female age (TRES) as additional fixed factors, and clutch initiation date as a covariate. For analyses of MOBL offspring phenotype we also included nestling sex as a fixed factor. Declines in offspring quality with timing of breeding have been well documented in TRES (e.g., [Wardrop and Ydenberg,](#page-7-0) [2003](#page-7-0)), so we included hatching date as a covariate in all analyses of TRES offspring performance. Relative position of individual nestlings within the size hierarchy established at hatching can have persistent effects on growth and size throughout the nestling period [\(Bitton et al.,](#page-7-0) [2006\)](#page-7-0); we therefore standardized hatching mass of each nestling relative to its brood mean, and included this relative hatching mass as a covariate in analyses of offspring phenotype. All first-order interactions were initially included in all models. In addition, to test for annual variation in resource availability (and hence annual variation in sensitivity of MOBL to supplemental food) on each study area, we initially included a Year\*Area\*Supplement interaction in LMM analyses of MOBL offspring performance, and we also included a Sex\*Area\*Supplement interaction to test for sex-specific responses of MOBL nestlings to food supplementation on each area. Brood identity was entered as a random effect in LMM analyses of offspring phenotypic traits to account for common brood environment and maternal effects among siblings. Nonsignificant terms were removed using a stepwise procedure. Known second broods produced by the same breeding pair after successfully fledging young within the same breeding season were excluded from all analyses. Means are presented ±1 SE, all statistical tests are twotailed, and the significance level was set at P = 0.05. Analyses were performed using SPSS 16.0 [\(SPSS Inc., 2007\)](#page-7-0).

### 3. Results

#### 3.1. Female reproductive investment

Nonetheless, effects of supplemental feeding on clutch initiation dates of MOBL did not vary by study area (Area\*Supp: P = 0.40). ## 3.2. Reproductive success

We assessed whether or not at least one egg hatched for 148 MOBL clutches; of these, only 14 failed to hatch, and there were no differences in hatching success between study areas (Table 2). There was also no effect of food supplementation on probability of successfully hatching, and no differences in relation to year or timing of breeding (Table 2). Hatching probability for 200 TRES clutches varied with clutch initiation date, but this effect depended on study area (Table 2). Analyzing each study area separately revealed that hatching success did not vary with timing of breeding on the REF area (P = 0.26), but increased with later dates of clutch initiation on HVC (Wald = 3.69, P = 0.05, Estimate = 0.308 ± 0.2).

For 131 MOBL nests that hatched at least one young, the probability of fledging at least one young was higher on the REF area than HVC, and on both study areas was increased by supplemental feeding (Table 2). Fledging probability was also greater in 2013 compared to 2012, and showed a marginal increase with clutch initiation date (Table 2). Analysis of 185 nests of TRES that hatched at least one young showed similar annual variation in fledging probability, with higher success in 2013 than 2012, but we also detected an interaction between study area and clutch initiation date (Table 2). In contrast to the probability of hatching, when we separated the data by study area we found that the probability of TRES fledging young on reclaimed mine land decreased with timing of breeding (Wald = 8.35, P = 0.004, Estimate = −0.13 ± 0.05) but did not vary with clutch initiation date on the REF area (P = 0.97).

For body mass, eighth primary feather length, and head-bill length of 625 nestling MOBL prior to fledging, we detected interactions between study area and food supplementation that varied both by year and nestling sex (Table 4). There was no effect of supplementation on mass or feather length of MOBL on the REF area in either year, although nestlings in supplemented broods were structurally larger than controls; however, on HVC, supplemental feeding of birds had opposing effects in each year [\(Fig. 3](#page-5-0)). In 2012, nestling MOBL in supplemented broods on HVC were structurally smaller, weighed less and developed shorter flight feathers than control broods on HVC and both treatment groups on the REF area. In contrast, supplementation in 2013 increased all nestling measures on HVC relative to control broods, and in fact allowed nestlings to attain body size and mass equivalent to that of both treatment groups on the REF area [\(Fig. 3\)](#page-5-0). In addition, female nestlings raised in supplemented broods on HVC were lighter, smaller and had shorter flight feathers than females in control broods, whereas there were no effects of supplementation on male nestlings raised on HVC (Table 4; [Fig. 4](#page-5-0)). In contrast, supplementation increased mass and head-bill length of male and female nestlings equally on the REF area [\(Fig. 4\)](#page-5-0).

Body mass of nestling TRES (N = 799) decreased with hatching date more strongly on the HVC area, and more so in 2013 than 2012, although birds raised on HVC were overall heavier by day 16 than on the REF area (main effect of Area, Area\*Hatch Date and Year\*Hatch Date interactions; [Table 5](#page-6-0)). Length of outer primary feathers at day 16 did not vary by hatching date, but increased more strongly with relative position in the brood size hierarchy for nestling TRES raised on REF than on HVC (Area\*Hatch Mass; [Table 5\)](#page-6-0). Head-bill length of nestling TRES was marginally greater overall on HVC than REF, and declined with hatching date more strongly in 2013 (main effect of Area, Year\*Hatch Date interaction; [Table 5](#page-6-0)).

## 4. Discussion

We studied life-history and phenotypic traits of MOBL and TRES breeding in nest boxes on reclaimed mine tailings and waste rock areas over two years to assess the quality of reclaimed terrestrial and aquatic systems as habitat for insectivorous birds. This study allowed us to identify variables that are most sensitive to variation in environmental quality, and hence that may be valuable early indicators of land reclamation success or environmental disturbance. MOBL breeding on reclaimed mine lands initiated egg laying later in the season, and in one year were both less likely to fledge young once eggs had hatched, and fledged a smaller proportion of young in their broods compared to birds on the REF area. We did not detect any differences in clutch size or hatching success between study areas; in combination, these results suggest that costs associated with egg production and incubation are not differentially experienced by MOBL on reclaimed mine lands, whereas in some years, MOBL breeding on these reclaimed lands have more difficulty rearing young to independence. Hence, measuring avian clutch size alone will not allow industry to differentiate between high- and low-quality reclaimed terrestrial environments, and we suggest that the focus of reclamation assessment should instead be on clutch initiation date as a measure of female avian reproductive investment that represents a more sensitive indicator of environmental quality.

Similar to MOBL, TRES breeding on reclaimed mine lands initiated clutches later in the season. Earliest clutches of TRES on HVC were less likely to hatch; however, TRES breeding on HVC produced heavier and marginally larger young than birds breeding on the REF area, and fledging success did not differ between study areas in either year. Additionally, offspring phenotype was more strongly influenced by timing of breeding and position of individual nestlings within the brood size hierarchy at hatching on surrounding reference areas than on reclaimed mine lands (Table 5), suggesting more consistent food availability over the breeding season, and lower within-brood resource competition, for aerial insectivores on HVC. Aerial insectivores such as swallows typically feed on prey with an aquatic larval phase [\(McCarty, 1997\)](#page-7-0); hence, these results indicate that conditions on reclaimed mine lands early in the season are generally less favourable for birds relying on aquatic invertebrate food resources, but resource availability for TRES is equivalent or greater on HVC compared to the REF area once birds reach the brood-rearing phase. Given that reclamation of aquatic ecosystems on HVC included the addition of aquatic invertebrates from nearby undisturbed systems [\(Hamaguchi et al., 2008\)](#page-7-0), our results suggest that this approach has effectively restored aquatic systems to a level that can support breeding of aerial insectivores such as TRES.

According to life-history theory, one of the most common trade-offs for organisms during reproduction is between number and quality of young ([Stearns, 1992\)](#page-7-0). Under conditions of food limitation, parents may focus their investment on those young with the greatest fitness value, to the detriment of the remaining offspring (e.g., [Howe, 1978](#page-7-0)). Between breeding attempts or seasons, songbirds may exhibit switches between brood reduction and brood survival strategies in response to variation in environmental conditions, particularly food availability (e.g., [Du et al., 2014\)](#page-7-0). Our supplemental feeding experiment revealed an apparent switch in life-history strategy of adult MOBL on reclaimed mine lands between study years, evidenced by changes in offspring phenotype in response to supplemental feeding of parents. In 2012, the year with later clutch initiation dates and overall reduced fledging success, the majority of offspring produced by supplemented parents on HVC exhibited reduced size and mass prior to fledging [\(Fig. 3\)](#page-5-0). This suggests that MOBL on HVC followed a brood reduction strategy by concentrating their feeding efforts on only the largest or most vocal young in the brood, to the detriment of all other nestlings, or by simply switching to self-maintenance when given access to supplemental food. In contrast, in 2013, supplemental food appeared to have been distributed more equitably among siblings to promote brood survival, as supplemental feeding of parents on HVC allowed nestlings to collectively achieve a mass and size at fledging comparable to all nestlings produced on the REF area, and which exceeded control broods on HVC [\(Fig. 3\)](#page-5-0). Consequently, MOBL on HVC in 2013 were also able to fledge a greater proportion of young in their broods [\(Fig. 2](#page-4-0)). These results provide further evidence for greater annual fluctuations in ecological conditions such as natural availability of terrestrial invertebrate food sources on reclaimed mine lands compared to habitats on reference areas located at similar elevations and exposed to similar climatic conditions, and highlight that inter-annual unpredictability or variability may be an important metric by which to assess quality of reclaimed terrestrial systems as habitat for wildlife.

For all nestling MOBL measures, female young raised on reclaimed mine land showed a decline in performance when parents were provided with supplemental food, whereas both sexes consistently benefited from supplementation on the REF area [\(Fig. 4](#page-5-0)). Indeed, this differential response of male and female nestlings to supplementation on reclaimed land resulted in sexual dimorphism in body mass by day 15 that was not evident under control conditions or on the REF area ([Fig. 4a](#page-5-0)). As with annual variation in food supplementation effects on offspring phenotype in MOBL on reclaimed mine land, this suggests greater withinbrood competition for limited resources on HVC: parent MOBL may preferentially feed supplemental food to sons on HVC, either selectively, or simply because male nestlings are more competitive than female siblings (due in part to higher levels of circulating androgen hormones; [von Engelhardt et al., 2006\)](#page-7-0). This further suggests that parent birds experience stronger trade-offs between offspring number and quality on reclaimed mine land, and so tend to exhibit a brood reduction strategy of focusing investment on young with the greatest apparent fitness value ([Howe, 1978; Stearns, 1992](#page-7-0)). In this context, such a strategy resulted in sexual dimorphism in offspring mass in response to experimental food supplementation [\(Fig. 4a](#page-5-0)). We therefore suggest that sexspecific responses to parental supplementation, and nestling growth responses to supplementation over multiple breeding seasons, may represent a sensitive index of environmental quality on reclaimed lands that effectively gauges avian parental strategies induced by variation in natural resource availability.

# 5. Conclusions

Our study showed that while reproductive success of TRES breeding on reclaimed mine lands was comparable to that of TRES breeding on the reference area, some measures of the performance of MOBL on reclaimed land were lower, and our results for MOBL additionally suggest greater annual fluctuations in ecological conditions within terrestrial systems on reclaimed land. Taken together, reproductive investment and success as well as offspring phenotypic traits of MOBL in this study indicate that additional reclamation measures, or time, may be necessary to convert tailings and waste rock areas on HVC to ecologically productive grassland systems for insectivorous birds. Nonetheless, in this specific study system, we suggest that further studies of other songbirds that similarly rely on terrestrial invertebrate food sources during the breeding season could measure those traits identified here in MOBL as showing the greatest sensitivity to environmental differences between HVC and REF areas. More generally, adaptively altering approaches to land reclamation to maximize the productivity and resilience of resulting ecosystems (Sgro et al., 2011) requires an enhanced ability to detect variation in environmental quality and assess the success of land reclamation protocols using environmentally sensitive and ecologically informative indices. Results of this study show that specific measures of avian lifehistory and phenotypic traits, such as timing of breeding, fledging success and offspring mass, size and feather length prior to fledging, particularly when assessed over multiple years and in the context of experimental approaches such as food supplementation, may be informative indicators of the health of reclaimed terrestrial and aquatic systems. We also suggest that it will be important for industries to collect similar data on appropriate reference areas, to provide benchmarks against which to assess performance of birds on reclaimed land. In combination, these approaches will contribute to the development of specific ecological assessment protocols for reclaimed land that are informed by life-history theory.
