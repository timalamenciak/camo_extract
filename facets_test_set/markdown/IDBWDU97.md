# Seven year remediation study at the Carrie Bay Atlantic salmon (Salmo salar) farm in the Broughton Archipelago, British Columbia, Canada

Kenneth M. Brooksa,\*, Annette R. Stierns <sup>a</sup> , Clare Backman<sup>b</sup>

a Aquatic Environmental Sciences, 644 Old Eaglemount Road, Port Townsend, WA 98368, USA <sup>b</sup> Stolt Sea Farms, 1761 Redwood Street, Campbell River, BC, Canada V9W 3K7

Received 4 August 2003; received in revised form 10 December 2003; accepted 15 December 2003

#### Abstract

Numerous studies describing the short-term recovery of benthic habitats impacted by organic wastes from commercial salmon farms have been published. A few of these studies have continued for up to 2 years. The Carrie Bay salmon farm in the Broughton Archipelago of British Columbia began operation in 1990 and produced four crops of Atlantic salmon. It was fallowed in 1998 after producing 2587 mt of Atlantic salmon in the preceding production cycle. Significant accumulations of organic waste were observed near the netpens in 1997 when the farm was close to maximum salmon biomass. Bottom sediments were blackened with iron sulfides and gas ebullition together with extensive Beggiatoa mats were observed to a distance of 145 m on the southern transect in 1997. Patchy Beggia remained near the netpens in 1999. Twelve benthic surveys have been completed in Carrie Bay over the last 7 years. The database reported herein describes slow, but steady, remediation of this site. Chemical remediation, including reductions in total volatile solids (TVS), free sulfides, redox potential and zinc were nearly complete at distances >80 m in 2002 and rapid and significant additional chemical remediation was observed throughout the area in 2003. Sediment concentrations of zinc have declined throughout this study at all distances and were approaching background concentrations in 2002 and 2003. Macrofaunal communities at Carrie Bay's reference stations were dominated by mollusks with significantly fewer annelids and very few crustaceans. This community did not respond to organic enrichment in the classic way described by Pearson and Rosenberg [Oceanogr. Mar. Boil. Annu. Rev. 16 (1978) 229], which has accurately predicted the macrobenthic response at many intensive aquaculture facilities. None of the eight opportunistic taxa frequently found proliferating in enriched sediments near B.C. salmon farms were a large component of the Carrie Bay macrobenthos at any place or at any time. Rather, a single annelid (Nephtys cornuta) was found in moderate abundance and several bivalve mollusks that

Biological remediation was nearing completion at distances ≥80 m from the netpens, but was not complete under and at shorter distances in 2002 following 4 years of fallow. Based on regression analysis, it is predicted that chemical remediation (sulfides ≤447 µM) sufficient to support half of the common taxa observed at the local reference station will be complete in 2004 following approximately 65 months in fallow. Unlike four other farms in the Broughton Archipelago, where sulfide concentrations exceeded 20,000 µM, and where chemical remediation has been complete in less than 6 months, sulfide concentrations at Carrie Bay have remained <9410 µM throughout this study. These relatively lower sulfide concentrations; the unique pattern of biological remediation; and the relatively long chemical and biological remediation times observed at Carrie Bay point out the need for a better understanding of the diagenesis of labile carbon compounds in sediments underlying intensive aquaculture operations. A better understanding of the underlying causes of the biochemical processes observed in this study will significantly aid farm operators and regulators in selecting benthic habitats with optimal sustainability for modern salmon farming. In addition to describing the transport of waste, computer programs designed to predict the environmental response to intensive aquaculture must incorporate modules assessing the biogeochemical and biological response to these inputs before their output can predict the unusual responses observed in Carrie Bay.

#### 1. Introduction

Brooks et al. (2003) reviewed the literature describing benthic remediation under and adjacent to Atlantic salmon (*Salmo salar*) farms worldwide and described chemical remediation through several successive production cycles at four highly productive British Columbia salmon farms that had all been in operation for at least 10 years. In each case, the farms were fallowed for short periods (4 to 6 months) between harvest and the introduction of a new year-class. He defined the following terms.

Chemical remediation is the reduction of accumulated organic matter with a concomitant decrease in free sediment sulfide (S<sup>=</sup>) concentrations and an increase in sediment redox potential under and adjacent to salmon farms to levels at which more than half the reference area taxa can recruit and survive.

Biological remediation is defined as the restructuring of the infaunal community to include those taxa whose individual abundance equaled or exceeded 1% of the total invertebrate abundance at local reference stations. Recruitment of rare species representing <1% of the reference abundance is not considered necessary for complete biological remediation.

Based on an analysis of concentrations of free sediment  $S^{=}$  (H<sub>2</sub>S, HS<sup>-</sup>), Brooks et al. (2003) predicted chemical remediation times of  $\leq 6$  months following harvest at the four Broughton Archipelago farms examined. These results were similar to the 185 day recovery, based on benthic respiration rates, reported by Woodward et al. (1992). Short chemical and biological benthic remediation times have also been reported by Ritz et al. (1989) in Tasmania; Brooks (1993) in Washington State and Arrow Pass in British

Columbia [\(Brooks et al., 2003\).](#page-41-0) [Anderson \(1992\)](#page-40-0) studied benthic effects at eight salmon farms in British Columbia and reported substantial recovery from moderate impacts in times as short as 6 months. Significant benthic recovery was observed to occur within 1 month at sites with comparatively high current speeds.

In contrast, [Mahnken \(1993\)](#page-41-0) reported a longer remediation time adjacent to a salmon farm in Clam Bay, Washington that was located in a depositional environment. During fallow, he observed a 3-month period of rapid biological recovery with large increases in macrofaunal abundance and diversity followed by a second period lasting 25 months in which community recovery proceeded more slowly. Species that were rare at the reference location remained absent from the Clam Bay site at the end of his study. [Morrisey et al.](#page-41-0) (2000) described the accumulation and catabolism of salmon farm waste at a New Zealand chinook salmon farm where a maximum current speed of 15 cm/s was recorded and where the minimum 2-h average speed of bottom currents was 1.1 cm/s. Sediments consisted of 33% to 70% mud at this shallow (26 m water depth) depositional site where organic enrichment was restricted to V50 m from the netpen's perimeter. Waste accumulated to depths of 27.5 to 43.1 cm under the farm's footprint and mean calculated remediation times, based on observed carbon catabolic rates varied between 3.3 and 7.5 years. Similarly, [Karakassis et al. \(1999\)](#page-41-0) reported long chemical and biological remediation times of greater than 23 months at a shallow (19 m depth), depositional (71 –72% silt and clay) Grecian site that had been annually producing a small quantity (140 mt) of sea bream and sea bass during the preceding 6 years. The physicochemical results of their study were compounded by significant blooms of autotrophs (benthic diatoms), which would not be expected to flourish in the deeper waters (>30 m) characterizing the enriched footprint at the salmon tenures studied in British Columbia. They reported an abundance of the carbon tolerant opportunist, Capitella cf. capitata, on the perimeter of the farm during the entire 23-month study. The abundance of this opportunist diminished at the 10 m station following 8 months of fallow.

[Morrisey et al. \(2000\)](#page-41-0) reported mean (F1.0 standard error) sediment zinc concentrations of 665F19.5 Ag Zn/g dry sediment under a salmon farm's footprint. The authors did not report temporal changes in zinc concentrations during chemical remediation. They noted that the bioavailability of zinc is decreased in anaerobic conditions and assumed that it would be released from organic carbon and sulfide bound phases resulting in increased toxicity that could inhibit biological recovery. In contrast, [Brooks and Mahnken \(2003a,b\)](#page-40-0) reported that initially high (200 Ag Zn/g dry sediment) concentrations at the Arrow Pass salmon farm in British Columbia declined to background concentrations (25 Ag Zn/g) during 11 months of chemical remediation and had no observable affect on biological remediation.

These reports indicated that benthic recovery times vary significantly from a few months at some sites to a several years at heavily impacted sites. It should be noted that all of these reports described remediation under intensive aquaculture facilities located in depositional environments. [Brooks and Mahnken \(2003a\)](#page-40-0) and [Nash \(2001\)](#page-41-0) reported significantly increased diversity and abundance of macrofauna under and downcurrent from Pacific Northwest salmon farms located over erosional environments characterized by fast currents (up to 125 cm/s) and coarse sediments. These results suggest that remediation times are proportional to at least the depth of accumulated waste, sediment and water physicochemical characteristics and water depths and current speeds.

## 2. Background

Stolt Sea Farm obtained a salmon aquaculture lease in Carrie Bay during 1990 [\(Fig.](#page-4-0) 1). Four crops of Atlantic salmon were raised at the farm prior to an extended fallow period, which began in August 1998. During the last of four production cycles, a total of 3881 mt of feed were used to produce 2587 mt of Atlantic salmon, which were harvested in July 1998. The producer initiated a benthic survey of the dominantly downcurrent southern transect in early 1997 when the farm was near peak biomass. The survey revealed a significant accumulation of waste on the netpen's perimeter. Sediments were black with iron sulfide, smelled strongly of hydrogen sulfide and were covered with patchy mats of the sulfide oxidizing bacterium Beggiatoa. These effects extended, in varying degrees to a distance of between 145 and 185 m south of the netpen's perimeter.

## 3. Methods

A regression approach was used throughout these studies. A dominantly downcurrent vector (196 jT) was identified based on video surveys; a 15 m depth current meter study carried out over one full lunar cycle; and a drifter study [\(Sea Consult](#page-41-0) Marine Research, 1997). Single sediment samples were collected along south bearing transects at 15 to 30 m intervals to a distance of 225 m and three replicate reference station samples were collected during most sample periods at hydrologically isolated locations >500 m from the farm. Three additional transects (023, 113 and 293 jT) were examined in July 1999 to determine the extent of the farm's effects and to confirm that the southern transect was most affected. Assessment methods at this site have included periodic video records coupled with physicochemical and biological surveys. Surveys were conducted at least annually in the summer or fall. Five surveys were completed in 2000 (February, April, June, August and October). Physicochemical and macrofaunal surveys were undertaken in August to October of 2000, 2001 and 2002 to minimize seasonal variation. The last physicochemical survey was completed in April 2003. These surveys are summarized in [Fig. 2](#page-5-0) and [Table 2.](#page-11-0)

#### 3.2. Sample collection

Sediment samples for physicochemical analyses were collected using a Petite Ponar grab with a footprint of 0.0225 m<sup>2</sup>. The upper 2 cm of the entire Petite Ponar grab sample was used for physicochemical analyses as these smaller samples were not used for macrofaunal evaluations. Seventeen samples for physicochemical and macrofaunal analyses were collected with a stainless steel 0.1 m<sup>2</sup> van Veen grab on each sample day in 2000, 2001 and 2002. Acceptable samples met the requirements of the Puget Sound Estuary Program (PSEP, 1996), viz:

- The sampler was deployed at a maximum speed of 30 cm/s.
- A minimum sediment penetration depth of 4 cm was required.
- The sampler contained overlying water, with low turbidity, indicating minimal leakage.
- The retained sediment surface was relatively flat indicating minimal disturbance.

Overlying water was siphoned from the sampler through a 500  $\mu M$  sieve to retain suspended organisms. Surficial sediments (top 2 cm) for physicochemical analyses were removed from not more than 5% of the surface area of the 0.1 m² van Veen grab (PSEP, 1996). Infaunal samples were placed in 5-gal buckets for processing at a shore station on 1.0 mm sieves on the day of collection.

#### 3.3. Sediment core samples

In April 2003, sediment cores were collected from van Veen grab samples using 3.8 cm inside diameter schedule 40 PVC pipe. The ends of each 15 cm tube were sealed with PVC caps, marked to designate the sediment's upper surface and frozen at  $-20~^{\circ}$ C. The sealed core tubes were placed under running cold water for 2 min and the still frozen cores extracted using a plexiglass plunger. The length of the core was measured and the RPD was determined prior to sectioning them into either 1.5 or 2.0 cm deep sections beginning at the sediment's surface. Total S<sup>=</sup> and TVS were measured in each section to assess these parameters as a function of depth beneath the sediment–water interface

#### 3.4. Total volatile solids (TVS)

TVS analysis was accomplished on 50-g surficial sediment samples collected from the top 2 cm of the grab (PSEP, 1996). Samples were dried at  $103\pm2$  °C in aluminum boats that had been tared following combustion at 550 °C for 30 min. Drying was continued until no further weight reduction was observed (generally overnight). The

samples were then combusted at 550 jC for 2 h or until no further weight loss was recorded. TVS were calculated as the difference between the dried and combusted weights. Triplicate analyses were completed on 5% of the samples or on a minimum of one per batch [\(PSEP, 1996\).](#page-41-0)

## 3.5. Sediment grain size (SGS) analysis

SGS was accomplished during the baseline survey and annually thereafter using the method of [Plumb \(1981\).](#page-41-0) The sediments were wet sieved on a 0.064 mm sieve. The fraction retained on the sieve was dried in an oven at 92 jC and sieved on mesh openings of 2.0, 0.89, 0.25, and 0.064 mm. Particles passing the 0.064 mm sieve during wet sieving were analyzed by sinking rates in a column of water (pipette analysis). Triplicate analyses were completed on 5% of the samples with a minimum of one per batch.

## 3.6. Reduction– oxidation potential discontinuity (RPD)

Prior to 1999, sediment redox potential was assessed by measuring the depth of the oxidation – reduction potential discontinuity (RPD). A clear 2-cm diameter corer was inserted into the undisturbed sediment in samples collected for physicochemical analysis. The depth at which the sediment color changed from the background (gray-green) color to black (indicative of iron sulfides associated with anaerobic conditions) was measured in cm.

## 3.7. Benthic infaunal analysis

The entire contents of the 0.1 m<sup>2</sup> infaunal grab samples (including the overlying water) were washed from the grab into a 5-gal bucket using 50 Am filtered seawater. Samples were then sieved on 1.0 mm stainless steel screens. The retained material was placed in 1.0 or 2.0 l HDPE bottles and fixed using 15% buffered formalin in seawater on the day of collection. Each sample jar had matching inside, outside and cap labels. Fixed samples were washed with filtered fresh water and preserved in 70% isopropyl alcohol after 4 days. Infaunal organisms were sorted from the background matrix under 10 magnification. A different technician repicked 20% of each sample. Quality assurance guidelines required a picking efficiency of z95%. Any sample failing this QA benchmark was completely repicked. Infauna was identified to the lowest level practicable—generally to species. All taxa were compared with verified specimens in a reference collection (Aquatic Environmental Sciences), or will be verified by an outside expert.

## 3.8. Macrofaunal wet tissue weights

All annelids were removed from their tubes, which were not included in the weights. The shells of living mollusks were left intact. Wet tissue weights were determined for macrofauna preserved in 70% isopropyl alcohol. The samples were

sieved on tared 100 Am Nytexk screens. The bottom of the sieve was blotted with absorbent tissue and allowed to dry for 60 s prior to weighing on an electronic balance to the nearest 0.1 mg.

## 3.9. Sediment zinc

Sediment zinc concentrations were determined by Analytical Resources in Seattle, Washington using ICP (EPA Method 6010) following strong acid digestion. Quality assurance included the concurrent determination of spiked samples (75 –125% of spike recovered), methods blanks (analyte not detected) and duplicates (one per run with V20% relative percent deviation) as specified in [PSEP \(1996\).](#page-41-0)

## 3.10. Redox potential (Eh)

Beginning in 1999, redox potential was measured in the field using an Orionk advanced portable ISE/pH/mV/ORP/temperature meter model 290A with a Model 9678BN Epoxy Sure-Flow Combination Redox/ORP probe. The stated accuracy of the meter in the ORP mode is F0.2 mV or F0.05% of the reading, whichever is greater. Detailed protocols for preparation of standards and conducting the analyses are provided in [Wildish et al. \(1999\).](#page-41-0) Surficial sediments (top 2.0 cm) were gently homogenized and placed in new 125 ml urine specimen jars with no headspace. These were immediately placed in a cooler and held on shaved ice until the samples were analyzed simultaneously for pH, Eh and sulfides. The samples were allowed to warm to ca. 10 jC (the approximate temperature of the sediments) prior to removal of 5 ml of sediment for the sulfide analyses. The redox probe, which was positioned on a ring-stand, was inserted approximately 2.0 cm into the sample container, which was gently agitated to insure that no air remained around the probe and that it had intimate contact with the sediments. The probe was then allowed to stabilize prior to taking the reading. All onboard analyses were completed within 30 min of collecting the samples. Quality assurance required checking of the meter against two standards at the beginning and end of each batch of samples and triplicate analyses completed on 5% of the samples with a minimum of one per batch of 20 samples.

## 3.11. Free sediment sulfides (S=)

Prior to 1999, S<sup>=</sup> were evaluated organoleptically on a scale of zero (no detected hydrogen sulfide) to five (very strong and lingering smell of H2S). Beginning in 1999, free sediment S<sup>=</sup> were evaluated using an Orionk advanced portable ISE/pH/mV/ORP/ temperature meter model 290A with a Model 9616 BNC Ionplus Silver/Sulfide electrode. The meter has a concentration range of 0.000 to 19,900 AM and a relative accuracy of F0.5% of the reading. The probe and meter were three point calibrated using 100, 1000 and 10,000 AM standards every 3 h. Detailed protocols for the preparation of standards are provided in [Wildish et al. \(1999\).](#page-41-0) Quality assurance required frequent checking of the meter against standards and triplicate analyses completed on 5% of the samples with a minimum of one per batch. Sulfide analyses were completed using 5 cm<sup>3</sup> subsamples of the homogenized surficial sediment samples described above. Sediment samples were held on shaved ice until analyzed for sulfides within 30 min of collection.

## 3.12. Underwater video

A cabled Outland Technologyk, UWC-160 camera, equipped with a UWL-22 (200 W) 115 V light was used to record video on a Sony combination TV-VCR. The camera and light were attached to the control unit with 100 m umbilical cord. The unit was housed in an aluminum frame which included a large stabilizing fin and a ''T'' probe marked in 5 cm increments to establish horizontal dimensions. The field of view at the length of the probe was 0.5 m. The unit was towed across the survey area at an average speed of 0.25 m/s. Camera position was recorded as a function of tape time using differential GPS with no correction made for hydrowire angle.

## 3.13. Statistical analyses

The experimental design and analyses relied primarily on correlation, principle components and linear and nonlinear regression analyses. Raw data was entered into a Microsoft Excelk spreadsheet and imported into Statisticak software for analysis. Proportional data (TVS and SGS) were arcsine(square-root) transformed prior to inferential analyses [\(Zar, 1984\).](#page-42-0) Biological count data were transformed (Ln(N+1)) prior to determination of Pearson correlation coefficients. Biological responses were summarized in three-dimensional contour graphs using Statistica's distance weighted least squares subroutine. The location in time and space of each sample is identified in each of these graphs. Predictions outside the range of these sample locations must be interpreted with caution because they are based on trends and not on actual measured values. Statistical significance throughout this report is associated with the probability of making a Type I error of 0.05.

Shannon's Index [\(Shannon and Weaver, 1949\).](#page-41-0) This index provides the average uncertainty per species in an infinite community of taxa. The form of the index used in this analysis is:

Shannon's Index = 
$$H' = -\sum_{\text{over species}} (n_i/n) \ln(n_i/n)$$

The value of the index is zero when a single species is present. It is maximized when there are a large number of equally represented species and it is reduced in communities dominated by a few highly abundant species. The value of Shannon's Index in a sample containing 20 taxa equally represented in a total abundance of 600 animals would be 3.0.

Pielou's Index [\(Pielou, 1977\)](#page-41-0) is a commonly used measure of community evenness. It expresses the observed value of Shannon's Index relative to the maximum possible value (ln(S)) where (S) is the number of taxa present. Pielou's index varies between 0 and 1.0 and generally covaries with Shannon's Index.

Pielou's Index = 
$$J' = H' \ln(S)$$

#### 4.1. Site characterization

Selected hydrodynamic and benthic physicochemical properties observed at Arrow Pass, Upper Retreat and Carrie Bay are summarized in Table 1. Water depths are similar at all three farms. Carrie Bay sediments had a significantly higher proportion of fines (silt and clay=71.5% to 92.4%) suggesting slow bottom current speeds and a more depositional benthos that was found at Upper Retreat or Arrow Pass. Total volatile solids were higher at Carrie Bay's reference stations (9.2% to 11.2%) than at either Upper Retreat (7.7% to 9.05%) or Arrow Pass (4.1% to 5.5%).

Carrie Bay's 15 m deep mean current speed (5.9 cm/s) and net current vector (5.4 cm/s at 196 °T) suggests that waste was preferentially carried to the south. However, the 30 m deep drifters released near the inner (west) end of the farm by Sea Consult (1997) were deflected into a clockwise gyre as they approached the southern shore of the bay. This apparent gyre concentrated flotsam in the bay, which was frequently confirmed by farm workers. Drifters released near the eastern end of the netpen array proceeded south down Retreat Passage with no deflection.

The farm's footprint was assessed on July 14, 1999 following 1 year in fallow, by determining TVS,  $S^{=}$  and redox potential in sediments at  $\sim 30$  m intervals from the center of the pre-existing cage system on four magnetic bearings. #### 5. Discussion

Anderson (1992) examined benthic effects at eight salmon farms in British Columbia and found that recovery from moderate to severe impacts could be substantial in times as short as 6 months. Significant improvement in benthic conditions was observed in 1 month at well flushed sites. He observed the proliferation of Capitella capitata and N. cornuta in highly enriched sediments. While he was unable to conduct long-term studies, he concluded that benthic recovery might take up to 2 years at heavily impacted sites. Mahnken (1993) studied benthic conditions in Clam Bay, Washington State for 2 years and observed complete chemical remediation and the return of all but rare macrobenthic taxa, which remained absent from the vicinity of the farm at the end of the study. More recently, Karakassis et al. (1999) reported improvement in sediment chemistry and all biological endpoints, but noted that neither chemical nor biological remediation was complete at a netpen site in Cephalonia Bay, Greece where sea bream and sea bass had been grown during the preceding 2 years. They noted that the model of Pearson and Rosenberg (1978) did not apply to their site during the study and hypothesized that seasonal variations in benthic chemistry associated with benthic macroalgal production at the shallow site was responsible. Johannessen et al. (1994) studied a Norwegian site with a similar production history and sediment grain size distribution as Carrie Bay. The site, located west of Bergen, Norway produced 2417 mt of Atlantic salmon between May 1988 and May 1990. Sediments on the perimeter of the netpens contained 94% to 97% silt compared with 77% to 78% at the reference station. Reference station TVS was 9.1% to 11.2% and TVS on the perimeter of the netpens varied between 14.0% and 17.3%. Between 30 and 68 taxa were identified at the reference station with a total abundance of between 495 and 1875 macrofauna/m<sup>2</sup> in the area before culture began. Macrofauna were reduced to five to seven taxa with an abundance of 1935 to 10,370/m<sup>2</sup> during production. At the end of the study in April 1991, after 1 year in fallow, TVS on the perimeter of the netpens remained at 16.2% and an average of 19 taxa and 21,970 macrofauna/m<sup>2</sup> were collected in two samples. The high abundance was associated with up to 15,300 C. capitata per sample. Thus, while neither chemical nor biological remediation was complete, both factors were improving.

In contrast to the long remediation times reported above, Ritz et al. (1989) observed significant physicochemical and biological remediation under Tasmanian salmon farms in 7 weeks with further improvement 14 weeks post harvest. Lu and Wu (1998) observed biological recovery of the benthos under fish farms at Three Fathom's Cove in Hong Kong within 4 to 5 months and concluded that the fast recovery might have been associated with the subtropical environment, when compared with longer recovery times reviewed in their paper for temperate climes. However, Brooks (1993) found nearly complete chemical recovery and significant biological activity under a Port Townsend, Washington salmon farm 13 weeks following harvest. More recently, Brooks et al. (2003) reported chemical and biological remediation complete at the Arrow Pass salmon farm after 6 months of fallow and chemical remediation was observed and/or predicted to occur rapidly (months) at the Upper Retreat, Midsummer Island and Port Elizabeth salmon farms. Each of these farms produced between 2000 and 2500 mt of Atlantic salmon during their previous production cycles. Thus it appears that rapid benthic recovery can occur in temperate as well as subtropical environments.

The results presented herein indicate that both chemical and biological remediation were progressing at Carrie Bay in 2002 and further reductions in TVS and S<sup>=</sup> were observed in 2003. However, neither chemical nor biological remediation could be considered complete in May 2003 when the farm had been fallow for 5 years. Based on carbon assimilation modeling using the [Findlay et al. \(1995\)](#page-41-0) model, [Morrisey et](#page-41-0) al. (2000) predicted long remediation times of 3.1 to 7.5 years under a fish farm at Stewart Island, New Zealand where waste had accumulated to depths of 39.3 cm. The underwater video surveys conducted in 1997 when Carrie Bay was at high biomass and in 1999 after 1 year in fallow revealed waste deposits that qualitatively appeared to vary between <1 and >10 cm deep within 30 m of the netpens. The failure of opportunistic macrofauna to proliferate at Carrie Bay, and other unknown factors, has resulted in slow remediation at this site. However, based on the report of [Morrisey et al. \(2000\)](#page-41-0) and the observation of accumulations of farm waste in Carrie Bay, it is possible that these accumulations may have contributed to the long remediation time.

## 5.2. Chemical remediation

[\(Fig. 18\)](#page-32-0) describes the history of TVS as a function of time on the perimeter of the Arrow Pass and Carrie Bay salmon farms. The increases described in [Fig. 4](#page-14-0) were small, but they were consistent and statistically significant. These results suggest that microbial catabolism proceeded more slowly at depth in Carrie Bay sediments.

#### 5.2.2. Zinc

Di Toro et al. (1992) produced a model indicating that several metals, including zinc and copper, were not biologically available and therefore nontoxic when the molar ratio of simultaneously extracted metal (SEM) to acid volatile sulfides (AVS) was <1.0. Brooks and Mahnken (2003a,b) reported a maximum free sulfide (FS) to sediment zinc extracted using a strong acid digestion (SADM) of 0.172 for all metal data collected at B.C. salmon farms. The FS:SADM method was found to be a more conservative predictor of biological effects than the SEM:AVS methodology and no adverse effects were predicted in association with sedimented zinc, which is an essential micronutrient in salmon feeds. A maximum FS:SADM ratio of 0.61 was observed at Carrie Bay indicating that the moderately high S<sup>=</sup> concentrations were sufficient to render the zinc not biologically available and nontoxic.

Cooper and Morse (1998) reported that up to 90% of estuarine pyrite can oxidize within 1 day of exposure to oxic seawater and release the accompanying trace metals. This behavior was reflected in the zinc history at Arrow Pass where Brooks and Mahnken (2003a,b) reported a decline in sediment zinc concentrations from 200  $\mu$ g Zn/g dry sediment to background (25  $\mu$ g/g) during chemical remediation. This same trend was apparent in Carrie Bay sediments (see Figs. 7 and 8) demonstrating that sediment zinc decreased from over 400  $\mu$ g Zn/g in 1999 to values that with a single exception (237  $\mu$ g/g at 45 m) were less than 120  $\mu$ g Zn/g in 2002 and 2003. All of these values were less than the Washington State Apparent Effects Threshold (AET) of 270  $\mu$ g Zn/g or the National Oceanic and Atmospheric Agency AET of 260  $\mu$ g Zn/g discussed by Brooks and Mahnken (2003b).

#### 5.3. Biological remediation

Mahnken (1993) and Brooks et al. (2003) have shown that following a refractory period of varying length, chemical and biological remediation occurs concurrently. Heilskov and Holmer (2001) found that *Nereis* and *C. capitata* stimulated the mineralization of organic matter by 135% and 87% as measured by O<sub>2</sub> uptake, SO<sup>2-</sup><sub>4</sub> reduction and the production of CO<sub>2</sub>. While anaerobic processes continued, the presence of both species appeared to primarily facilitate aerobic catabolism. The authors concluded that "Overall, it is clear that the presence of benthic fauna and bioturbation enhances sediment metabolism compared to azoic sediments". They noted that relatively refractory compounds (like macroalgae and terrigenous plant detritus) are mineralized up to 10 times faster under aerobic than

anaerobic conditions, but that the degradation of more labile compounds (such as fish feces) was unaffected by redox conditions in sediments. Consistent with Kristensen et al. (1995), they suggested that the influence of benthic fauna might be expected to be smaller in fish-farm sediments but asserted the importance of "maintaining macrofaunal populations" to enhance decomposition of organic matter near intensive aquaculture facilities. In contrast, Mackin and Swider (1989) concluded that sulfate reduction was primarily responsible for the decomposition of organic matter in marine sediments and suggested that bioturbation caused by abundant infauna in Long Island Sound sediments had a minor affect on sediment chemistry and the decomposition of organic matter.

The macrofaunal community at Carrie Bay and its reference stations was dominated by mollusks with reduced numbers of annelids and a near absence of crustaceans. #### 6. Conclusions

The uniqueness of the remediation characteristics at Carrie Bay challenges our ability to predict recovery rates at only a very small percentage of operating salmon farms in British Columbia. Those challenges also present opportunities and a careful analysis of Carrie Bay, Upper Retreat and Arrow Pass sediments may provide clues helping identify sites where long remediation times might be expected. Unfortunately, resolution of the following questions requires a multidisciplinary approach and cannot be answered with the data collected in support of this study. However, an answer to these questions should be considered important to optimal future siting of intensive aquaculture facilities.

- O Sulfide concentrations quickly exceeded 20,000  $\mu$ M at Upper Retreat and Arrow Pass where chemical remediation occurred very quickly as evidenced by decreases in S<sup>=</sup> to <1000  $\mu$ M within a few months. Why did S<sup>=</sup> at Carrie Bay remain at moderate levels ( $\leq$ 9410  $\mu$ M) for several years?
- O Did elevated sediment concentrations of iron, manganese, magnesium, or other divalent cations provide a sink that removed S<sup>=</sup> from the reactive pool and diminishing the rate of anaerobic metabolism?
- O Were there other factors at Carrie Bay interdicting the cycling of sulfide to sulfate, which drives anaerobic processes in marine sediments?
- O Why have carbon opportunists proliferate at some sites and not at others? Macrofaunal bioturbation may be important with respect to ventilation of sediments, but its value in reducing chemical remediation times associated with catabolism of highly labile organic aquaculture waste is largely unknown and somewhat controversial. Understanding why these fauna have not proliferated at Carrie Bay, where S<sup>=</sup> concentrations remained at tolerable levels for these species, could be important to future siting of intensive aquaculture operations.

<span id="page-40-0"></span>Chemical remediation is nearly complete at Carrie Bay and a further reduction in sediment S<sup>=</sup> to V447 AM is predicted by 2004. Biological remediation is proceeding, but was not complete at distances Vca. 80 m in 2002. Why this area was characterized by low S= and a long remediation time is unknown but is an area worthy of additional research to provide guidance for siting future intensive aquaculture operations. Based on the information at hand, it appears that locating salmon farms over areas with depositional bottoms containing >70% to 80% fines may create adverse benthic effects that extend to at least 200 m from the netpens and that may take 5 to 6 years for chemical remediation and somewhat longer for complete biological remediation. However, it should be emphasized that Carrie Bay represented the worst case these authors are aware of in the Pacific Northwest and that while chemical and biological remediations at this site have been much slower than reported by [Brooks](#page-41-0) [et](#page-41-0) [al.](#page-41-0) [\(2003\)](#page-41-0) for four other farms located in the same area, remediation is undeniably proceeding. There has been no evidence of significant accumulation of carbon at depth in sediments and possible advection of waste has not resulted in an expansion of the benthic area originally impacted by this farm. As has been previously reported for Arrow Pass, sediment zinc concentrations declined during chemical remediation at Carrie Bay and there is no evidence of toxicity associated with this metal to benthic organisms at any time during the operation or remediation of this site.

The uniqueness of Carrie Bay's chemical and biological response to farm waste suggests that environmental effects predictions, based on modeling of the dispersion of waste from intensive aquaculture, will be greatly improved by modules that predict the fate of sedimented labile farm waste. None of the published models currently have that capability. It is likely that a reasonable understanding of these processes could be synthesized from the existing literature. However, that synthesis is beyond the scope of this current report.
