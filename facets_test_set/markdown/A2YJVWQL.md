# **ARTICLE**

## Decadal soil and stand response to fire, harvest, and salvage-logging disturbances in the western boreal mixedwood forest of Alberta, Canada1

B.E. Kishchuk, E. Thiffault, M. Lorente, S. Quideau, T. Keddy, and D. Sidders

**Abstract:** Empirical knowledge of long-term ecosystem response to single and compound disturbances is essential for predicting disturbance effects and identifying management practices to maintain productive capacity of managed and restored landscapes. We report on soil, foliar nutrition, and regeneration growth response to wildfire, clearcut harvesting, and postfire salvage logging, as well as undisturbed control stands within the first year following disturbance and 10–11 years after disturbance in trembling aspen – white spruce mixedwood forests near Lesser Slave Lake, north-central Alberta, Canada. The compound disturbance of salvage logging resulted in greater long-term impacts on forest floor properties than either wildfire or harvesting alone. Changes in forest floor properties such as carbon and nitrogen pools and cation exchange capacity under salvage logging have persisted for 10 years and exhibit a different recovery trajectory than fire or harvesting. Forest floor properties under harvesting, including depth, carbon content, pH, extractable ammonium, and extractable sulphur, were not different from the control condition 10 years after harvest. Effects on soil and foliar nutrition were not reflected in productivity (height and diameter) of regenerating vegetation. Our results show differences between short- and long-term responses to disturbance, among single natural and anthropogenic disturbances, and among single and compound disturbances.

*Key words:* compound disturbance, salvage logging, soil carbon, soil nutrients, Boreal Plains Ecozone, boreal mixedwood forest.

As this disturbance mosaic evolves in extent, intensity, and complexity, empirical knowledge of longterm ecosystem response is essential for accurately projecting disturbance impacts and identifying appropriate management practices for maintaining the productive capacity of managed and restored landscapes.

Over the past two decades, there has been increasing interest in developing forest management approaches based on an understanding of natural disturbance dynamics [\(Bergeron et al. 2002\)](#page-10-5). Large areas of the Canadian boreal forest were historically maintained naturally by stand-replacing wildfires [\(Brandt et al. 2013\)](#page-10-3). To mimic the effects of wildfire, forest management approaches and silvicultural techniques have been designed to try to replicate the periodicity and spatial patterns of fire to maintain a spectrum of forest composition and structure at different scales in the landscape. However, wildfire and forest practices such as clearcutting affect more than the spatial configuration of the landscape, having unique effects on soils and biogeochemical processes at the stand and site levels [\(Maynard et al. 2014\)](#page-10-6). These changes to ecosystem processes may push ecosystem functioning of harvested and wildfire-affected sites along different trajectories, with specific effects varying depending on site conditions [\(Thiffault et al. 2007;](#page-11-2) [Rothstein and Spaulding 2010;](#page-10-7) [Lorente et al. 2013\)](#page-10-8). Therefore, the ecological significance of stand-level differences in soil biogeochemical patterns, processes, and trajectories between clearcutting and wildfire are important considerations in developing and improving natural disturbance based models of forest management.

There is emerging and increasing economic pressure in Canada's boreal forests to salvage merchantable timber from burned or insect-killed forests [\(Schmiegelow et al. 2006\)](#page-11-3) as it is considered a promising feedstock source for future expansion of the bioenergy sector [\(Dymond et al. 2010\)](#page-10-9). Compound disturbances occur when multiple disturbances happen in rapid succession and may result in changes to ecosystem recovery processes [\(Buma and](#page-10-10) [Wessman 2011\)](#page-10-10). Activities such as salvage logging may act as a compound disturbance by altering the ecosystem's recovery mechanisms. The potential impacts of salvage logging on soil biogeochemical processes and stand productivity have not received focused attention, in part because long-term ecosystem recovery processes after natural disturbance are still poorly understood and potential cumulative effects of natural and human disturbance have not been well documented [\(Lindenmayer and Noss](#page-10-11) [2006\)](#page-10-11)*.*

Wildfires usually remove the nutrient-rich crown material, understory, and part or all of the forest floor [\(Certini 2005\)](#page-10-12) and may also have an impact on soil organic carbon of mineral layers [\(Chen](#page-10-13) [and Shrestha 2012\)](#page-10-13). However, most of the large woody material remains on the site [\(Wei et al. 1997;](#page-11-4) [Tinker and Knight 2000;](#page-11-5) [Johnson et al. 2005\)](#page-10-14), likely playing a role in soil fertility and nutrient availability in the subsequent stand rotation [\(Marañón-](#page-10-15)[Jiménez and Castro 2013\)](#page-10-15), as well as carbon (C) accumulation. In the boreal forest of Quebec, on sites subject to high-severity fires, salvage logging (removal of merchantable timber following wildfire) has led to depleted soil calcium (Ca), magnesium (Mg), and phosphorus (P), which may not return to prefire levels within the planned rotation time of the stand [\(Brais et al. 2000\)](#page-10-16). Reduced densities of saproxylic beetles in postfire salvage logging are implicated in complex shifts in soil nitrogen (N) availability and the growth of postfire-colonizing plant species [\(Cobb et al. 2010\)](#page-10-17).

Much of our knowledge of impacts of disturbance on ecosystem functioning is based on studies of single disturbances [\(White 1979;](#page-11-6) [Turner 2010\)](#page-11-7). However, with multiple competing land uses, interactions between multiple disturbances are becoming of greater concern due to their potential for nonlinear behavior and longlasting impacts on ecosystem structure and function [\(Buma and](#page-10-10) [Wessman 2011\)](#page-10-10). Interactions between multiple disturbances may cause novel disturbance characteristics [\(Buma and Wessman 2011\)](#page-10-10) and unexpected recovery pathways [\(D'Amato et al. 2011\)](#page-10-18). Given the potential for disturbances to cause large changes in ecosystem functioning, we require quantitative evaluation of the potential compounding effects of salvage logging in relation to the effects of single disturbances.

This study was initiated to determine the short-term and longer term effects of fire, harvesting, and the compound disturbance of salvage logging on soil biogeochemical cycles and site productivity in the western boreal mixedwood forest of Alberta. The specific objectives were to (*i*) quantify and compare changes in carbon and nutrients of the forest floor and upper soil mineral layers and in foliar nutrition following severe wildfire, conventional clearcut harvesting, and postfire salvage logging relative to undisturbed controls in mixed trembling aspen – white spruce stands, (*ii*) compare initial (within 1 year) after disturbance with 10- or 11-year postdisturbance changes in those properties to establish recovery trajectories, and (*iii*) relate trajectories in soil fertility and foliar nutrition under these disturbance types to development and productivity of regenerating stands.

## **Material and methods**

## **Study sites**

Research plots were established in 2001 and 2002 near Chisholm, Slave Lake, and Canyon Creek, Alberta [\(Fig. 1\)](#page-2-0). The research plots are in the Mid-Boreal Uplands EcoRegion within the Boreal Plains Ecozone [\(Wiken 1986;](#page-11-8) [EcoRegions Working Group 1989\)](#page-10-20), corresponding to the Lower Foothills Natural Subregion [\(Alberta](#page-10-21) [Environmental Protection 1994\)](#page-10-21). The research plots are located between 54°54.5=N and 55°42.0=N and between 114°4.42=W and 115°50.5=W. Soils are formed on glacial till and glacio-lacustrine deposits derived from sedimentary materials [\(La Roi 1992\)](#page-10-22). Terrain is generally flat to rolling, and soils are Eutric, Melanic, and Dystric Brunisols and Dark Gray Luvisols with predominantly silt, silty loam, and silty clay loam surface textures [\(Soil Classification](#page-11-9) [Working Group 1998\)](#page-11-9), with surface soil texture relatively consistent across the study area.

Prior to disturbance, stand cover types were white spruce – deciduous mixedwoods and contained between 60% and 40% white spruce and between 40% and 60% trembling aspen or balsam poplar. Only sites deemed productive based on composition (i.e., 51%–70% crown closure), density ("C" grade density; [Alberta](#page-10-23) [Forestry, Lands, and Wildlife 1991\)](#page-10-23), and size of trees (i.e., >25 m) in the stand were selected for the study.

Plots were located on upland boreal mixedwood sites that are mesic moisture regime and medium nutrient regime [\(Beckingham](#page-10-24) [and Archibald 1996\)](#page-10-24), with low-bush cranberry, feather moss, and wild rose present on all sites in varying abundance. Consistency in overstory cover type, surface soil texture, ecosite, and drainage were the basis of site selection. Due to the nature of the wildfire and harvesting operations, replicates within a treatment were, in some cases, tens of kilometres apart but consistent with respect to vegetation, site, and soil properties. The minimum distance between replicates was 400 m.

Plots were established in four disturbance types (treatments): (1) undisturbed control, (2) burned, (3) clearcut harvesting, and (4) postfire salvage logging. Each of the four disturbance types was replicated three times for a total of 12 research plots of approximately 2 ha each. Clearcut sites that subsequently burned exist within the region, however, not on a scale and pattern that would have allowed for replicated incorporation into the trial. #### Soil sampling and analysis

Soils were initially sampled within the first year following disturbance: in July 2001 for burned plots (approximately 6 weeks after fire) and in July 2002 for clearcut and salvage-logged plots (approximately 5 months after harvest) and control plots. This sampling time is hereafter referred to as "initial after disturbance" or simply "initial". Plots were resampled again in late June 2012, i.e., 10 or 11 years after disturbance. Soils were sampled from a random selection of points within a systematic grid layout, with grid intersections located at 20 m × 20 m intervals in each direction and each grid containing 16 sampling points. Grids were positioned to maximize the effective sampling area for the shape of the plot and were established consistently by cardinal directions. An area of approximately 1 ha (50 m width) remained around each sampling grid as a buffer area. Soils were sampled at six randomly chosen grid points of the 16 within each plot, with the same grid points used in the initial after disturbance and 10 or 11 years postdisturbance sampling.

Separate forest floor (combined organic layer of litter (L), fibric (F), and humic (H) horizons) samples, excluding surface woody debris, were collected for chemical and mass analysis at each sampling location. Forest floor material was completely removed by the fire at some sampling points; where no forest floor was present at a given sampling location, it was recorded as a zero within the plot mean. Forest floor depth at four corners of a 15 × 15 cm quadrat was measured and averaged to provide a mean forest floor depth for each sampling location. Forest floor mass was determined by removing the complete forest floor within the quadrat. Oven-dried mass of the forest floor was used to determine bulk density, which was in turn used to calculate elemental content (kilograms per hectare). Surface mineral soil (0–7 cm

depth) samples were taken at each sampling location for chemical analysis, and a separate 500 cm³ soil core using a sliding-hammer corer was taken to determine bulk density. The 0–7 cm depth included Ah, Ae, and AB horizons. Samples for chemical analysis were kept in coolers with ice packs until transport to Edmonton and then refrigerated (3 °C) until analysis. Forest floor samples were homogenized in a blender while moist, with subsamples air-dried for analyses as required. Mineral soil samples were air-dried, sieved to 2 mm, and ground as required for analysis.

Samples were analyzed according to the methods of Kalra and Maynard (1991). Forest floor samples were analyzed for pH (1:4 ratio of soil to liquid in 0.01 mol·L-1 calcium chloride (CaCl2)); total C was determined by LECO analysis; total nitrogen (N) was determined by the Kjeldahl method; and extractable NH<sub>4</sub>-N and NO<sub>3</sub>-N were determined on fresh samples in 2 mol·L<sup>-1</sup> KCl. Extractable P was extracted using Bray P-1 extractant, sulphur (S) was determined using 0.01 mol·L<sup>-1</sup> NH<sub>4</sub>Cl, and cation exchange capacity (CEC), exchangeable Ca, magnesium (Mg), potassium (K), and sodium (Na) were determined in unbuffered ammonium chloride with elemental analysis by inductively coupled plasma atomic absorption spectrometry. Mineral soils were analyzed for pH in a 1:2 ratio of soil to liquid (0.01 mol·L<sup>-1</sup> CaCl<sub>2</sub>), total C and N and extractable P were determined as for the forest floor, S was determined using Ca(H<sub>2</sub>PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>2</sub> (500 mg·L<sup>-1</sup>P), and CEC, exchangeable Ca, magnesium Mg, potassium K, and sodium Na were determined as for the forest floor. Particle size analysis was done on mineral soil samples to determine surface soil texture.

#### Foliar sampling and analysis

Foliage was sampled from clonally regenerating trembling aspen in plots in burned, clearcut harvested, and salvage-logged treatments in June 2003 and again in June 2012. #### **Tree mensuration**

Overstory and regenerating tree mensuration was completed in 2012 on the same randomly selected subset of three plots for each replicate in control, burned, clearcut harvested, and salvagelogged treatments. Mensuration results for regeneration in the latter three treatments are reported here. A minimum sample size of 25 regenerating trembling aspen was required for each plot sampled. To facilitate the minimum sample size, plot radii of 1.78 m or 3.99 m were used based on regenerating trembling aspen density. Species and diameter at breast height (DBH) were recorded for all trees with a total height greater than 50 cm, and the total height of all white spruce and the three tallest trembling aspen were measured.

#### **Statistical analysis**

Soil, foliar, and mensuration data were analyzed as a two-factor experiment arranged in a completely randomized design using mixed linear models [\(Littell et al. 1996\)](#page-10-26). The two fixed factors were time of measurement (initial after disturbance or 10 or 11 years after disturbance) and treatment (undisturbed control, burned, clearcut harvested, and salvage logged for soil properties and burned, clearcut harvested, and salvage logged for foliar and mensuration properties). We verified the absence of temporal autocorrelation between samples immediately after disturbance and 10 or 11 years after disturbance by adding a dependence parameter using a first-order regressive model (AR1) [\(Pinheiro and Bates 2000\)](#page-10-27). Due to the spatial structure of the data (i.e., samples from the same plot are more likely to be similar), the plot was defined as a random factor. Significant differences were tested using analysis of variance (ANOVA), with post hoc Tukey's multiple comparison tests to determine significant differences among treatments.

Plots of fitted and observed values and residuals were examined for deviations from normality assumptions [\(Pinheiro and Bates](#page-10-27) [2000\)](#page-10-27); data did not require transformation to meet the assumptions of the analyses. Analyses were conducted in SAS (version 9.2, SAS Institute Inc., Cary, North Carolina), using PROC MIXED and PROC ARIMA. Statistical significance was declared at = 0.05.

## **Mineral soil**

Neither time of measurement nor treatment had a significant effect on CEC, total C, total N, extractable NO3-N, and exchangeable Mg concentrations in the mineral soil [\(Table 2\)](#page-5-0). However, values for pH and extractable P differed significantly with time; highest values occurred immediately after disturbance for extractable P, whereas they occurred at 10 or 11 years after disturbance for pH. Treatments also differed significantly for pH; values were higher under both burned and salvage-logged plots compared with harvested plots, whereas they did not differ from the undisturbed control [\(Table 2\)](#page-5-0).

All other variables (extractable NH4-N, extractable S, exchangeable Ca and K concentrations, and total C and N contents) showed significant interactions between times of measurement and treatments [\(Table 2\)](#page-5-0). For all these variables, although there were some differences between treatments at the initial measurement, all treatments converged to similar values after 10 or 11 years. Moreover, even at the initial measurement, differences between treatments were not large and most were not significant [\(Fig. 4\)](#page-7-0).

## **Foliar nutrition and growth of regenerating stands**

Foliar Ca, P, S, and Cu concentrations changed significantly through time [\(Table 3\)](#page-8-0). Values were significantly higher immediately after disturbance relative to the 10 or 11 year measurements for foliar P, S and Cu, whereas the inverse was true for foliar Ca. Regardless of time, treatments differed significantly for foliar N [\(Table 3\)](#page-8-0), with concentrations being higher under harvesting compared with both burning and salvage logging [\(Fig. 5\)](#page-8-1). Trembling aspen regeneration DBH and height did not differ among treatments 10 or 11 years after disturbance [\(Fig. 5\)](#page-8-1).

#### **Discussion**

#### **Incremental effects of postfire salvage logging**

This study has identified that the compound disturbance of postfire salvage logging produced impacts on forest floor biogeochemistry that were cumulatively greater than the impacts of either individual fire or clearcut-harvest disturbances. Salvage logging also resulted in a recovery trajectory that was different from either the fire or harvesting disturbances. Disturbance type effects and recovery trajectories were most pronounced in the forest floor horizon.

A fundamental question addressed in this study was the comparative degree of forest floor change under fire and harvesting and the compounding effect of postfire salvage logging. The forest floor was almost completely removed by fire over large portions of this area, which, by itself, could potentially impact long-term site productivity through loss of soil organic matter and altering

Immediate increases in extractable NH4-N under both fire [\(Wan et al. 2001\)](#page-11-13) and harvesting [\(Staaf and Olsson 1994\)](#page-11-14) have been observed on other boreal sites; however, in this study, after 10 or 11 years, NH4-N levels remained higher under harvesting than fire or salvage logging.

Harvesting did not result in significant N losses elsewhere (e.g., [Simard et al. 2001\)](#page-11-15), and in that study, harvesting increased potentially mineralizable N relative to fire and to undisturbed controls. Therefore, harvesting may stimulate a long-term increase in available N that is not observed under fire or undisturbed conditions. This may be due to greater N pools remaining on the site after harvest relative to after fire but warmer soil conditions than in the control due to canopy opening. This increase in available N was reflected here in greater concentrations of foliar N in the harvesting treatment 10 or 11 years after harvest.

Although greater levels of foliar N in regeneration would be expected to correspond to increased regeneration growth, to date, this has not been realized. These stands are still in early stages of development; the influence of the soil nutritional environment on productivity may not be evident until canopy closure as other factors (e.g., microclimate) likely drive growth in the first decade of stand development (e.g., [Thiffault et al. 2011;](#page-11-16) [Ponder et al. 2012\)](#page-10-33). The role of other site influences (e.g., soil physical properties, water balance, drainage, soil microbial processes) on regenerating stands requires further investigation.

Exchangeable base cations showed initial increase (K), decrease (Mg), or no difference (Ca) compared with the control, but after a

decade, exchangeable cations had converged and showed no treatment differences [\(Fig. 3B\)](#page-4-0), and only in the case of K was the decadal level less than the initial control. Other work in the western boreal mixedwood forest shows that K and NH4-N exhibited the most definitive responses to harvesting [\(Kishchuk et al. 2014\)](#page-10-31). These soils, formed on fine-textured glacial deposits under minimal acidifying deposition, do not warrant immediate concerns for base cation depletion. This is in contrast to base cation depletion under harvesting and acidifying conditions and following fire and salvage logging in eastern Canadian forests [\(Brais et al.](#page-10-16) [2000;](#page-10-16) [Watmough and Dillon 2003;](#page-11-17) [Duchesne and Houle 2006;](#page-10-34) [Thiffault et al. 2007;](#page-11-2) [Hazlett et al. 2011\)](#page-10-35).

Cation exchange capacity has been shown to be an important large-scale and long-term indicator of western boreal mixedwood soil response to disturbance and stand development [\(Kishchuk](#page-10-31) [et al. 2014\)](#page-10-31). Forest floor CEC decreased under salvage logging over the 10-year period considered here [\(Fig. 3C\)](#page-4-0). This could be due to changes in organic matter quantity but also quality, for example, in the nature and availability of functional groups, which are known to influence charge density per unit surface area of organic matter [\(Stevenson 1994\)](#page-11-18). The incorporation into the soil of the highly condensed and aromatic C forms of charred organic material after fire may be critical to soil long-term exchangeable cation availability and retention capacity [\(Thiffault et al. 2008\)](#page-11-19), a phenomenon that may be hampered on salvage-logged areas where sources of charred woody material are removed. Contrary to other studies (e.g., [Simard et al. 2001;](#page-11-15) [Neff et al. 2005\)](#page-10-36), fire did not appear to increase long-term soil P availability. Fire has been shown to convert organic P to orthophosphate, and an increase in pH should also have a positive effect on P availability, but this pHinduced effect is usually short-lived [\(Certini 2005\)](#page-10-12). By the time of the decadal remeasurement, extractable P levels had converged in the control, burned, and clearcut-harvest treatments to a level lower than the initial control condition; this pattern of decreasing P concentration over stand rotation has also been observed by [Shrestha and Chen \(2010\).](#page-11-12) Increases in boreal soil pH following fire as observed here are well documented [\(Pietikäinen and Fritze](#page-10-37) [1993;](#page-10-37) [Simard et al 2001;](#page-11-15) [Gómez-Rey and González-Prieto 2013\)](#page-10-38); however, it is the influence of pH on properties such as soil nutrient availability that define its utility as a response parameter.

Increased forest floor extractable S over time under undisturbed conditions and low atmospheric S deposition has been observed elsewhere in the western boreal mixedwood forest [\(Kishchuk et al. 2014\)](#page-10-31) and may be related to increased solubilization under stand development. Initial increases in extractable S in the burn and salvage-logged treatments may also be related to solubilization of both inorganic and organic S forms. However, the long-term decrease in extractable S under salvage logging could be related to persistent organic matter loss [\(Kishchuk and](#page-10-39) [Brockley 2002\)](#page-10-39).

Differences in mineral soil properties under disturbance were less evident than for forest floor. Where present, they occurred immediately after disturbance (e.g., available NH4-N, extractable S, exchangeable K, and C and N content) but did not persist over time, results that are similar to those observed by [Chen and](#page-10-13) [Shrestha \(2012\).](#page-10-13) [Johnson and Curtis \(2001\)](#page-10-28) noted that the influence of harvesting on the mineral soil was generally minimal. Moreover, the heat of wildfire often does not allow the temperature of the mineral layers to reach levels high enough to trigger significant changes in physical and chemical properties [\(DeBano 2000;](#page-10-40) [Neff et al. 2005\)](#page-10-36). At the site level, the mineral soil therefore appears to be buffered from acute changes occurring in the forest floor caused by disturbances (e.g., [Thiffault et al. 2011\)](#page-11-16).

#### **Ecological and management implications**

The properties most clearly affected under salvage logging are related to soil organic matter and C. The large removal of forest floor during fire, coupled with the subsequent removal of standing woody biomass has substantially reduced the C pool at this site. We have demonstrated the incremental effects of postfire salvage logging on boreal mixedwood soils beyond that of either fire or harvesting alone. This interactive effect of multiple disturbances occurring within a short time period demonstrates that the compound effects are greater than those of the individual disturbances [\(D'Amato et al. 2011;](#page-10-18) [Bradford et al. 2012\)](#page-10-41).

At 10 years after harvest, forest floor properties under clearcut harvesting, including depth, C content, pH, extractable NH4-N, and extractable S, were not different from the control condition. In fact, harvesting alone resulted in long-term increases in available soil N, although, to date, this has not been reflected in regeneration productivity. This indicates that clearcut harvesting in these productive mixedwood stands has not resulted in adverse long-term soil changes. In contrast, changes in soil organic matter properties under salvage logging have persisted over 10 years. Relationships among these properties and stand productivity before and after canopy closure require further investigation.

Anthropogenic and naturally occurring impacts in the Canadian boreal forest are becoming more difficult to separate in, for example, the influence of changing climate on fire occurrence [\(Price et al. 2013\)](#page-10-4). These interactions increase challenges in predicting impacts and require strategies for managing forested lands that take into account the direction, magnitude, and duration of compound effects. Results such as those that we present here provide empirical data that can contribute to longer term projections for western boreal forests. Management of these forests going forward will require a degree of flexibility that can accommodate mixedwood ecology in the context of the land-use pressures, disturbance, and changing climate increasingly coming to bear in Canada's boreal forests. With forest management for sustainable fibre supply remaining a core activity of this region (e.g., [Keddy et al. 2013\)](#page-10-42), opportunities exist to anticipate change and implement practices at the intersection of ecological and management trajectories that are aimed at maintaining or restoring a healthy and productive managed forest.

## **Conclusions**

We have quantified persistent (decadal) soil changes resulting from specific disturbance types in the western boreal mixedwood forest. We have demonstrated that although many short-term changes in soil properties occur as a result of these disturbances, not all persist over the long term. Postfire salvage logging, as a compound disturbance, was shown to have an incremental effect on soil properties beyond the single disturbances with which it was compared, with the effects of postfire salvage logging persisting over a 10-year period. Clearcut harvesting did not result in adverse changes in soil properties over the decadal period. Instead, harvesting increased available soil and foliar nutrients. The profound reduction in soil C pools under severe fire and salvage logging, with a lower rate of recovery under salvage logging, has demonstrated differences in the effects of single and multiple disturbances on soil organic matter at these sites.

Our results show differences between short- and long-term responses to disturbance, among single natural and anthropogenic disturbances, and between single and compound disturbances in the western boreal mixedwood forest. The distinction between natural and anthropogenic disturbances is becoming increasingly difficult to delineate. We are facing an array of changes in the type, intensity, and frequency of disturbances at different scales. These changes require that we continue to develop and apply empirical knowledge of disturbance impacts to aid in projecting recovery of our forests and the development of future management practices.
