# Tracking Recovery Patterns in Acidified Lakes: A Paleolimnological Perspective

John P. Smol<sup>1</sup> Brian F. Cumming<sup>1</sup> Aruna S. Dixit<sup>1</sup> Sushil S. Dixit<sup>1</sup>

### Abstract

Long-term data are often lacking to effectively assess patterns of lake acidification and recovery. Fortunately, paleolimnological techniques can be used to infer past changes in lakewater acidity and related variables by means of biological indicators, such as diatom valves and chrysophyte scales, preserved in <sup>210</sup>Pb-dated sediment cores. We summarize paleolimnological data that we have gathered from 36 Sudbury (Ontario) and 20 Adirondack Park (New York) lakes to estimate the magnitude of lake acidification and any subsequent recovery in these lake systems. In both regions, many lakes were shown to have acidified considerably, some over two pH units, since the 1850s. Although some recovery was noted in both lake regions, Sudbury lakes generally showed larger increases in inferred lakewater pH with recent declines in sulfur emissions. Possible explanations of these differences include the greater decrease in sulfate deposition in the Sudbury area, as well as generally longer residence times of lakes in Sudbury, perhaps allowing for more in-lake alkalinity generation. In addition, Sudbury lakes generally had higher pre-industrial pH levels, suggesting that lakes with higher natural buffering capacities are more likely to recover more quickly with declines in deposition, even if they had been acidified to a great extent.

### Introduction

Por several decades, lake acidification has been recognized as an important international problem affecting the water quality and biota of many lake systems. Research on the limnological effects of the atmospheric deposition of strong acids has been a major endeavour, especially in the 1980s, which has resulted in a large number of publications and reports (National Acid Precipitation Assessment Program 1990). These data frequently documented the adverse effects of acidification on lake ecosystems. Largely because of these studies, a number of legislative and voluntary measures were instigated which decreased significantly the amount of acidic deposition falling on many lake systems.

Although acidic deposition has declined in many lake regions, it is still unclear why some lakes seem to have responded positively to the decreased emissions, while others remain unresponsive. Part of the problem in dealing with these complex environmental issues is the lack of reliable long-term environmental monitoring data. A frequent complaint echoed by most environmental managers is that without such data it is often difficult to assess the magnitude of an environmental problem and, equally important, to prescribe any mitigation actions (Smol 1992, 1995). Similarly, it is difficult to evaluate whether mitigation efforts are having any desired effects without long-term monitoring data.

Fortunately, sediments often preserve a reliable archive of past limnological changes. Paleolimnological techniques have progressed to the point where statistically robust and ecologically sound approaches have been developed so that past lakewater conditions can be inferred with a high degree of reliability (Charles & Smol 1994). Paleolimnological studies of lake acidification have been used in a number of lake regions to determine if lakes have acidified and, if so, when and by how much (Charles et al. 1989; Battarbee et al. 1990; Charles & Smol 1990; Cumming et al. 1992a, 1994; Dixit et al. 1995).

We have published extensively on the use of paleolimnological techniques to infer patterns of lake acidification and recovery in the Sudbury region of Ontario (Dixit et al. 1995) and Adirondack Park of New York (Cumming et al. 1992a, 1994), as well as other regions (Charles et al. 1989). Collectively, these studies clearly indicate that different lakes follow different acidification trajectories, as well as different patterns of recovery. In some systems, no recovery was recorded, even though deposition had decreased.

# **Study Regions**

The Sudbury region of Ontario (Fig. 1) is well known for the mining and smelting of copper-nickel ore, which began in the early 1880s (Keller 1992). It is a classic example of pollution from a point source. The emissions from Sudbury have affected thousands of lakes, with many of the lakes closest to the smelters becoming very acidic (pH , 5) during the periods of peak smelting activity. The region is also famous, however, for the dramatic decreases in emissions that have occurred since the 1970s, and the striking recovery in water chemistry patterns that have been witnessed in many of the local ecosystems. Most of the studies conducted in Sudbury and the surrounding region have been summarized in our various publications cited in this paper, as well as in a recent book (Gunn 1995).

The lakes in Adirondack Park are located in a forested, mountainous region of northern New York state (Fig. 1). In contrast to Sudbury, these lakes are distant

# **Methods**

The paleolimnological methods we use are described in detail elsewhere (Cumming et al. 1994). Briefly, we remove lake sediment cores from a deep, flat basin near the center of a lake using a gravity corer specifically designed for high-resolution paleoenvironmental studies (Glew 1989, 1991). These sediments are sectioned at close intervals (between 0.25 and 1 cm, depending on the temporal interval being studied) with vertical extruders designed for high-resolution studies (Glew 1988), and the sediments are dated by 210Pb chronology (for details, see Cumming et al. 1994).

Although we use many paleolimnological indicators to reconstruct lakewater conditions, our primary indicator groups for lake acidification studies in Sudbury and the Adirondacks are the siliceous diatom valves and scales of chrysophyte algae. For both these groups we have developed statistically robust and ecologically sound transfer functions whereby we can infer, with a high degree of reliability, past lakewater pH and related variables (Dixit et al. 1989*a*, 1991, 1993*b*; Cumming et al. 1992*b*).

Diatoms and/or chrysophyte assemblages are identified and enumerated in the various sediment sections. The assemblage data are then processed through the transfer functions, from which lakewater variables (e.g., pH) can be inferred (Charles & Smol 1994; Birks 1995). From these data we can reconstruct past environmental conditions and provide trajectories of past changes in lakewater acidity, from which the timing and rates of acidification and recovery can be assessed. By comparing our paleolimnological inference data to available long-term monitoring data, we have confirmed that these paleolimnological approaches are robust and defensible (Dixit et al. 1989*b*). Paleolimnological techniques therefore provide investigators with the ability to reconstruct past limnological conditions that were not measured in the past.

### **Sudbury Lakes**

Baby Lake is small (surface area 11.7 ha) but relatively deep, with a maximum depth of 25 m. # **Adirondack Lakes**

In a population-based paleolimnological study, Cumming et al. (1992*a*) showed that approximately 80% of Adirondack study lakes with present-day pH below 6.5 have acidified since pre-industrial times. Subsequently, Cumming et al. (1994) addressed the issue of recovery patterns in Adirondack Mountain lakes using paleolimnological approaches. This large project will be summarized briefly here.

Cumming et al. (1994) used the species assemblage changes of scaled chrysophytes in 210Pb-dated sediment cores from 20 low-alkalinity lakes in Adirondack Park to determine when these acid-sensitive lakes began to acidify and if they are still acidifying. Using cluster analysis of the chrysophyte-inferred pH profiles, they differentiated four categories of lake response to acidic deposition: (1) naturally well-buffered lakes that showed little or no evidence of acidification since pre-industrial times; (2) lakes with pre-industrial pH values (inferred from the fossil chrysophyte assemblages) between 5 and 6 that began to acidify around 1900; (3) "naturally" acidic lakes that acidified even further around 1900; and (4) lakes with pre-industrial pH values around 6 that acidified around 1930–1950.

Canonical variates analysis (CVA) in the above study clearly showed that background (or pre-impact) acidity was the most important factor that could account for the four categories of lake response. For example, lakes that acidified first were those that naturally had lower pre-industrial pH values. But the above data also indicated that, despite considerable declines in acidic deposition since the 1970s (Husar et al. 1991), post-1970 trends in lakewater pH have been small and variable, suggesting that these low-alkalinity lakes have been relatively unresponsive to the post-1970 declines in sulfate deposition. An interesting finding of this survey was that some Adirondack lakes were naturally somewhat acidic, although they acidified further in this century.

The study by Cumming et al. (1994) used only chrysophyte microfossils, so no diatom data are presented here for the Adirondacks. An analysis similar to the one presented for Sudbury chrysophytes has been constructed for the 20 high-resolution Adirondack lake cores (Fig. 4) described by Cumming et al. (1994). As with the Sudbury study lakes (Fig. 3), the chrysophyte inferences of pH indicate that these low-alkalinity lakes have acidified since 1850 (Fig. 4). The average arithmetic decline in pH since 1850 (excluding lakes that were limed, see Fig. 4) was similar to that for the Sudbury region (20.96 pH units, *N* 5 15). The Adirondack data also indicate that about 50% of the lakes have shown recent increases in pH, but these increases were much smaller (average pH increase 0.15) in the Adirondacks (*N* 5 15) than those recorded in the Sudbury area (0.38 pH units, *N* 5 29).

# **Synthesis and Conclusions**

Our paleolimnological studies indicate marked acidification in a large suite of acid-sensitive lakes in both Sudbury and Adirondack Park. In the Adirondack data set, lakes that were somewhat naturally acidic (pH 5–6) were the first to acidify and showed little evidence of recovery with recent declines in sulfate deposition. In the Sudbury lake data set, we also recorded marked pH declines, especially in lakes that are located in close proximity to the smelters (e.g., Swan, Clearwater, Baby, and Daisy lakes). Interestingly, some of the Sudbury lakes with relatively high background pH values (e.g., Daisy and Baby lakes) still acidified greatly, despite their relatively high buffering capacities. This is a testament to the amount of acidic deposition that these lakes received. Nonetheless, many experienced striking recovery patterns (Figs. 2 & 3).

Our data sets are not sufficiently large to determine unequivocally why some lakes have recovered while others appear to have undergone little change. But some tentative general observations can be made.

Lakes in the Sudbury region tended to show more recovery than lakes in Adirondack Park. As noted above, Sudbury lakes tended to have higher pre-industrial pH values and had naturally higher buffering capacities. Undoubtedly, an important factor has also been that the recent declines in acidic deposition have been strikingly different between the two regions. Husar et al. (1991) estimated that sulfate deposition on Adirondack lakes has declined about 30% since the 1970s. Unfortunately, except for relatively recent measurements, we do not know how high past sulfate deposition was in Sudbury, but it was definitely much higher than in the Adirondack region, with both large wet and large dry components (Dillon et al. 1982). In the 1980s, study lakes within 100 km of Sudbury had average annual sulfate concentrations ranging from 220 to 265 meq/L (Keller et al. 1992), and this was after sulfate had already shown large declines since the 1970s, when sulfate concentrations up to 400–500 meq/L were recorded in some lakes (Keller & Pitblado 1986). In contrast, most Adirondack lakes had sulfate concentrations in the range of 50–200 meq/L during 1984–1987 (Kretser et al. 1989; Driscoll et al. 1991). Thus, historical sulfur deposition was clearly much higher in Sudbury.

Decreases in lake sulfate levels have also been much more dramatic in Sudbury. During the 1980s, lake-water sulfate declined about 3 meq/L/yr, on average, in the Sudbury study lakes (Keller et al. 1992), while sulfate in Adirondack lakes declined, on average, only 1.8 meq/ L/yr from 1982–1994 (Driscoll et al. 1995). Moreover, large lake sulfate declines had already occurred around Sudbury between the mid 1970s and the early 1980s (Keller & Pitblado 1986). Unfortunately, total acidic deposition in these two regions has not been well documented. However, the relative differences in past sulfate loading and changes in deposition are consistent with our paleolimnological data. Additional factors complicating recovery include increased nitrogen leaching from Adirondack watersheds, and decreases in the atmospheric deposition of base cations, which may be offsetting the benefits of sulfate reductions on lake acidity (Driscoll & Van Dreason 1993).

A further complicating factor in assessing trends between Sudbury and the Adirondacks is differences in hydrology. Although hydrologic data are not available for many of the lakes used in our analyses, water renewal times are typically measured on the order of years for the Sudbury lakes (W. Keller, personal communication), while flushing rates in our Adirondack lakes are typically measured in months (Cumming et al. 1994). Within-lake alkalinity generation from sulfate and nitrate reduction would be expected to be greater in lakes with longer renewal times (Schindler 1986).

This study shows that even if monitoring data are not available (e.g., pH readings over the last 150 years), these data can still be inferred using paleolimnological techniques. As noted by Smol (1992), Sörenson only established the pH convention in 1909, yet for many management questions it is important to know what limnological conditions were like in the 1800s. As exemplified in this paper, by knowing the pre-industrial pH levels in a suite of study lakes and by documenting the changes in lake-water chemistry that occurred as a result of industrialization, we can better understand the stresses that human activities have placed on lake ecosystems. Paleolimnological techniques are now available to assess many different types of environmental degradation and recovery in addition to acidification (Smol 1995).
