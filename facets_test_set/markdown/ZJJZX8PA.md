# Restoration Ecology of an Endangered Plant Species: Establishment of New Populations of *Cirsium pitcheri*

Jennifer Rowland<sup>1</sup> M. A. Maun<sup>1,2</sup>

#### **Abstract**

We determined the effects of shade, burial by sand, simulated herbivory, and fertilizers on the survival and growth of artificially planted population of Cirsium pitcheri—an endangered plant species of the sand dunes along Lake Huron. Sand burial experiments showed that greenhouse grown plants should optimally be transplanted into areas receiving 5 cm of sand deposition: burial at this depth maximized emergence, survivorship, and below-ground biomass. Under field conditions, simulated herbivory of up to 50% of the plant height produced a slight increase in biomass after one year of growth. Field observations showed that when white-tailed deer removed more than 50% of the transplant's leaf tissue, the plant died. The application of a 20:20:20 (N:P:K) water-soluble fertilizer produced a significant increase in the dry leaf biomass, total leaf area, and total dry biomass relative to control plants. We also tested for the presence or absence of a persistent seed bank. Few seeds were recovered from soil samples collected from Pinery Provincial Park and Providence Bay. However, C. pitcheri has the ability to form a persistent seed bank under field conditions but only at soil depths of ≥15 cm. Cirsium pitcheri seeds are able to germinate and seedlings can emerge from a burial depth of up to 6 cm. Thus, seeds planted in open, sunny areas will probably maximize emergence, growth, and survivorship of seedlings. Populations of

C. pitcheri can be restored by planting seeds at shallow depths, transplanting greenhouse-grown plants, applying water soluble fertilizers, and protecting plants from herbivores.

**Key words**: *Cirsium pitcheri*, restoration ecology, burial in sand, seed bank, simulated herbivory.

#### Introduction

The successful restoration of a rare or endangered ■ species requires an understanding of not only its life history and population dynamics but also the complex interactions between external and internal factors within the ecological community (Miller et al. 1992). Pavlik (1994) proposed that, for the preservation of rare and endangered species, ecologists should (1) conduct a careful study of species demography (births, deaths, survival, etc.) to assess the potential of the population to maintain its present size; and (2) identify both intrinsic and extrinsic factors that are responsible for the decline of a population. Several researchers (McEachern 1992; McEachern et al. 1994; D'Ulisse & Maun 1996; Maun et al. 1996; Phillips & Maun 1996; Maun 1997) have studied critical factors affecting the populations of Cirsium pitcheri ([Torr. Ex. Eaton] Torrey & Gray) (Pitcher's thistle or beach thistle) since 1990.

Cirsium pitcheri is a rosette-forming monocarpic perennial which establishes from seeds, and the plants may grow as rosettes for 3 to 10 years before flowering and dying (McEachern 1992). Both stems and lower surfaces of green leaves are densely covered with long white hairs, which give the plant a silvery white appearance. The species exists as metapopulations (a set of local populations linked together through dispersal) in sand dune systems and exhibits large yearly variability in density, both within and among metapopulations, mainly because of variable rates of seedling establishment per year and variable sizes of juvenile plants (McEachern 1992). Similarly, recruitment to metapopulations is variable across sites because of (1) variance in the number of flowering adults between years (D'Ulisse & Maun 1996); (2) variable insect predation (Stanforth et al. 1997); and (3) herbivory during vegetative and reproductive phases (Phillips & Maun 1996). Pavlovic (1994) classified C. pitcheri as a species that persists only under natural disturbance conditions and declines under human impact. In Canada, the species is considered endangered because of a loss of habitat, habitat degradation, and continued risk to remaining populations from recreational use and development (Maun 1999).

restoration of endangered species. For example, the existing seed banks may be enhanced by artificial planting of seeds. However, the most successful method for the restoration of *C. pitcheri* populations has been transplantation of greenhouse-grown, well-established plants, because transplants show high rates of establishment and require less time to reach flowering and fruiting stages (Loveless 1984; Bowles & McBride 1996). Once established, greenhouse-grown plants are able to adapt their growth patterns to the field environment. The only drawback of this technique would be the introduction of certain genotypes in the greenhouse grown plants that would not have survived environmental selection at the seedling stage in the field (Fahselt 1988; Bowles & McBride 1996). However, even the greenhouse-grown transplants face strong environmental selection and must cope with many environmental stresses during their establishment, growth, and reproduction.

In this study, our objective was to determine the effects of three critical stress factors: (1) herbivory by white-tailed deer; (2) burial by sand; and (3) nutrient limitation, on a recently established population of greenhouse grown plants of *C. pitcheri*. We also examined the probable existence of a seed bank and the effects of shade on the establishment of seedlings of *C. pitcheri*.

## **Methods**

# **Seed Bank Dynamics**

Answers were sought for the following two questions: (1) do the populations of *C. pitcheri* maintain a seed bank; and (2) would the seeds remain dormant and viable in the soil for an extended period of time?

**Experiment 1: Existence of a Seed Bank.** Sand cores were taken from a naturally occurring population in the Wilderness Area at Pinery Provincial Park (43815'N, 81850'W) and the shoreline sand dunes at Providence Bay (45839'N, 82816'W), Manitoulin Island, Canada during 1997 and 1998 (Table 1). At Pinery Provincial Park on both July sampling dates, transect lines were established perpendicular to the shoreline and sand cores were obtained from seven sites within the dunes (Table 1). A similar procedure was also used at Providence Bay, Manitoulin Island. In 1998, at both sites, the number of samples was decreased but sample diameter was increased. Samples taken in 1998 were located around plants that had reproduced in 1997. Sand cores were passed through a 0.5-mm sieve to remove the majority of sand particles. Detritus and litter left on the sieve were carefully examined for the presence of seeds. All *C. pitcheri* seeds were placed in petri dishes containing a layer of filter paper (Anchor Paper Co., St. Paul, MN) and 10 ml of distilled water. The dishes were then set to germinate for 30 days in an incubator (258C day, 108C night, 14 hour photoperiod). As seeds germinated, they were recorded and taken out of the dishes. After 30 days, the seed coats of remaining seeds were removed and embryos classified as rotten or potentially viable. All potentially viable embryos were then placed in a petri dish and set to germinate for another 30 days in an incubator (258C day, 108C, 14 hour photoperiod). An embryo was considered viable (germinated) when the radicle had grown to 2 mm.

**Experiment 2: Seed Bank Potential and Stratification.** To determine if *C. pitcheri* had the ability to maintain a seed bank, seeds (from artificially grown plants in a garden plot) were counted into batches of 50 seeds each (*n* 5 100) on 15 August 1997. Each batch was mixed with 60 g of beach sand from Pinery Provincial Park and sewn into nylon bags (8312 cm). Treatments consisted of five retrieval dates (1 April, 15 May, 30 June, 15 August, and 1 October 1998) and four burial depths (1, 5, 15, and 25 cm), with five replications per treatment. Bags were buried in a fenced site near Picnic Area # 9 at Pinery Provincial Park on 1 October 1997. For burial, sand was removed to the appropriate depth and randomly chosen bags were placed 10 cm apart from each other. Sand was then replaced to the original sand level. Square wooden frames (20-cm tall) were placed on top of the sand to prevent sand erosion and burial. Plots were checked regularly and all weeds were removed. On each of five dates (1 April, 15 May, 30 June, 15 August and 1 October 1998), five bags were retrieved from each of four burial depths and brought back to the lab at The University of Western Ontario. The bags were opened and all 50 seeds retrieved by sifting the samples through a sieve (0.5-mm openings). Seeds were carefully examined and separated into germinated and nongerminated seeds. Ungerminated seeds were placed in petri dishes and set to germinate as in Experiment 1. At the end of the experiment there were three categories of seeds: (1) germinated in the field; (2) dead (rotten); and (3) dormant (germinated after the removal of seed coat).

**Experiment 3: The Effect of Shade in Seedling Establishment.** The objective of this study was to determine if providing shade to young seedlings improved their establishment. On 15 September 1997, two plots (332.5 m) were created at the Environmental Science Western Field Station in London, Ontario, Canada (70 km from the Pinery) by digging pits in a loam soil to a depth of one meter and then filling them with beach sand from Pinery Provincial Park. The plots were divided into 12 subplots of 2.5 m 3 25 cm. The subplots were raked flat. At the south end of each plot a onemeter high snow fence was erected. The snow fence was covered with black landscape fabric to provide shade. Fabric was replaced every three months to prevent holes from developing. Each subplot was then divided into 10 quadrats of 25 3 25 cm each. Within each quadrat, 10 seeds of *C. pitcheri* were sown 2 cm below the sand surface.

During summer 1998, the date of seedling emergence, the number of emerged seedlings per quadrat and their survival were recorded regularly. To eliminate edge effects, subplots 1 and 12 from each large plot were deleted from analysis. Within each subplot, quadrats 1 through 4 were used as shade quadrats, and 7 through 10 as open quadrats. To eliminate error from the varying lengths of shadow during summer, quadrats 5 and 6 were not used in the analysis. The amount of photosynthetically active radiation (PAR) was measured hourly (15 readings from 7:00 a.m. to 9:00 p.m.) in both shade and open areas for three days during the summer using a Li-Cor light meter Model LI-250 (LI-COR Inc., Lincoln, NE). Simultaneously, the temperature was recorded at 1 cm above the sand surface. All plants were harvested from 22–24 August 1998, by carefully collecting all leaves and digging up all root material. The sand was washed from the plants using tap water. The plants were towel dried and then the number of leaves per plant, length of the longest leaf, root length, and total leaf area per plant were recorded. Leaf area was measured using a Li-Cor portable leaf area meter (LI-COR Inc., Lincoln, NE). Plant fractions were then placed in paper bags, oven dried to a constant weight at 808C, and then weighed.

#### **Experiments on Transplants**

For these experiments, populations of *C. pitcheri* plants (6 months old) were artificially established in September 1996 at Picnic Area # 9 within the fenced area beside the washrooms (*n* 5 42) and in a blowout (*n* 5 226) in the Wilderness Area of the Pinery Provincial Park.

**Experiment 4: Effects of Simulated Herbivory.** On 7 August 1997, 30 plants of similar size were chosen randomly from the transplanted population of *C. pitcheri*. They were randomly assigned to one of the following three herbivory treatments: control (no herbivory), 25% and 50% of plants clipped (by height). For clipping at each height, leaves were held upright, measured with a ruler from the soil surface and then clipped from the top by 25 or 50% using a razor blade. Ten replicates were used for each treatment. Survivorship and growth (number of leaves and length of longest leaf) were recorded regularly during summers 1997 and 1998. On 4 September 1998, each plant was harvested by collecting all live aboveground and belowground plant material. The following measurements were recorded: number of leaves per plant, length of the longest leaf (cm), and total leaf area per plant (cm2). Total cumulative leaf area per plant was measured using a Li-Cor portable leaf area meter. The above- and belowground plant material were then placed in paper bags, oven dried to a constant weight for seven days at 808C, and then weighed.

**Experiment 5: Effects of Burial by Sand.** On 1 November 1997, 40 plants were chosen randomly from the transplanted *C. pitcheri* population. Each plant was then randomly assigned to one of the following treatments: control (0 cm), 5, 15, and 25 cm of burial by sand. Square (50 3 50 cm) wooden frames with variable depths (depth of each frame was 3 cm more than the burial depth), were placed

around each plant and the plants were buried to the appropriate depth. Ten replicates per treatment were used. During summer 1998, emergence, survivorship, and growth (number of leaves, length of longest leaf) of each plant were recorded regularly. On 3 September 1998, all aboveground live tissue per plant was harvested and then all belowground tissue was dug up and collected. The procedure for processing the material and collecting data was identical to that of the experiment on simulated herbivory.

**Experiment 6: Application of Fertilizer.** For this experiment, 40 plants of *C. pitcheri* were chosen randomly from the transplanted population. The following treatments were then imposed on 25 April 1998: control (64 ml of distilled water) per plant, 64 ml of 1 g/L of water soluble 20:20:20 (N:P:K) per plant, 64 ml of 2.8 g/L of water soluble 20:20:20 (N:P:K) per plant, and 1.6 g of slow release 14:14:14 (N:P:K) fertilizer pellets (Nutricote) per plant. Water soluble fertilizer solutions were poured in a circle 3 cm from the plant. The fertilizer pellets were buried at 2 cm below the sand surface in a circle about 5 cm from the plant. During summer 1998, survivorship and the growth (number of leaves, length of longest leaf) of each plant were recorded regularly. On 3 September 1998, all aboveground live tissue was harvested and then all belowground tissue was dug up and collected. The procedure for processing the material and recording data was identical to the Experiment on simulated herbivory.

# **Statistical Analysis**

All statistical tests were performed using MINITAB version 11.2. The percent values in all experiments were transformed to arcsine square root to normalize the data before analysis. Student's *t*-tests were used to determine differences between shade and sun treatments in the experiment on the effect of shade. In the experiments on simulated herbivory, burial by sand, and application of fertilizer, one- or two-way analysis of variance (ANOVA) were used to determine significance. The data for the number of leaves per plant were log transformed before the use of ANOVA.

Sand samples collected from Pinery Provincial Park and Providence Bay contained very few seeds. In 1997, none of the 700 samples collected from the Pinery Provincial Park in July contained seeds. Only one dormant and three dead seeds were found in 1998. Similar results were recorded from samples collected from Providence Bay, Manitoulin Island, with only two dormant and three dead seeds in 1997 and five dormant and eight dead seeds in 1998.

#### **Experiment 5: Effects of Burial by Sand**

All plants emerged and survived burial depths of 0, 5, and 15 cm. However, only five of ten plants emerged from the 25-cm burial depth and of these, only three survived until September 1998. The date of emergence in spring was related to burial depth. The greater the burial depth, the slower was the rate of emergence of plants. All plants in the 0, 5, and 15 cm burial treatments emerged by 10 May 1998, except two plants buried at 15-cm depth whose emergence was delayed by four weeks. In contrast, only three of the ten plants had emerged from the 25 cm burial depth by 10 June and two more grew out of the sand between 10 June and 25 June.

The number of leaves per plant was significantly higher in the control and 5-cm burial depths than 15 and 25-cm burial treatments until about 10 June (Table 4). Thereafter, the 15- and 25-cm treatments started to catch up. By 25 July, the number of leaves in the 15- and 25-cm treatments had caught up to the control and there were no significant differences among treatment means. When the plants were harvested on 3 September, the plants in both the 15- and 25-cm treatments had significantly greater numbers of leaves per plant than the control plants (Table 4).

On 25 April, the leaves were significantly longer in the 0- and 5-cm burial treatments than in the 15- and 25-cm burial treatments (Table 4). By the end of the experiment, the total leaf area in the 25-cm burial treatments was almost four times that of

the control plants. The total biomass per plant did not differ among all burial treatments.

# **Discussion**

# **Seed Bank**

A seed bank, defined as viable seeds present both above and below the soil surface (Thompson & Grime 1979), is potentially important to the dynamics of an endangered plant species because it can maintain the population over time (Pavlik et al. 1993; Teo-Sherrell et al. 1996), and can serve as a pool of genetic material that enables a species to withstand a range of responses to ordinary environmental conditions (Teo-Sherrell et al. 1996). Does *C. pitcheri* maintain a persistent seed bank [seeds that are viable for more than one year (Thompson & Grime 1979)] in its natural field populations? There are strong indications from this study that a persistent seed bank may not be a contributing factor in the population growth of *C. pitcheri*. These results are consistent with those found at Sleeping Bear Dunes National Lakeshore, along Lake Michigan, in an area containing a large natural population of *C. pitcheri* (S. Louda 1998, University of Nebraska, Lincoln, personal communication). A similar conclusion was also reached by Baptista and Shumway (1998) who showed that the existing seed banks in four different coastal sand dunes were very small and hence unlikely to enhance species diversity during restoration.

Does *C. pitcheri* have the potential to maintain a persistent seed bank? The data indicated that *C. pitcheri* does have the potential to have a small persistent seed bank because after one year, more than 1, 6, 27, and 43 percent of the seeds had not germinated but remained viable at 1-, 5-, 15-, and 25-cm depths, respectively. The optimal depth at which seeds formed a persistent seed bank was more than or equal to 15 cm since a minimum of 28% of the seeds remained dormant at 15 and 25 cm until October. The greater the burial depth, the greater was the proportion of seeds that remained dormant. Similar results were reported by Lee and Ignaciuk (1985) for seed germination of three species (*Atriplex laciniata* Torr.*, Cakile maritima* Scop. and *Salsola kali* L.); by Doucet and Cavers (1996) for seed germination of *C. vulgare*, and by Zhang and Maun (1994) for seed germination of seven species on lacustrine sand dunes of Lakes Huron and Erie. Thus, the microenvironment of deeply buried seeds is not conducive to their germination and prevents them from premature germination.

Would these seeds contribute to the future growth of the population and aid in the long-term survival of the species? Since they are deeply buried, erosion of sand would have to occur before the seedlings from germinated seeds would be able to emerge above the sand surface. Chen and Maun (1999) showed that the maximum depth from which seedlings of *C. pitcheri* could emerge was 6 cm. Hence, the erosion of at least 9 to 19 cm of sand would have to take place from 15- and 25-cm depths, respectively, before dormant seeds would contribute to the population. Thus, the contribution of a persistent seed bank to the population is questionable. Although the species has the potential to maintain a seed bank, the seeds must be buried deeply at greater than 15- to 25-cm depths. A transient seed bank (seeds are viable for less than one year) could be formed each year at depths of less than 6 cm (Chen & Maun 1999). This would aid in the growth of the populations, while allowing natural selection to operate on seedlings.

# **The Effect of Shade in Seedling Establishment**

Nurse-plants are known to improve survival of seedlings in their vicinity (McLeod & Murphy 1977; Kellman & Kading 1992). Shade cast by nurse-plants lowers surface soil temperatures and reduces evapo-transpiration of young seedlings, which allows them to escape desiccation. Does *C. pitcheri* require nurse-plants for increased survival and establishment of seedlings in sand dunes? Whereas the presence of nurse-plants might be useful for increased seedling survival of some plant species such as *Ptelea trifoliata* (McLeod & Murphy 1977), *Calamovilfa longifolia,* or *Artemisia caudata* (Maun 1990), it is doubtful that it would benefit *C. pitcheri* because seedlings grown in the shade exhibited lower survival, produced fewer leaves per plant and showed lower biomass per plant. Clearly, *C. pitcheri* is a heliophyte that requires full sunlight for maximum survival and growth. Thus, for artificial increase of its populations the species should be planted in areas receiving full sunlight.

## **Herbivory by White-Tailed Deer**

Transplanted populations of *C. pitcheri* must survive many natural causes of mortality. Within Pinery Provincial Park, one of the leading causes of *C. pitcheri* mortality has been herbivory by white-tailed deer (Phillips & Maun 1996). The data showed that *C. pitcheri* plants exhibited a temporary set-back soon after the imposition of herbivory but the plants had sufficient time to recover and compensate for lost tissue by the end of the next year of growth. Similar results were obtained by Phillips and Maun (1996), who showed that juvenile plants (90- and 125-day-old) compensated for limited amounts of tissue removal. However, regardless of the amount of tissue removed, the re-growth of shoots occurred at the expense of belowground biomass (Phillips & Maun 1996). Since the probability of flowering is dependent on the amount of stored root reserves (Keddy & Keddy 1984), plants subjected to bouts of herbivory would require a longer juvenile period to reach the reproductive stage, thus increasing their period of vulnerability to herbivory, insect attack, excessive burial, and other environmental stresses.

## **Burial by Sand**

Another important factor controlling the population dynamics of *C. pitcheri* populations is recurring sand burial resulting from wind, wave action, and recreational activities (Maun et al*.* 1996). Burial in sand had both negative and positive effects on *C. pitcheri*. In small amounts, it stimulated plant growth and in large amounts it inhibited both emergence and survival. The increase in aboveground tissue came at the expense of belowground biomass. Following the burial treatments, experimental plants were able to increase their growth rates so that they were either equal to or surpassed control. For emergence through a sand deposit, the plant requires energy reserves in which the amount available is dependent on the season and the stage at which a plant is buried (Maun et al. 1996). Growth stimulation as a result of small amounts of burial in sand may be a result of improved soil conditions (Disraeli 1984), mycorrhizal fungi association (Little & Maun 1996), and lowered water stress (Olson 1958). When plants are buried in sand there is a decrease in both the temperature and concentration of oxygen in the root zone, but there is an increase in the amount of moisture (Maun 1998). These factors may have allowed an increase in the growth of plants.

#### **Mineral Nutrition**

The dune systems in general are deficient in three macronutrients: nitrogen, phosphorus, and potassium (Baldwin & Maun 1983). The addition of water-soluble fertilizer had a positive effect on *C. pitcheri*, showing that the application of additional nutrients improved the growth of plants. Since nutrients are quickly leached from the sand dunes, water-soluble fertilizers should be applied early in the growing season to improve growth. In general, the application of fertilizers is both expensive and time-consuming, and therefore should only be used as a short-term conservation method. Once the plant population is large enough it must be able to survive on its own. In contrast, slow release fertilizer pellets were found to be detrimental to both the growth and biomass of *C. pitcheri*, probably due to very low levels of precipitation during summer 1998.

Based on these studies, we propose that in the short term the population size of *C. pitcheri* along Lake Huron can be increased by (1) planting seeds and creating a transient seed bank in open sunny area of the sand dunes; (2) using greenhouse-grown transplants to create new populations; (3) applying water-soluble fertilizers early in the growing season; and (4) protecting plants from herbivores such as the white-tailed deer. It must be emphasized that sand dune habitats for establishing transplanted populations be carefully selected. The best approach would be to choose areas that approximately mimic the physical and biological conditions prevailing in the naturally-occurring populations.
