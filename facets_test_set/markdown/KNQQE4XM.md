# **ARTICLE**

## Effects of harvesting and drought on CO2 and H2O fluxes in an aspen-dominated western boreal plain forest: early chronosequence recovery

R.M. Petrone, L. Chasmer, C. Hopkinson, U. Silins, S.M. Landhäusser, N. Kljun, and K.J. Devito

**Abstract:** This study examines the hydrological recovery of two regenerating boreal trembling aspen (*Populus tremuloides* Michx.) dominated stands and the sensitivity of that regeneration to drought within the first 5 years of establishment. The results indicate that evapotranspiration fluxes and water-use efficiency rebounded quickly as a result of new vegetation foliage growth and wet conditions found within the first 2 years following the harvest. However, a period of dry years had a significant influence on rates of postharvest growth, carbon dioxide (CO2), and water fluxes at these sites. The northern study area (NSA) and southern study area (SSA) were harvested in the winters of 2007 and 2008, respectively. The first and second years of regeneration at the SSA and NSA, respectively, were marked by an early spring thaw and higher-than-normal precipitation, while air temperatures remained slightly above the 30-year normal. During this period, mean measured height of vegetation tripled at both sites, and cumulative evapotranspiration was approximately 60% of that prior to harvest by the end of the second year of growth. By the third year (2009), the NSA became a sink for atmospheric CO2 during the snow free season (days of the year 128–238) despite low precipitation during the latter half of the summer. Volumetric soil moisture content in 2009 was the highest (on average) of the 5 years examined due to heavy snowfall and a late start to the growing season (where air temperatures consistently exceeded 0 °C), resulting in sustained productivity. However, cumulative annual precipitation also declined to 79% and 57% in 2009 and 2010, respectively, of the 30-year normal for that region, leading to significant (lagged) declines in forest productivity at the NSA in 2010 and 2011. This resulted in the site becoming a source of CO2 to the atmosphere during the 2010 and 2011 growing seasons (annual balance was not measured). Throughout the drought period (2009, 2010, and 2011), mean stand height increased by only 15%, 11%, and 14%, respectively, compared with the mean stand height in 2008. Water-use efficiency also declined in 2010 and 2011, whereas differences in light-use efficiency did not vary significantly because foliage was maintained (i.e., leaves did not abscise as a result of drought). The results of this study indicate that regenerating aspen stands are sensitive to drought and respond relatively quickly to changes in the soil moisture regime. This is important because regional drying as a result of predicted climatic changes combined with increased industrial activity may result in significant decline in productivity within these stands over broad regions.

*Key words:* trembling aspen, evapotranspiration, net ecosystem exchange, water-use efficiency, harvest.

### **Introduction**

The northern boreal forest covers approximately 29% of North America [\(Brandt 2009\)](#page-11-0) and is a significant sink for atmospheric carbon dioxide (CO2) due to broad area coverage and the prevalence of highly productive deciduous trees [\(Gower et al. 2001\)](#page-12-0). Trembling aspen (*Populus tremuloides* Michx.) is the most widespread deciduous broadleaf tree species in North America, occurring over a wide range of site conditions with the greatest continuous expanse found in the western boreal forest [\(Little 1971;](#page-12-1) [Rowe 1972;](#page-12-2) [Walter and Breckle 1991;](#page-12-3) [Peterson and Peterson 1992\)](#page-12-4). Recently there have been several northern boreal forest initiatives examining energy, evapotranspiration (ET), and carbon (C) fluxes such as the Boreal Ecosystem Atmosphere Study (BOREAS) and the Boreal Ecosystem Research and Monitoring Sites (BERMS) projects [\(Griffis et al. 2003;](#page-12-5) [Black et al. 1996;](#page-11-1) [Kljun et al. 2007;](#page-12-6) [Gower et al.](#page-12-7) [1997;](#page-12-7) [Barr et al. 2007\)](#page-11-2). These studies indicate that despite their short growing season, aspen-dominated forests are more productive than evergreen sites [\(Griffis et al. 2003\)](#page-12-5). More recently, the BERMS project has incorporated three harvested and three previously burned jack pine sites [\(Mkhabela et al. 2009;](#page-12-8) [Amiro et al.](#page-11-3) [2006;](#page-11-3) [Howard et al. 2004;](#page-12-9) [Zha et al. 2009\)](#page-13-0), as well as later chronosequence regenerating stands. Currently, there is only one chronosequence study of a one-year-old harvested aspen site [\(Amiro](#page-11-4) [2001\)](#page-11-4), and no study has investigated the short-term impact of the harvesting of aspen forests on ecosystem C and water balances in the western boreal forest in general, or in the subhumid, droughtprone climate of the boreal plains (BP) ecozone specifically.

Under atmospheric conditions favourable to growth of other boreal species, e.g., jack pine (*Pinus banksiana* Lamb.) and black spruce (*Picea mariana* (Mill.) Britton, Sterns & Poggenb.), maximum photosynthetic capacity of trembling aspen is more than double that of these other species, resulting in greater cumulative net ecosystem production (NEP) [\(Griffis et al. 2003;](#page-12-5) [Zha et al. 2013\)](#page-13-1). Warming and earlier spring snowmelt may hasten budburst and increase growing-season length [\(Barr et al. 2007;](#page-11-2) [Kljun et al. 2007\)](#page-12-6), resulting in substantial increases in gross ecosystem production (GEP) during years with adequate soil moisture [\(Arain et al. 2002\)](#page-11-5). However, there is strong evidence that increased warming and periodic droughts are affecting the health and productivity of boreal forest ecosystems in some regions [\(Michaelin et al. 2011\)](#page-12-10). Air temperatures (*T*a) and precipitation (*P*) are expected to increase through the 21st century [\(Intergovernmental Panel on](#page-12-11) [Climate Change \(IPCC\) 2007\)](#page-12-11), which may lead to higher productivity within the boreal zone [\(Zha et al. 2013\)](#page-13-1). However, [Zha et al.](#page-13-1) [\(2013\)](#page-13-1) also caution that increased productivity under relatively normal, nonlimiting conditions does not account for the possibility of water stress due to increased ET. For example, periods of drought, shifting *P* patterns, and high *T*<sup>a</sup> extremes may be responsible for the declining forest health observed in southern and central parts of the boreal forest [\(Goetz et al. 2005;](#page-12-12) [Zhang et al.](#page-13-2) [2009;](#page-13-2) [Goetz et al. 2011;](#page-12-13) [Michaelin et al. 2011\)](#page-12-10).

Despite their broad distribution, aspen forests are acutely sensitive to drought reoccurring over a period of years [\(Krishnan et al.](#page-12-14) [2006;](#page-12-14) [Kljun et al. 2007;](#page-12-6) [Michaelin et al. 2011\)](#page-12-10). Within a mature aspen stand, [Kljun et al. \(2007\)](#page-12-6) found that NEP was a significant sink for CO2 (more than double that of an average year) during the first year of drought because soil moisture storage within deeper layers of the soil profile could be accessed. During the second and third years of drought, NEP declined to well below average levels [\(Krishnan et al. 2006;](#page-12-14) [Kljun et al. 2007\)](#page-12-6), and rates of height growth and foliage amount declined significantly [\(Krishnan et al. 2006\)](#page-12-14). Using remote-sensing data and aerial photography beyond the stand level, [Michaelin et al. \(2011\)](#page-12-10) found substantial mortality (>55%) of some aspen and mixed broadleaf stands in the southern boreal zone as a result of the same drought examined in [Krishnan](#page-12-14) [et al. \(2006\)](#page-12-14) and [Kljun et al. \(2007\).](#page-12-6) Over broad areas, declines in ecosystem productivity may be a visual sign of drought stress due to reduced *P* and (or) increased drying in these regions [\(Goetz et al.](#page-12-12) [2005,](#page-12-12) [2011;](#page-12-13) [Zhang et al. 2009;](#page-13-2) [Michaelin et al. 2011\)](#page-12-10); however, the actual mechanisms for change and species sensitivity to drought are not well understood. As climate-warming trends increase, widespread aridity may have substantial but relatively unknown influences on ecosystem dynamics [\(Kurz et al. 2008\)](#page-12-15). To date, no studies have examined the sensitivity of recently harvested and regenerating aspen-dominated stands to prolonged drought and water stress [\(Zha et al. 2013\)](#page-13-1).

This study examines two regenerating aspen-dominated stands following harvest within the BP ecozone of the Canadian boreal forest. This region contains the greatest proportion of trembling aspen stands [\(Peterson and Peterson 1992\)](#page-12-4) and is characterized as a mosaic of fragmented upland forests, riparian ecosystems, and pond–peatland complexes [\(Petrone et al. 2008;](#page-12-16) [Rizzo and Wiken](#page-12-17) [1989\)](#page-12-17). The BP ecozone is also prone to periods of significant drought, where ET exceeds *P* [\(Marshall et al. 1999;](#page-12-18) [Devito et al. 2005\)](#page-12-19), and there is heavy anthropogenic disturbance from resource extraction. The objectives of this study are as follows:

- 1. quantify water and CO2 exchanges within two regenerating aspen stands for the first 5 years following harvest;
- 2. examine the resiliency of stands to a period of drought by reporting on growing-season trajectories of production efficiencies (water-use efficiency, WUE, and light-use efficiency, LUE), soil moisture influences, and changes in growth.

Sensitivity to drought and long-term feedbacks will provide important insights into aspen-dominated stand dynamics and response to soil moisture deficits (all else being equal).

### **Site characteristics**

Two recently harvested upland trembling aspen stands were established as part of a larger study within the Utikuma Regional Study Area (URSA), located in the BP ecozone in north-central Alberta (56°6=N, 116°32=W) [\(Fig. 1](#page-2-0)*a*) [\(Devito et al. 2005\)](#page-12-19). The climate in this area is subhumid, with mean annual *P* ranging from 481 to 518 mm [\(Environment Canada 2010\)](#page-12-20) and 30-year monthly mean temperatures ranging from –14.6 to 15.8 °C [\(Ecoregions Working](#page-12-21) [Group 1989\)](#page-12-21). The BP ecozone is a glacial landscape characterized by small variations in topography (655–670 m above sea level), heterogeneous upland moraine landforms, and adjacent pond– peatland complexes.

Harvested areas examined in this study [\(Fig. 1](#page-2-0)*b*) are located on nearby uplands (within 200 m of each other) and share an adjacent pond between them [\(Devito et al. 2005\)](#page-12-19). Both preharvest aspen stands had regenerated from a wildfire in 1962 and were approaching maturity when they were harvested. In March 2007, the northern study area (NSA) was harvested, and approximately 1 year later (February 2008), the southern study area (SSA) was harvested, both with minimal root damage as a result of deep snow (>60 cm) and frozen ground. ## **Data collection and methods**

### **Meteorological data collection**

Hydrometeorological data were collected throughout the year on short-stature (4.3 m) towers at each harvest site, coincident with energy and mass exchange measurements. Variability in temporal measurements and inter- or intra-annual climate were examined throughout the year and more specifically during the snow-free period from 8 May to 26 August (days of the year 128– 238) in 2007–2011 (NSA) and 2008–2009 (SSA). Mean half-hourly atmospheric measurements included *T*<sup>a</sup> (°C), measured at a height of 1.3 m above ground level (a.g.l) (HOBO Onset Pro Temperature; Hoskin Scientific, Vancouver, Canada), and relative humidity (RH, %), measured at a height of 0.5 m above the canopy (HOBO Onset Pro RH; Hoskin Scientific). Radiation measurements included abovecanopy net radiation (*Q*\*, W·m−2), measured at a height of approximately 4 m a.g.l. (NR Lite radiometers; Kipp & Zonen B.V., the Netherlands). Wind speed and direction were measured using wind monitors (R.M. Young Company, Michigan, USA) and were used primarily to locate the origin of half-hourly flux footprints.

Soil measurements including moisture, temperature, and heat fluxes were collected at sites located 20 m, 25 m, and 85 m from each tower and then averaged. Soil heat fluxes (*QG*) were measured using two heat-flux transducers (HFT-03; Campbell Scientific Inc., Utah, USA) at each site, buried 0.05 m below the litter fall horizon – mineral soil interface. Soil temperature and heat storage in the upper 0.05 m were measured using a thermocouple probe (TCAV-L; Campbell Scientific Inc.) inserted at 0.025 and 0.075 m below the soil surface. Soil volumetric moisture content (VMC, %) (CS616 TDR; Campbell Scientific Inc.; calibrated for study-site soils) and soil temperatures (107B thermistors; Campbell Scientific Inc.) were recorded at depths of 0.01, 0.10, 0.30, 0.50, and 1.0 m below the litter fall horizon – mineral soil interface at each site [\(Redding and](#page-12-22) [Devito 2008;](#page-12-22) [Brown et al. 2013\)](#page-11-6). VMC data were corrected for soil bulk density and temperature. Groundwater levels were measured using well data adjacent to eddy-covariance towers and at two sites at the riparian or stand edge. Cumulative *P* was measured within an open area near the NSA using hand gauges and a tipping-bucket rain gauge (R.M. Young Company).

#### **Eddy-covariance instrumentation and processing**

Net ecosystem exchange (NEE), latent (Le) and sensible (H) energy exchanges, and friction velocity were measured using eddycovariance instrumentation for the snow-free period, coincident with hydrometeorological data. Instrumentation deployment followed the same protocols for setup and data processing for both the NSA and SSA so that measurements between sites would be intercomparable. Instrumentation consisted of an open-path infrared gas analyzer (IRGA) (LI-7500; LI-COR, Inc., Nebraska, USA) and a three-dimensional sonic anemometer (CSAT3; Campbell Scientific Inc.) installed at a height of 4.3 m a.g.l. (approximately 1 m above the top of the vegetation canopy by year 5 of growth for the

NSA) per site. Fluxes were sampled at a rate of 20 Hz and averaged over half-hourly periods using a data logger (CR23X; Campbell Scientific Inc.). Briefly, NEE correction procedures (Giroux 2012) included filtering for periods of low friction velocity (<0.35 m·s<sup>-1</sup>) (Petrone et al. 2007) and rotation of vertical and horizontal wind velocities to zero (Kaimal and Finnigan 1994). Gaps within eddycovariance data were filled based on the mean moving windows over 14-day periods (Falge et al. 2001) and quality controlled to remove outliers exceeding two standard deviations of the mean (Papale et al. 2006). GEP was estimated from half-hourly estimates of NEP (where NEP = -NEE), assumed positive when the ecosystem is a sink for atmospheric CO<sub>2</sub>, and total ecosystem respiration (R<sub>e</sub>). Nighttime NEP was used as a direct estimate of R<sub>e</sub> during periods when friction velocity was greater than the minimum threshold. For periods below this threshold, R<sub>e</sub> was estimated using an empirical model as a function of within-canopy Ta. Daytime GEP was estimated as the sum of NEP and  $R_e$  (GEP = NEP +  $R_e$ ), where daytime R<sub>e</sub> was determined from the same empirical model used for nighttime R<sub>e</sub> (Griffis et al. 2003).

A flux footprint parameterization based on a full-scale Lagrangian particle model (Kljun et al. 2002; Kljun et al. 2004) was used to estimate contribution areas for mass fluxes at both sites during the summer of 2009. The footprint is defined as the probability of contribution by  $\mathrm{CO}_2$  and water fluxes per unit area upwind of the eddy-covariance system. Although most inputs were obtained from eddy covariance and wind direction, roughness length (z0m) and zero plane displacement (d) of vegetation were mapped at 1 m resolution within 10° wind sectors based on canopy height from airborne light detection and ranging (LiDAR) data (z0m = 1/10 height; d = 2/3 height of trees) (Chasmer et al. 2011). Wind sectors were used to constrain the iterative footprint model, which was then accumulated over the growing season.

### Efficiency parameters

In this study, LUE, WUE, and Landsberg light-response curves were used to assess resource use through the growth period per year. LUE, the use of light by vegetation for photosynthesis (Schwalm et al. 2006; Chasmer et al. 2008), was determined as the ratio of GEP to intercepted photosynthetically active radiation (IPAR). Above- and below-canopy intercepted and reflected photosynthetically active radiation (PAR) measurements were not acquired for stands due to the low height, dense cover, and lack of definitive understory immediately following the harvest. Therefore, IPAR was estimated as follows using the site mean effective LAI (LAI<sub>e</sub>) from ceptometer measurements:

(1) 
$$IPAR = PAR[1 - e^{(-LAI_ek)}]$$

where *k* is an assumed extinction coefficient of 0.5 for both sites. The relationship between IPAR (at the top of the canopy) and GEP was also determined using a Landsberg light response curve (e.g., Turner et al. 2002, 2003):

(2) 
$$GEP_{Landsberg} = P_{max} [1 - e^{-a(PAR - I_{comp})}]$$

where  $P_{\rm max}$  is the maximum mean GEP,  $\alpha$  is the slope (scaling factor) of the initial rise of GEP with incoming PAR, and  $I_{\rm comp}$  is the point at which GEP is zero, i.e., the light compensation point.

WUE, the total amount of biomass produced by photosynthesis for every gram of water lost through transpiration (Rosenberg et al. 1983), was determined from the ratio of GEP to ET (for dry periods only, where dry periods are defined as no precipitation + one day to allow for drying).

### Stand mensuration

Vegetation structure was sampled every year at the time of peak foliage (second week in July) in three 15 m  $\times$  15 m plots per site

(Brown et al. 2010). Measurements included the following: tree height (Vertex hypsometer; Sweden); diameter at breast height (DBH; 1.3 m a.g.l.) (Vernier caliper); and site mean LAI<sub>e</sub> (AccuPAR LP-80 ceptometer; Decagon Devices Inc., Washington, USA). Measurements of PAR by the ceptometer were acquired on overcast days along two (east–west and north–south) 100 m transects per site, and were sampled at a height of approximately 0.25 m a.g.l. every 5 m at centre and again at a distance of 2 m perpendicular to the transect (left and right of centre). LAI<sub>e</sub> estimates from ceptometer measurements were checked and calibrated using digital hemispherical photography (processed with DHP and TRACwin software; Leblanc et al. 2005) acquired at 17 and 14 coincident locations within the NSA and SSA, respectively.

#### Airborne LiDAR data collection and processing

Airborne LiDAR remote-sensing data were used to compare spatial changes in canopy height and cover between 2008 and 2011 (and preharvest conditions in 2002). LiDAR data were collected in September 2008 for Alberta Sustainable Resource Development using an ALTM 3100EA (Optech Inc., Canada). The system was operated at a flying height of 1400 m a.g.l., with a laser pulserepetition frequency of 50 kHz and a scan angle of ±25°. Data collected in August 2011 was acquired and processed by the authors using a slightly different sensor model (ALTM3100; Optech Inc.) operated at a flying height of 1800 m a.g.l. The laser pulserepetition frequency was 50 kHz, with a scan angle of ±23°. A 50% overlap of scan lines was adopted to reduce laser "shadowing" by canopies and to ensure sampling of both sides of trees. LiDAR data were also collected in 2002 and were used to briefly describe preharvest stand structural characteristics (Hopkinson et al. 2005; Chasmer et al. 2011).

LiDAR data were classified and processed in TerraScan (TerraSolid, Finland) and were output into ground and nonground (vegetation) classes, after the removal of outliers. Several gridded products were derived following Chasmer et al. (2011), including a digital elevation model (DEM, m), a digital surface model (DSM, m) of canopy heights plus topography, and a canopy height model (CHM (m) = DSM – DEM). The difference between the 2008 and 2011 CHMs quantify growth characteristics of regenerating species throughout the two sites (Fig. 1c), residual mature aspen trees that were left over after harvest, and slash piles that were burned during the winter following the LiDAR survey.

### Results

### Site climate variability

Climatology during the 5 years of study (2007-2011) was marked by 2 years of wetter-than-normal conditions directly after harvest (2007-2008) followed by a 2-year drought period that began approximately 2.5 and 1.5 years after the harvest at the NSA and the SSA, respectively. Harvest years 2007 (at the NSA) and 2008 (at the SSA) were the wettest in 13 years and exceeded the 30-year normal for the region (Table 1). Above-average rainfall and snow accumulation in 2007 and 2008 maintained high soil VMC into June 2009 (maximum mean of 0.42 m<sup>3</sup>·m<sup>-3</sup>; Fig. 2). Higher-than-normal rates of P continued with above-average snow accumulation into spring 2009. By summer 2009, both sites experienced significant declines in P (Table 1; Fig. 6e), whereas initially high VMC declined through the July-August period (2009) and remained low through 2010 and 2011 as a result of low cumulative rainfall (Table 1; Fig. 2). Minimum mean soil moisture of 0.27 m<sup>3</sup>⋅m<sup>-3</sup> occurred in the summer 2010, whereas July groundwater levels ranged from 4 to 6 m below the surface at the towers and from 1 to 2 m below the surface near the riparian edge. Groundwater levels measured near the towers were lowest during the wettest year in 2007 as a result of a previous dry period, peaking in 2009 (with shallowest levels) in response to P inputs in 2007 and 2008. ### **Energy exchanges**

Although  $Q^*$  did not vary significantly between sites or years (Fig. 2), the partitioning of  $Q^*$  into latent ( $Q_e$ ) and sensible ( $Q_h$ ) heat exchanges did vary significantly (Fig. 3). Both sites exhibited similar changes in  $Q_h$  and  $Q_e$  following the harvest, with slightly greater proportions of mean  $Q^*$  used for  $Q_e$  (Table 1).  $Q_e$  increased through the growing season, peaking in late June or early July, whereas  $Q_h$  peaked earlier in the season (June) and declined through July and August with increased foliage cover and ET rates. The decline in  $Q_e$  through 2011 (mean = 32% of  $Q^*$ ) occurred as a lagged response to drought, showing little rebound to earlier (2007–2009) levels by the end of the study period (Fig. 3).

## Spatial and temporal changes in vegetation growth following the harvest

The NSA had the longest record of structural measurements (Table 2). At both sites, mean canopy height tripled during the first 2 years of establishment. However, growth rates of regeneration species also appeared to be affected by drought between mid2009 and 2011, as observed at the NSA. Growth rates at the SSA were not influenced by drought during the second year of growth, likely because soil moisture was retained from previous warm, wet years.

Between 2008 and 2009, growth rates slowed to 15% of total growth between 2007 and 2008 and 11% of total growth between 2009 and 2010 (compared with 2008-2009) and increased slightly in 2011 to 14% (Table 2). It is not known what the growth rate throughout the 5-year period would have been if conditions had been similar to the first 2 years of growth; however, a decline in growth rate in 2010, followed by slight rebound in 2011, indicates that arid conditions may have had some influence. Mean LAI<sub>e</sub> estimates measured in July of each year doubled between years 1 and 2 of growth at both sites, but declined to 21% of growth by year 3, to 5% by year 4, and to 7% by year 5 at the NSA (Table 2). DBH also more than doubled between years 1 and 2 of growth, but declined to 26% by 2009, 15% by 2010, and 11% by 2011 compared with the mean growth of the previous year. This indicates that foliage growth rates were most affected by drought over the 3-year period and the late start to the growing season in 2009.

Spatial variability of growth rates determined from two airborne LiDAR surveys (2008 and 2011) (Fig. 4) were greatest at the NSA as expected (due to the timing of harvest in 2007) and even exceeded 2 m in some areas, whereas at the SSA, harvested 1 year later, site mean growth rates were approximately 1.3 m over the 3-year period. Interestingly, growth rates at both sites were greatest in areas adjacent to mature aspen residuals, adjacent to trees that were not cleared during harvest, and near edges where harvested areas were adjacent to mature trees (Fig. 4c). A total of 53 mature trees remained at the NSA following the harvest, whereas only 13 remained at the SSA. Areas with higher growth rates did not correspond with parts of sites that were characterized by having taller trees preharvest (Fig. 4a) or by variations in topography.

### **Postharvest efficiency**

WUE increased steadily during the first 3 years of growth at the NSA, reaching a mean WUE of 3.3 g C·(g H2O)–1 (repeated measures ANOVA on ranks, *p <* 0.05 for differences between years) [\(Fig. 7](#page-9-0)*a*). Slightly higher efficiencies were recorded during the first 2 years of growth at the SSA compared with the first 2 years of growth at the NSA; however, they were not significantly different. At the NSA, WUE decreased by 57% of 2009 amounts in 2010 (1.89 g C·(g H2O)–1) in response to decreased soil moisture content, declining foliage growth rates, and reduced GEP. WUE then rebounded to 2.44 g C·(g H2O)–1 in 2011 in response to increased *P* during the latter part of the summer.

Mean LUE was slightly less during the second year of growth at SSA (1.35 g C·MJ−1) compared with NSA (1.58 g C·MJ−1), in response to differences in measured LAIe (used to estimate IPAR) and C uptake [\(Fig. 7](#page-9-0)*b*). LUE peaked in 2009 (1.63 g C·MJ−1) and declined to 1.42 g C·MJ−1 and 1.23 g C·MJ−1, on average, in 2010 and 2011, respectively. Unlike drought responses of mature aspen stands (e.g., [Kljun et al. 2007\)](#page-12-6), rates of foliage growth and intercepted radiation declined, but foliage was not lost from the canopy. Therefore, LUE was relatively stable through the drought period as growth matched GEP.

Similar responses of GEP compared with intercepted PAR (Landsberg light response curves; [Fig. 8\)](#page-10-0) illustrate a slightly different trend to [Fig. 7](#page-9-0)*b*. The use of light for photosynthesis increased during the first 3 years of rapid growth at the NSA (first 2 years at the SSA), and *P*max leveled off with increasingly greater amounts of light per year (approximately 52, 88, and 137 W·m−2 for years 2007 to 2009, respectively, at the NSA). By 2010, *P*max was lower than the maximum rates of increase observed in 2009 (0.32 g C·m−2·(30 min)−1), declining to 0.18 g C·m−2·(30 min)−1. At the SSA, *P*max reached 0.11 g C·m−2·(30min)−1 and 0.21 g C·m−2·(30min)−1 within the first 2 years of harvest (2008 and 2009, respectively), indicating an increased productivity with light interception in year 2 compared with the NSA at the same stage of growth. The GEP at the SSA also leveled off at greater amounts of incident PAR than at the NSA (approximately 78 and 161 W·m−2 in 2008 and 2009, respectively).

### **Regional implications of drought**

There was an extreme drought that began in July 2001 and lasted through 2003 [\(Barr et al. 2007\)](#page-11-2) within western parts of the Canadian boreal zone. Many studies (e.g., [Krishnan et al. 2006;](#page-12-14) [Barr et al. 2007;](#page-11-2) [Kljun et al. 2007;](#page-12-6) [Michaelin et al. 2011\)](#page-12-10) suggest that drought poses a significant threat to boreal forest species, especially because drying is expected to increase with climate warming in these regions [\(Hogg and Bernier 2005;](#page-12-42) [Hogg et al. 2008;](#page-12-43) [Michaelin et al. 2011\)](#page-12-10). Our results suggest that rapidly regenerating stands are not immune to drought and may be as sensitive (if not more so) to soil moisture deficits that occur in response to extreme temperatures and drying. These findings are especially important for the western BP ecozone, where anthropogenic disturbance is significant and lengthy periods of regional drying occur with greater frequency [\(Petrone et al. 2007\)](#page-12-24).

Detailed (high spatial resolution) remote-sensing studies, e.g., [Michaelin et al. \(2011\),](#page-12-10) have also examined pre- and post-drought trembling aspen mortality using aerial photography over a broad area extending through the southern boreal forest of Alberta and Saskatchewan. They found that mature aspen parkland was most severely affected by the 3-year (2001–2003) western boreal drought, resulting in net reduction of boreal aspen biomass by 20% 4 years following the drought. Within 150 immature and mature aspen plots within the same regional area studied by [Michaelin et al. \(2011\),](#page-12-10) [Hogg et al. \(2008\)](#page-12-43) noted similar declines in aspen health as a result of the same drought, with regional declines in stem growth approximating 30% through the drought period and into the following years. Spatial declines in growth were directly attributed to stress from low soil moisture [\(Hogg](#page-12-43) [et al. 2008;](#page-12-43) [Michaelin et al. 2011\)](#page-12-10).

### **Climate similarities to the western boreal forest drought of 2001–2003**

The climate conditions for drought in this study were similar to that found during the 2001–2003 severe western boreal drought described in [Krishnan et al. \(2006\),](#page-12-14) [Barr et al. \(2007\),](#page-11-2) [Kljun et al.](#page-12-6) [\(2007\),](#page-12-6) and others. Within a mature aspen stand located in southcentral Saskatchewan, [Kljun et al. \(2007\)](#page-12-6) describe the first 2 years of study as having relatively normal (during year 2000) and slightly above mean *T*<sup>a</sup> but with a growing season that started earlier than usual. Similar predrought conditions were observed in this study, with slightly warmer (and wetter) than normal conditions during the 2 years leading up to drought. At the height of drought (2002), the mature aspen stand experienced colder-thannormal *T*<sup>a</sup> and a late start to the growing season (end of April) [\(Kljun et al. 2007\)](#page-12-6). Similarly, we also observed colder-than-normal *T*<sup>a</sup> during the year of initial drought conditions, where mean *T*as were 7 °C colder. A late spring thaw (6 April 2009) also occurred during the first drought year in this study. [Krishnan et al. \(2006\)](#page-12-14) found significant declines in *P* to approximately 55% of the 30-year mean over the 3-year drought period in Saskatchewan. In this study, annual cumulative *P* measured in 2010, when the drought was most severe, was 59% of the 30-year regional mean.

### **Implications of drought on rates of aspen growth**

Regenerating aspen within the BP ecozone experienced significant declines in height, leaf area, and stem-diameter growth. Sensitivity to drought may have been buffered, in part, by a relatively high water table in 2009 [\(Table 1\)](#page-4-0), measured at the boundary between the peatland–pond ecosystem adjacent to the two harvested sites and within close proximity of each tower. Declining growth rates occurred with greatest magnitude in 2010, which was approximately 1 year after declines in *P* were observed [\(Table 2\)](#page-6-0). During this period, mean VMC and depth to groundwater were lower than in other years, which contributed to drought stress and reduced growth rates [\(Table 1\)](#page-4-0). [Bernier et al. \(2002\)](#page-11-9) suggest that direct linkages between transpiration and the closure of leaf stomata as a result of declines in soil moisture conditions might significantly impact the regeneration of aspen in areas prone to drought (e.g., [Hogg et al. 2005\)](#page-12-44). In our study, LAIe was most significantly affected in 2010 (5% of predrought growth rates), followed by declines in growth rates of vegetation height (11% of predrought growth rates). [Krishnan et al. \(2006\)](#page-12-14) found a 30%–40% decrease in aspen leaf area and stem growth following the 2001–2003 drought within a mature aspen stand, indicating that regenerating stands may be more sensitive to drought, assuming linear growth rates under "normal" conditions. ## **Conclusions**

This research has demonstrated that the atmospheric demand of the dry climate exerts a strong control on stand ET and CO2 uptake but is ultimately limited by moisture available in the rooting zone to meet the demands of transpiration. As long as soil moisture exceeds wilting-point values following harvest, ET and CO2 uptake increase with growth of foliage (LAIe and canopy height). The major results of this study are as follows.

- 1. The 5 years examined progressed from wet periods in 2007 and 2008 to a prolonged dry period starting midway in 2009 and lasting into 2011.
- 2. ET fluxes rebounded quickly as a result of the combined influence of bare soils, new vegetation foliage growth, and wet conditions found within the first 2 years following harvest. The drought through 2010 and 2011 resulted in much lower ET levels similar to those found in 2007 by 2011. Rapid redevelopment of leaf area as a result of high-density regeneration of aspen through rapid root suckering returns the stand ET to near (60% of) preharvest conditions and exhibits rapid increases in WUE within 3 years.
- 3. WUE increases through wet years at the two sites, but decreases as a result of drought by 2010 and into 2011 (lagging the dry period) could be indicative of possible stomatal sensitivity to moisture stress because, over time, vegetation photosynthetic mechanisms and health may start to decline (e.g., [Barr](#page-11-10) [et al. 2006\)](#page-11-10). A rapid increase in WUE between years 1 and 2 coincides with rapid foliage development.
- 4. The NSA and SSA were both net sources of CO2 through the May–August period in the first 2 years of growth, as expected, but became a net sink for C by the end of the third year. NEP became negative again, through years 4 and 5 as a result of sensitivity to *R*<sup>e</sup> and drought (lag) in 2010 (2011). This indicates that these rapidly regenerating stands within the boreal forest may eventually become a net sink for C during the optimal growth conditions within the summer (May, June, July, and August) months. However, during the remainder of the year, the system could still be a significant source of C. Thus, there is a need to further examine full-year CO2 and ET fluxes from these rapidly regenerating deciduous aspen clone stands. LUE also increased slightly through years 1–3 as a result of increased foliage but was affected by stomatal sensitivity to drought in years 4 and 5, resulting in decreased LUE.
- 5. Vegetation foliage amount increased substantially during the first 2 years of study and declined in the final 3 years of study as a result of drought. Stand vegetation heights increased by 1.15 m (SD = 1.10 m at the SSA) and 1.24 m (SD = 1.34 at the NSA), on average, between 2008 and 2011. LAIe also doubled at the SSA and NSA between years 1 and 2, but declined significantly to between approximately 10% and 20% increases, respectively, from years 3 to 5.
- 6. The effects of low moisture availability were also observed in the partitioning of *Q* e/*Q*\*. The percent contribution of *Q* <sup>e</sup> started to decrease by 2010 as a result of very dry soil moisture conditions. ET continued to be suppressed in 2011 even though soil moisture conditions and *P* began to increase, indicating a lag in soil moisture recharge and water use by vegetation.
- 7. During the wet periods, namely 2007, 2008, and into 2009, VMC and VPD had the greatest correspondence with ET fluxes. Plant foliage in the rapid-development phase plus groundmoisture contributions to flux may have contributed to this linkage. By 2010, VMC and *Q*\* became greater drivers as a result of greater foliage and sensitivity and competition for light resources.

Thus, as a result of their clonal structure, the intact root systems following the harvesting of aboveground biomass means that aspen are adept at making the most of the available resources (i.e., moisture, nutrients, and light) and rapidly recovering in the years immediately following harvest. However, they are also sensitive to prolonged dry periods and drought and may become significant sources of CO2 through reduction in growth rates. This has a profound effect on the potential hydrological response of aspen-dominated catchments that experience a range of disturbances that will result in the wholesale removal of mature aboveground biomass. Thus, as forestry and other naturalresource industries operating within the BP ecozone are mandated to manage pre- and post-disturbance water yields, information on this early chronosequence trajectory of regeneration and its use of soil moisture as influenced by climate cycles to meet the atmospheric demand of this subhumid climate are vital information around which to base harvest and regeneration strategies.
