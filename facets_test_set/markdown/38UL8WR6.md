## Legacy Arsenic Pollution of Lakes Near Cobalt, Ontario, Canada: Arsenic in Lake Water and Sediment Remains Elevated Nearly a Century After Mining Activity Has Ceased

Dale D. Sprague & Jesse C. Vermaire

Received: 10 September 2017 /Accepted: 12 February 2018 /Published online: 28 February 2018 # Springer International Publishing AG, part of Springer Nature 2018

Abstract Century old mine tailings in the Cobalt and Silver Center areas are widely dispersed throughout the terrestrial and aquatic environments and contain high concentrations of arsenic. Arsenic concentrations were found to be as high as 972 μg/L in surface waters and 10,800 mg/kg in lake sediment. The mean values for arsenic in surface waters and sediment from 9 lakes directly influenced by mining activity were 431 μg/L and 1704 mg/kg, respectively, whereas in the 12 control lakes with no mining activity in their catchment had mean values of 2.2 μg/L and 11 mg/kg in their water and sediment, respectively. Lakes impacted by downstream tailing migration (n = 4) were also assessed and had intermediate concentrations of arsenic. Principal component analysis identified contaminated lakes as having different geochemical signatures than control lakes but lake sediment that was sampled below tailings in contaminated lakes, deposited pre-mining, can resemble the geochemistry of those found in control lakes. ### 1 Introduction

Legacy contamination is a global environmental problem and legacy arsenic pollution can be a common result of historical mining activity in Canada (Wang and Mulligan [2006](#page-15-0); Houben et al. [2016](#page-14-0); Thienpont et al. [2016](#page-15-0)). Cobalt is a historical silver mining town in northeastern Ontario, Canada, named after the BCobalt bloom^ (i.e., erythrite) that was famously associated with many of the major silver discoveries in the area. Mining in Cobalt boomed in the early 1900s, but by the 1930s, the majority of the mining operations shut down

![](_page_0_Picture_14.jpeg)

with the exception of some limited mining activity that continued until 1989; however, no milling or tailing production occurred in the region since the 1930s. In Cobalt, silver and cobalt ore were the primary commodities, and by 1962, more than 420 million ounces of silver and nearly 11 million kg of cobalt were produced. Arsenic was mineralogically associated with many of the complex silver, and cobalt ores in the Cobalt mining camp and were mainly present as Co-Ni arsenides but were also present as arsenates and sulfararsenides (Petruk, [1971;](#page-15-0) Boyle, [1968\)](#page-14-0) and as such are naturally elevated in the host rock around Cobalt. However, the arsenic was not removed during processing and instead remained in the waste rock and tailing material that was conveniently dumped into nearby depressions, which were often wetlands and lakes. The arsenic associated with the waste material amounts to an estimated 71,689,435 kg of arsenic in the surface environment (Thomson [1964](#page-15-0)). The tailings also contain abundant amounts of carbonates that maintain a neutral to weakly acidic environment (Dumaresq [1993](#page-14-0); Sprague et al. [2016](#page-15-0)).

These legacy mine tailings remain on land, in rivers, and in lakes and are freely migrating throughout the environment today (Sprague et al. [2016](#page-15-0)). In the Cobalt region, arsenic concentrations in tailings can vary greatly depending on the location and the enrichment processes occurring but are orders of magnitude greater than Canadian Sediment Quality Guidelines for the Protection of Aquatic Life Probable Effect Level (PEL) of 17 mg/kg (CCME [1999](#page-14-0)). Generally speaking, documented studies from the Cobalt area have found arsenic concentrations in tailings to be anywhere between 417 and 17,900 mg/kg with typical values being between 800 and 1500 mg/kg (Hawley [1980;](#page-14-0) Dumaresq [1993](#page-14-0); Sprague et al. [2016](#page-15-0)). The surface water concentrations of arsenic are typically an order of magnitude lower than sediments but are still orders of magnitude above water quality standards, which is 5 μg/L, for the protection of aquatic life (CCME [2001;](#page-14-0) Sprague et al. [2016](#page-15-0)).

Although it is not disputed that historical mining activity has increased arsenic concentrations in certain lakes around Cobalt, determining natural arsenic concentrations in lakes in the Cobalt region is complicated because of the lack of long-term environmental data and on-going migration of the tailing material and contaminated water (Sprague et al. [2016](#page-15-0); Sprague [2017](#page-15-0)). Further complications arise due to arsenic being a redox-

The second objective was to examine probable background concentrations of arsenic in contaminated lakes as potential realistic restoration targets for these sites.

## 1.1 Study Area

Sampling sites were located south of the Town of Cobalt and around the smaller mining camp of Silver Center in northeastern Ontario (Fig. [1\)](#page-2-0). Although Silver Center is no longer populated, the Town of Cobalt remains a vibrant community with a population of approximately 1100 residents.

The Cobalt mining area occurs along the north and northeastern margins of the Cobalt Embayment, part of the Huronian Supergroup, which is in contact with isolated inliers of Archean-aged basement rocks (Andrews et al. [1986\)](#page-14-0). The oldest rocks in the study area are the Archean volcanics with younger granitic rocks that intrude into them. Unconformably overlying the Archean volcanics and granites is the Gowganda Formation, which is a variable sedimentary unit characterized by conglomerate, arkose, greywacke, and argillite (Thompson [1964;](#page-15-0) McIlwaine [1970](#page-15-0); Jambor, [1971\)](#page-14-0). ## 2 Methods

### 2.1 Field Sampling

Water and sediment sampling took place in the summer of 2015. A total of 25 lakes were sampled that targeted 13 contaminated and 12 uncontaminated (control) lakes. Contaminated lakes and control (uncontaminated) lakes were initially selected based on if they had or did not have mining activities in their catchment. In some cases, control lakes downstream from mining activity were found to have been impacted by tailings migration from upstream mining activity; this was then termed a migration lake (contaminated in Table [1\)](#page-4-0). Contaminated lakes were sampled to assess the current contaminant levels in lakes adjacent to historical mining operations, whereas uncontaminated lakes were sampled to establish variances in background metal concentrations for lakes in the region. In each lake, a temperature, pH, conductivity, and dissolved oxygen profile was taken using a YSI Professional Pro series multimeter and a water sample was collected for total metal analyses at a depth of approximately 20 cm below the water's surface.

### 2.2 Laboratory Analyses

To estimate the water, organic, and mineral content of the sediment, loss on ignition was completed at 1-cm intervals for each of the sediment core. Loss on ignition followed standard procedure (Heiri et al. [2001](#page-14-0)). Briefly, water content was determined for each sample by measuring the weight loss of 1-cm3 sediment sample following heating of the sediment for 24 h at 110 °C. Similarly, the percent organic content of the sediment was determined by the weight loss of the sediment following heating to 550 °C for 4 h. Any material after loss on ignition for water and organic content was presumed to be mineral content.

For the uncontaminated lakes, sediment geochemistry was conducted on the surface sediments, at 20– 25 cm depth, and at the bottom of the sediment core for each core. For contaminated lakes, containing visible tailings in their sediment record, a more detailed sampling protocol was undertaken. For these lakes, geochemistry was conducted on the surface sediments in addition to sediment above, below, and within the tailings. If the bottom sediment was organic material instead of visible tailings, it was also analyzed. Surface water samples for total metals were unfiltered, preserved in a 0.5% HNO3 acid, and kept cool and void of oxygen prior to analysis. The metals of interest for this study were selected based on their relationship to the Cobalt metallic ores and their potential for environmental harm. ## 2.3 Obtaining Background Samples and Statistical Analyses

In addition to establishing the current concentrations of metals in the contaminated and uncontaminated

![](_page_4_Picture_9.jpeg)

lakes, the reliability and validity of the results from two approaches for establishing natural arsenic conditions were investigated in this study. The first approach for establishing background concentrations of metals was simply to sample metal concentrations below the contaminated sediments in the contaminated lakes. This is the simplest method of determining natural arsenic concentrates and reduces variability of metal concentrations due to inter-lake differences in morphometry and catchment characteristics but the mobility of arsenic and other metals in reducing and saturated conditions to move up and down core may limit the usefulness of this methodology (Nikolaidisa et al. [2004;](#page-15-0) Andrade et al. [2010](#page-14-0); Couture et al. [2010](#page-14-0)).

The second approach relied upon a fundamental assumption that lake sediment geochemistry is largely controlled by the bedrock composition in the lake catchment; this relationship has been demonstrated in southern Nunavut's boreal forest (Harris and Grunsky [2015\)](#page-14-0), Nunavut high arctic (Grunksy et al. [2014\)](#page-14-0), and adjacent areas of Temagami and Gowganda (Panahi et al. [2004](#page-15-0); Sprague [2017](#page-15-0)). The second method uses non-contaminated (control) lakes of the same, or similar, catchment geology in the Cobalt and Silver Center areas to establish a range of background arsenic values for the contaminated lakes, where possible catchments with documented mineralization were selected. Cumulative frequency curves (hereafter termed CF curves) were used as a statistical approach to determine the natural range of background conditions in control lakes. The inflection point separates natural anomalous values from the range of background variation and in most cases is a well-defined break in the linear portion of the graph (Reimann et al. [2005](#page-15-0); Matchullat et al. [2009](#page-15-0); Wei and Wen [2012\)](#page-15-0).

To investigate changes in the multivariate metal concentration data for both the water and sediment samples, principal component analysis (PCA) was carried out using the vegan package (Oksanen et al. [2016\)](#page-15-0) in R (R Core Team [2016](#page-15-0)). Before the PCA was conducted, metal concentrations were center log-ratio transformed to correct for closure and non-normal sample distribution using the robCompositions package (Templ et al. [2011](#page-15-0)) in R (R Development Core Team 2016). PCA was selected because it is a versatile multivariate statistical method, commonly used in geochemical studies,

# 3.2 Relationship Between Surface Waters and Lake Sediment

There is a strong relationship between the arsenic concentration in surface water and surface sediments in the study lakes ( $r^2 = 0.7919$ , p = < 0.01) (Fig. 4) but arsenic concentrations in the surface sediment are generally an order of magnitude greater than the water. For contaminated lakes whose surface sediments are tailings, there is likely exchange between the water column and the tailings, which can contribute to this relationship, especially if the redoxcline is above the water-sediment interface (Andrade et al. 2010; Kihlman and Kauppila 2010). This was particularly evident at New Lake, where a beaver dam blocks tailings from physically migrating downstream, and no tailings were detected in the lake sediment. Yet, concentrations of arsenic in surface sediments and surface waters at the sample locations of North and Middle New Lake were substantially higher than nearby lakes (Fig. 5). In New Lake, surface water arsenic concentrations were 16 times the mean arsenic concentration found in control lakes and arsenic concentrations in surface sediments were up to 40 times more enriched than arsenic in surface sediments of control lakes. The high concentrations of arsenic can likely be attributed to dissolved arsenic effluent from the contaminated catchments upstream (containing Ibsen Pond and Brady Lake) that flow downstream into New Lake. This site demonstrates that effluent from contaminated areas can have equivalent arsenic loading on the lake environments downstream as physical tailings migration.

### 3.3 Geochemistry of Sediment

#### 3.3.1 Contaminated Lakes

The results of the sampling program targeting contaminated lakes were variable with respect to the concentration of arsenic (and related elements) in tailings layers and in lake sediment below these layers (Fig. 5). The samples within tailings layers are clearly identified by the high concentration of arsenic, antimony, cobalt, nickel, and silver and low organic content of the sample (Table S1; supplementary data). The maximum concentration of arsenic in sediments was from Cobalt Lake where the arsenic concentration in the surficial lake sediment was 10,800 mg/kg, which is 635 times the regulatory objective of 17 mg/kg (CCME 1999). The mean value for arsenic concentration in lake sediments directly influenced by mining activity was 1704 mg/kg (100 times the objective). The mean arsenic concentrations were lowered by samples below tailings layers that were closer to background values. Within the samples that were clearly influenced by arsenic enrichment (either directly by mining or through mobilization), the mean was 1780 mg/kg. For contaminated lakes, arsenic concentrations in tailings are typically within the 1000– 3000-mg/kg range (Fig. S1; supplementary data).

## 4 Background Concentrations

## 4.1 Samples Below Tailings Layers in Contaminated Lakes

Sediment from contaminated lakes below the tailings layers did contain some samples, which appeared to be similar to the control lake samples and may approach background levels. These sediment cores include samples from Ibsen Pond, Brady Lake, Kirk Lake, South Peterson Lake, North Peterson Lake, Cart Lake, Fourclaim Lake, Kerr Lake, New Lake, Maidens Lake, Tooth Lake, and Crosswise Lake (Fig. [5\)](#page-8-0). These samples are identified by their lower arsenic values, relative to the tailings above them, and higher organic content (Table S1; supplementary data). Low silver concentration (< 3.5 mg/kg) also acted as a valuable tracer for the presence of tailings in these lakes due to its comparatively greater abundance of silver in tailings (> 10 with a mean of 102 mg/kg) (Table S1; supplementary data) and low mobility of silver compared to arsenic, especially in organic-rich material under alkaline conditions (Boyle [1968\)](#page-14-0). Using sediment samples below the tailings from these cores that predate mining in the region, the range of sediment arsenic concentrations was between 4.4 and 185 mg/kg, with a mean of 77 mg/kg and median of 62 mg/kg.

The mean values of arsenic in control lakes were substantially lower than those found in contaminated lakes. Surface sediment concentrations of arsenic averaged 21 mg/kg while mean values were between 5 and 10 mg/kg of arsenic below 5 cm depth in the sediment core. The average organic and water content of the sediment cores from control lakes was greater than those in contaminated lakes. ### 4.3 Principal Component Analysis of Sediment

The PCA conducted on the lake sediment geochemistry was effective at separating lakes influenced by mining activities from those that were not (Fig. [7\)](#page-11-0). Principal component 1 (44%) and principal component 2 (21%) was combined to explain a total of 65% of the variance in lake sediment geochemistry. Arsenic release to the environment associated with early mining in the 1900s in Cobalt resulted in contamination that persists to the present day. The legacy of mining has resulted in arsenic concentrations in sediments and water relative to pre-mining background determined for this region.

The majority of tailings in lakes throughout Cobalt are located adjacent to the mills that processed the ore. Migration of tailings from these locations is substantial and controlled by the streams and creeks that run through the low-lying tailings deposits, transporting them downstream. Previous work (Sprague et al. [2016](#page-15-0)) and this study show that even small streams have the potential to transport large amounts of arsenic-rich tailings downstream over time (e.g., Ibsen Pond and Kirk Lake). Even if physical barriers exist (e.g., beaver dams) effluent from tailings deposits can significantly alter surface water and lake sediment geochemistry in lakes downstream. This lack of containment has led to the contamination of several lakes in the area that had no historical mining activity in their catchment (e.g., New Lake, Ibsen Pond, and Kirk Lake).

Arsenic sampled from the sediment of contaminated lakes greatly exceeds regulatory standards by at least a factor of 18.5 times but as high as 635 times the regulatory limit of 17 mg/kg (CCME [1999](#page-14-0); probable effect level). Surface waters from these lakes also contain alarming concentrations of arsenic and are far above the guidelines for safe drinking water, of 10 ppb (Health Canada, [2014\)](#page-14-0) and 5 μg/L for the protection of aquatic life (CCME [2001\)](#page-14-0). Some lakes that were initially selected as controls, because they were located in areas that were not mined and isolated from mining areas with extensive mining and milling activity, were also found to contain elevated concentrations of arsenic in both surface waters and sediment, likely through the migration of arsenic-rich tailings and surface water. This confirms what previous studies (Dumaresq [1993;](#page-14-0) Sprague et al. [2016](#page-15-0)) have postulated that the migration of tailings and associated wastewaters are substantial and impact an area disproportionately larger than the area of the waste itself. An excellent example of this can be found at Kirk Lake, where ongoing migration of tailings from the Beaver Mine forms a deltaic deposit

![](_page_11_Picture_9.jpeg)

along the shoreline of Kirk Lake (Sprague et al. 2016). Unbeknown without this research is that the migration of tailings impacts a significantly larger area in the lake; a curtain of tailings covers nearly the entire lake bottom with arsenic concentrations that can exceed 3600 mg/kg. This was also evident at Ibsen Pond and New Lake where the tailings in the drainage area have impacted lakes downstream that account for a lake area that is approximately seven times larger than the area of the tailings.

The situation in Cobalt is similar to the legacy arsenic contamination at Giant Mine near Yellowknife, Northwest Territories, Canada. At Giant Mine, arsenic contamination is attributed to both physical migration of tailings into Yellowknife bay (Andrade et al. 2010) as well as airborne contamination from the roasting process (Houben et al. 2016). At the Giant Mine site, arsenic concentrations from the mill can contain up to 40,000 mg/kg, while arsenic in sediment cores from the mine as well as downstream were found to be less than 3670 mg/kg (Fawcett et al. 2015). In Yellowknife bay, submerged tailings were found to be as high as 3685 mg/kg but were typically in the 1000-mg/kg range (EBA 2001; Golder 2002). Some samples were found to be in excess of 10,000 mg/kg in sediment located in areas off the mining lease (Galloway et al. 2017). Similar samples obtained from a mill in Cobalt had arsenic concentrations in excess of 218,000 mg/kg (Dumaresq 1993) and lake sediment samples of tailings material in contaminated lakes can be anywhere from 470 to 10,800 mg/kg, but are more typically between 1500 and 2500 mg/kg, indicated that the magnitude and scope of arsenic contamination in Cobalt are of similar severity to that occurring at the Giant Mine site.

Arsenic in surface waters from the control lakes are typically below the regulatory limits for drinking water and for the protection of aquatic life (CCME 2001; Health Canada 2014). However, arsenic in the most contaminated lakes, and even lakes influenced by downstream migration, contain arsenic that greatly exceed background levels and regulatory standards (Fig. 2). These waters flow out of the area into Lake Temiskaming, the head water for the Ottawa River, where an estimated 18,000 kg of arsenic is released each year (Dumaresq 1993). Arsenic toxicity is highly influenced by speciation and the arsenic in surface waters of the Cobalt area are typically in the less toxic, oxidized form As(V) which represents around 85% of

aqueous arsenic. The more toxic As(III) is found but is more prevalent in groundwater due to reducing conditions (Percival et al. 2004).

There is a strong positive relationship between arsenic concentrations in surface sediments and concentrations in surface waters ( $r^2 = 0.79$ , p = < 0.01) with an order of magnitude decrease in concentration of surface waters compared to sediment (Fig. 4). This relationship is important because contaminated sediment and surface water can act as a source of arsenic to previously uncontaminated systems. Tailings migrating away from source can act as a source of arsenic to surface waters. as in the case of Kirk Lake. Also, arsenic-rich effluent flowing through catchments with mine tailings into areas where no mining occurred can act as a source of arsenic for surface sediment. This was evident at New Lake where no tailings were observed, but high arsenic concentrations in waters and sediment were found. This process of arsenic enrichment in surface sediments via surface waters, and vice versa, can be attributed to the strong affinity of dissolved arsenic to be sequestered from solution through adsorption or coprecipitation onto mineral surfaces, particularly Mn-Fe oxides, which are effective at scavenging metals and metalloids under oxidizing conditions at the water-sediment interface (Bowell 1994; Nikolaidisa et al. 2004; Andrade et al. 2010; Couture et al. 2010). Upon burial, these oxides undergo reductive dissolution and release the previously scavenged metals to pore waters that return to the watersediment interface to be scavenged again (Couture et al. 2010). This redox loop perpetually enriches surface sediments with arsenic, which can have substantial biological implications for remediation and recovery of the lakes with relatively high concentrations of arsenic in surface sediments.

In the ice-free months, the majority of our study lakes become thermally stratified and these seasonal changes can have an impact on the mobility of arsenic. In a study on lake sediments of Great Slave Lake (a large subarctic lake with similar arsenic issues) in the Northwest Territories, Canada, Andrade et al. (2010) speculated seasonal variability may have an impact on arsenic mobilization because in summer and fall months the redoxcline can be found 2–3 cm above the water-sediment interface; this would inhibit surficial Fe-Mn oxides from scavenging upward migrating arsenic released from the submerged tailings below. A similar study on mining impacted Balmer Lake, northwestern Ontario, also noted that seasonal variability could significantly impact

![](_page_12_Picture_8.jpeg)

arsenic release during summer and fall months when reducing conditions can be found near the watersediment interface (Martin and Pedersen [2002](#page-15-0)). This study also showed that evolution of the lake, with reduced anthropogenic inputs, actually resulted in more arsenic release over time (Martin and Pedersen [2002\)](#page-15-0). They attributed this to increases in oxygen demand in the near surface sediments due to increased decomposition of organic matter that promoted reductive dissolution of Fe-Mn oxides and release of arsenic (Martin and Pedersen [2002](#page-15-0)). This process may explain why, after nearly a century since these lakes were first impacted, the arsenic concentrations remain enriched in the upper sediments even after they have been buried by new lake sediment. In fact, some lakes displayed higher concentrations of arsenic in surficial sediments than arsenic in tailings directly below them. Seasonal variability was not assessed in our study, but if the redoxcline were found above the water-sediment interface, reductive dissolution of surficial Fe-Mn oxides could be releasing metals to surface waters. Under these conditions, submerged tailings could act as a source of arsenic to surface waters, especially during ice-free months when biological activity is at its greatest.

Without historical data, realistic background arsenic concentrations for contaminated lakes are difficult to estimate but essential for remediation efforts. Using the control lake method (i.e., a space for time substitution) comes with limitations as control lakes do not necessarily represent the highly mineralized areas where historical mining occurred and it is difficult to control for inter-lake differences in dissolved organic matter, pH, and oxygen concentrations that, as well as the depositional environment, influence metal(loid) accumulation in sediments. Employing paleolimnological methods to examine metal concentration in pre-mining sediments below tailings also has limits because they are potentially influenced from the downward movement of metals from the tailings and contaminated surface sediment above them. When using sediment samples obtained from contaminated lakes, that resemble the geochemistry of control lakes (identified by the PCA confidence interval), silver concentrations were low (a less mobile metal than arsenic), organic content was high, and tailings were not visible; arsenic concentrations from lake sediment sampled below tailings layers were as high as 177 mg/kg with a mean concentration of 61 mg/kg.

## 6 Conclusion

This study has provided new and important data from lakes in the Cobalt and Silver Center mining areas. Not surprisingly, this resulted in the identification of many lakes that, prior to this study, were thought to be in a pristine state but are now known to be contaminated from the nearby mining activity. This study has shown that arsenic contamination throughout the region in areas influenced by early mining activity is severe and widespread throughout the water network. The probable effect levels for the protection of aquatic life for arsenic in sediment and surface waters were exceeded by as much as 635 times the objective (17 mg/kg; CCME [1999\)](#page-14-0) and 194 times the objective (5 μg/L; CCME [2001](#page-14-0)), respectively. Many of these lakes are either near the Town of Cobalt or drain into the nearby Lake Temiskaming, which is a recreational center and headwater for the Ottawa River. Our study was primarily concerned with investigating background conditions and conclusively determined that the Cobalt region possesses a naturally elevated geochemical background that is due to the unique, large, and extensive silver-arsenide (Cobalt-type) deposits in the region. From control lakes, the natural variation of background arsenic concentrations was determined to be as high as 14 mg/kg.

![](_page_13_Picture_7.jpeg)

<span id="page-14-0"></span>However, these determinations were from unexploited areas that likely lack any significant mineralization. With this in mind, and the fact that Cobalt boasted some of the richest silver deposits in the world, background values for arsenic concentrations (100–200 mg/kg) obtained from sediment cores below tailings from contaminated lakes in Cobalt and Silver Center may be reasonable estimates of natural background for this region and could serve as restoration targets for this legacy contaminated site.
